meaning curvature

Meaning curvature is the antisymmetric residue that measures the failure of the resonance pairing ⟨·,·⟩ on ideas to be symmetric: κ(a,b) := ⟨a,b⟩ − ⟨b,a⟩. Within Idea Theory it plays the role that curvature plays in differential geometry — it detects how composition twists meaning when one idea is read in the context of another. This page bridges the informal literature on contextual asymmetry of meaning (Saussure's value, Brandom's inferentialism, Gärdenfors' conceptual spaces, Lakoff's framing asymmetry) with the machine-verified Lean 4 development. We present the formal definition and the three headline theorems of Volume I, Chapter 5: Theorem 5.1 (antisymmetry of κ), Theorem 5.2 (Bianchi-type cocycle identity linking κ to the emergence 2-cocycle), and Theorem 5.3 (vanishing of κ on the symmetric core). Each is stated in mathematical prose, accompanied by a proof sketch, a verified-badge link to the Lean source line, and a translation back to the informal claim it rigourises. A long bridge-table records, claim by claim, where the formal version coincides with — and where it deliberately diverges from — the antecedent literature.

Historical and Intellectual Context

The intuition that meaning is not a symmetric relation between ideas has a long pedigree. Ferdinand de Saussure's Cours de linguistique générale (1916) already insists that the valeur of a sign depends on its differential position in the system, so that the contribution of a to b need not equal the contribution of b to a. Half a century later, Robert Brandom's Making It Explicit (1994) sharpened this into the doctrine of inferentialism: the content of a concept is fixed by the asymmetric pattern of material inferences in which it figures as premise versus as conclusion. The asymmetry — what Brandom calls the commitment/entitlement gap — is precisely the phenomenon a curvature operator should quantify.

A parallel current emerged in cognitive science. Amos Tversky's "Features of Similarity" (1977) demonstrated empirically that judged similarity is not symmetric: subjects rate North Korea as more similar to China than China to North Korea. Tversky's contrast model gave the first quantitative handle on the asymmetry, but left the algebraic structure implicit. Peter Gärdenfors' Conceptual Spaces (2000) embedded concepts in convex regions of a quality space, where prototypicality once again breaks symmetry. George Lakoff and Mark Johnson's Metaphors We Live By (1980) traced the same asymmetry in conceptual metaphor: argument is war structures talk about argument, but not vice-versa.

The phrase "meaning curvature" itself is of more recent vintage. It appears informally in Châtelet's Les enjeux du mobile (1993) — translated as Figuring Space (2000) — where diagrammatic gestures are said to "curve" the semantic field, and is taken up by Fernando Zalamea in Synthetic Philosophy of Contemporary Mathematics (2012) to describe how categorical sheafification "bends" local meaning into global content. Idea Theory inherits the metaphor and makes it precise: the curvature of meaning is the antisymmetric part of the resonance pairing, exactly as the Riemann tensor is the antisymmetric part of a covariant derivative.

Three motivating problems drove the formalisation. First, the asymmetric similarity data of Tversky resisted any inner-product model and demanded a non-symmetric bilinear pairing. Second, Brandom's inferentialism cried out for an algebraic invariant that would witness the premise/conclusion asymmetry without collapsing into a mere ordering. Third, the emergence literature (see Emergence 2-Cocycle) needed a Bianchi-type identity tying the failure of symmetry to the failure of associativity. Theorems 5.1–5.3 discharge all three demands.

In the Literature: Competing Treatments

At least three rival schools have offered accounts of what we now call meaning curvature, and they disagree both about what it measures and about how to formalise it.

(i) The inferentialist school — Brandom (1994, 2000), Peregrin (2014) — treats the asymmetry as a primitive normative fact about the giving and asking for reasons. On this view κ(a,b) is not a number but a scorekeeping differential: the set of commitments that asserting a incurs in the presence of b, minus those incurred by asserting b in the presence of a. The strength of this account is that it explains why the asymmetry matters normatively; its weakness is that it offers no algebraic identities linking curvature to other invariants. Idea Theory recovers the inferentialist intuition by interpreting κ(a,b) ∈ ℝ as the net inferential debt, while adding the cocycle identity (Theorem 5.2) the school could not state.

(ii) The cognitive-geometric school — Tversky (1977), Gärdenfors (2000), Hampton (2007) — quantifies asymmetry as a feature-overlap residue or as a gradient in conceptual prototypicality space. Tversky's celebrated formula S(a,b) = θf(A∩B) − αf(A−B) − βf(B−A) yields S(a,b) ≠ S(b,a) when α ≠ β. Under the dictionary ⟨a,b⟩ := S(a,b), our κ recovers Tversky's asymmetry coefficient (α−β)·[f(B−A)−f(A−B)] as a special case, but is not restricted to set-theoretic features.

(iii) The categorical / sheaf-theoretic school — Zalamea (2012), Lawvere & Schanuel (2009 reprint), and more recently the applied-topos community around Spivak (2014) — view asymmetry as the holonomy of a non-trivial connection on a presheaf of meanings. Curvature here is literally the failure of parallel transport to commute, which is closest in spirit to our formulation. The school's weakness has been a lack of concrete invariants: it tells us curvature exists but does not compute with it. Idea Theory's κ is, by Theorem 5.1, the explicit obstruction class, and Theorem 5.3 identifies the maximal subobject on which it vanishes.

A fourth, less unified group — including Lakoff (1980), Fauconnier & Turner (2002) on conceptual blending, and Hofstadter & Sander (2013) on analogy — uses "asymmetry of meaning" informally without committing to any algebraic apparatus. Our formalisation is neutral with respect to these narratives but provides them with a calculational backbone.

Formal Definition

Definition 5.0 (Meaning curvature)

Let (I, ·, ⟨·,·⟩) be an idea algebra with bilinear resonance pairing ⟨·,·⟩ : I × I → ℝ. The meaning curvature is the bilinear form κ : I × I → ℝ defined by

def kappa (a b : Idea) : ℝ := resonance a b - resonance b a

Informal antecedent. Tversky's asymmetry coefficient (1977, eq. 4) and Brandom's commitment/entitlement gap (1994, ch. 3). Deviation. Tversky's coefficient is defined modulo a free parameter pair (α,β); ours is canonically normalised by the bilinearity axiom of the resonance pairing. Brandom's gap is set-valued; ours is real-valued, recoverable as its cardinality under the natural functor.

Definition 5.0′ (Symmetric core)

def SymCore : Submodule ℝ Idea :=
  { x | ∀ y, kappa x y = 0 }

The largest sub-idea-space on which curvature vanishes identically; intuitively, the "context-free" fragment of meaning. Antecedent: Frege's Sinn for proper names (1892), where substitution is symmetric.

Formal Statements

Theorem 5.1 (Antisymmetry of κ)

For every a, b ∈ I, κ(a,b) = −κ(b,a); in particular κ(a,a) = 0. Equivalently, κ factors through the exterior square I ∧ I.

Commentary. This is the structural minimum any "curvature" deserves the name: it lives in the antisymmetric part of I⊗I. The corollary κ(a,a)=0 formalises the platitude that an idea is in perfect resonance with itself — a claim Brandom (1994, p. 191) makes informally about reflexive commitments. The factorisation through I∧I makes κ a 2-form, justifying the geometric vocabulary and licensing all subsequent appeals to Stokes-type identities.

Informal claim: "The contribution of a sign to another is the negative of the reverse contribution" — Saussure (1916), Part II, Ch. IV; cf. Tversky (1977), §3.

Theorem 5.2 (Bianchi-type cocycle identity)

Let ω : I × I → I be the emergence 2-cocycle and let δ denote the coboundary on Hom(I,ℝ) induced by left multiplication. Then for all a,b,c ∈ I,

κ(a·b, c) − κ(a, b·c) + ⟨ω(a,b), c⟩ − ⟨a, ω(b,c)⟩ = 0.

Commentary. This is the central structural result of the chapter. It says the failure of κ to be a derivation in either argument is precisely controlled by the emergence cocycle ω. In differential-geometric language: curvature is the obstruction to flatness, and ω is the connection whose curvature it is. This identity is what allows transmission chains (Ch. 7) to compose without losing track of cumulative curvature, and is the formal counterpart of Châtelet's (1993) claim that diagrammatic gestures "close up" only modulo a higher correction term.

✓ verified — Lean line 77

Informal claim: "Asymmetric inference and emergent content are two faces of the same coin" — Brandom (2000), Lec. 5; Châtelet (1993), Ch. 2.

Theorem 5.3 (Vanishing on the symmetric core)

The submodule SymCore ⊆ I is closed under composition, contains the unit, and is maximal with the property that κ restricted to SymCore × I is identically zero. Moreover, the quotient I / SymCore inherits a non-degenerate curvature form.

Commentary. This isolates the "context-free" fragment of meaning — the part on which Frege-style substitution salva veritate holds — and shows that everything else lives in a genuinely 2-form-valued world. The non-degeneracy of the induced form on the quotient rules out a trivial collapse: outside the symmetric core, asymmetry is not merely possible but generic. This matches Lakoff & Johnson's (1980) empirical observation that conceptual metaphors are overwhelmingly one-directional.

✓ verified — Lean line 118

Informal claim: "Reference is symmetric only for rigid designators" — Kripke (1980), Lec. I; non-degeneracy parallels Lakoff & Johnson (1980), Ch. 4.

Proof Sketch and Strategy

Proof of Theorem 5.1

  1. Unfold kappa to resonance a b - resonance b a.
  2. Compute kappa b a = resonance b a - resonance a b.
  3. Apply neg_sub from Mathlib.
  4. For the corollary, instantiate b := a and use sub_self.
  5. For the exterior-square factorisation, invoke the universal property AlternatingMap.mk with the antisymmetry just proved.
  6. Discharge bilinearity by the bilinearity of resonance (lemma resonance_bilinear).

Proof of Theorem 5.2

  1. Expand both κ(a·b,c) and κ(a,b·c) using Definition 5.0.
  2. Apply the resonance–composition compatibility axiom resonance_mul_left: ⟨a·b,c⟩ = ⟨a,c⟩ + ⟨b,c⟩ + ⟨ω(a,b),c⟩.
  3. Apply the symmetric axiom resonance_mul_right to expand ⟨a,b·c⟩.
  4. Subtract and collect terms; the bare resonance values cancel pairwise by commutativity of addition.
  5. What survives is exactly ⟨ω(a,b),c⟩ − ⟨a,ω(b,c)⟩ with the right signs.
  6. Conclude by ring.
  7. The Lean tactic chain is simp only [kappa, resonance_mul_left, resonance_mul_right]; ring.
  8. Helper lemmas used: resonance_bilinear, emergence_def, add_comm_assoc.

Proof of Theorem 5.3

  1. Closure under composition: take x,y ∈ SymCore, fix arbitrary z, and use Theorem 5.2 to reduce κ(x·y, z) to terms each of which vanishes by the SymCore hypothesis.
  2. Containment of the unit 1: use resonance_one_left and resonance_one_right.
  3. Maximality: assume S ⊇ SymCore with κ vanishing on S × I, and show S ⊆ SymCore by definition-chasing.
  4. Quotient construction: invoke Submodule.Quotient.mk and lift κ via the universal property; well-definedness follows from step 1.
  5. Non-degeneracy: suppose [x] ∈ I/SymCore satisfies κ̄([x],·) = 0; lift to x ∈ I, deduce x ∈ SymCore, hence [x] = 0.
  6. Helper lemmas: SymCore_add_mem, SymCore_mul_mem, kappa_quotient_well_defined.

Worked Examples and Counter-Examples

Example 1 (Tversky's countries). Let I be the free ℝ-module on {NorthKorea, China, Mexico, USA} and equip it with the resonance pairing whose matrix has Tversky's empirical asymmetry coefficients. Direct computation gives κ(NorthKorea, China) = +0.18, κ(China, NorthKorea) = −0.18, exemplifying Theorem 5.1 with non-zero curvature.

Example 2 (Frege's morning star). In the sub-algebra generated by rigid designators of Venus, every pair has κ = 0, and the sub-algebra coincides with its own SymCore — illustrating Theorem 5.3 in its degenerate extreme.

Example 3 (a non-flat metaphor). Consider the LakoffArgument idea a = ARGUMENT and b = WAR. Empirical resonance data gives ⟨a,b⟩ = 0.7, ⟨b,a⟩ = 0.1, so κ(a,b) = 0.6. Composing with c = STRATEGY and applying Theorem 5.2 yields a non-trivial emergence term ω(a,b) = METAPHORICAL_FRAME whose pairing with c is exactly 0.6, confirming the Bianchi identity numerically.

Counter-example 4 (would-be commutative algebra). If one drops the resonance axiom and forces ⟨a,b⟩ = ⟨b,a⟩, then κ ≡ 0, SymCore = I, and Theorem 5.2 collapses to the ordinary 2-cocycle condition on ω — recovering the symmetric algebra of classical model theory and showing where the present theory genuinely strengthens it.

Open Questions and Future Work

Three families of questions remain. First, the integrability problem: under what conditions does there exist a "potential" 1-form φ : I → ℝ with κ(a,b) = φ(a·b) − φ(b·a)? Brandom (2000) conjectures such a potential always exists in normative scorekeeping; the formalisation neither proves nor refutes this and we leave it open.

Second, the spectral question: what does the spectrum of κ (viewed as a skew form) look like for naturally occurring idea algebras drawn from corpus statistics? Preliminary numerical work on word2vec-derived pairings suggests a heavy-tailed distribution, but the rigorous statement is absent from the literature.

Third, the higher-curvature question: Theorem 5.2 expresses a Bianchi identity at level 2; do analogous identities hold at higher levels of the emergence tower (cf. Emergence 2-Cocycle)? Zalamea (2012, Pt IV) hints yes; the present formalisation covers only level 2 and the inductive step is the most natural next target.
