Resonance Pairing

The resonance pairing is a function \(\rho:\mathcal{I}\times\mathcal{I}\to\mathbb{R}\) measuring the degree to which two ideas fit together, support each other, or — when negative — interfere.

Definition. A resonance pairing on \(\mathcal{I}\) is a function \(\rho\) that extends to a bilinear form \(\tilde\rho:\mathbb{R}\langle\mathcal{I}\rangle\times\mathbb{R}\langle\mathcal{I}\rangle\to\mathbb{R}\), and such that \(\tilde\rho\) is non-degenerate.

Crucially, \(\rho\) is not required to be symmetric. The decomposition \(\rho = \rho_{s}+\rho_{a}\) into symmetric and antisymmetric parts gives, on the antisymmetric side, the meaning curvature \(\kappa = \rho_{a}\) (see Curvature).

Worked example

Take Mikolov-style word embeddings \(v:\text{words}\to\mathbb{R}^{n}\) and define \(\rho(a,b)=\langle v(a),v(b)\rangle\). This \(\rho\) is symmetric, so \(\kappa=0\). Now replace it with the directed co-occurrence count \(\rho(a,b)=\#\{a\;b\}\) (occurrences of \(a\) immediately followed by \(b\)) and \(\kappa\) becomes nontrivial: "salt and pepper" appears far more often than "pepper and salt".

Hume's association as resonance

Hume's three principles of association — resemblance, contiguity, cause-and-effect — each define a resonance pairing on the algebra of perceptions. Resemblance is symmetric; contiguity in time is antisymmetric; causation is asymmetric and partly antisymmetric. The full pairing is a sum of all three.

Derived Primitives Anchored Here

The following derived primitives in the 10-tuple algebra are constructed using this concept as a structural ingredient:

  • ι — Intentionality. The intentional correlate of an idea is exactly the horizon-indexed family of resonance functionals \(\rho_h(I,-)\), made natural by \(\xi\)-restriction. A desire for water, for instance, is directed because it has a patterned resonance profile over possible satisfiers.
  • β — Rigid reference. Rigid reference requires a global section whose \(\rho\)-extension is a singleton at every horizon. The name “Aristotle” is rigid only when its resonance target remains uniquely picked out across the relevant modal horizons.
  • λ — Potentiality. Potentiality is the colimit of future resonance profiles \(\rho_{h'}(\xi(g)^{-1}I,-)\) over refinements of a horizon. A scientific concept has unrealized potential when later experimental horizons make it resonate with phenomena it does not yet explain.
  • δ — Differentiation. Differentiation selects ideas that fail to combine under \(\sigma_h\) while still satisfying \(\rho_h(I,J)\ne0\). A phoneme or signifier becomes determinate by resonating with contrasts it cannot simply absorb.
  • ψ — Signed valence. Signed valence evaluates the symmetric and antisymmetric parts of a self-idea's composition with \(I\) by resonance against \(e\). Attraction and aversion are thus not added affects but positive or negative resonance traces of the agent's compositional relation to an idea.

Resonance is the methodological bridge between formal algebra and directed meaning. This is why intentionality, reference, potentiality, differentiation, and valence can appear primitive in phenomenological or semantic theories but derived in the 10-tuple. The choice of horizon category and the use of \(\rho\) together determine which directed relations are available.
