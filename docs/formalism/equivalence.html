Structural Equivalence

Two idea algebras are structurally equivalent if there is a bijection between their carriers that preserves every piece of structure.

Definition. A morphism of idea algebras \(\Phi:\mathcal{I}\to\mathcal{I}'\) is a graded monoid homomorphism such that \(\rho'(\Phi a,\Phi b)=\rho(a,b)\), \(\sigma'(\Phi a,\Phi b)=\Phi\sigma(a,b)\), \(\nu'(\Phi a,\Phi b)=\Phi\nu(a,b)\), and \(\omega'(\Phi a,\Phi b)=\omega(a,b)\). An isomorphism is a bijective morphism.

Theorem 8 says that a graded monoid isomorphism that preserves \(\rho,\sigma,\nu,\omega\) automatically preserves \(\eta\), \(\kappa\), and the cohomology class of \(\omega\) — i.e. the Aufhebung and curvature data.

Why this matters

It tells us when two seemingly different theories of ideas — say, a Smolensky tensor-binding model and a Plate HRR model — are the same theory dressed differently. Conversely, if two algebras have different curvature classes, no relabeling can make them agree.

Derived Primitives Anchored Here

The following derived primitives in the 10-tuple algebra are constructed using this concept as a structural ingredient:

  • β — Rigid reference. Rigid reference is reference preserved up to structural equivalence across horizons, not mere sameness of spelling or local description. The same theorem or person can be named rigidly when every admissible isomorphism carries the name to the corresponding singleton extension.
  • χ — Schema / Paradigm. A paradigm is a connected component of \(\mathcal{H}\), and within such a component horizons are equivalent by chains of translations. Kuhnian normal science is a region where theories remain mutually interpretable up to this structural equivalence.
  • R — Reflexion. Self-equivalence is a fixed point: the algebra can map to a representation of itself and return without loss of structure. A reflective theory is stable when its self-interpretation is isomorphic to the theory it interprets.

Structural equivalence supplies the invariance criterion behind rigidity, paradigms, and reflexive stability. This is why philosophers may disagree about whether identity, schema, or self-reference are primitive: they may be choosing different equivalence relations on horizons and idea algebras. Once \(\xi\) and the admissible isomorphisms are fixed, those derived primitives become determinate.
