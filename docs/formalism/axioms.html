Axioms of an Idea Algebra

An idea algebra in its full form is a 10-tuple \((\mathcal{H};\,\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\) consisting of a horizon category \(\mathcal{H}\), a carrier set, a composition with identity, a resonance pairing, an Aufhebung decomposition, an emergence cocycle, and a grading — with an implicit horizon functor \(\xi:\mathcal{H}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{IdeaAlg}_8\) that fibres the eight operations over \(\mathcal{H}\). The bare idea algebra \((\mathcal{I},\circ,e)\) drops everything except composition; the 8-tuple idea algebra \((\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\) is the special case where \(\mathcal{H}=\mathbf{1}\) (a single horizon).

Definition (8-tuple idea algebra).

An 8-tuple idea algebra is a tuple \((\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\) such that

  1. \((\mathcal{I},\circ,e)\) is a monoid: \(\circ\) is associative, \(e\circ a = a\circ e = a\) for all \(a\).
  2. \(\rho:\mathcal{I}\times\mathcal{I}\to\mathbb{R}\) extends bilinearly to \(\mathbb{R}\langle\mathcal{I}\rangle\) and is non-degenerate after this extension.
  3. For each pair \((a,b)\) there exist \(\sigma(a,b),\nu(a,b),\eta(a,b)\in\mathbb{R}\langle\mathcal{I}\rangle\) with \(a\circ b=\sigma(a,b)+\nu(a,b)+\eta(a,b)\), unique modulo \(\ker\rho\).
  4. \(\omega:\mathcal{I}\times\mathcal{I}\to A\) (with \(A\) abelian) is a normalized 2-cocycle: \(\partial\omega=0\) and \(\omega(e,a)=\omega(a,e)=0\).
  5. \(\deg:\mathcal{I}\to G\) for some totally ordered abelian group \(G\), and \(\deg(a\circ b)=\deg a+\deg b\).

Write \(\mathbf{IdeaAlg}_8\) for the category whose objects are 8-tuple idea algebras and whose morphisms are tuple-component-preserving monoid homomorphisms (preserving \(\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta\) up to \(\ker\rho\), respecting \(\omega\) up to coboundary, and order-preserving on \(\deg\)).

Definition (10-tuple / full idea algebra).

A full (10-tuple) idea algebra is a tuple \((\mathcal{H};\,\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\) together with a horizon functor \(\xi\) such that

  1. \(\mathcal{H}\) is a small category — the horizon category. Objects \(h\in\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{H})\) are horizons (contexts, language-games, paradigms, possible worlds, time-slices, frames). Morphisms \(f:h'\to h\) are horizon-translations (refinement, restriction, framing, accessibility).
  2. \(\xi:\mathcal{H}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{IdeaAlg}_8\) is a presheaf of 8-tuple idea algebras: to each horizon \(h\) it assigns an 8-tuple \[\xi(h) \;=\; \bigl(\mathcal{I}_h,\circ_h,e_h,\rho_h,\sigma_h,\nu_h,\eta_h,\omega_h,\deg_h\bigr),\] and to each \(f:h'\to h\) a restriction homomorphism \(\xi(f):\xi(h)\to\xi(h')\) in \(\mathbf{IdeaAlg}_8\), with \(\xi(\mathrm{id}_h)=\mathrm{id}_{\xi(h)}\) and \(\xi(g\circ f)=\xi(f)\circ\xi(g)\) (contravariance).
  3. The bare carriers fit together: \(\mathcal{I} \;=\; \int_{\mathcal{H}}\xi\) (the Grothendieck construction / total category of the presheaf), so an "idea" globally is a horizon-indexed family of local ideas, glued by the restriction maps.
  4. Axioms (1)–(5) of the 8-tuple definition hold in each fibre \(\xi(h)\).
  5. Sheaf condition (optional, for hermeneutic models). When \(\mathcal{H}\) carries a Grothendieck topology, \(\xi\) is required to be a sheaf: for every covering family \(\{f_i:h_i\to h\}\), local sections \(a_i\in\mathcal{I}_{h_i}\) that agree on overlaps glue uniquely to a section \(a\in\mathcal{I}_h\). This is the formal seat of T13 (Hermeneutic Sheaf).

The 8-tuple algebra of axiom (1) above is recovered from the 10-tuple by setting \(\mathcal{H}=\mathbf{1}\) (the terminal one-object category): \(\xi\) collapses to a single 8-tuple and all horizon-relativity vanishes.

Definition (derived primitives).

A number of features that other formalisms posit as separate primitives are derived from the 10-tuple, not added to it. None expand the signature:

  • τ (temporality) — a totally-ordered subcategory \(\mathcal{T}\hookrightarrow\mathcal{H}\) of time-slices.
  • ι (intentionality / aboutness) — a global section \(\bigl(\rho_h(I,\,\cdot)\bigr)_{h\in\mathcal{H}}\) of the resonance fibration.
  • π (perspective / agent) — a connected sub-category \(\mathcal{H}_\pi\subseteq\mathcal{H}\) of horizons accessible to an agent.
  • φ (affect / conatus) — the gradient of \(\deg\) along morphisms of \(\mathcal{H}\).
  • δ (Saussurean differentiation) — the negative trace of \(\sigma_h\).
  • β (rigid reference) — a constant global section of \(\xi\).
  • λ (potentiality) — the colimit of \(\rho\) along future-refinements.
  • χ (schema / paradigm / episteme) — a connected component of \(\mathcal{H}\).
  • μ (credence) — a measure on \(\mathcal{F}(h):=\xi(h)\).
  • ψ (signed valence) — the symmetric+antisymmetric decomposition of \(\sigma_h(\text{self},I)\).
  • R (reflexion) — fixed-point self-application of \(\xi\) (i.e., \(\xi\) regarded as an object of its own target category).

See the horizon-functor page for the full derivation of each.

Worked example A: free 8-tuple idea algebra on two generators

Let \(X=\{x,y\}\) and let \(\mathcal{I}=X^{*}\) be the free monoid (finite words). Compose by concatenation; \(e\) is the empty word. Define \(\rho(u,v)=\) the length of the longest common prefix; then \(\rho\) is non-symmetric in the obvious way once we extend it to \(\mathbb{R}\langle X^{*}\rangle\). Take \(\sigma(u,v)=\) common prefix, \(\nu(u,v)=\) the part of \(u\) past the common prefix, \(\eta(u,v)=\) the suffix of \(v\) after the common prefix. Set \(\omega(u,v) = |uv| - |u| - |v| = 0\) (an honest cocycle, trivial here). Let \(\deg(w) = |w|\) into \(G=\mathbb{Z}\). All five 8-tuple axioms are satisfied; this is the smallest non-trivial 8-tuple idea algebra.

Worked example B: a non-trivial 10-tuple — the indexical pair

Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be the two-object category \(\{h_1\rightrightarrows h_2\}\) with two parallel non-identity morphisms \(f,g:h_1\to h_2\) (a minimal example with non-trivial monodromy). Take \(\xi(h_1)=\xi(h_2)=\) the free 8-tuple of Example A, but let \(\xi(f)\) and \(\xi(g)\) act on the alphabet by different permutations of \(\{x,y\}\): say \(\xi(f)=\mathrm{id}\) and \(\xi(g)\) swaps \(x\leftrightarrow y\). Then a section \(a\in\mathcal{I}_{h_2}\) restricts to two genuinely distinct elements \(\xi(f)(a)\) and \(\xi(g)(a)\) in \(\mathcal{I}_{h_1}\); the sheaf condition fails, and the obstruction class lives in \(H^1(\mathcal{H};\underline{\eta})\). This is the minimal instance of T14 (Indexical Pullback): a section of "meaning" exists locally on each horizon but cannot be glued globally — the categorial bare bones of indexicality, framing effects, and Kuhnian incommensurability.
