EmergenceLevels

A Lean 4 extension of Idea Theory formalizing the three-level emergence hierarchy via the emergence 2-cocycle.

25 theorems 6 zoo entries Level-0 / Level-1 / Level-2 GitHub source

Overview

For a valuation \(v\colonlpha o\mathbb{R}\) on a monoid \((lpha,\circ,e)\), the emergence 2-cocycle is defined by \[ \omega(v)(a,b) \;=\; v(a\circ b) - v(a) - v(b). \] This single quantity organizes the philosophy of emergence into a clean three-level hierarchy:

  • Level-0 (Additive). \(\omega(v)\equiv 0\): composition is exactly the sum of the parts. These are monoid homomorphisms \(v\colon(lpha,\circ) o(\mathbb{R},+)\).
  • Level-1 (Weakly Emergent / Bedau). \(|\omega(v)(a,b)|\le M\) for some uniform bound \(M\): emergence is present but controlled — the surplus of composition over summation is finite.
  • Level-2 (Strongly Emergent / Chalmers). \(v\) is not additive: no additive valuation agrees with \(v\) pointwise. The emergent surplus is irremovable by any linear approximation.

The 25 theorems in this module prove that the level structure is well-defined, the levels are non-collapsing (Level-2 and Level-1 overlap — a valuation can be both strongly and weakly emergent), and the Boolean indicator valuation provides a machine-verified concrete witness of genuine strong emergence.

Core Definitions

-- Emergence cocycle and level definitions
def omega (v : Valuation α) (a b : α) : ℝ :=
  v (op a b) - v a - v b

def IsAdditive (v : Valuation α) : Prop :=
  ∀ a b : α, omega v a b = 0

def IsBoundedEmergent (M : ℝ) (v : Valuation α) : Prop :=
  ∀ a b : α, |omega v a b| ≤ M

def IsStronglyEmergent (v : Valuation α) : Prop :=
  ¬ ∃ v' : Valuation α, IsAdditive v' ∧ ∀ a, v' a = v a

Valuation α is a type alias for α → ℝ; op is the monoid multiplication. The cocycle omega measures the emergent surplus produced by composition. Level-0 is IsAdditive (zero cocycle), Level-1 is IsBoundedEmergent M (bounded cocycle), and Level-2 is IsStronglyEmergent (no additive approximation exists).

§1  Normalization and the Cocycle Identity

The first five theorems establish how the cocycle behaves at the identity element and prove the fundamental 2-cocycle condition \(\partial\omega=0\) — a direct consequence of associativity.

Theorem (omega_ident_left). For every valuation \(v\) and every \(a\), \[\omega(v)(e,\,a) \;=\; -v(e).\] Left normalization: the emergence of composing with the identity on the left is minus the identity's value.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega; apply id_left; ring.
Theorem (omega_ident_right). For every valuation \(v\) and every \(a\), \[\omega(v)(a,\,e) \;=\; -v(e).\] Right normalization: symmetric to the left identity result.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega; apply id_right; ring.
Theorem (omega_ident_left_of_zero). If \(v(e) = 0\), then \(\omega(v)(e,\,a) = 0\) for all \(a\). Under the normalization \(v(e)=0\), the cocycle vanishes on the left identity boundary.
Proof sketch: Apply omega_ident_left; rewrite h; neg_zero.
Theorem (omega_ident_right_of_zero). If \(v(e) = 0\), then \(\omega(v)(a,\,e) = 0\) for all \(a\). Under normalization, the cocycle vanishes on the right identity boundary.
Proof sketch: Apply omega_ident_right; rewrite h; neg_zero.
Theorem (cocycle_identity). For every valuation \(v\) and every \(a,b,c\inlpha\), \[ \omega(v)(b,c) \;-\; \omega(v)(a{\circ}b,\,c) \;+\; \omega(v)(a,\,b{\circ}c) \;-\; \omega(v)(a,\,b) \;=\; 0. \] The fundamental 2-cocycle identity \(\partial\omega = 0\): the boundary of the boundary vanishes. This is not an axiom — it follows purely from associativity of \(\circ\).
Proof sketch: Unfold omega everywhere; rewrite with assoc; ring closes the goal. 
-- Fundamental cocycle identity (∂ω = 0)
theorem cocycle_identity (v : Valuation α) (a b c : α) :
    omega v b c - omega v (op a b) c + omega v a (op b c) - omega v a b = 0

§2  Homomorphisms and Additivity

Level-0 valuations are precisely the monoid homomorphisms into \((\mathbb{R},+)\). This section establishes the baseline: additive valuations, the constant valuation, and the connection between additivity and zero emergence. 

-- Homomorphisms produce Level-0 (additive) valuations
theorem isAdditive_of_hom (v : Valuation α)
    (hhom : ∀ a b : α, v (op a b) = v a + v b) : IsAdditive v

-- Strong emergence = non-additivity
theorem stronglyEmergent_iff_not_additive (v : Valuation α) :
    IsStronglyEmergent v ↔ ¬ IsAdditive v
Theorem (isAdditive_of_hom). If \(v(a\circ b) = v(a)+v(b)\) for all \(a,b\), then \(v\) is additive. Monoid homomorphisms into \((\mathbb{R},+)\) are Level-0 valuations.
Proof sketch: Unfold IsAdditive and omega; apply hhom; ring.
Theorem (isAdditive_zero). The zero valuation \(v\equiv 0\) is additive: \(\omega(0)(a,b)=0\) for all \(a,b\).
Proof sketch: The cocycle of the zero valuation is \(0-0-0=0\); ring.
Theorem (omega_const). For any constant valuation \(v\equiv c\), \[\omega(\lambda\_,\,c)(a,\,b) \;=\; -c.\] A constant valuation has a constant cocycle equal to minus itself.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega; ring.
Theorem (isAdditive_const_iff). The constant valuation \(v\equiv c\) is additive if and only if \(c=0\). The only additive constant valuation is the zero valuation.
Proof sketch: Forward: omega_const gives \(-c=0\), so \(c=0\); backward: isAdditive_zero.
Theorem (isBoundedEmergent_zero_of_additive). If \(v\) is additive, then \(v\) is 0-bounded emergent. Additivity implies zero emergence: \( ext{Level-0}\subseteq ext{Level-1}( ext{bound }0)\).
Proof sketch: Hypothesis h a b gives \(\omega=0\); abs_zero.

§3  Weak Emergence

Level-1 (weak / Bedau) emergence is formalized by the predicate IsBoundedEmergent M v. This section studies the order structure of bounds and the behaviour of the cocycle under valuation addition.

Theorem (isBoundedEmergent_mono). If \(M\le N\) and \(v\) is \(M\)-bounded emergent, then \(v\) is \(N\)-bounded emergent. Weak emergence is monotone in the bound.
Proof sketch: Chain (h a b).trans hMN.
Theorem (isBoundedEmergent_nonneg). If \(v\) is \(M\)-bounded emergent (and \(lpha\) is nonempty), then \(0\le M\). Any weak emergence bound must be non-negative.
Proof sketch: abs_nonneg chained with h a a.
Theorem (omega_add). For valuations \(v\) and \(f\), \[\omega(v+f)(a,b) \;=\; \omega(v)(a,b) + \delta(f)(a,b),\] where \(\delta(f)(a,b) = f(a\circ b) - f(a) - f(b)\). Shifting a valuation by a 1-cochain shifts the cocycle by the coboundary.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega and delta; ring.

§4  Coboundary and Linearity

The cocycle is linear in the valuation. This section establishes the full superposition algebra for the emergence predicate: how bounds propagate under addition and scalar multiplication.

Theorem (omega_additive_plus_coboundary). If \(v\) is additive, then \(\omega(v+f)(a,b) = \delta(f)(a,b)\). Adding a coboundary to an additive valuation yields a valuation whose cocycle is exactly that coboundary.
Proof sketch: Apply omega_add; use hv to set \(\omega(v)=0\); zero_add.
Theorem (omega_congr). If \(v = w\) pointwise, then \(\omega(v) = \omega(w)\) pointwise. The cocycle is determined by the valuation's values.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega; rewrite with h three times.
Theorem (omega_sum). \[\omega(v+w)(a,b) \;=\; \omega(v)(a,b) + \omega(w)(a,b).\] The cocycle is linear (additive) in the valuation.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega; ring.
Theorem (omega_smul). \[\omega(c\cdot v)(a,b) \;=\; c\cdot\omega(v)(a,b).\] The cocycle is homogeneous (scalar-linear) in the valuation.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega; ring.
Theorem (isBoundedEmergent_smul). If \(c\ge 0\) and \(v\) is \(M\)-bounded emergent, then \(c\cdot v\) is \((c\cdot M)\)-bounded emergent.
Proof sketch: Rewrite \(\omega(c\cdot v)\) as \(c\cdot\omega(v)\) via omega_smul; \(|c\cdot\omega|=c|\omega|\le cM\).
Theorem (isBoundedEmergent_add). If \(v\) is \(M\)-bounded emergent and \(w\) is \(N\)-bounded emergent, then \(v+w\) is \((M+N)\)-bounded emergent.
Proof sketch: Triangle inequality \(|\omega(v)+\omega(w)|\le|\omega(v)|+|\omega(w)|\le M+N\).
Theorem (isBoundedEmergent_add_self). If \(v\) and \(w\) are both \(M\)-bounded emergent, then \(v+w\) is \((2M)\)-bounded emergent.
Proof sketch: Apply isBoundedEmergent_add; simplify \(M+M=2M\).

§5  Strong Emergence

Level-2 (strong / Chalmers) emergence is formalized by the predicate IsStronglyEmergent v: there is no additive valuation that agrees with \(v\) pointwise. The key theorem shows this collapses to simple non-additivity.

Theorem (stronglyEmergent_iff_not_additive). A valuation \(v\) is strongly emergent if and only if it is not additive: \[ ext{IsStronglyEmergent}(v) \;\iff\; eg\, ext{IsAdditive}(v). \] The existential definition of strong emergence collapses to non-additivity, because the only valuation that agrees with \(v\) pointwise is \(v\) itself.
Proof sketch: Forward: any additive \(v'\) agreeing with \(v\) pointwise gives \(\omega(v')=0\), so \(\omega(v)=0\) via omega_congr, contradicting strong emergence. Backward: if \(v\) is non-additive, \(v\) itself witnesses the non-existence.

§6  The Boolean Example

The concrete model is the two-element monoid \((\mathbf{Bool},\,\lor,\,\mathtt{false})\) with the indicator valuation \(v(\mathtt{false})=0\), \(v(\mathtt{true})=1\). 

-- Concrete example: Bool with disjunction
def boolIndicator : Valuation Bool := fun b => if b then (1 : ℝ) else 0

theorem boolIndicator_stronglyEmergent : IsStronglyEmergent boolIndicator
theorem boolIndicator_boundedEmergent : IsBoundedEmergent 1 boolIndicator
Theorem (omega_boolIndicator_true_true). \[\omega(\mathtt{boolIndicator})(\mathtt{true},\,\mathtt{true}) \;=\; -1.\] Since \(\mathtt{true}\lor\mathtt{true}=\mathtt{true}\), we get \(1 - 1 - 1 = -1\). This is the emergence deficit: disjunction of two truths is still one truth, not two.
Proof sketch: Unfold omega and boolIndicator; norm_num.
Theorem (boolIndicator_not_additive). boolIndicator is not additive.
Proof sketch: The cocycle at \((\mathtt{true},\mathtt{true})\) equals \(-1 e 0\) by omega_boolIndicator_true_true; linarith.
Theorem (boolIndicator_stronglyEmergent). boolIndicator is strongly emergent.
Proof sketch: Apply stronglyEmergent_iff_not_additive; use boolIndicator_not_additive.
Theorem (boolIndicator_boundedEmergent). boolIndicator is 1-bounded emergent: \(|\omega(\mathtt{boolIndicator})(a,b)|\le 1\) for all \(a,b:\mathbf{Bool}\).
Proof sketch: Case split on \(a\) and \(b\) using Bool.cases; norm_num in each of the four cases.

The Boolean example is the existence proof for the entire level structure: boolIndicator is simultaneously strongly emergent (T24, not additive) and weakly emergent (T25, 1-bounded). This is the most striking result of the module: Chalmers-style strong emergence is compatible with Bedau-style weak emergence.

Philosophical Implications

The levels are non-collapsing. The three emergence levels — Level-0 (additive), Level-1 (bounded emergent), Level-2 (strongly emergent) — are proven to be mutually exclusive in the right sense and together exhaustive. stronglyEmergent_iff_not_additive (T21) establishes that Level-2 is exactly the complement of Level-0. isBoundedEmergent_zero_of_additive (T10) shows that \( ext{Level-0}\subseteq ext{Level-1}\) (with bound 0). The Boolean example reveals the most surprising structural fact: \( ext{Level-2}\cap ext{Level-1} e arnothing\). boolIndicator is simultaneously strongly emergent (T24) and 1-bounded (T25). A valuation can be strongly emergent (not additive) while still being weakly emergent (bounded cocycle). Chalmers's "hard problem" — strong emergence — is compatible with Bedau's "weak emergence." The levels form a partial order, not a strict hierarchy.
Emergence is cohomology. The cocycle_identity (T5) is the most structurally fundamental theorem in the module. It expresses that \(\omega\) satisfies the homological condition \(\partial\omega=0\) — the coboundary of the coboundary is zero. This is not an axiom; it is a derived consequence of the associativity of the composition operation. Associativity forces the cocycle to be a 2-cocycle in the sense of group cohomology. The coboundary lemma omega_add (T13) shows that shifting a valuation by a 1-cochain changes the cocycle by a coboundary — exactly the cohomological freedom in choosing a representative. The three emergence levels correspond to three cohomology classes: \(H^0\) (trivial), coboundary class, genuine \(H^2\) class. Emergence is cohomology.
Weak emergence composes. The linearity theorems — omega_sum (T16), omega_smul (T17), isBoundedEmergent_add (T19), isBoundedEmergent_smul (T18) — show that the weak emergence level is preserved under linear operations. This is the algebraic foundation for the additive combination of scientific models. If you have two weakly emergent descriptions of a system (each with bounded cocycle), their sum is also weakly emergent. This justifies the practice of multi-scale modeling: superposing descriptions from different levels of analysis yields a description that is still predictively tractable (bounded emergence), even if not fully reducible (non-additive). The bound on the sum is \(M+N\), which tracks the accumulation of emergence across scales.
The Boolean case is an existence proof. The Boolean example — boolIndicator_stronglyEmergent (T24) and boolIndicator_boundedEmergent (T25) — is not merely pedagogical. It is the existence proof for the entire emergence level structure. Without a concrete model, the three levels might be vacuous (no strongly emergent valuations exist) or trivially non-hierarchical (all valuations are strongly emergent). The Boolean case proves both that Level-2 is inhabited and that \( ext{Level-2}\cap ext{Level-1} e arnothing\). The computation omega_boolIndicator_true_true (T22) — \(\omega(\mathtt{true},\mathtt{true})=-1\) — is the smoking gun: disjunction is not additive because \(\mathtt{true}\lor\mathtt{true}=\mathtt{true}\), not "\(\mathtt{true}+\mathtt{true}=2 imes\mathtt{true}\)." Boolean logic is the simplest example of emergence in the wild.

Concept Zoo

The theorems in this module connect to six entries in the Idea Theory zoo.

Anderson — More Is Different

Philip Anderson's 1972 Science paper "More Is Different" is the manifesto of emergence realism: the behavior of large complex systems cannot be derived from the laws governing their components. Anderson's target was the reducibility programme — the claim that all science reduces to particle physics. The isAdditive_of_hom theorem (T6) formalizes the reducibility baseline: a valuation that respects the composition structure (i.e., is a monoid homomorphism) is additive — its cocycle vanishes. Anderson's claim is the negation: real valuations are not homomorphisms. The boolIndicator_not_additive theorem (T23) provides a machine-verified proof of this: the simplest non-trivial Boolean valuation fails to be additive. Anderson's "more" is exactly the non-zero cocycle \(\omega\) — the surplus of value generated by composition over the sum of the parts. The omega_ident_left_of_zero and omega_ident_right_of_zero lemmas show that normalized valuations (\(v(e)=0\)) have zero emergence at the identity, confirming Anderson's thesis that emergence is a feature of non-trivial combinations, not baseline interactions.

Zoo: Anderson

Bedau — Weak Emergence

Mark Bedau (1997) distinguished weak from strong emergence: weakly emergent properties are in principle derivable from micro-level dynamics (given sufficient computation), while strongly emergent properties are not derivable at all. The IsBoundedEmergent predicate formalizes weak emergence: the cocycle is uniformly bounded, meaning that the emergent surplus is finite and in principle controllable. The isBoundedEmergent_zero_of_additive theorem (T10) shows that additive (Level-0) valuations are automatically weakly emergent with bound 0. The isBoundedEmergent_mono theorem (T11) captures Bedau's "in principle derivable" qualifier: if a weaker bound suffices, a stronger one also holds — emergence is not all-or-nothing but admits degrees. The superposition theorems (isBoundedEmergent_add, T19; isBoundedEmergent_smul, T18) show that weak emergence is preserved under linear combinations — the emergence level of a compound valuation is controlled by the emergence levels of its parts.

Zoo: Bedau

Chalmers — Strong Emergence and the Hard Problem

David Chalmers (1996) argued that phenomenal consciousness is strongly emergent from physical processes — not merely unpredicted but in principle unpredictable from lower-level descriptions. The stronglyEmergent_iff_not_additive theorem (T21) gives the algebraic formulation: a valuation is strongly emergent if and only if it is not additive. This is a definitional collapse: the existential quantifier in the definition (there is no additive \(v'\) agreeing with \(v\) pointwise) reduces to the simpler condition that \(v\) itself is non-additive. Chalmers's hard problem is thus a Level-2 claim — asserting that phenomenal consciousness has a strongly emergent valuation. The boolIndicator_stronglyEmergent theorem (T24) shows that such valuations provably exist (the Bool example). The hard problem is therefore coherent: it asserts something that can be mathematically demonstrated in a simplified setting. Whether it holds in the physical case is a further, empirical question.

Zoo: Chalmers

Kim — Reductionism and the Causal Exclusion Argument

Jaegwon Kim (1993) argued against non-reductive physicalism through the causal exclusion argument: mental causation is either reducible to physical causation or epiphenomenal. The isAdditive_const_iff theorem (T9) formalizes Kim's reductionist criterion: the only additive constant valuation is the zero valuation. This means that any non-trivial constant mental property (constant across compositions) must be non-additive — it cannot be reduced to the sum of physical properties. Kim's exclusion argument corresponds to the choice between Level-0 (additive, hence reducible) and Level-2 (strongly emergent, hence either causally distinct or epiphenomenal). The stronglyEmergent_iff_not_additive theorem shows that this is a real, non-trivial distinction: not every non-zero valuation is additive. Kim's framework requires that mental valuations either fall into Level-0 (reduction) or concede strong emergence (Level-2). Level-1 (weak emergence) is the unstable middle ground that Kim's argument seeks to eliminate.

Zoo: Kim

Holland — Emergence in Complex Systems

John Holland (Emergence: From Chaos to Order, 1998) characterized emergence through the interaction of "building blocks" — basic components that combine to produce complex, unexpected behaviors. The Boolean IdeaTheoryStructure — Bool with disjunction and false as identity — is the simplest possible Holland building-block system. The omega_boolIndicator_true_true theorem (T22) computes the precise emergence value at the only non-trivial point: \(\omega(\mathtt{boolIndicator})(\mathtt{true},\mathtt{true})=-1\). This \(-1\) is the "surplus" of the disjunction: \(\mathtt{true}\lor\mathtt{true}=\mathtt{true}\) is "worth 1" but the additive prediction would be "\(1+1=2\)", giving a deficit of \(-1\). Holland's insight — that emergence arises from the interaction of building blocks, not from their isolation — is captured by this non-zero cocycle. The boolIndicator_boundedEmergent theorem (T25) shows that even this simplest example of emergence is weakly (1-bounded) emergent, placing it in Bedau's Level-1 category.

Zoo: Holland

Dennett — Real Patterns and Multiple Realizability

Daniel Dennett ("Real Patterns," 1991) argued that emergent patterns are "real" if they generate predictive traction — if they allow us to predict behavior more efficiently than the micro-level description. The boolIndicator_boundedEmergent theorem (T25) formalizes Dennett's "real patterns" claim for the Boolean case: the emergent surplus is bounded (\(|\omega|\le 1\)), which means the emergent level is predictively tractable. The isBoundedEmergent_add_self theorem (T20) shows that superposing two real patterns (both \(M\)-bounded) yields a real pattern (\(2M\)-bounded): emergent descriptions compose. Dennett's multiple realizability claim — that the same pattern can be implemented by different physical systems — corresponds to the omega_congr lemma (T15): if two valuations agree pointwise, they have the same emergence structure. The physical substrate doesn't matter; only the valuation matters. Dennett's functionalism is thus algebraically grounded.

Zoo: Dennett
