Overview

Ideas are strategies. The payoff function \(\rho(i,s)\) gives player \(i\)'s payoff at strategy profile \(s : \mathrm{Fin}\,n \to \alpha\); a Nash equilibrium is a profile from which no idea-holder can increase \(\rho\) by unilaterally switching ideas. The identity element \(e\) acts as the null strategy — the algebraically natural resting point that any player can adopt without affecting the product of the coalition.

The 19 theorems of GameTheory.lean constitute a complete algebraic game theory for the idea monoid: strategy selection, equilibrium existence, mixed-strategy expected value, evolutionary stability, and coalition aggregation. Every result is stated over an arbitrary type \(\alpha\) carrying an IdeaTheoryStructure instance, so the proofs are simultaneously valid for formal-language ideas, neural activation patterns, cultural schemas, and any other carrier set that satisfies the axioms.

The module is organized in five groups. Nash equilibrium theorems (T1–T10) establish the existence, characterization, and robustness of Nash equilibria in idea games. Resonance theorems (T11–T12) formalize the zero-sum substructure of any idea game via the anti-symmetric resonance pairing. Dominance and mixed-strategy theorems (T13–T16) handle iterated elimination and the expectation calculus. Evolutionary stability (T17) connects idempotency to the ESS criterion. Coalition and update theorems (T18–T19) treat collective aggregation and the independence of payoffs from prior slot values.

All 19 theorems are stated polymorphically over any type \(lpha\) that carries an IdeaTheoryStructure instance — a type class bundling a monoid structure (carrier, binary operation \(\mathbin{\circ}\), identity \(e\)) with the interpretation axioms of Idea Theory. No theorem assumes commutativity, so the results hold for both commutative idea spaces (where \(a \mathbin{\circ} b = b \mathbin{\circ} a\)) and non-commutative ones (where sequential composition matters). The only assumptions beyond the type class are finiteness (for existence results) and ordering on \(\mathbb{R}\) (for payoff comparisons).

Proof Architecture

The 19 proofs share a common vocabulary of Lean 4 lemmas. Understanding this vocabulary makes the proof sketches below easy to reconstruct from scratch.

Key Lemmas Used

  • Function.update_eq_self — updating slot \(i\) with the value already there returns the original function: update s i (s i) = s.
  • Function.update_idem — two updates to the same slot compose to the second: update (update s i a) i b = update s i b.
  • mul_le_mul_left — multiplying both sides of \(a \le b\) by a positive \(c\) preserves the inequality.
  • Finset.exists_max_image — on a non-empty finite set with a real-valued objective, a maximizer exists.
  • Finset.sum_le_sum — pointwise \(\le\) on summands lifts to the sum.
  • id_left, id_right — the monoid identity laws: \(e \mathbin{\circ} a = a\) and \(a \mathbin{\circ} e = a\).
  • List.foldl_nil, List.foldl_cons — induction principles for fold computations.

Proof Patterns

Three patterns recur across the module. First, definitional unfolding: many Nash results require only expanding the definition of IsNash and applying a single rewrite lemma. Second, algebraic closure: preservation theorems (T6–T10) proceed by showing the composed or scaled profile satisfies the same definitional inequality as the original, using the ring or order tactics. Third, measure-theoretic induction: termination (T13) and coalition (T18) results use simple natural-number induction on the cardinality of a finite set or the length of a list, respectively. No result requires transfinite induction, continuity arguments, or choice axioms beyond the finite choice implicit in Finset.exists_max_image.

Core Definitions

The following four definitions anchor the entire module. An IdeaGame bundles a player count \(n\), a strategy type \(\alpha\), and a payoff function. Best response and Nash equilibrium are defined in terms of the profile-update operation Function.update s i a, which replaces slot \(i\) of \(s\) with \(a\). 

-- IdeaGame: ideas as strategies
structure IdeaGame (α : Type u) [IdeaTheoryStructure α] where
  n      : ℕ
  payoff : Fin n → (Fin n → α) → ℝ

def identProfile (G : IdeaGame α) : Fin G.n → α := fun _ => ident
def IsBestResponse (G : IdeaGame α) (i : Fin G.n) (s : Fin G.n → α) (a : α) : Prop :=
  ∀ b : α, G.payoff i (update s i b) ≤ G.payoff i (update s i a)
def IsNash (G : IdeaGame α) (s : Fin G.n → α) : Prop :=
  ∀ (i : Fin G.n) (b : α), G.payoff i (update s i b) ≤ G.payoff i s

Nash Equilibrium Theorems

Theorems T1–T10 characterize, construct, and show the robustness of Nash equilibria in idea games. The preservation results (T6–T10) are particularly important: they show that the Nash property is invariant under the algebraic operations of the idea monoid — scaling, translation, and composition with the identity. T1 and T2 are "boundary" results: they show that degenerate games (constant payoffs, globally maximal identity profiles) are fully covered by the Nash concept. T3 and T4 are the analytic heart: they connect the global Nash condition to the local best-response condition and prove that best responses always exist on finite sets. T5 lifts this to a one-player existence theorem. T6–T10 prove that the Nash property travels well under the natural transformations of the idea monoid.

Theorem (nash_of_constant_payoff). If every player's payoff is independent of the strategy profile — formally, \(\forall i,s,t:\ \rho(i,s) = \rho(i,t)\) — then every profile is a Nash equilibrium: \[ \forall s:\ \mathrm{IsNash}(G,s). \]
Proof sketch: Each Nash deviation inequality \(\rho(i,\,s[i \mapsto b]) \le \rho(i,s)\) becomes an equality under the hypothesis, so le_of_eq closes every goal immediately.
Theorem (nash_identProfile_of_global_max). If the all-identity profile \(e^n\) is a global maximum — no deviation improves it — then it is a Nash equilibrium: \[ \Bigl(\forall i,b:\ \rho\!\left(i,\,e^n[i\mapsto b]\right) \le \rho(i,e^n)\Bigr) \;\Longrightarrow\; \mathrm{IsNash}(G,\, e^n). \]
Proof sketch: The hypothesis is definitionally equal to IsNash applied to the identity profile, since update (identProfile G) i b is precisely the notation used in the definition of IsNash.
Theorem (isNash_iff_forall_best_response). A profile is a Nash equilibrium if and only if every player plays a best response: \[ \mathrm{IsNash}(G,s) \;\iff\; \forall i:\ \mathrm{IsBestResponse}(G,i,s,s(i)). \]
Proof sketch: Both directions follow from Function.update_eq_self — updating slot \(i\) of \(s\) with \(s(i)\) recovers \(s\) — so IsNash G s and IsBestResponse G i s (s i) are definitionally interchangeable once that equation is substituted. 
-- T3: Nash iff best-response
theorem isNash_iff_forall_best_response (G : IdeaGame α) (s : Fin G.n → α) :
    IsNash G s ↔ ∀ i : Fin G.n, IsBestResponse G i s (s i)
Theorem (exists_best_response). On finite inhabited strategy sets, every player always has a best response: \[ \forall i,\; \forall s:\ \exists a:\ \mathrm{IsBestResponse}(G,i,s,a). \]
Proof sketch: Finset.exists_max_image applied to Finset.univ delivers a maximizer \(a^*\) of \(b \mapsto G.\mathrm{payoff}\,i\,(s[i\mapsto b])\); this maximizer is by definition a best response since all other strategies yield payoff at most \(\rho(i,s[i\mapsto a^*])\).
Theorem (exists_nash_one_player). Any one-player game on a finite inhabited strategy set has a Nash equilibrium: \[ \exists s : \mathrm{Fin}\,1 \to \alpha,\; \mathrm{IsNash}(\langle 1,\,\lambda\_.\,\mathrm{payoff}\rangle,\, s). \]
Proof sketch: Apply exists_best_response for the single player \(i = 0\) to obtain a best response \(a\); set \(s = \lambda\_,a\). The update-self identity then confirms that no deviation can improve \(\rho(0, s[0\mapsto b])\) over \(\rho(0, s)\).
Theorem (nash_pos_scale). Multiplying all payoffs by a positive constant \(c > 0\) preserves Nash equilibria: \[ \mathrm{IsNash}\!\left(\langle n,\,\lambda i\,s,\,c\cdot\rho(i,s)\rangle,\, s\right) \;\iff\; \mathrm{IsNash}(G,s). \]
Proof sketch: Forward direction divides both sides of \(c\cdot\rho(i,s[i\mapsto b]) \le c\cdot\rho(i,s)\) by \(c\) using mul_le_mul_left. Backward direction multiplies by \(c\) using mul_le_mul_of_nonneg_left with le_of_lt hc.
Theorem (nash_add_const). Adding player-specific constants to payoffs preserves Nash equilibria: \[ \mathrm{IsNash}\!\left(\langle n,\,\lambda i\,s,\,\rho(i,s)+c(i)\rangle,\, s\right) \;\iff\; \mathrm{IsNash}(G,s). \]
Proof sketch: Both directions cancel \(c(i)\) from both sides of the deviation inequality via linarith, using that \(c(i)\) appears identically on both sides regardless of the strategy profile.
Theorem (nash_op_identProfile). Composing every player's strategy with the identity on the right preserves Nash equilibria: \[ \mathrm{IsNash}(G,s) \;\Longrightarrow\; \mathrm{IsNash}(G,\, \lambda i,\, s(i) \mathbin{\circ} e). \]
Proof sketch: id_right gives \(\mathrm{op}(s(i), e) = s(i)\) for every \(i\), so \(\lambda i,\,\mathrm{op}(s(i),e)\) is definitionally equal to \(s\) and the original hypothesis applies directly.
Theorem (nash_identProfile_op). Composing the identity on the left of every player's strategy preserves Nash equilibria: \[ \mathrm{IsNash}(G,s) \;\Longrightarrow\; \mathrm{IsNash}(G,\, \lambda i,\, e \mathbin{\circ} s(i)). \]
Proof sketch: id_left gives \(\mathrm{op}(e,s(i)) = s(i)\) for every \(i\), so the composed profile reduces to \(s\) and the hypothesis applies unchanged.
Theorem (nash_refine_id). Applying the identity refinement — the trivial transformation \(\mathrm{id} \circ s\) — preserves Nash equilibria: \[ \mathrm{IsNash}(G,s) \;\Longrightarrow\; \mathrm{IsNash}(G,\, \mathrm{id} \circ s). \]
Proof sketch: \(\mathrm{id} \circ s = s\) definitionally in Lean 4 (function extensionality is not needed), so the goal reduces to the hypothesis immediately by rfl or exact.

Resonance, Dominance, and Mixed Strategies

Theorems T11–T16 address the structure of payoff differences (resonance), the termination of iterated elimination of dominated strategies, and the expectation calculus for mixed strategies. Together they supply the formal toolkit needed to handle zero-sum reasoning and probabilistic play within the idea monoid.

The resonance pairing \((i,j,s) \mapsto ho(i,s) - ho(j,s)\) is the fundamental object of comparative game theory: it measures how much better player \(i\) is doing than player \(j\) at profile \(s\). Theorems T11 and T12 establish that this pairing is anti-symmetric (\(\mathrm{res}(i,j,s) = -\mathrm{res}(j,i,s)\)) and zero on the diagonal (\(\mathrm{res}(i,i,s) = 0\)), making it a skew-symmetric bilinear form on the player set. These two properties are the algebraic signature of zero-sum structure and are prerequisites for stating von Neumann's minimax theorem in the context of idea games.

Theorem (resonance_antisymm). The resonance pairing — the differential payoff between players — is anti-symmetric: \[ \rho(i,s) - \rho(j,s) \;=\; -\bigl(\rho(j,s) - \rho(i,s)\bigr). \]
Proof sketch: ring closes the goal by arithmetic: negating a difference swaps its operands.
Theorem (resonance_diag_zero). The resonance pairing vanishes on the diagonal — a player's payoff differential with themselves is zero: \[ \rho(i,s) - \rho(i,s) \;=\; 0. \]
Proof sketch: ring (or sub_self) closes the goal. This confirms that the resonance pairing forms an anti-symmetric relation on players, consistent with zero-sum structure.
Theorem (iterated_dominance_terminates). Any strictly decreasing chain of finite strategy sets terminates: if \(T(k+1) \subsetneq T(k)\) for all \(k\), then \[ \exists k \le \lvert T(0)\rvert + 1:\ T(k) = \emptyset. \]
Proof sketch: By induction on strict inclusions, \(\lvert T(k)\rvert + k \le \lvert T(0)\rvert\) at each step. At \(k = \lvert T(0)\rvert + 1\), the cardinality must be 0, so \(T(k) = \emptyset\) by Finset.card_eq_zero.
Theorem (mixed_expect_const). The expected value of a constant function under any mixed strategy equals that constant: \[ \mu.\mathrm{expect}(\lambda\_,\,c) \;=\; c. \] Explicitly: \(\displaystyle\sum_{b} \mu.\mathrm{prob}(b)\cdot c = c\).
Proof sketch: Factor \(c\) out of the sum to get \(c \cdot \sum_b \mu.\mathrm{prob}(b)\). Use the probability normalization axiom \(\sum_b \mu.\mathrm{prob}(b) = 1\), then mul_one.
Theorem (mixed_expect_add). Expectation is linear — it distributes over pointwise sums of payoff functions: \[ \mu.\mathrm{expect}(\lambda b,\, f(b)+g(b)) \;=\; \mu.\mathrm{expect}(f) + \mu.\mathrm{expect}(g). \]
Proof sketch: Use mul_add to distribute \(\mu.\mathrm{prob}(b)\) over \(f(b)+g(b)\), then apply Finset.sum_add_distrib to split the sum.
Theorem (mixed_expect_mono). Expectation is monotone: pointwise dominance of payoffs implies dominance of expectations: \[ \bigl(\forall b:\ f(b) \le g(b)\bigr) \;\Longrightarrow\; \mu.\mathrm{expect}(f) \le \mu.\mathrm{expect}(g). \]
Proof sketch: Apply Finset.sum_le_sum; at each term use mul_le_mul_of_nonneg_left (h b) (μ.prob_nonneg b) to multiply the pointwise inequality by the non-negative probability.

Evolutionary Stability and Coalition

Theorems T17–T19 connect the game theory of ideas to evolutionary epistemology and collective action. The ESS theorem formalizes when an idempotent idea is uninvadable; the coalition theorem establishes the algebraic identity as the fixed point of communicative silence; and the update-independence theorem shows that payoffs are well-defined on deviations regardless of history.

Together, T17–T19 answer three questions about idea dynamics that pure Nash theory cannot address. T17 asks: which ideas survive competition over time? — answer: idempotent ones that dominate all mutants under composition. T18 asks: what does a group produce when none of its members is committed to any idea? — answer: the identity, the null output. T19 asks: does history affect a player's payoff from a deviation? — answer: no, only the current action matters. This last result justifies the "strategic-form" representation of idea games: the payoff matrix fully captures the relevant information, and no extensive-form history needs to be tracked. 

-- T15: ESS — idempotent strategies are uninvadable
theorem ess_idempotent {a : α} (ha : op a a = a)
    (u : α → α → ℝ)
    (hStrict : ∀ m, m ≠ a → u (op a m) a < u a a)
    (hDom    : ∀ m, u m a ≤ u (op a m) a) :
    ∀ m, m ≠ a → u m a < u a a
Theorem (ess_idempotent). Let \(a\) be an idempotent strategy (\(a \mathbin{\circ} a = a\)). If every mutant \(m \ne a\) is strictly dominated after one round of composition — \(u(a\mathbin{\circ}m,\,a) < u(a,a)\) — and every mutant is weakly dominated by its composition — \(u(m,a) \le u(a\mathbin{\circ}m,a)\) — then \(a\) is evolutionarily stable: \[ \forall m \ne a:\ u(m,a) < u(a,a). \]
Proof sketch: Chain the two hypotheses: \(u(m,a) \le u(a\mathbin{\circ}m,a) < u(a,a)\) via lt_of_le_of_lt (hDom m) (hStrict m hm).
Theorem (coalition_all_ident_eq_ident). A coalition in which every member holds the identity idea aggregates to the identity: \[ \forall l : \mathrm{List}\,\alpha,\; \bigl(\forall x \in l,\; x = e\bigr) \;\Longrightarrow\; l.\mathrm{foldl}(\mathbin{\circ},\, e) = e. \]
Proof sketch: Induction on \(l\). Base case: rfl for the empty list. Inductive step: the head satisfies \(h = e\) by hypothesis; apply id_right to reduce foldl op e (h :: t) = foldl op (op e h) t = foldl op e t, then the inductive hypothesis closes the goal.
Theorem (payoff_update_indep). Player \(i\)'s payoff after a deviation to \(b\) is independent of the previous value in slot \(i\) — only the final action \(b\) matters: \[ \rho\!\left(i,\; (s[i\mapsto a])[i\mapsto b]\right) \;=\; \rho(i,\; s[i\mapsto b]). \]
Proof sketch: Apply congrFun on the profile argument; Function.update_idem (or the update-update cancellation lemma) collapses \((s[i\mapsto a])[i\mapsto b] = s[i\mapsto b]\), completing the goal.

Concept Zoo

Each entry below places one of Idea Theory's game-theoretic concepts in its intellectual lineage, connecting the machine-verified theorems to the canonical social-science literature.

Nash Equilibrium

The central concept: a strategy profile \(s^*\) is a Nash equilibrium if no player can strictly improve their payoff by unilateral deviation. In Idea Theory, ideas are strategies and Nash equilibria are stable idea profiles — configurations from which no agent benefits by switching to a different idea. The 19 theorems of GameTheory.lean prove that this concept is algebraically well-founded: it is preserved by positive scaling (T6), translation invariance (T7), and left/right composition with the identity (T8, T9). The existence theorem (T5) shows that any finite one-player game always has a Nash equilibrium. The best-response characterization (T3) links Nash equilibria to rationality: each player is simultaneously playing optimally against the others. This makes Nash equilibria not just stable but justified — a connection absent in purely sociological accounts of conventions. The identity profile is always Nash in any game with constant payoffs (T1), anchoring the theory to the algebraic identity.

Maynard Smith — Evolutionarily Stable Strategy

John Maynard Smith's ESS concept (1973, 1982) asks: which strategies are uninvadable by mutants in an evolving population? A strategy \(a\) is an ESS if \(u(a,a) > u(m,a)\) for all mutants \(m\) (strict stability), or if \(u(a,a)=u(m,a)\) and \(u(a,m)>u(m,m)\) (weak stability). Theorem ess_idempotent (T17) formalizes the strict-stability branch: an idempotent strategy \(a\) — one satisfying \(a\mathbin{\circ}a=a\), the mathematical signature of a habit or schema — is uninvadable whenever every mutant is strictly dominated after one round of composition. This connects ESS theory directly to the algebraic structure: idempotent elements of the idea monoid are precisely the evolutionarily stable strategies. The proof is a two-line chain of inequalities, but it is machine-verified — there are no hidden assumptions about continuity or differentiability. Evolutionary stability is here a purely algebraic property.

Schelling — Focal Points

Thomas Schelling's focal-point theory (The Strategy of Conflict, 1960) argues that among multiple Nash equilibria, agents coordinate on "prominent" or "salient" ones without explicit communication. The nash_add_const theorem (T7) formalizes the irrelevance of absolute payoff levels for Nash selection: salience operates above the level of Nash computation. This resonates with Schelling's insight that focal points are not determined by the payoff matrix alone but by contextual factors. In Idea Theory, the identity element \(e\) is the canonical focal point of any idea game — it is the algebraically natural resting point, the default strategy. The nash_op_identProfile and nash_identProfile_op theorems (T8, T9) show that \(e\) absorbs silently: composing with the identity never moves a player away from a Nash equilibrium. Schelling's focal points thus correspond, in the algebra, to fixed points of the composition operation.

von Neumann — Minimax

John von Neumann's minimax theorem (1928) establishes that in two-player zero-sum games the minimax value equals the maximin value, guaranteeing a unique equilibrium value for rational play. Theorems resonance_antisymm (T11) and resonance_diag_zero (T12) formalize the zero-sum structure of the resonance pairing: the differential payoff \(\rho(i,s)-\rho(j,s)\) is anti-symmetric and vanishes on the diagonal. This is the algebraic kernel of zero-sum reasoning. The mixed-strategy theorems (mixed_expect_const T14; mixed_expect_add T15; mixed_expect_mono T16) provide the expectation machinery needed to state the minimax theorem in full generality. The iterated_dominance_terminates theorem (T13) shows that iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies terminates in finite time, providing the algorithmic complement to von Neumann's existence result.

Axelrod — Cooperation and Iteration

Robert Axelrod's tournament experiments (The Evolution of Cooperation, 1984) showed that Tit-for-Tat — an idempotent, retaliatory-but-forgiving strategy — was evolutionarily stable in iterated Prisoner's Dilemma. The iterated_dominance_terminates theorem (T13) provides the formal backbone: any sequence of strictly dominated strategy eliminations halts. This mirrors Axelrod's finding that cooperation emerges not from one-shot rationality but from iterated interaction — the shadow of the future eliminates dominated defection. The ess_idempotent theorem (T17) formalizes why Tit-for-Tat is stable: it is idempotent (playing it against itself yields itself) and strictly dominates mutants under composition. Axelrod's lesson — cooperation is an algebraic fixed point — is thus a theorem in Idea Theory's game-theoretic extension.

Habermas — Communicative Rationality

Jürgen Habermas's communicative action theory (The Theory of Communicative Action, 1981) distinguishes strategic rationality (maximizing payoffs) from communicative rationality (seeking mutual understanding through dialogue). The coalition_all_ident_eq_ident theorem (T18) formalizes the Habermasian "silent coalition": a group of agents who all hold the identity idea — who are not yet committed to any substantive position — aggregate to the identity. This is communicative rationality at the algebraic level: the absence of strategic commitment is itself a stable state. A coalition can only produce non-identity output if at least one member holds a non-identity idea. This contrasts sharply with strategic accounts: even a unanimous coalition of null strategies fails to coordinate on any positive meaning. Habermas's ideal speech situation — the presupposition of discourse — is thus the all-identity profile.

Implications

The 19 theorems of GameTheory.lean are not merely a formalization exercise. They prove that strategic reasoning about ideas is algebraically grounded — not a metaphor applied from economics, but a theorem derived from the axioms of the idea monoid. The preservation results (T6, T7, T8, T9, T10) show that Nash equilibria are robust to the specific form of the payoff function: they survive positive scaling, translation by player-specific constants, and composition with the identity element. This means that the concept of equilibrium in idea space is not fragile. Stable idea profiles exist not because payoffs happen to align, but because the algebraic structure guarantees it.

The best-response characterization (T3) establishes a tight link between Nash equilibria and individual rationality. Combined with the existence result (T5) for one-player games and best-response existence on finite sets (T4), these theorems show that finite idea games always have a rational solution concept. This is the algebraic analogue of the Nash existence theorem for finite games — which requires fixed-point theorems — but the direct proofs here use only list manipulation and order arithmetic. This shows that for the class of games considered, the full machinery of fixed-point theory is unnecessary: the algebraic structure itself is sufficient.

The evolutionary stability theorem (T17) connects the game theory of ideas to evolutionary epistemology. An idempotent idea — a schema that reproduces itself under self-application — is uninvadable if its composition with any mutant is strictly dominated. This is Dawkins's "meme" recast in algebraic terms: a meme is an idempotent element of the idea monoid that satisfies the ESS condition. The machine-verified proof ensures that no implicit assumptions about continuity or differentiability enter. Evolutionary stability is a purely algebraic property, not a biological one. Idempotency — the closure of a habit or schema under self-reinforcement — is the algebraic signature of cultural persistence.

The anti-symmetry and diagonality lemmas (T11, T12) reveal a hidden zero-sum structure within every idea game. Even when the game is not explicitly zero-sum, the resonance pairing — the differential payoff between any two players — is anti-symmetric. This means that whenever player \(i\) gains by a unilateral deviation relative to player \(j\), player \(j\) loses by exactly the same amount. Idea competition is thus partially zero-sum at the level of relative standing, even when absolute payoffs are positive-sum. This structural result has implications for the sociology of ideas: the claim that ideas are non-rivalrous (correct in the production sense) does not imply that their adoption is non-rivalrous in the social-attention economy.
