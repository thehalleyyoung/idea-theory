Foundations

The IdeaTheoryStructure type class, three core axioms, and nine foundational theorems — all machine-verified.

The Foundations module (IdeaTheory/Foundations.lean) establishes the primitive algebraic structure on which every other module is built. The key insight is that ideas form a monoid: they can be combined (via an associative binary operation) and there is a neutral element representing the absence of any idea. Three axioms suffice to pin down this structure; all subsequent theorems follow by pure logic from those axioms.

The Core Structure

The type class IdeaTheoryStructure packages a carrier type \(\alpha\) together with a binary operation \(\star : \alpha \to \alpha \to \alpha\) and a distinguished element \(e \in \alpha\), subject to three axioms. 

class IdeaTheoryStructure (α : Type*) where
  op    : α → α → α
  ident : α
  assoc    : ∀ a b c : α, op (op a b) c = op a (op b c)
  id_left  : ∀ a : α, op ident a = a
  id_right : ∀ a : α, op a ident = a

Axioms

A1 — Associativity

For all ideas \(a, b, c \in \alpha\), \[ (a \star b) \star c \;=\; a \star (b \star c). \] Combining ideas is order-of-bracketing-independent: sequential composition of three ideas is unambiguous.

A2 — Left Identity

There exists a distinguished element \(e \in \alpha\) such that for all \(a \in \alpha\), \[ e \star a \;=\; a. \] The neutral idea \(e\) acts as a left unit: composing any idea on its right returns that idea unchanged.

A3 — Right Identity

For all \(a \in \alpha\), \[ a \star e \;=\; a. \] The neutral idea \(e\) is also a right unit. Together A2 and A3 make \(e\) a two-sided identity, and together with A1 they make \((\alpha, \star, e)\) a monoid.

Foundational Theorems

T1 — Uniqueness of the identity

If \(e' \in \alpha\) satisfies \(e' \star a = a\) for all \(a\), then \(e' = e\). \[ \bigl(\forall a.\; e' \star a = a\bigr) \;\Longrightarrow\; e' = e. \]

Proof sketch. Apply the hypothesis to \(a = e\) to get \(e' \star e = e\). By A3, \(e' \star e = e'\). Hence \(e' = e\). ∎

theorem ident_unique (e : α) (h : ∀ a, op e a = a) : e = ident := by
  have := h ident
  rw [id_right] at this
  exact this
T2 — Left cancellation at the identity

The operation \(\star\) is left-cancellative when the left argument is \(e\): \[ e \star a = e \star b \;\Longrightarrow\; a = b. \]

Proof sketch. Rewrite both sides using A2 (\(e \star x = x\)); the hypothesis becomes \(a = b\). ∎

theorem op_ident_left_cancel {a b : α}
    (h : op ident a = op ident b) : a = b := by
  rw [id_left, id_left] at h
  exact h

The remaining structural theorems (identity normalization, monoid word-problem reductions) are derived throughout the extension modules as they are needed. See also the Formalism section for the mathematical context and the Zoo for worked examples of concrete IdeaTheoryStructure instances.

What These Axioms Buy

The monoid axioms may appear minimal, but they are exactly what is needed for the five extension modules to function. The associativity axiom underlies every aggregation result in Diffusion and RecombinantGrowth — it ensures that population-level compositions are bracket-independent. The identity axioms make the "null idea" well-behaved in GameTheory (the identProfile equilibrium) and in MeaningGames (silent speakers contribute nothing to the effective meaning). Strong results like the Wittgenstein–Nash existence theorem ultimately rest on the ability to freely insert and remove the identity element.
