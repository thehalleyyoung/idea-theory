The Ideas Zoo

~95 positions — each a sub-instance of the same eight-component algebra

The central thesis of Idea Theory is not metaphorical: every serious theory of ideas — from Plato's Forms to the attention mechanism in transformer networks — is a sub-instance of a single formal structure, the idea algebra \((\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\). Not "inspired by" the algebra, not "structurally analogous to" it, but literally instantiated as a particular choice of the eight components. The~95 entries collected here span twelve traditions across 2,500 years of thinking about ideas. They disagree radically about everything: about whether universals are real, whether concepts are prototypes or definitions, whether ideas can be private, whether cultural transmission is Darwinian, whether knowledge is socially constituted. The claim of this zoo is that their disagreements are, precisely, disagreements about the structure of the algebra — about which axioms hold, which cocycles vanish, which sub-monoids exist. They are all playing the same game; they differ about the rules.

What does "sub-instance" mean, technically? Given any theory \(T\) with a native notion of ideas, a sub-instance is an explicit assignment: define \(\mathcal{I}\) (the carrier), \(\circ\) (composition), \(e\) (the empty idea), \(\rho\) (resonance), \(\sigma\) (the preserved part after collision), \(\nu\) (the negated part), \(\eta\) (the emergent remainder), \(\omega\) (the emergence cocycle), and \(\deg\) (grading into an abelian group \(G\)). Once these eight components are fixed, the nine theorems T1–T9 either hold automatically or impose constraints — and the central claims of \(T\) either follow as theorems in that instance, or are revealed as contingent choices of parameters, or (rarely) turn out to be inconsistent with the axioms. Extensions beyond the core — the GameTheory module, Diffusion, RecombinantGrowth, EmergenceLevels, MeaningGames — cover the economic and sociological entries. The zoo is a stress test: can the same eight definitions absorb everything? So far, yes.

How to Read the Zoo

Entry structure

Each entry in the domain pages (Philosophy · Science · Social) follows a five-part format. Position: the thinker's central claim in their own terms, with source citations. Instantiation: explicit definitions of all eight algebra components for this thinker's framework — this is the technical work, and the most demanding part to get right. Theorem: which of T1–T9 makes the central claim follow, or what additional axiom must be assumed. Adds: what the formalization predicts or clarifies that the original thinker did not notice — new structure forced by the algebra, unexpected connections to other entries. Diverges: where the formalization must idealize, compress, or part ways with the thinker's intent — intellectual honesty about the limits of the instantiation.

The status verdict in each entry is one of three values. True: the claim is provable from T1–T9 alone, holding in every instance of the algebra. Contingent: the claim holds in some algebras and not others — it encodes a substantive empirical or metaphysical commitment about which algebra the world instantiates. False: the claim is inconsistent with the axioms, at least as stated — though typically a nearby weaker claim survives.

In the summary tables below, the Instantiation insight column gives a single sentence identifying the key algebraic move — the one step that, once seen, makes the whole translation obvious. The Theorems used column lists which of T1–T9 do the main work (T1 = monoid axioms, T2 = resonance non-degeneracy, T3 = Aufhebung, T4 = cocycle identity, T5 = curvature formula, T6 = conjugation invariance, T7 = chain rule, T8 = structural equivalence, T9 = grading homomorphism).

Theorem Key For Zoo References

The zoo pages use theorem labels constantly because the same small family of results reappears across philosophy, cognitive science, economics, sociology, and language. But the label is only useful if you know what job the theorem is doing in the argument. Use the guide below whenever an entry cites a theorem number.

T1 Composition / identity The theory needs a stable way to combine ideas, ignore silent inputs, or talk about reversibility and fixed points.
T2 Resonance The theory depends on similarity, fit, payoff, explanatory relevance, affinity, or semantic support.
T3 Aufhebung The theory claims that composition both preserves and destroys while also creating a higher-order synthesis.
T4 Emergence cocycle The theory treats novelty as measurable and compositionally coherent rather than magical or unconstrained.
T5 Curvature The theory needs asymmetry, path dependence, framing, or direction-sensitive interpretation.
T6 Conjugation The theory is about context, reframing, perspective shift, or interpretation under a changed background.
T7 Chains The theory concerns multi-step transmission, iteration, inheritance, education, or repeated interaction over time.
T8 Structural equivalence The theory distinguishes surface form from deep structure or asks when two different expressions count as the same idea.
T9 Grading The theory needs levels, hierarchy, salience, abstraction, ontological rank, or complexity.
T10 Coalitions The theory concerns social aggregation, group cohesion, alliance formation, or collective action.
T11 Cultural transmission The theory turns repeated passing-on into a structure with its own algebraic laws.
T12 Novelty The theory distinguishes genuine innovation from mere rearrangement inside an existing closure.

🆕 Worked Example: Frege

Position

Sense (Sinn) is the mode of presentation; reference (Bedeutung) is the object denoted. Compositionality: the sense of a complex expression is determined by the senses of its parts. Senses are mind-independent, inhabiting a "third realm." The puzzle: "Hesperus is Phosphorus" is informative even though both names co-refer.

Instantiation

\(\mathcal{I}\) = the set of Fregean senses. \(\circ\) = functional application (saturation of an unsaturated function-concept by an argument-sense). \(e\) = the empty context (identity under saturation). \(\rho(a,b)\) = the degree to which sense \(a\) is semantically relevant to sense \(b\) — a positive bilinear form on the space of senses. The valuation \(v:\mathcal{I}\to A\) (where \(A\) is the domain of references) is given by \(v(a\circ b) = v(a)(v(b)) + \omega(a,b)\), where \(\omega\) is the emergence cocycle measuring how much the reference of the complex exceeds the naive functional composition of references. \(\deg(a)\) counts the number of unsaturated argument-places remaining (type level). \(\sigma(a,b)\) = the preserved logical form; \(\nu(a,b)\) = the suppressed indexical content; \(\eta(a,b)\) = the emergent pragmatic content of the combination.

Theorem

Compositionality modulo \(\omega\): \(v(a\circ b) = v(a)(v(b)) + \omega(a,b)\) holds by T4 (cocycle identity). Strict compositionality (\(\omega=0\)) is contingent. The Hesperus–Phosphorus puzzle is the non-vanishing of \(\omega(\text{Hesperus},\text{Phosphorus})\): the two names have the same reference but different senses, and the informativeness of the identity statement equals exactly the cocycle term at that pair. The sense/reference distinction itself is forced by T1+T4: any monoid with a non-trivial valuation must distinguish the structural level from the value level.

Adds

The formalism reveals that Frege's "third realm" is simply the graded sub-algebra \(\mathcal{I}/{\sim}\times A\), and that the puzzles of oblique contexts (why "Hesperus" and "Phosphorus" differ in belief reports) correspond to conjugation by the belief operator — a special case of T6. The algebra also predicts that any system with strict compositionality (\(\omega=0\)) cannot represent informativeness: a connection Frege never made explicit.

Diverges

The algebra treats senses as elements of a monoid, which forces some degree of compositionality even when \(\omega\neq 0\). Frege's holistic claim that the sentence is the primary unit of sense sits uneasily with the bottom-up generation by \(\circ\). The "third realm" metaphysics is deflated to a structural observation — some will regard this as a philosophical loss, even as it clarifies the formal structure.

Status

True (T1+T4) for sense/reference distinction and compositionality modulo \(\omega\). Contingent for strict compositionality (\(\omega=0\)). Full entry: philosophy.html → Frege

How to read a zoo entry slowly

Start with Position: that tells you what the original thinker was trying to say before any algebra is imposed. Then read Instantiation and ask a concrete question for each symbol: what is being treated as an idea here, what counts as composition, what counts as fit or resonance, what counts as novelty, and what counts as level? Only after that should you read Theorem, because the theorem citation is not a decoration. It is the claim that some part of the thinker's view is now a consequence of a general result rather than a fresh assumption.

The final two rubrics matter just as much as the proof-style ones. Adds tells you what the algebra predicts beyond the original text. Diverges tells you where the fit stops being exact. Those two rubrics are the safeguard against turning every historical figure into empty confirmation of the framework.

Traditions Overview

Each section below gives a brief narrative of how the tradition conceptualizes ideas, followed by a complete table of every entry from that tradition. Click "Entry →" for the full instantiation in the domain page.

1. Ancient & Medieval #

Ancient and medieval thinkers inaugurated the two poles between which all later positions oscillate: ideas as mind-independent universals (Plato's Forms, Aquinas's species intelligibilis) versus ideas as immanent in things (Aristotle's \(\sigma\)-invariants). The algebraic reading shows these are not rival metaphysics but rival choices about the fixed-point structure of conjugation: Plato centralizes the algebra (\(\mathrm{Fix}_\circ(\mathcal{I})\) is large), Aristotle distributes it into \(\sigma\)-orbits. Plotinus adds the emanation gradient — the grading \(\deg\). Ockham's razor is the minimality condition on generators. The unity of this tradition, across a millennium of fierce debate, is that all parties agree on the basic ontology of a structured space of thinkable things; they differ only on the topology of that space.

2. Early Modern #

Early modernity shifts the question from what ideas are to how they arise in minds, and what they can certify. The tradition splits between rationalists (innate structure constrains all possible ideas — formally, the algebra has non-trivial fixed-point generators) and empiricists (ideas are composed from sensory simples — formally, the algebra is freely generated by a set of primitive elements). Kant's synthesis is not a compromise but a structural observation: the algebra's grading distinguishes the purely formal (the categories, the monoid structure) from the content-bearing (the manifold of intuition). Hume's association is the resonance pairing \(\rho\); his missing-shade-of-blue argument is a non-trivial coboundary in \(H^1(\mathcal{I},A)\).

3. German Idealism #

The German Idealists are the tradition that most explicitly theorizes the dynamics of ideas — not just what they are but how they transform, conflict, and produce new content. Hegel's Aufhebung (preserve–negate–elevate) is the literal source of the algebra's third theorem: \(\sigma,\nu,\eta\) are the Hegelian triple. Fichte's self-positing \(I\) is the emergence of \(e\) as a fixed point. Schelling's Nature and Spirit are the two sub-algebras whose tension drives emergence. Schopenhauer's Platonic Ideas are \(\rho\)-eigenvectors of the Will. Marx's materialism is a choice of which sub-algebra (\(\mathcal{I}_\text{material}\)) generates the rest by composition — a statement about generators, not about substance.

4. Phenomenology & Hermeneutics #

Phenomenology asks how ideas are given to consciousness — not what they are in themselves but how they appear, and what the structure of appearing is. Husserl's intentionality is the resonance pairing \(\rho\): a consciousness is always a consciousness of something, which is precisely the non-degeneracy condition T2. The horizonal structure — the field of co-given surrounding objects — is the chain of resonance couplings that T7 makes explicit. Heidegger's thrownness is the conjugation orbit of Dasein (T6); the hermeneutic circle is the self-referential structure of the chain rule (T7). Gadamer's fusion of horizons is Aufhebung (T3) applied to two sub-algebras representing interpreter and text.

5. Post-Structuralism #

Post-structuralism is, formally, the discovery that \(\omega\neq 0\). Where structuralism (Saussure) assumes that the meaning of a sign is fully determined by its position in a system of differences — a commutative, \(\omega=0\) algebra — post-structuralism insists that meaning is never fully present, always deferred, always producing surplus or remainder. Derrida's différance is the non-vanishing of the cocycle \(\omega\). Foucault's épistème is the conjugation orbit of knowledge under power (T6). Deleuze's difference and repetition is the claim that the curvature \(\kappa\) is fundamental, not derivative. Bakhtin's dialogism is the Aufhebung of two voices in which the emergent third voice \(\eta\) cannot be reduced to either.

6. Analytic Philosophy #

Analytic philosophy of language and mind is the tradition that has most aggressively pressed for formal rigor in the theory of ideas, and it is accordingly the tradition most directly vindicated — and constrained — by the algebra. Frege's sense/reference distinction is forced by T1+T4 (see worked example above). Russell's type hierarchy is the grading \(\deg\). Wittgenstein I's picture theory is T8 (structural equivalence). Wittgenstein II's family resemblance is the \(\sim_\theta\) equivalence relation on \(\rho\)-components. Quine's holism is T7; his indeterminacy is \([\omega]\neq 0\). Fodor's LOT systematicity is the bilinearity of \(\rho\) (T2). Brandom's inferential semantics is the commitment/entitlement pairing \((K(a),E(a))\) — the asymmetric curvature (T5).

7. Cognitive Science #

Cognitive science approaches concepts as empirical objects — things measurable via reaction times, similarity ratings, categorization accuracy, and neural imaging. The tradition has produced a rich taxonomy: classical definitions (necessary-and-sufficient features), prototypes (Rosch), theory-laden concepts (Murphy–Medin), perceptual symbols (Barsalou), conceptual metaphors (Lakoff–Johnson), conceptual blending (Fauconnier–Turner), conceptual spaces (Gärdenfors), and dual-process cognition (Kahneman). In algebraic terms, these are different claims about the resonance structure of \(\mathcal{I}\): classical theory is a flat \(\rho\) (all features equally weighted); prototype theory is a peaked \(\rho\) (centroid concentration); theory-theory is a \(\rho\) structured by causal relations (T7 chain rule). Gärdenfors's convexity hypothesis is a geometric condition on the \(\rho\)-metric space.

8. Connectionism & AI #

Connectionism and AI provide the most quantitatively precise instantiations in the zoo: here, the eight algebra components have numerical implementations in specific architectures. Holographic reduced representations (Plate) instantiate a bandwidth-bounded approximate algebra where the cocycle \(\omega\) is the reconstruction noise. Word embeddings (word2vec, GloVe) instantiate the linear shadow of conjugation (T6): analogical arithmetic \(\mathbf{v}(\text{king})-\mathbf{v}(\text{man})+\mathbf{v}(\text{woman})\approx\mathbf{v}(\text{queen})\) is the first-order approximation to \(g\circ\mathit{king}\circ g^{-1}\). 9. Cultural Evolution #

Cultural evolution asks how ideas spread, transform, and accumulate across populations and generations. The algebraic representation maps this directly onto transmission chains. Each transmission step is an idea \(a_i\in\mathcal{I}\); fidelity is \(\rho(a_i\circ m,m)/\rho(m,m)\); the irreducible departure introduced by each step is the cocycle term \(\omega(a_i,m)\). Dawkins's meme is a replicator in this framework (T7). Sperber's cultural attractor is a \(\sigma\)-stable sub-monoid (T3). Henrich's ratchet is the monotone growth of cumulative \(\omega\) under long chains (T7). Boyd–Richerson's dual inheritance is two parallel sub-monoids with different transmission cocycles. Tomasello's shared intentionality is the emergence of a common sub-algebra from the Aufhebung of individual intention algebras (T3).

10. Emergence #

The emergence literature asks when and how higher-level properties arise from lower-level components in ways not predictable by simple aggregation. In the algebra, this question has a precise answer: emergence is the non-vanishing of the cocycle \(\omega\). Weak emergence (Bedau) is \(\omega\neq 0\) but \([\omega]=0\in H^2\) — the emergent property is in principle deducible but computationally costly. Strong emergence (Chalmers, consciousness) is \([\omega]\neq 0\) — the property is not deducible even in principle. Mill's heteropathic laws are the case \(\omega(a,b)\neq 0\) for specific \(a,b\) in chemistry. Anderson's "More is Different" is the claim that the degree-\(n\) sub-algebra has structure not reducible to degree-\((n-1)\) components — a statement about the non-surjectivity of the degree map's section.

11. Economics & Game Theory #

Arrow's non-rivalry is Theorem 1 (monoid closure: using \(a\) does not remove it). Aumann's correlated equilibrium is a \(\rho\)-symmetric state over the joint algebra.

12. Sociology & Language #

Sociology and the philosophy of language converge, in the algebra, on a single question: what is the relationship between an individual's idea-algebra and the collective one? Lovejoy's unit-ideas are the generators of a free sub-monoid (T1). Granovetter's weak ties are the non-unit elements that determine the aggregate (T7 silent-agent invariance). Bourdieu's habitus is conjugation by class position (T6). Latour's translation chains are composition chains with per-step cocycle costs (T7). Speech-act theory (Austin, Searle) treats illocutionary force as the Aufhebung (T3) applied to a locutionary idea: the act preserves propositional content (\(\sigma\)), negates prior felicity conditions (\(\nu\)), and produces new institutional facts (\(\eta\)). Kripkenstein's rule-following paradox is the non-vanishing of the curvature cocycle on the interpretation map.
