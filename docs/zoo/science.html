Zoo: Cognitive Science, AI & Emergence

Thirty-two theories — from prototype effects to transformers, from memes to weak emergence — each reconstructed as a full sub-instance of the idea algebra 10-tuple \((\mathcal{H};\,\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\varrho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\), where \(\mathcal{H}\) is the horizon category over which the eight operations are fibered as a presheaf (the horizon functor ξ).

Every entry gives (1) a faithful reconstruction of the position, (2) an explicit instantiation of all eight components of the algebra plus its Horizon Structure (ξ), with a display equation, (3) the central claim restated as a theorem or definition in that instantiation, (4) what the formalism adds — new predictions or clarifications — and (5) where the formalism diverges from the original theory. Cross-references point to the relevant theorems and Lean modules. The horizon-functor theorems are T13 (Hermeneutic Sheaf), T14 (Indexical Pullback), and T15 (Profundity / Trans-horizonal Stability). Derived primitives (τ temporality, ι intentionality, π perspective, χ paradigm, μ credence, λ potentiality, …) are read off the 10-tuple rather than added to it.

How To Decode Theorem References On This Page

Science-side entries use theorem references as explanatory shortcuts. T2 usually means the theory needs a principled notion of similarity, affinity, or payoff. T3 means composition yields preserved structure, discarded structure, and a synthesis. T4 means novelty can be tracked coherently across multi-step constructions. T6 means context transforms a representation without destroying its deeper organization. T8 means two differently expressed structures can count as the same underlying object. T9 means the theory relies on levels, complexity, depth, or abstraction.

The important reading habit is this: when an entry claims, for example, that Rosch or Barsalou "is an instance of T2," ask what the algebra is measuring and why that measure matters. The theorem label tells you the shape of the explanation; the instantiation tells you what concrete psychological or computational phenomenon fills that shape.

1Classical Concept Theory — Smith & Medin
Smith & Medin, Categories and Concepts (1981)
The Position

Concepts have necessary and sufficient defining conditions: to fall under the concept bachelor one must satisfy adult, male, and unmarried — no more, no less. Membership is all-or-nothing, and concepts can be cleanly conjoined by intersecting their feature sets. The classical theory dominated cognitive psychology until the 1970s, when graded typicality effects and the conjunction fallacy became difficult to explain.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be the set of all predicates (features and feature-bundles). The composition \(a \circ b\) is feature-set intersection. The resonance \(\varrho(a,b) = |\text{features}(a) \cap \text{features}(b)| / |\text{features}(a) \cup \text{features}(b)|\) (Jaccard similarity). Aufhebung: \(\sigma(a,b) = a \cap b\) (shared features), \(\nu(a,b) = a \setminus b\) (features lost from \(a\)), \(\eta(a,b) = \emptyset\) (no emergence — a defining property of the classical theory). Grading \(\deg(a) = |\text{features}(a)|\).

\[ a \text{ is a concept} \iff a \circ a = a \text{ (idempotent) and } a = \arg\max_{x} \varrho(x, \cdot) \text{ over its extension.} \]

The intension of a concept is \(\{\, b \mid \varrho(a,b) = 1 \,\}\); membership is \(\varrho(x,c) \geq 1\) (all conditions met).

Central Claim as Theorem

The classical theory is the special case of T3 (Aufhebung) in which \(\eta \equiv 0\): no composition of features ever produces a genuinely new feature. This is a strict structural constraint — it forces \(\omega \equiv 0\) (by T4) and eliminates any curvature \(\kappa \equiv 0\) (by T5). Every conjunctive concept \(a \circ b\) is therefore fully determined by \(\sigma(a,b)\).

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism explains exactly why the conjunction fallacy (Tversky & Kahneman) is possible: in any realistic idea algebra, \(\omega(\text{bank-teller}, \text{feminist}) > 0\), so the conjunction produces emergent resonance not present in either conjunct alone. Classical concept theory implicitly assumes the special algebra where this cocycle vanishes identically. The formalism thus diagnoses the classical theory as choosing a specific — and empirically unusual — sub-instance.

Where It Diverges

The formalism does not model the psychological process of checking necessary and sufficient conditions; it captures only the algebraic structure of the concepts, not the cognitive dynamics of applying them.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For classical theory, \(\mathcal{H}\) is deliberately degenerate: one horizon, one identity morphism, and hence no genuine ξ-relativity. Concepts have invariant intensions, so every section of the presheaf is constant and the derived primitives \(\chi\), \(\pi\), and \(\mu\) collapse into a single context-free perspective. Morphisms cannot reweight membership or typicality because the theory treats definition as independent of use, task, or culture.

This makes T15 trivially satisfied while T14 is denied, marking exactly the breakage point later exposed by prototype and framing effects.

Derived Primitives
  • β. Definitions function as rigid global sections: bachelor must pick out the same feature bundle in every admissible horizon.
  • δ. Category boundaries are differential only by exclusion of non-defining features, not by graded contrast among neighboring cases.
  • π. Perspective is suppressed: the theory assumes no agent-relative access conditions alter membership or intension.
  • χ. Schema-relativity is denied because concepts are treated as context-free definitions rather than paradigm-local organizations.

Classical theory is diagnosed by β and its denial of π/χ: it requires rigid, global definition where later theories find horizon-local variation.

2Prototype Theory — Rosch
Rosch, "Natural categories" (1973); Rosch & Mervis (1975)
The Position

Natural categories are organized around prototypes — the most representative members — rather than defining conditions. Membership is graded: robins are more bird-like than penguins. Basic-level categories (chair, not furniture; not armchair) are cognitively privileged, having the highest ratio of within-category similarity to between-category similarity.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be a set of natural-kind ideas. The resonance \(\varrho(a,b)\) is feature-overlap similarity. The prototype \(p_c\) of category \(c\) is the resonance-maximizing element:

\[ p_c = \arg\max_{p \in c} \sum_{x \in c} \varrho(p, x). \]

Basic-level categories are the \(\deg\)-minimal categories with adequate prototype coherence: \(\arg\min_c \deg(c)\) subject to \(\sum_{x \in c} \varrho(p_c, x) > \theta\). Typicality of \(x\) in \(c\) is \(\varrho(x, p_c)\) — distance from the prototype in the \(\varrho\)-metric. The emergence cocycle \(\omega(a,b)\) captures the extra typicality of blends (e.g., a pet fish is neither a prototypical pet nor a prototypical fish, but the blend has its own emergent prototype).

Central Claim as Theorem

The typicality gradient follows from T2: because \(\varrho\) is a non-degenerate pairing, the resonance-maximizing element (the prototype) is unique up to \(\ker\varrho\). The graded membership structure is an instance of the \(\varrho\)-metric topology implicit in T2. Basic-level privilege is the statement that \(\deg\) achieves a minimum compatible with non-trivial category coherence, which follows from the properties of the grading map in T9.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism predicts that prototype shift — the way a prototype can change when a category is viewed in a different context — is a conjugation (\(\text{T6}\)): the context \(g\) acts as \({}^g\!p_c = g \circ p_c \circ g^{-1}\), shifting the resonance structure without changing the underlying category. This explains context-sensitivity of typicality judgements without ad hoc adjustments to the prototype itself.

Where It Diverges

The formalism treats all similarity as derived from a single resonance pairing; Rosch's empirical data suggest that different feature dimensions (colour, shape, function) may contribute independently, which would require a multi-dimensional resonance structure not captured in a single \(\varrho\).

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Rosch, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the lattice of category-contexts: ecological setting, task demand, contrast class, culture, and basic-level focus. Morphisms are refinements or translations between these contexts, such as moving from bird-in-general to backyard-bird or arctic-bird. Typicality is therefore \( arrho_h(x,p_c)\), not an absolute score; \(\chi\) names the local category schema and \(\mu\) records graded membership expectations within that schema.

Prototype shift is a paradigmatic T14 case: \( \varrho\) and \(\sigma\) fail to pull back invariantly across category horizons.

Derived Primitives
  • μ. Graded membership is a credence-like typicality measure: robins receive higher category expectation than penguins.
  • χ. Basic-level privilege depends on the active category schema, contrast class, and ecological task horizon.
  • δ. Typicality is differential: category position is determined by distances from prototypes and neighboring alternatives.
  • π. Judgments vary with the perceiver's task perspective, such as identifying birds for cooking, taxonomy, or backyard observation.

Prototype theory is most diagnostic for μ, χ, and δ: categories become graded, schema-relative fields rather than rigid definitions.

3Theory-Theory — Gopnik & Meltzoff
Gopnik & Meltzoff, Words, Thoughts, and Theories (1997)
The Position

Children (and scientists) possess genuine theories — coherent, causally structured representations that generate predictions and support counterfactual reasoning. Concepts derive their meaning from their role in a theory, not from intrinsic similarity. Conceptual change in development mirrors theory change in science: anomaly accumulates, coherence breaks down, and a new theory replaces the old in a Kuhnian restructuring.

The Instantiation

A theory \(T\) is a sub-algebra of \(\mathcal{I}\): a subset closed under \(\circ\) and with its own internal resonance \(\varrho_T\). A concept \(c\) belongs to theory \(T\) iff \(\varrho_T(c, \cdot)\) is well-defined (the concept has determinate inferential role within \(T\)). Incommensurable theories (Kuhn) are sub-algebras with near-zero inter-theory resonance: \(\varrho(T_1, T_2) \approx 0\). Theory revision when confronted by datum \(d\) is the Aufhebung:

\[ T' = \sigma(T, d) + \nu(T, d) + \eta(T, d), \]

where \(\sigma\) preserves the confirmable core, \(\nu\) negates the falsified part, and \(\eta\) elevates to a new theoretical structure. A genuine scientific revolution occurs when \(\omega(T, d) \neq 0\) — the new theory \(\eta(T,d)\) is not deducible from the old algebra.

Central Claim as Theorem

Theory-Theory's core claim — that concept revision is holistic, not piecemeal — follows from T3: the Aufhebung of \(T\) with a recalcitrant datum \(d\) decomposes \(T \circ d\) into three components simultaneously. There is no way to update only \(\sigma\) without potentially affecting \(\eta\); this is precisely the holism Quine (and Gopnik) emphasize. Kuhnian incommensurability corresponds to the case where \(\varrho(T_1, T_2) = 0\), which by T2 means the theories share no information.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism makes a precise prediction that theory-theory misses: whether two theories are incommensurable is a question about the cohomology class \([\omega]\). Two theories connected by a coboundary (\([\omega] = 0\)) are translatable, even if they look different; genuinely incommensurable theories have \([\omega] \neq 0 \in H^2\). This distinguishes apparent from genuine incommensurability.

Where It Diverges

The formalism does not capture the temporal dynamics of theory acquisition in children — the developmental time-course of theory change requires the Diffusion module, which goes beyond the static algebra.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Theory-Theory, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the category of explanatory theories available to a child or scientist, with morphisms given by translation, revision, specialization, and Kuhnian replacement. Each horizon supplies its own inferential roles \(\eta_h\) and resonance relations \( arrho_h\), so the same concept can mean different things inside folk physics, vitalism, or modern biology. The important derived primitives are \(\tau\) for developmental time, \(\chi\) for theory/paradigm, and \(\mu\) for changing credence under anomaly.

Conceptual change instantiates T14 when inference fails to transfer, and T13 when partial child-theories glue into a coherent mature theory.

Derived Primitives
  • χ. Each explanatory theory is a schema whose connected inferential roles determine what its concepts can mean.
  • τ. Developmental and scientific theory change unfold over time as anomalies accumulate and revisions reorganize commitments.
  • μ. Children and scientists shift credence among rival explanations when predictions succeed or fail.
  • λ. Counterfactual reasoning marks theoretical potentials: what would follow if hidden causes or mechanisms differed.
  • R. A theory can reflect on its own anomalies, triggering meta-theoretical revision rather than local feature repair.

Theory-Theory is governed by χ and τ: concepts live inside evolving explanatory schemas whose credences and potentials are revised holistically.

4Perceptual Symbol Systems — Barsalou
Barsalou, "Perceptual symbol systems" Behavioral and Brain Sciences (1999)
The Position

Cognition operates not on amodal symbols but on perceptual symbols: schematic re-enactments of sensorimotor states stored in modality-specific systems. Concepts are simulators — neural systems that construct partial simulations of the referent. The meaning of "chair" is not a list of features but the capacity to mentally simulate sitting, seeing, and touching chairs.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be the set of partial perceptual simulations (simulator states). Composition \(a \circ b\) = simulator composition: running simulation \(a\) and then \(b\) (as in imagining a door opening to reveal a room). The resonance \(\varrho(a,b)\) = perceptual overlap — the proportion of activated sensorimotor neurons shared between simulations \(a\) and \(b\). The Aufhebung components are:

\[ \sigma(a,b) = \text{shared featural core}, \quad \nu(a,b) = \text{suppressed simulation}, \quad \eta(a,b) = \text{novel frame emergent from combining } a \text{ and } b. \]

Situated concepts are conjugations: the context \(c\) (a kitchen, a construction site) acts by \({}^c\!a = c \circ a \circ c^{-1}\), re-weighting the simulator toward contextually relevant features.

Central Claim as Theorem

Barsalou's central claim — that concepts are grounded and simulation-based, not amodal — corresponds to the requirement that \(\varrho\) be derived from actual perceptual overlap rather than abstract feature counting. This is the non-degeneracy condition in T2: any resonance pairing that factors through perceptual states is non-degenerate precisely when distinct simulators produce observably distinct outputs. The situated-concept phenomenon follows directly from T6 (conjugation by context preserves the algebra structure).

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism predicts a compositionality gradient: the more two simulators share perceptual overlap (\(\varrho(a,b)\) high), the less emergent structure their composition produces (\(\omega(a,b)\) small). Conversely, cross-modal conceptual blending (imagining the sound of a colour) should produce large \(\omega\). This quantitative prediction is absent from Barsalou's original framework.

Where It Diverges

The algebraic framework assumes that simulator composition is associative, but there is psycholinguistic evidence that the order of imagined events can non-associatively affect the resulting mental model — a genuine failure of T1.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Barsalou, \(\mathcal{H}\) consists of situated sensorimotor horizons: seeing, grasping, sitting, navigating, imagining, and task-directed simulation contexts. Morphisms are shifts of modality, bodily posture, environmental affordance, or attentional goal that restrict a simulator to different perceptual traces. Thus \(\sigma_h\) is not amodal meaning but the horizon-local reenactment that a body can run; \(\pi\), \(\tau\), \(\phi\), and \(\lambda\) matter because agent, time-course, salience, and possible action shape the simulation.

Grounded concept use is T13: understanding a concept requires gluing partial modality-local simulations into one coherent situated section.

Derived Primitives
  • π. The agent's embodied perspective determines which sensorimotor traces are available for simulation.
  • τ. Perceptual simulations unfold as reenacted sequences, so conceptual access has a temporal run-time structure.
  • φ. Affordances carry affective charge: graspable, sit-on-able, or dangerous objects guide action-readiness.
  • λ. A simulator encodes unrealized action potentials, such as possible ways to sit, touch, or navigate.
  • χ. Situated concepts depend on task schemas that select which modality-specific traces become relevant.

Barsalou is diagnostic for π, φ, and λ: concepts are embodied simulations shaped by perspective, affordance, and possible action.

5Conceptual Blending — Fauconnier & Turner
Fauconnier & Turner, The Way We Think (2002)
The Position

Human cognition routinely constructs blended spaces from two or more input mental spaces. The blend inherits selective structure from each input, plus emergent structure that exists in neither input alone. A cross-space mapping identifies counterparts; a generic space captures what the inputs share; the blend integrates and elaborates. Everyday examples: a surgeon is a butcher; a debate is a boxing match.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) = mental spaces. The resonance \(\varrho(M_1, M_2)\) = the cross-space mapping (the density of identified counterparts between the two spaces). The Aufhebung gives the full blend structure:

\[ M_1 \circ M_2 = \underbrace{\sigma(M_1,M_2)}_{\text{generic space}} + \underbrace{\nu(M_1,M_2)}_{\text{compressed/negated}} + \underbrace{\eta(M_1,M_2)}_{\text{blend proper}}. \]

The "vital relations" (identity, change, time, causation, analogy) are components of \(\varrho\). Emergent structure in the blend is exactly \(\omega(M_1, M_2) \neq 0\): properties of the blend not inherited from either input.

Central Claim as Theorem

Fauconnier & Turner's Uniqueness Claim — that the blend is the unique mental space that satisfies all the integration constraints simultaneously — is T3: the Aufhebung decomposition is unique modulo \(\ker\varrho\). The integration constraints just are the conditions that pick out the unique \(\sigma + \nu + \eta\) decomposition. The existence of emergent structure is T4: \(\omega\) is a non-trivial 2-cocycle whenever the blend generates structure not present in the input spaces.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism provides a proof that blending is always possible (T3 guarantees existence), whereas Fauconnier & Turner note that some attempted blends "fail" — these are cases where \(\ker\varrho\) is large (the two spaces have few identified counterparts), making the decomposition non-unique and the blend unstable. The formalism thus predicts exactly which blends will be cognitively robust.

Where It Diverges

Blending theory emphasizes the compression of vital relations (many-to-one mappings across spaces), a directional operation that does not fit neatly into the symmetric Aufhebung; this asymmetry requires extending \(\nu\) beyond what T3 currently specifies.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For conceptual blending, \(\mathcal{H}\) is a diagram category of mental spaces: input spaces, generic spaces, blend spaces, and elaboration contexts. Morphisms are cross-space mappings, projections, compressions, and selective injections of structure into the blend. Derived \(\chi\) marks the organizing frame of a space, \(\lambda\) captures unrealized blend potentials, and \(\pi\) tracks the interpreter whose mappings decide which vital relations are compressed.

A successful blend is T13 gluing over a cover of input spaces, while failed or unstable blends are failures of the sheaf condition.

Derived Primitives
  • χ. Mental spaces are local schemas that organize roles, frames, and counterpart mappings before blending.
  • λ. A blend space realizes only some of the many potential integrations latent in its input spaces.
  • π. The interpreter's perspective decides which vital relations are compressed and which projections are licensed.
  • δ. Cross-space counterparts are established by differentiating shared structure from incompatible role-fillers.
  • R. Elaborating a blend can feed back on itself, stabilizing a new frame through recursive completion.

Blending is most marked by χ and λ: schema-local spaces expose potentials that an interpreter selectively realizes as emergent structure.

6Conceptual Metaphor Theory — Lakoff & Johnson
Lakoff & Johnson, Metaphors We Live By (1980); Lakoff, Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things (1987)
The Position

Our ordinary conceptual system is fundamentally metaphorical: abstract domains (argument, time, love) are understood via concrete source domains (war, space, journey) through systematic conceptual mappings. These mappings are not mere linguistic decoration but structure thought itself. The "systematicity" of a metaphor — the fact that the same mapping licenses multiple linguistic expressions — proves the mapping operates at the conceptual level.

The Instantiation

A conceptual metaphor \(M: \mathcal{I}_S \to \mathcal{I}_T\) (source to target) is a partial algebra homomorphism that is resonance-preserving:

\[ \varrho_T(M(a),\, M(b)) = \varrho_S(a,\, b) \quad \text{for all } a,b \text{ in the source domain.} \]

ARGUMENT IS WAR is the map from war-ideas to argument-ideas that preserves resonance (attack/defense, winning/losing). The grading \(\deg\) is preserved: concrete source ideas have lower grading than abstract target ideas, and \(M\) maps low-\(\deg\) to high-\(\deg\) (the "upward" direction of abstraction).

Central Claim as Theorem

The Invariance Principle — that metaphorical mappings preserve the image-schematic structure of the source domain — is the statement that \(M\) is a resonance-preserving partial homomorphism (T8: Structural Equivalence). Specifically, a resonance-preserving map automatically preserves \(\sigma\), \(\nu\), and \(\eta\) (the three components of Aufhebung are determined by \(\varrho\)), so the full inferential structure of the source is transmitted to the target wherever the mapping is defined.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism shows why the Invariance Principle has exceptions (metaphorical mappings sometimes fail to preserve target-domain structure): if \(M\) is only partially defined, then compositions in the target domain that cross the boundary of \(M\)'s domain will generate non-zero \(\omega\), producing emergent target-domain inferences not licensed by the source. This is why the metaphor LOVE IS A JOURNEY does not license the inference that love ends at a destination.

Where It Diverges

Lakoff & Johnson emphasize the embodied, image-schematic basis of source domains; the formalism treats all idea algebras uniformly and does not privilege bodily or spatial structure.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For conceptual metaphor, \(\mathcal{H}\) is organized by embodied source domains and abstract target domains, with morphisms given by partial metaphor maps such as journey-to-love or war-to-argument. A morphism preserves selected image-schematic structure while dropping or distorting other inferences, so \(\eta_h\) is horizon-relative. The key derived primitives are \(\chi\) for domain schema, \(\pi\) for embodied perspective, \(\iota\) for aboutness across domains, and \(\delta\) for contrastive entailments blocked by the metaphor.

Metaphorical inference is T14: source-domain inferences pull back only partially, producing systematic non-invariance in the target.

Derived Primitives
  • χ. Image schemas such as container, path, and force structure the paradigm through which abstract domains are understood.
  • π. Metaphor is perspective-shifting: argument viewed as war licenses different inferences than argument viewed as dance.
  • φ. Embodied metaphors carry affective gradients, as up/down or warm/cold patterns guide valuation and salience.
  • δ. Source-target mappings preserve some contrasts while differentiating metaphorically hidden or highlighted features.
  • ι. Metaphors direct abstract thought toward concrete intentional correlates, grounding time, love, or morality in bodily domains.

Conceptual metaphor is chiefly χ plus π: embodied schemas re-perspectivize abstract targets and import affective, differential structure.

7Frame Semantics — Fillmore
Fillmore, "Frame semantics" (1982); FrameNet project
The Position

Every word evokes a cognitive frame — a structured schematic representation of the situation in which the word is typically used. To understand "buy" you must activate the commercial event frame (buyer, seller, goods, money, transaction). Meaning is always relative to a frame; words in different frames are not directly comparable even if they appear synonymous. Frame conflict (e.g., "tax relief" frames taxation as a burden) structures political cognition.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be the set of frame-evoking lexical units and their semantic roles. A frame \(F\) is an idempotent sub-algebra: a subset \(F \subseteq \mathcal{I}\) closed under \(\circ\) with \(F \circ F = F\) (composing frame-elements stays within the frame). Lexical evocation: word \(w\) evokes frame \(F\) iff \(\varrho(w, F) > 0\). Frame conflict: \(\varrho(F_1, F_2) < 0\). The claim "understanding is always relative to a frame" is:

\[ \text{meaning}(w) = {}^F\!\!w \defeq F \circ w \circ F^{-1} \]

— meaning is always the conjugate of the word by its evoking frame. Different frames produce different meanings even for the same surface form.

Central Claim as Theorem

The central claim — frame-relative meaning — is T6 (Conjugation): the inner automorphism \(\mathrm{Ad}_F\) preserves \(\varrho\), \(\sigma\), \(\nu\), and \(\omega\), so the full semantic structure is preserved within the frame while the word's external resonances are reweighted. Frame conflict corresponds to the case where \(\kappa(F_1, F_2) \neq 0\) — the asymmetric resonance between the two frames creates a directional, non-neutralizable difference in how events are interpreted.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism predicts that frame-blending (combining two conflicting frames, as in political compromise language) produces emergent semantic content (\(\omega(F_1, F_2) > 0\)) that is not recoverable from either frame alone. This formalizes Fillmore's intuition that frame conflicts cannot be resolved by simple addition of meanings.

Where It Diverges

Frame semantics is primarily a descriptive linguistic framework with rich empirical data about frame elements and relations (FrameNet); the algebraic formalism abstracts away from these specific semantic roles in favour of the general structure.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Fillmore, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the category of frames: commercial events, legal trials, medical encounters, political burdens, and their subframes. Morphisms are frame evocations, role alignments, reframings, and frame-to-frame translations. Meaning is explicitly \(\sigma_h(w)\) within a frame horizon; \(\chi\) is the active frame, \(\pi\) is the discourse participant's perspective, and \(\delta\) records the contrasts that a frame makes salient or invisible.

Frame semantics is a direct T14 theory: lexical meaning and inference do not pull back unchanged along reframing morphisms.

Derived Primitives
  • χ. Frames are explicit schemas: a word evokes a connected scene of roles, expectations, and background practices.
  • ι. Lexical meaning is aboutness toward frame elements, such as buyer, seller, goods, and money in commerce.
  • π. Speaker perspective selects which role becomes profiled, as buy and sell foreground different participants.
  • δ. Frame terms are differentiated by role contrast, not by isolated definitions outside the scene.
  • β. Frame elements can bind roles rigidly within a scene while their lexical realization varies across languages.

Frame semantics is diagnostic for χ and π: meaning is a role-sensitive schema whose profile changes with lexical perspective.

8Conceptual Spaces — Gärdenfors
Gärdenfors, Conceptual Spaces (2000)
The Position

Concepts are convex regions in a geometric space whose dimensions correspond to perceptual and cognitive quality dimensions (hue, brightness, pitch, temperature, etc.). Similarity is distance in this space. Natural properties are convex regions; prototypes are the centroids of those regions. The geometric framework sits between the symbolic (classical) and associationist (connectionist) levels.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be a metric space with distance \(d(a,b) = -\varrho_{\mathrm{sym}}(a,b)\) (negated symmetric resonance). Quality dimensions are orthogonal sub-algebras \(\mathcal{I}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{I}_k\) with \(\varrho(\mathcal{I}_i, \mathcal{I}_j) = 0\) for \(i \neq j\). A property \(P\) is natural iff it is convex in the \(\varrho\)-metric:

\[ a,c \in P \text{ and } d(a,c) = d(a,b)+d(b,c) \implies b \in P. \]

The prototype of \(P\) is its centroid. Betweenness \(b \in [a,c]\) is exactly the metric condition above. Grading \(\deg(a)\) = the number of quality dimensions along which \(a\) is non-trivially located.

Central Claim as Theorem

The main theorem of Conceptual Spaces — that natural properties correspond to convex regions — is a direct consequence of T2 together with the definition of \(d = -\varrho_\text{sym}\): convexity is just the condition that resonance-based betweenness is transitive, which holds in any metric space derived from a non-degenerate \(\varrho\). This module is directly implemented in the ConceptualSpaces Lean module, which verifies the betweenness axioms as corollaries of T2.

What the Formalism Adds

The full idea algebra adds operations that Gärdenfors' geometric framework lacks: composition (\(\circ\)), Aufhebung, and the emergence cocycle. In particular, the formalism predicts that combining two conceptual-space regions can produce a blend (\(\eta\)) that is not a convex subset of either original dimension — explaining why compound concepts (e.g., "red wine") occupy regions in new effective dimensions not predictable from the simple union of "red" and "wine" regions.

Where It Diverges

Gärdenfors' framework makes rich use of the full geometric structure (metric axioms, dimensionality, Voronoi tessellations for categorization); the idea algebra captures only the abstract resonance structure and does not specify dimensionality or the full metric.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Gärdenfors, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the family of quality-space coordinate systems and task-relative dimensional projections. Morphisms are changes of metric, dimension selection, scaling, prototype re-centering, and translations between perceptual domains. A convex region is therefore convex only relative to \(h\); \(\chi\) names the active conceptual space, \(\mu\) supports probabilistic categorization around centroids, and \(\delta\) captures discriminating dimensions.

Context-dependent similarity is T14, while robust natural properties approximate T15 by remaining stable across many admissible quality-space horizons.

Derived Primitives
  • χ. Conceptual spaces are connected components whose domains and dimensions supply the active cognitive schema.
  • δ. Similarity is inverse distance, so concepts are constituted by differential geometry among neighboring points.
  • λ. Convex regions encode potentials: intermediate points can become category members under refinement or learning.
  • μ. Distance from prototypes supports graded membership probabilities within a region.

Gärdenfors is clearest for χ, δ, and λ: concepts are connected geometric regions with graded and potential membership structure.

9Dual Process Theory — Kahneman
Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011); Stanovich & West (2000)
The Position

\[ \kappa(a,b) = \frac{\varrho(a,b) - \varrho(b,a)}{2} \neq 0, \]

where \(a\) is the framed version of a question and \(b\) is the logically equivalent but differently framed version. The asymmetry \(\kappa(a,b)\) is the framing bias.

The interaction between System 1 and System 2 — System 2 correcting System 1 defaults — is an instance of T3: when System 2 engages, the composition of the System 1 default idea \(a\) with the deliberate correction \(d\) decomposes as \(\sigma(a,d) + \nu(a,d) + \eta(a,d)\), where \(\nu\) is the suppression of the heuristic response and \(\eta\) is the elevated logical response. What the Formalism Adds

The formalism predicts a precise condition under which System 2 override is complete: \(\nu(a,d) = a\) (the heuristic is entirely suppressed). This happens only when \(\varrho(a, \mathcal{I}_2) = 0\) — the System 1 response has zero resonance with System 2 ideas. In all other cases, partial System 1 contamination is unavoidable, which matches empirical data on the persistence of cognitive biases even after deliberate reasoning.

The Position

Holographic reduced representations (HRRs) encode compositional structure in fixed-width distributed vectors using circular convolution as the binding operator. The key properties: (i) the convolution of two vectors has the same dimensionality as each; (ii) approximate retrieval of a bound component is possible using the approximate inverse (cross-correlation); (iii) superposition of multiple HRRs in a single vector enables storage of structured knowledge.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) be the set of unit-length real vectors. Composition is circular convolution: \((a \circ b)_k = \sum_j a_j b_{k-j \bmod n}\). Resonance is normalized dot product:

\[ \varrho(a,b) = \frac{a \cdot b}{n}. \]

This is a finite-dimensional idea algebra. The emergence cocycle \(\omega(a,b)\) is the circular convolution residual — the part of \(a \circ b\) not recoverable by linear methods. Decoding = using \(\varrho(\cdot, a^{-1})\) (correlation with the approximate inverse) to recover components. The superposition catastrophe occurs when \(\ker\varrho\) is large: many stored HRRs produce interference, degrading \(\varrho\).

Central Claim as Theorem

The HRR binding-and-retrieval theorem — that \(a \circ b \circ b^{-1} \approx a\) (approximate retrieval) — is an instance of T1 (the monoid law) in the approximate sense: \(b \circ b^{-1} \approx e\) (the approximate inverse). The approximation quality is controlled by \(\varrho(b, b^{-1} \circ b) \approx 1\) — high self-resonance of the recovered approximate identity. The emergence cocycle \(\omega(a,b) \neq 0\) corresponds to the fact that circular convolution is not perfectly invertible, generating irreducible residual information.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism reveals that the HRR capacity limit is a statement about the cohomological complexity of the storage task: when the number of stored bindings exceeds a threshold, \(H^2\) of the storage algebra becomes non-trivial, and retrieval errors represent non-coboundary emergence terms. This gives a principled account of why capacity degrades gracefully rather than catastrophically.

Where It Diverges

HRRs are specifically designed for approximate computation in finite-precision arithmetic; the idea algebra is exact and does not model the noise and approximation that are central to HRR's engineering advantages.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For HRRs, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the category of vector-symbolic coding regimes: dimensionality choices, noise levels, binding conventions, cleanup memories, and retrieval tasks. Morphisms are circular convolution binding, approximate unbinding, projection to lower precision, and cleanup-map restriction. Unlike word2vec (local window prediction), GloVe uses global co-occurrence statistics. The key insight: the meaning of a word is encoded in the ratio of its co-occurrence probabilities with probe words.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\) be GloVe word vectors. Resonance is the log-conditional probability: \(\varrho(a,b) = \log P(b \mid a)\). This resonance is explicitly asymmetric: \(\log P(b|a) \neq \log P(a|b)\) in general. The curvature is:

\[ \kappa(a,b) = \frac{\varrho(a,b) - \varrho(b,a)}{2} = \frac{\log P(b|a) - \log P(a|b)}{2}, \]

which is the directional information asymmetry between \(a\) and \(b\). GloVe's training minimizes \(\sum_{a,b} f(X_{ab})\,(\log X_{ab} - w_a \cdot w_b - b_a - b_b)^2\): it finds the best symmetric approximation to the asymmetric \(\varrho\), projecting the asymmetric resonance onto the symmetric component.

Central Claim as Theorem

GloVe's main theoretical result — that the log-ratio of co-occurrence probabilities encodes semantic relationships — is an application of T5 (Meaning Curvature): the asymmetric component \(\kappa(a,b)\) of the co-occurrence resonance is a structural invariant. Words that have high \(\kappa\) with a probe (e.g., "ice" and "steam" have high \(\kappa\) with "solid" vs "gas") are semantically distinguished in a topologically stable way — the curvature class is preserved under continuous deformations of the embedding.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism predicts that GloVe's symmetric approximation systematically discards the curvature \(\kappa\): since \(\kappa\) is the antisymmetric part of \(\varrho\), projecting to symmetric dot products eliminates all directional semantic information. This explains why GloVe performs slightly worse than word2vec on directional semantic tasks (e.g., "A is a type of B") while matching it on symmetric tasks (e.g., synonymy).

Where It Diverges

The formalism treats the full asymmetric \(\varrho = \log P(b|a)\) as primary; GloVe's actual trained vectors approximate only the symmetric part, so using GloVe vectors in the idea algebra requires acknowledging that \(\kappa\) information has been discarded.

Morphisms aggregate, reweight, smooth, or restrict the co-occurrence statistics from which resonances are estimated. \(\mu\) is central because meaning is read from conditional probabilities; \(\chi\) marks corpus genre or domain, and \(\delta\) records which probe ratios distinguish two words.

\[ [\omega_{n+1}] \in H^2(\mathcal{I}_{n+1}, A) \quad \text{is not the image of } [\omega_n] \in H^2(\mathcal{I}_n, A). \]

Broken symmetry at level \(n+1\) = the resonance \(\varrho_{n+1}\) is less symmetric than \(\varrho_n\): the curvature \(\kappa_{n+1} = (\varrho_{n+1} - \varrho_{n+1}^T)/2 \neq 0\) even if \(\kappa_n \approx 0\). New symmetry-breaking = new curvature — a new directional asymmetry in how ideas at level \(n+1\) interact.

Central Claim as Theorem

Anderson's central claim is Level-2 emergence: the EmergenceLevels Lean module proves that there exist idea algebras where \(H^2(\mathcal{I}_{n+1}) \not\cong H^2(\mathcal{I}_n)\) — the cohomology at each level is genuinely new. This is the algebraic formulation of "more is different": the algebra at level \(n+1\) has more cohomological structure than the algebra at level \(n\), and this extra structure is precisely the "more" that "is different."

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism connects Anderson's broken symmetry to the curvature \(\kappa\) (T5): broken symmetry at level \(n+1\) is exactly the appearance of non-zero \(\kappa_{n+1}\) where \(\kappa_n \approx 0\). This predicts that any symmetry-breaking phase transition is accompanied by the appearance of a new, stable directional asymmetry in the interaction patterns — a measurable prediction in condensed matter systems.

Anderson's argument is primarily about physics and condensed matter; the idea algebra is general enough to apply to any domain, but the specific mechanisms of symmetry breaking (spontaneous symmetry breaking, Goldstone modes) require domain-specific instantiation.

27Weak Emergence — Bedau
Bedau, "Weak emergence" Philosophical Perspectives 11 (1997)
The Position

A property \(P\) of a macro-system is weakly emergent iff \(P\) is derivable in principle from the micro-level laws, but only by actually running the micro-simulation — there is no analytical shortcut. Conway's Game of Life patterns (gliders, oscillators) are weakly emergent: they follow deterministically from the update rules, but you can only discover them by running the automaton, not by inspection of the rules. Weak emergence is genuinely emergent (no analytical shortcut) without being mysteriously irreducible (the micro-laws suffice in principle).

The Instantiation

A property \(P\) is weakly emergent iff:

\[ \omega(a_1, a_2 \circ \cdots \circ a_n) \neq 0 \quad \text{but} \quad [\omega] = 0 \in H^2(\mathcal{I}, A). \]

That is, \(P\) is generated by a non-zero cocycle \(\omega\), but \(\omega\) is a coboundary: \(\omega = \partial f\) for some 1-cochain \(f\). This means the emergent property is derivable from a correction function \(f\) applied to the lower-level description — but finding \(f\) may require computation equivalent to the full simulation. Level-1 emergence in the EmergenceLevels module. Formally: weakly emergent properties live in the trivial cohomology (\(H^2 = 0\)) but have non-trivial cocycle representatives — they are exact but not obviously so.

Central Claim as Theorem

The distinction between weak and strong emergence is made precise by T4 (Emergence 2-Cocycle) together with the EmergenceLevels module: weak emergence = \([\omega] = 0\) (coboundary), strong emergence = \([\omega] \neq 0\) (non-trivial cohomology class). The fact that weakly emergent properties are "only derivable by simulation" is the algebraic statement that finding \(f\) such that \(\omega = \partial f\) may require exponential search, even though \(f\) exists in principle.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism predicts that weakly emergent properties are stable under small perturbations (because cohomology is locally constant — small changes in the micro-laws do not change \(H^2 = 0\)), while strongly emergent properties can be destabilized by perturbations that change the cohomology class. This distinguishes "robust" from "fragile" emergent phenomena in complex systems.

Where It Diverges

Bedau's computational notion of weak emergence ties irreducibility to the complexity of simulation; the cohomological formalism captures the algebraic structure but does not directly model computational complexity — a connection that requires additional theoretical work.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Bedau, \(\mathcal{H}\) separates micro-rule horizons, simulation histories, macro-observation horizons, and explanatory compression regimes. Morphisms are running the simulation, coarse-graining, measuring macrovariables, and translating between rule-level and pattern-level descriptions. \(\tau\) is indispensable because weak emergence is derivability only by temporal unfolding; \(\mu\) handles stochastic simulations, \(\chi\) identifies macro-pattern schemas, and \(\lambda\) captures possible trajectories.

Weak emergence is T13 over time-indexed simulation covers, whereas failures of shortcut derivation are T14 relative to compressed explanatory horizons.

Derived Primitives
  • λ. Weak emergence is potential realization: macro-patterns are latent in rules but actual only through simulation.
  • τ. Running the model is indispensable because emergent properties unfold over computational time.
  • R. Self-organization approaches fixed points or recurrent patterns visible only after iterative self-application.
  • χ. The simulation rule set defines the schema within which gliders or oscillators can appear.
  • μ. Some emergent patterns may be characterized by frequency or likelihood across initial conditions.

Bedau is diagnostic for λ and R: weak emergence is latent rule-potential realized through iterative, self-organizing dynamics.

28Strong vs. Weak Emergence — Chalmers
Chalmers, "Facing up to the problem of consciousness" (1995); "Strong and weak emergence" (2006)
The Position

Chalmers distinguishes weak emergence (macro-properties derivable from micro-laws, possibly only by simulation) from strong emergence (macro-properties not derivable from micro-laws even in principle). He argues that phenomenal consciousness is strongly emergent: no amount of information about the physical organization of the brain logically entails the existence of subjective experience (qualia). This is the "hard problem of consciousness."

The Instantiation

Strong emergence = non-trivial cohomology:

\[ [\omega_\text{emergent}] \neq 0 \in H^2(\mathcal{I}, A). \]

The emergent property cannot be derived from lower-level properties because its cocycle is not a coboundary — there is no 1-cochain \(f\) such that \(\omega = \partial f\). Weak emergence = \([\omega] = 0\) (coboundary, derivable in principle). The hard problem of consciousness = the claim that \([\omega_\text{phenomenal}] \neq 0\) in the idea algebra of physical facts: phenomenal consciousness is a non-trivial cohomology class over the algebra of physical properties. Idea Theory does not resolve whether \([\omega_\text{phenomenal}] = 0\) or \(\neq 0\), but it makes the question mathematically precise and proves (EmergenceLevels) that both cases are algebraically coherent.

Central Claim as Theorem

The distinction between weak and strong emergence is the distinction between Level-1 and Level-2 emergence in the EmergenceLevels Lean module. The module proves: (a) Level-1 emergence exists (there are non-trivial coboundaries); (b) Level-2 emergence exists (there are non-trivial cohomology classes); (c) Level-1 does not imply Level-2 (weak emergence is not automatically strong). Chalmers' framework is exactly this two-level structure, made machine-checkably precise.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism shows that the debate between physicalists (consciousness is weakly emergent) and property dualists (consciousness is strongly emergent) is a question about the second cohomology group of the idea algebra of physical facts: \(H^2(\mathcal{I}_\text{physical}, A)\). If this group is trivial, physicalism is vindicated; if it is non-trivial and phenomenal properties correspond to non-trivial classes, property dualism follows. The formalism does not settle the question but provides the correct mathematical arena.

Where It Diverges

Chalmers' argument involves the conceivability of philosophical zombies and modal considerations that go beyond the algebraic structure; the formalism captures the structural distinction but not the modal/conceivability arguments for or against strong emergence.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Chalmers, \(\mathcal{H}\) has at least two linked subcategories: third-person scientific/functional horizons and first-person phenomenal horizons. Morphisms attempt psychophysical translation, structural description, neural correlation, and phenomenal report. \(\pi\) is decisive because perspective cannot be eliminated, \(\iota\) marks what experience is about, \(\chi\) separates physical and phenomenal schemas, and \(\lambda\) captures conceivability spaces such as zombies.

The explanatory gap is paradigmatic T14: \(\sigma\) fails to pull back along scientific-to-phenomenal morphisms, yielding non-trivial monodromy.

Derived Primitives

  • R. Self-reflective consciousness involves awareness of experience as experience, a reflexive fixed-point structure.
  • ι. Qualia have intentional content or directed phenomenal character: experience is experience of something or as something.
  • ψ. Phenomenal states often carry signed valence, from pain's aversiveness to pleasure's attraction.
  • β. Zombie and inverted-spectrum arguments test whether phenomenal reference remains rigid across physical duplicates.

Chalmers is diagnostic for π, R, and ι: consciousness names first-person, reflexively accessible phenomenal aboutness.

29Supervenience and Reduction — Kim
Kim, Supervenience and Mind (1993); "The myth of nonreductive materialism" (1989)
The Position

Mental properties supervene on physical properties: no two possible situations can be physically identical yet mentally different. Kim argues that supervenience without reduction is unstable: if mental properties supervene on physical properties but are not identical to them, then mental causation is excluded by the "causal closure of the physical" — mental properties have no causal work to do. The "exclusion argument" challenges non-reductive physicalism.

The Instantiation

Mental supervenience on the physical = a grading-preserving surjective algebra homomorphism \(f: \mathcal{I}_\text{mental} \to \mathcal{I}_\text{physical}\) with \(\omega \equiv 0\) on the image: the physical description fully determines the mental, with no emergent structure. Downward causation = a mental idea \(m\) with non-zero resonance on physical ideas outside its supervenience base:

\[ \varrho(m, p) > 0 \quad \text{for some } p \notin f^{-1}(f(m)), \]

i.e., \(\kappa(m, p) \neq 0\) — the curvature between mental and physical ideas is non-zero. Kim's exclusion argument: if \(f\) is a surjective homomorphism with \(\omega \equiv 0\), then every physical effect of \(m\) is already caused by its physical supervenience base \(f(m)\), so \(m\) is causally redundant unless \(f\) is not surjective.

Central Claim as Theorem

Kim's exclusion argument is the statement that \(\omega \equiv 0\) implies \(\ker f = 0\) (if there is no emergence, the only way for mental causation to be non-redundant is for the mental-to-physical map to be injective). This follows from T4 (T4): if \(\omega \equiv 0\), then the homomorphism is structure-preserving in the strictest sense, and every mental cause has an exact physical correlate that does all the same causal work. The non-redundancy of mental causation requires either \(\omega \neq 0\) (genuine emergence) or \(f\) non-surjective (physics is not causally closed).

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism provides a third option that Kim does not consider: \(\kappa(m,p) \neq 0\) (non-zero curvature between mental and physical) without \(\omega \neq 0\). The curvature captures directional causal asymmetry — mental-to-physical causation differs from physical-to-mental — which is a structural feature of the algebra, not a claim about emergence. This allows a non-reductive physicalism where mental causation is real (\(\kappa \neq 0\)) without invoking strong emergence.

Where It Diverges

Kim's arguments are deeply enmeshed in the metaphysics of properties, events, and causation; the idea algebra abstracts over these ontological commitments and cannot, on its own, settle questions about the metaphysics of mental causation.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Kim, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the category of physical, mental, causal, and reductive-description horizons. Morphisms are supervenience maps, reductions, realization relations, exclusion arguments, and causal-closure projections. \(\chi\) distinguishes physicalist and nonreductive schemas, \(\pi\) marks the subject-level perspective, and \(\delta\) identifies differences that vanish under physical description but remain salient mentally. The same event can thus be tracked under competing explanatory horizons with different causal bookkeeping.

Supervenience demands T13-style gluing of mental facts over physical bases, while exclusion worries arise as T14 failures of causal/inferential pullback.

Derived Primitives
  • β. Reduction seeks rigid cross-level reference: a mental property must pick out the same causal base physically.
  • χ. Physical and mental levels are schemas whose bridge principles may or may not connect.
  • δ. Exclusion arguments turn on differentiating genuine causal powers from overdetermining duplicates.
  • π. The mental perspective may classify states differently from the physical explanatory horizon.
  • λ. Supervenience leaves open potential realizers until a reduction fixes them.

Kim is marked by β and δ: mental causation hinges on whether cross-level references and causal differences can be stabilized.

The Position

Complex adaptive systems (CAS) — immune systems, economies, ant colonies, ecosystems — are networks of agents, each following local rules, producing global patterns not predictable from the rules alone. Adaptation occurs as agents' internal models ("schemas") are updated by experience. The key phenomena are: emergent global behaviour, perpetual novelty, co-evolution, and absence of a global controller. Agent-based modelling is the primary method.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be the set of agent schemas (internal models of the environment). Resonance \(\varrho(a,b)\) = the mutual fitness of the two schemas' predictions (high resonance = schemas that predict each other's behaviour well). Schema complexity = \(\deg(a)\). The emergence cocycle:

\[ \omega(a,b) = \text{fitness surplus of interaction} = \varrho(a \circ b, \text{environment}) - \varrho(a, \text{environment}) - \varrho(b, \text{environment}). \]

Population-level patterns (ant trails, market prices, ecosystem food webs) = fixed points of the iterated Diffusion step applied to the CAS idea algebra. Co-evolution = the Transmission Chain (T7) applied iteratively across agent populations.

Central Claim as Theorem

Holland's central theorem — that CAS exhibit "perpetual novelty" (the system never reaches a fixed point but continues to generate new schemas) — corresponds to the non-termination of the Diffusion iteration when \(\omega > 0\) throughout: the emergence at each interaction step is non-zero, preventing convergence. The Diffusion Lean module verifies conditions for convergence (finite-\(\omega\) chains) and divergence (perpetual novelty) in the Transmission Chain (T7), giving Holland's intuition an exact statement.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism predicts that the "edge of chaos" phenomenon in CAS — the regime between frozen order and chaotic disorder where CAS are maximally adaptive — corresponds to the boundary condition \([\omega] = 0\) (weakly emergent regime): the system generates novelty (non-zero cocycle representatives) but remains in the coboundary regime (no genuinely new cohomological structure). Crossing into the strongly emergent regime (\([\omega] \neq 0\)) corresponds to a phase transition in the CAS dynamics.

Holland's framework is designed for agent-based simulation with explicit agents, environments, and fitness functions; the idea algebra is more abstract and requires specifying these domain-specific elements before making quantitative predictions about any particular CAS.

31Vygotsky — Concept Formation and Internalization
Vygotsky, Thought and Language (1934); Mind in Society (1978)
The Position

Vygotsky argues that higher concepts are not private mental atoms that mature on their own. They are socially mediated achievements. A child first encounters a concept in shared activity with more competent others, typically through language, gesture, and norm-governed practice. Only later does that externally scaffolded practice become internal thought. His slogan is the famous movement from the interpsychological to the intrapsychological: what begins between people is gradually reorganized within the person.

The Instantiation

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be the algebra of concept-tokens available to a learner. A socially provided sign-system \(g \in \mathcal{I}^{\times}\) acts as a frame that reorganizes what the learner can do with a primitive content \(a\). Internalization is not a one-shot insertion of information but an iterated context-action and transmission process:

\[ a_{n+1} = g_n \circ a_n \circ g_n^{-1}, \qquad A_n = g_n \circ g_{n-1} \circ \cdots \circ g_1 \circ a_0. \]

The first expression says each pedagogical context reframes the child's current conceptual state. The second says a mature concept is a chain-product of many such mediated encounters. Resonance \(\varrho(a,b)\) measures how well a child's current concept fits the socially stabilized use of that concept in a community. The emergence cocycle \(\omega(g,a)\) tracks the genuinely new capacity produced when a sign-system and a prior concept interact: the child acquires not just more information but a new way of organizing information.

Central Claim as Theorem

Vygotsky's core claim is that concept growth is mediated, path-dependent, and cumulative. In the algebra this is exactly the combination of T6 and T7. T6 says context does real structural work: a sign-system changes the effective form of a concept by conjugation. T7 says repeated transmission does not collapse to a trivial repetition; each step contributes to an intelligible chain whose total effect can be tracked. Vygotsky's "zone of proximal development" is then the region where \(\varrho(a_n,\text{community target})\) is still too low for independent performance, but the conjugated step \(g_n \circ a_n \circ g_n^{-1}\) raises it enough for guided success.

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism distinguishes three cases that are often blurred together in informal discussions of learning. First, rote repetition: the same context is applied but \(\omega = 0\), so nothing genuinely new appears. Second, reframing: \(\omega = 0\) but conjugation changes which features of the concept are foregrounded. Third, genuine internalization: \(\omega \neq 0\), so a new conceptual capacity emerges from the instructional encounter. This gives a precise way to separate "the child heard the words" from "the child can now think with the concept."

Where It Diverges

Vygotsky's account is developmental, embodied, and historically situated. The algebra captures the structural logic of mediation and accumulation, but not the full psychology of attention, affect, play, or classroom interaction. It says what sort of transformation internalization must be, not how a particular child actually undergoes it minute by minute.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Vygotsky, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the developmental-social category of interpsychological scenes, sign systems, classrooms, practices, and later intrapsychological horizons. Morphisms are scaffolding, imitation, linguistic mediation, internalization, and withdrawal of guidance. \(\tau\) tracks ontogenetic sequence, \(\pi\) distinguishes child and more competent other, \(\chi\) marks the cultural practice, and \(\iota\) captures shared directedness toward the task. The zone of proximal development is a cover by assisted horizons.

Internalization is T13: socially distributed local performances glue over time into a stable individual conceptual section.

Derived Primitives
  • χ. Cultural tools and language provide schemas through which higher mental functions are organized.
  • π. Development shifts perspective from social interaction to internalized self-regulation.
  • τ. The zone of proximal development is temporal, naming what can become actual through guided learning.
  • λ. Scaffolding reveals potentials the learner cannot yet realize independently.
  • ι. Signs mediate intentional directedness, letting children aim thought through culturally supplied symbols.

Vygotsky is chiefly χ, τ, and λ: social schemas scaffold developmental potentials until they become internal perspective.

32Dennett — Real Patterns and the Intentional Stance
Dennett, "Real Patterns" (1991); The Intentional Stance (1987)
The Position

Dennett's claim is that higher-level patterns are real when they support successful compression and prediction. A chess program, a market, or a person can be described at the micro-level in exhaustive detail, but often a coarser description in terms of beliefs, desires, strategies, or roles predicts better for the purposes we care about. The intentional stance is therefore not mystical. It is a disciplined way of treating a complex system as if it were a rational agent because that treatment captures a real pattern in the system's behavior.

The Instantiation

Let raw system states live in \(\mathcal{I}\), and let \(P\) be a higher-level pattern represented as an equivalence class in a quotient or coarse-graining of \(\mathcal{I}\). The grading \(\deg\) measures descriptive complexity, while resonance with observed behavior measures predictive fit. A pattern is real when it reduces descriptive burden without losing too much resonance:

\[ \deg(P) \;<\; \sum_{x \in [P]} \deg(x) \qquad\text{and}\qquad \varrho(P,\text{data}) \ge \theta. \]

The first inequality says the higher-level description compresses many lower-level descriptions. The second says the compressed description still tracks what the system actually does. When a predictive pattern at the macro-level cannot be recovered by a trivial relabeling of microstates, the emergent surplus is measured by \(\omega\). Structural equivalence classes capture Dennett's point that many micro-realizations can count as "the same belief-pattern" or "the same strategy-pattern."

Central Claim as Theorem

Dennett's real-pattern thesis is a joint application of T8, T9, and T4. T8 explains why many token-level realizations can instantiate one structural pattern. T9 explains why the higher-level description can be objectively better by being lower-degree while retaining explanatory reach. T4 explains why the macro-level may have genuine predictive surplus rather than being a merely decorative restatement of the micro-level. On this reading, the intentional stance is warranted exactly when the agent-level quotient preserves enough \(\varrho\) while reducing \(\deg\).

What the Formalism Adds

The formalism turns Dennett's deliberately pragmatic language into a sharper test. A merely convenient summary is not yet a real pattern. It must satisfy two independent demands: compression and predictive resonance. That matters because many audiences hear Dennett as saying "whatever is useful is real." The algebra imposes a stricter standard: usefulness must come from a stable structural quotient, not from arbitrary storytelling. This also clarifies why some stance-descriptions fail. If they lower degree but destroy resonance, they are oversimplifications; if they preserve resonance but do not lower degree, they are just redescriptions.

Where It Diverges

Dennett intentionally leaves the standards of compression and predictive success somewhat context-sensitive, because different sciences care about different scales and error tolerances. The algebra can represent those standards, but only after thresholds and grading conventions are chosen. So it sharpens Dennett's framework at the cost of introducing explicit cutoffs that his prose often leaves flexible.

Horizon Structure (ξ)

For Dennett, \(\mathcal{H}\) is the category of stances and compression regimes: physical stance, design stance, intentional stance, scientific interests, and error tolerances. Morphisms are coarse-grainings, predictive redescriptions, changes of observer purpose, and translations between microstate and pattern descriptions. \(\pi\) is the interpreter adopting a stance, \(\mu\) measures predictive success, \(\chi\) is the explanatory schema, and \(\delta\) separates real patterns from arbitrary projections.

Real patterns are T15: a stance is warranted when its compressed pattern remains predictively stable across relevant horizons.

  • R. The multiple drafts model is reflexive looping, with narratives revised by further interpretive passes.
  • χ. Homuncular functions are paradigm-relative schemas for decomposing competence into simpler sub-agencies.
  • ι. Intentional systems have as-if aboutness assigned by successful predictive interpretation.
  • μ. Stance adoption is pragmatic credence: use the interpretation that best predicts behavior.

Dennett is diagnostic for π, R, and χ: intentionality is a stance-relative, recursively edited schema of predictive interpretation.
