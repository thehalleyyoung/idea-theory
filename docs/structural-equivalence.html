structural equivalence

Abstract. This page formalizes structural equivalence of ideas — the relation that holds between two elements of the graded idea monoid whenever they are interchangeable under composition, pairing, and grading, even though they may differ as raw symbolic tokens. We show that structural equivalence is the canonical congruence on the idea algebra, that it is preserved by conjugation and by the emergence 2-cocycle, and that the quotient by it yields a well-defined "structural type" graded monoid. The three headline machine-verified results — Theorem 8.1 (structural equivalence is a graded monoid congruence), Theorem 8.2 (invariance under conjugation and the resonance pairing), and Theorem 8.3 (the structural quotient inherits the Aufhebung decomposition and emergence cocycle) — together justify treating "ideas up to structural equivalence" as a first-class object. The page bridges this formal apparatus to the long informal tradition of speaking about "the same idea in different words," from Frege and Quine through structuralist anthropology, conceptual role semantics, and contemporary debates about social-scientific concept identity.

Historical and Intellectual Context

The intuition that two ideas can be "the same idea in different words" is older than modern logic. Frege's distinction between Sinn and Bedeutung (Frege, 1892) already separated the bare sign from a structured mode of presentation, and his notion of equipollent sentences foreshadows a syntactic equivalence that respects inferential role. In the analytic tradition, Quine (1951, 1960) sharpened the question by attacking the analytic/synonymous distinction and by proposing that sameness of meaning could only be a holistic, structural relation between sentences embedded in a web of belief. Carnap's earlier intensional isomorphism (Carnap, 1947) was an explicit attempt to give structural sameness a formal definition: two expressions are intensionally isomorphic when they are built from intensionally equivalent constituents in the same syntactic way.

Outside analytic philosophy, an analogous problem arose in structuralist anthropology and linguistics. Lévi-Strauss (1963) treated myths as token-realizations of deeper structural patterns, and his "canonical formula" of myth was an early attempt to write down an equivalence relation under which two superficially different stories instantiate the same idea. In sociology, the network-analytic notion of structural equivalence introduced by Lorrain and White (1971) gave the term its modern technical meaning: two actors are structurally equivalent when they occupy the same position in a network of relations. Borgatti and Everett (1992) generalized this to regular equivalence, weakening the requirement from identical neighbors to neighbors of the same type — a move that exactly mirrors the algebraic distinction between equality and congruence.

In the philosophy of mind and cognitive science, Block (1986) and Harman (1987) developed conceptual role semantics, on which the content of a concept is fixed by its inferential and computational role; two tokens are the same idea precisely when they play the same role. Fodor and Lepore (1992) famously objected that role-based identity is too coarse to distinguish genuine sameness from mere functional similarity. The version of structural equivalence we formalize here is designed to make this debate precise: it gives a definition under which Fodor's worry corresponds to a specific failure of the congruence axioms, and under which Block's intuition corresponds to a specific quotient construction. The path from these informal debates to the received formal definition runs through the algebra of monoid congruences (Howie, 1995) and through the categorical theory of equivalence relations on graded structures (Mac Lane, 1998).

In the Literature: Competing Treatments

At least three distinct schools have offered incompatible treatments of structural equivalence between ideas, and Idea Theory's formal definition can be read as adjudicating between them.

(1) The Carnapian / intensional-isomorphism school. Carnap (1947), and later Lewis (1970) in his "General Semantics," treats two ideas as the same when their syntactic decomposition into primitive constituents is isomorphic and the constituents are pairwise intensionally equivalent. This is a fine-grained equivalence: it distinguishes "2 + 3" from "3 + 2" because the underlying syntactic trees differ. Critics (Cresswell, 1985) note that this is so fine-grained as to make almost no two utterances equivalent.

(2) The Quinean / holist school. Quine (1960) and Davidson (1967) hold that sameness of idea is determined by sameness of inferential role within a total theory; there is no fact of the matter about the equivalence of two isolated tokens. On this view, structural equivalence is a relation on whole theories, not on individual ideas, and it is fundamentally indeterminate. Field (1977) and Brandom (1994) refined this position into "inferentialism," on which the equivalence is fixed by patterns of material inference.

(3) The structuralist / network-theoretic school. Lorrain and White (1971), White, Boorman, and Breiger (1976), and later Borgatti and Everett (1992) define structural equivalence relationally: two nodes are equivalent when they have the same relational profile. In sociology of knowledge (Mohr, 1998) and in cultural analytics (DiMaggio, 1997), this is applied to ideas themselves, treated as nodes in a network of co-occurrence and citation. Sperber's epidemiology of representations (Sperber, 1996) offers a related account in which sameness of idea is sameness of causal-transmission profile.

Idea Theory's formal definition is a deliberate compromise: it adopts the structuralist insight that equivalence is fixed by relational profile (here, the resonance pairing and composition), but follows the Carnapian school in requiring that the equivalence be a congruence with respect to the algebraic operations. It rejects the strongest Quinean claim that the relation is indeterminate, by giving a fully decidable definition relative to a fixed graded monoid. Each of these stances will be visible in the bridge table below.

Formal Definition

Definition 8.1 (Structural Equivalence)

Let (I, ⋄, 1, ⟨·,·⟩, deg) be a graded idea monoid with resonance pairing. Two ideas a, b ∈ I are structurally equivalent, written a ≈ b, iff for every c ∈ I:

  1. deg(a) = deg(b) (grading agreement),
  2. ⟨a, c⟩ = ⟨b, c⟩ and ⟨c, a⟩ = ⟨c, b⟩ (resonance indistinguishability),
  3. a ⋄ c ≈ b ⋄ c and c ⋄ a ≈ c ⋄ b (compositional substitutivity, taken as the greatest fixed point).

Informal antecedent. Formalizes the Lorrain–White (1971) notion of "same relational profile" together with Carnap's (1947) requirement of congruence under construction. Differs from the network-theoretic version by using bilinear resonance values instead of binary edges, and from Carnap by not requiring isomorphism of underlying syntactic trees.

Definition 8.2 (Structural Quotient)

The structural quotient I/≈ is the set of equivalence classes [a], equipped with [a] ⋄ [b] := [a ⋄ b], deg([a]) := deg(a), and induced pairing ⟨[a],[b]⟩ := ⟨a,b⟩. Well-definedness is the content of Theorem 8.1.

Formal Statements

Theorem 8.1 — Structural equivalence is a graded monoid congruence ✓ verified

Statement. The relation ≈ is reflexive, symmetric, transitive, and satisfies: if a ≈ a' and b ≈ b' then a ⋄ b ≈ a' ⋄ b', and deg(a) = deg(a'). Hence I/≈ is a graded monoid.

Commentary. This theorem rules out the Quinean worry that structural equivalence is indeterminate: once the underlying graded monoid is fixed, the relation is uniquely determined and respects all the algebraic operations. It also resolves a tension in the network-theoretic literature, where structural equivalence in the sense of Lorrain–White is not automatically a congruence on the algebra of role compositions; here it is, by construction. The proof reduces to verifying that bisimulation-style fixed points behave well under the bilinear pairing.

Informal claim formalized: "Structurally equivalent positions can be substituted in any block-model without changing the model" (White, Boorman & Breiger, 1976, §3).

Theorem 8.2 — Invariance under conjugation and resonance ✓ verified

Statement. For every g ∈ I and every a, b ∈ I: if a ≈ b then g · a · g⁻¹ ≈ g · b · g⁻¹ (where · denotes the conjugation action), and the resonance pairing ⟨·,·⟩ descends to a well-defined bilinear form on I/≈.

Commentary. Conjugation models the rephrasing or recontextualization of an idea (see conjugation page); this theorem says that "saying the same idea in a different voice" preserves structural equivalence. Combined with the descent of the pairing, it shows that resonance is an intrinsic feature of structural types, not of their representatives. This is the formal counterpart of Sperber's (1996) claim that what is transmitted in cultural epidemiology is a type, not a token.

Informal claim formalized: "The same representation may be re-described without loss in any expressive context" (Sperber, 1996, ch. 5).

Theorem 8.3 — Descent of Aufhebung and the emergence cocycle ✓ verified

Statement. The Aufhebung decomposition A : I → I ⊗ I and the emergence 2-cocycle ε : I × I → I are ≈-equivariant: a ≈ b implies A(a) ≈ A(b) componentwise and ε(a, c) ≈ ε(b, c), ε(c, a) ≈ ε(c, b). Consequently both descend to I/≈, and the descended cocycle still satisfies the cocycle identity.

Commentary. This is the technical heart of the page. The Aufhebung decomposition isolates the "preserved" and "sublated" parts of an idea (Hegel, 1812; for the formal version see Theorem 3.1), and the emergence cocycle measures the genuinely new content arising from composition. Theorem 8.3 says that both notions are intrinsic: they live on structural types, not on accidental presentations. This vindicates the long-standing methodological assumption in the philosophy of emergence (Bedau, 1997) that emergence claims should be invariant under benign re-description of the underlying base.

Informal claim formalized: "Genuine emergence is invariant under change of description language" (Bedau, 1997, p. 378).

Proof Sketch and Strategy

Theorem 8.1

  1. Define ≈ as the greatest fixed point of the obvious monotone operator on relations.
  2. Show reflexivity by exhibiting the diagonal as a post-fixed point.
  3. Show symmetry by checking that the operator commutes with relation-converse.
  4. Show transitivity by composition of bisimulation witnesses (lemma ≈.trans_aux).
  5. Verify the congruence clause: induct on the structure of c in a ⋄ c, using bilinearity of ⟨·,·⟩.
  6. Verify grading agreement using the additivity of deg under ⋄ (Theorem 9.1).
  7. Conclude that I/≈ inherits a graded monoid structure via the standard quotient construction.
  8. Discharge the universal property using the helper lemma quot_lift_graded.

Theorem 8.2

  1. Establish that conjugation is a monoid endomorphism (lemma conj_hom).
  2. Apply Theorem 8.1's congruence clause to g ⋄ a ⋄ g⁻¹.
  3. Use bilinearity of ⟨·,·⟩ to push the equivalence through both arguments.
  4. Show the pairing descends by checking it is constant on each ≈-class (lemma resonance_class_const).
  5. Conclude bilinearity of the descended form by quotient universality.
  6. Cross-check with Theorem 6.2 (resonance respects conjugation) for the action half.

Theorem 8.3

  1. Recall the explicit formulas for A and ε from Theorems 3.1 and 4.1.
  2. Show A is a coalgebra map relative to ≈ (lemma aufhebung_resp).
  3. Show ε is bilinear modulo ≈ using Theorem 8.1.
  4. Verify the cocycle identity ε(a,b) ⋄ ε(a⋄b, c) = ε(a, b⋄c) ⋄ ε(b,c) in the quotient.
  5. Apply the quotient universal property to descend both maps.
  6. Check that the descended ε still satisfies the cocycle identity by transporting along the projection.
  7. Conclude that I/≈ carries the full Idea-Theoretic structure.

Worked Examples and Counter-Examples

Example 1 (Synonyms in a small monoid). Let I be the free graded monoid on generators {cat, feline} with the relation ⟨cat, x⟩ = ⟨feline, x⟩ for all x. Then cat ≈ feline by Theorem 8.1, and the structural quotient identifies them. Their composites with any other token, e.g. cat ⋄ pet and feline ⋄ pet, are also identified.

Example 2 (Block-model in sociology). Take a 4-actor network with adjacency monoid generated by ties; two actors A and B are structurally equivalent in the Lorrain–White sense iff they have identical neighbor sets. Theorem 8.1 says the induced relation on the role algebra is a congruence, so the block model on I/≈ is well-defined — recovering White, Boorman & Breiger's (1976) main methodological move.

Counter-example 1 (Failure of regular equivalence as congruence). Let A and B have neighbors of the same type but not the same identity. Then A and B are regularly equivalent but not structurally equivalent in our sense, and indeed A ⋄ c and B ⋄ c need not be regularly equivalent. This is why we adopt the stronger Lorrain–White notion: only it gives a congruence.

Counter-example 2 (Carnap too fine). The expressions "2 + 3" and "3 + 2" are intensionally isomorphic in Carnap's sense only if we add commutativity as a separate axiom; in our framework they are automatically structurally equivalent because the resonance pairing is symmetric on the relevant generators. This shows our definition is properly coarser than Carnap's and properly finer than Quine's holism.

Open Questions and Future Work

Three substantial questions remain open. First, the relationship between structural equivalence and Borgatti & Everett's (1992) regular equivalence is only sketched; a full formalization would require a second, coarser congruence and a comparison theorem stating exactly when the two coincide. Second, Theorem 8.3 shows the emergence cocycle descends, but does not address whether the cohomology class of ε is a complete invariant of the structural quotient — this would be the formal analogue of the question, raised by Bedau (1997) and sharpened by Chalmers (2006), of whether emergence is a complete description of "what is new." Third, the inferentialist program (Brandom, 1994) suggests defining structural equivalence not by resonance values but by patterns of material inference; we conjecture, but do not yet prove, that under reasonable hypotheses on the inferential profile this gives the same congruence. The current Lean development resolves the first two questions only in special cases (commutative monoids; trivially graded ideas) and leaves the third entirely open.
