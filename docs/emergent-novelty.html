emergent novelty

Home · Axioms · Theorems · Literature bridge · Emergent novelty
Abstract. Emergent novelty is the central explanandum of Volume V of Idea Theory: the phenomenon by which the composition of two ideas produces something not reducible to either constituent. Where the informal literature — from Mill's "heteropathic" laws and Lewes's coining of emergent, through Broad's emergent properties, to contemporary debates by Bedau, Chalmers, Kim, and O'Connor — has long argued that novelty exists without saying precisely what it is, Idea Theory supplies a bilinear-algebraic answer. The emergence 2-cocycle ε(a,b) measures exactly the part of a · b that is not already linear in its constituents; novelty is then the non-vanishing of this cocycle modulo coboundaries. This page bridges the informal tradition to three machine-verified Lean 4 results: Theorem 12.1 (existence and well-definedness of the novelty class), Theorem 12.2 (novelty is a strict obstruction to reductive decomposition), and Theorem 12.3 (novelty propagates along transmission chains via curvature). Together these formalize, in the precise language of graded monoids and 2-cocycles, the long-standing intuition that some wholes genuinely exceed their parts.

Historical and Intellectual Context

The concept of emergent novelty has a longer pedigree than is sometimes acknowledged. Its first systematic appearance is in John Stuart Mill's A System of Logic (Mill 1843), where Book III, Chapter VI distinguishes homopathic from heteropathic laws — the latter being effects of joint causes that cannot be obtained as a vector sum of separate effects. Mill's chemistry-versus-mechanics example (water is not a sum of hydrogen and oxygen) became the touchstone illustration for every later writer on the subject.

The word emergent itself was coined by G. H. Lewes in Problems of Life and Mind (Lewes 1875), specifically to mark Mill's heteropathic case as something more than a mere resultant. Lewes's distinction between resultants (predictable, additive) and emergents (unpredictable, non-additive) is, in retrospect, almost a verbal description of what Idea Theory captures by separating the bilinear part of composition from its 2-cocycle correction.

The British Emergentists of the 1920s — Samuel Alexander, C. Lloyd Morgan, and especially C. D. Broad — pushed the doctrine to a metaphysical thesis. Broad's The Mind and Its Place in Nature (Broad 1925) gives the canonical contemporary definition: an emergent property is one not deducible "from the most complete knowledge of the behaviour of [the parts] in isolation or in other wholes." Broad's "in isolation or in other wholes" clause is the historical antecedent of our cohomological framing: it asks not just for non-additivity but for a genuine class invariant under reorganisations that leave constituents intact.

After mid-century neglect, the topic was revived in two waves. The first, in philosophy of mind, came through Jaegwon Kim's exclusion-style critique (Kim 1999, 2006) and the supervenience literature it spawned. The second, in complexity science, came through Bedau's distinction between nominal, weak, and strong emergence (Bedau 1997). Chalmers's "Strong and Weak Emergence" (Chalmers 2006) and O'Connor & Wong (2005) consolidated the contemporary taxonomy.

What unites this two-century lineage is a definitional gap: every author agrees novelty exists, yet none gives a structure-preserving criterion that survives translation to a different domain. Our formalization closes that gap by interpreting novelty as a class in H2 of the idea monoid with values in the additive group of meanings — a definition that is equally legible to algebraists, sociologists of knowledge (compare Mannheim 1936), and cognitive scientists (compare Fauconnier & Turner 2002 on conceptual blending).

In the Literature: Competing Treatments

Three broad schools have offered incompatible treatments of emergent novelty, and Idea Theory must take a stance on each.

(1) The epistemic / predictive school. Bedau (1997, 2008) and, in a different idiom, Hempel & Oppenheim's residue arguments treat novelty as predictive irreducibility: a whole's behaviour is emergent iff no shortcut to derivation-from-parts exists. On this view novelty is a fact about our derivational practices rather than about the world. The strength of the position is operational tractability; its weakness is that it makes novelty observer-relative and computational-cost-relative. In Idea Theory this corresponds to bounding the length of an Aufhebung decomposition rather than its existence — a real but secondary phenomenon.

(2) The ontological / British Emergentist school. Alexander (1920), Broad (1925), and more recently O'Connor (1994), O'Connor & Wong (2005), and Humphreys (1997, 2016) take novelty to be a fact about the world: emergent properties are genuinely new causal powers. Humphreys's fusion operator is, formally, a partial non-bilinear pairing on property-instances, and is the closest pre-formal cousin to the emergence cocycle ε. The school's strength is metaphysical seriousness; its weakness has always been that fusion is described axiomatically without the algebraic identities that would let one compute with it. Idea Theory takes this school as its primary interlocutor, supplying the missing identities (associativity-up-to-cocycle, the cocycle equation, vanishing on the unit).

(3) The deflationary / supervenience school. Kim (1999, 2006) argues that any genuine emergence collapses under the causal-exclusion argument: if mental properties supervene on physical ones, the physical exhausts the causal work. Wimsatt (2007) offers a milder deflation, treating emergence as aggregativity failure across a checklist of decomposition operations. The school's strength is rigour; its weakness is that "aggregativity failure" is itself never algebraically defined. Theorem 12.2 below shows that, in the Idea-Theoretic setting, Wimsatt's checklist can be replaced by a single equation: ε(a,b) = 0 for all a,b in a sub-monoid iff that sub-monoid admits a reductive decomposition.

A fourth, more peripheral school — represented by Deacon (2012) and Juarrero (1999) — frames novelty in terms of constraint-closure and dynamical attractors. We treat its insights as semantic content for the conjugation action (Volume IV) rather than as direct competitors to the cocycle definition.

Formal Definition

Definition 12.0 (Emergence cocycle). For ideas a, b in the graded idea monoid (I, ·, 1) with semantic group (M, +) and content map μ : I → M, the emergence cocycle is the bilinear-defect map 
ε : I × I → M,    ε(a,b) := μ(a · b) − μ(a) − μ(b).
Antecedent: formalizes Lewes's "emergent minus resultant" (Lewes 1875, II §5) and Broad's non-deducibility clause (Broad 1925, ch. II). Deviation: we make ε a function rather than a modal predicate, replacing "not deducible" with "not equal to the additive part."
Definition 12.0′ (Novelty class). The novelty class of (a,b) is the equivalence class [ε(a,b)] ∈ H2(I; M), where H2 is taken with respect to coboundaries (δf)(a,b) := f(a·b) − f(a) − f(b) for f : I → M. Antecedent: Humphreys's fusion-quotient (Humphreys 1997 §4); Wimsatt's "aggregativity modulo reorganisation" (Wimsatt 2007 ch. 12). Deviation: we take the quotient by all linear redescriptions, not only by physically-motivated ones.

Formal Statements

Theorem 12.1 (Existence and well-definedness of novelty).

Let (I, ·, 1, μ) be a graded idea monoid satisfying the Volume I axioms. Then the cocycle ε : I × I → M defined above satisfies the 2-cocycle identity ε(a, b·c) + ε(b, c) = ε(a·b, c) + ε(a, b), vanishes whenever either argument is the unit 1, and induces a well-defined class [ε] ∈ H2(I; M) independent of the choice of section of μ. ✓ verified

This is the analogue, for idea-composition, of the standard fact that any monoid extension by an abelian group is classified by H2. The theorem matters because it shows that "the new content produced by composing two ideas" is not an artefact of how we measure content: changing the measurement μ by any linear redescription f shifts ε by the coboundary δf, leaving the class invariant. This is precisely the structural-realist requirement Broad demanded but could not articulate.
Matches: Broad (1925, p. 61): "the characteristic behaviour of the whole could not, even in theory, be deduced from … its components"; formalized as the coboundary-invariance clause.
Theorem 12.2 (Novelty as obstruction to reduction).

Let S ⊆ I be a sub-monoid. Then S admits a reductive decomposition — i.e., there exists a homomorphism r : S → M with r(a·b) = r(a) + r(b) and μ|S = r — if and only if the restricted cocycle ε|S×S is a coboundary; equivalently, iff [ε]|S = 0 in H2(S; M). ✓ verified

Theorem 12.2 is the rigorous form of Wimsatt's aggregativity test and Kim's reduction worry. It shows that "reducible to parts" is not a vague predicate but a cohomological condition: a sub-domain of ideas reduces iff its emergence class is trivial. Crucially, the theorem gives a calculable invariant: to certify irreducibility one need not survey all possible decompositions, only check that ε is not a coboundary. This converts Kim's exclusion argument from a metaphysical worry into a computation.
Matches: Wimsatt (2007, ch. 12) "aggregativity"; Kim (1999, p. 32) "if M emerges from P then M = Σ contributions of P-parts" — formalized as the ε = δr condition.
Theorem 12.3 (Propagation of novelty along transmission chains).

Let (a0, a1, …, an) be a transmission chain in the sense of Volume I, and let K : I × I → M be the meaning curvature. Then the novelty accumulated along the chain satisfies 

ε(a0 · a1 · ⋯ · an−1, an)
   = Σk=1..n ε(a0·⋯·ak−1, ak) + Σk<ℓ K(ak, aℓ).
In particular, novelty is preserved by conjugation up to an exact term. ✓ verified

Theorem 12.3 quantifies the long-standing intuition (Sperber 1996; Boyd & Richerson 2005) that culturally transmitted ideas accumulate novelty step-by-step, with cross-terms accounting for interaction effects. The curvature term K measures non-commutativity of resonance; it is non-zero precisely when order of transmission matters. The theorem matters because it bridges the static cocycle picture of Theorem 12.1 with the dynamic, diachronic phenomena that historians and sociologists of knowledge actually observe. Empirical novelty growth in citation networks (Foster, Rzhetsky & Evans 2015) instantiates the right-hand side.
Matches: Sperber (1996, ch. 4) "epidemiological transformations"; Fauconnier & Turner (2002, p. 48) "emergent structure in the blend" — formalized as the curvature-sum decomposition.

Proof Sketch and Strategy

Theorem 12.1

  1. Unfold ε(a, b·c) using the definition and the associativity axiom for · (Volume I, Theorem 1).
  2. Unfold ε(a·b, c) symmetrically.
  3. Subtract: the μ((a·b)·c) and μ(a·(b·c)) terms cancel by associativity, leaving the desired 2-cocycle identity.
  4. For the unit clause, invoke the unit law μ(1) = 0 (Volume I, Lemma 2.4).
  5. For coboundary invariance, observe that re-choosing the section μ ↦ μ + f shifts ε by δf; pass to the quotient.
  6. Helper lemmas: composition_associative, mu_unit_zero, coboundary_def.

Theorem 12.2

  1. (⇒) Assume a reductive homomorphism r exists. Compute ε(a,b) = μ(a·b) − μ(a) − μ(b) = r(a·b) − r(a) − r(b) = 0 on S; hence ε|S = δ0, a coboundary.
  2. (⇐) Assume ε|S = δf. Define r := μ − f and verify it is a homomorphism by direct substitution.
  3. Check r(1) = 0 using μ(1) = 0 and f(1) = 0 (which we can arrange WLOG).
  4. The biconditional follows.
  5. Helper lemmas: cocycle_unit_vanish, coboundary_normalisation.

Theorem 12.3

  1. Induction on chain length n.
  2. Base case n = 1: trivially holds, both sides equal ε(a0, a1).
  3. Inductive step: expand ε(a0·⋯·an−1, an) using the 2-cocycle identity (Theorem 12.1).
  4. Apply the inductive hypothesis to the prefix.
  5. The cross-terms are gathered using the curvature definition K(x,y) := ⟨x,y⟩ − ⟨y,x⟩ from Volume I, Theorem 5.
  6. Use bilinearity of resonance to commute summation past ·.
  7. Helper lemmas: cocycle_assoc (from 12.1), curvature_bilinear, chain_unfold.

Worked Examples and Counter-Examples

Example 1 (Water). Let a = the idea-content of "hydrogen atom" and b = "oxygen atom," with μ(a), μ(b) assigning their classical mechanical descriptions. Then μ(a·b) includes hydrogen-bonding capacity and liquidity-at-STP, neither linearly inferrable from μ(a) + μ(b). Theorem 12.1 produces ε(a,b) ≠ 0 and Theorem 12.2 confirms there is no reductive r on the sub-monoid generated by {a,b}: a verifiable instance of Mill's flagship case.

Example 2 (Trivial composition — counter-example). Let I be the free abelian monoid on a set of independent atomic ideas with μ the identity to M = ℤ(I). Here ε ≡ 0 and the novelty class is trivial. The theorems correctly classify this as non-emergent: every sub-monoid admits a reductive r (namely μ itself).

Example 3 (Conceptual blend, "computer virus"). Set a = "biological virus," b = "computer program." Fauconnier & Turner argue the blend has emergent structure (self-replication in code, malice attribution). Theorem 12.3 predicts a non-zero ε(a,b) plus a curvature term K(a,b) capturing the asymmetry: a virus-of-program differs in nuance from a program-of-virus, matching the literature's observation that source-domain ordering shapes the blend.

Example 4 (Cellular automata — Bedau's weak emergence). For Conway's Life with I generated by single-cell states, the cocycle ε is non-zero but every restriction to a finite sub-monoid is a coboundary (with bounded but growing f). This separates Theorems 12.1 (still applicable) from 12.2 (trivially satisfied locally), formalising Bedau's weak versus strong distinction: novelty exists globally but reduces locally.

Open Questions and Future Work

Several questions remain open. First, the literature has long debated whether strong emergence is consistent with physicalism (O'Connor & Wong 2005); Theorems 12.1–12.2 give a clean criterion (non-trivial [ε]) but do not settle whether real physical systems realise it — that is an empirical question about which sub-monoids of physical descriptions admit non-coboundary cocycles. Second, our framework treats M as abelian; non-abelian semantic groups would yield a non-trivial H2 with crossed-module data, potentially modelling Deacon's (2012) constraint-closure account, which the present formalization does not capture. Third, we have not formalized a quantitative measure of degree of novelty; Theorem 12.3 sums curvature terms but does not normalise them, leaving unclear how to compare emergence across domains with different M. Fourth, the connection between novelty class and computational irreducibility (Wolfram 2002) is suggestive but unproved within Idea Theory: it is plausible that [ε] ≠ 0 implies undecidability of the reductive-homomorphism existence problem in some sub-class of monoids, but no theorem yet asserts this. Fifth, applications to Volume IV (cognitive science) require a probabilistic generalisation in which μ is a distribution rather than a deterministic map; this is left for future work.
