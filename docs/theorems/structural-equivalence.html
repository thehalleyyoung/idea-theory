1. Headline Theorems

From Theorems8.lean

Throughout, \(\llbracket w \rrbracket\) denotes the evaluation of word \(w\) in the idea algebra \((\mathcal{I}, \circ, e)\), and \(w_1 \simeq w_2\) denotes structural equivalence.

Theorem 8.1 (Structural Equivalence Theorem). Let \(w_1, w_2 \in \mathrm{Word}\,\mathcal{I}\). The following hold:
  1. Characterisation. \(\displaystyle w_1 \simeq w_2 \;\iff\; \llbracket w_1 \rrbracket = \llbracket w_2 \rrbracket.\)
  2. Cons-congruence. If \(w_1 \simeq w_2\) then \(a :: w_1 \simeq a :: w_2\) for any \(a \in \mathcal{I}\).
  3. Append-congruence. If \(w_1 \simeq w_1'\) and \(w_2 \simeq w_2'\) then \(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \simeq w_1' \mathbin{+\!+} w_2'\).
Theorem 8.2 (Canonical Form / Normal Form).
  1. Every word \(w\) is structurally equivalent to its canonical singleton: \(w \simeq [\llbracket w \rrbracket]\).
  2. Two words are structurally equivalent iff their canonical forms are equal as lists: \(w_1 \simeq w_2 \iff \mathrm{canonical}(w_1) = \mathrm{canonical}(w_2)\).
  3. The canonical form always has length exactly 1: \(|\mathrm{canonical}(w)| = 1\).
Theorem 8.3 (Universal Property of Structural Equivalence).
  1. \(\simeq\) is the coarsest equivalence relation on \(\mathrm{Word}\,\mathcal{I}\) contained in the evaluation kernel.
  2. Any relation \(R\) satisfying \(R\,w_1\,w_2 \Rightarrow \llbracket w_1 \rrbracket = \llbracket w_2 \rrbracket\) is contained in \(\simeq\).
  3. Any relation that exactly characterises the evaluation kernel is equal to \(\simeq\).

From Theorems9.lean

An IdeaPair \(p = (a, b)\) carries \(p.\mathrm{compose} = a \circ b\). An IdeaTriple \(t = (a,b,c)\) carries \(t.\mathrm{composeL} = (a \circ b) \circ c\) and \(t.\mathrm{composeR} = a \circ (b \circ c)\). The padding maps are \(\mathrm{padR}(a,b) = (a,b,e)\), \(\mathrm{padL}(a,b) = (e,a,b)\), \(\mathrm{padM}(a,b) = (a,e,b)\).

Theorem 9.1 (Padding Theorem for Pairs). For any IdeaPair \(p = (a,b)\), all three padding operations yield the same composition: \[\mathrm{padR}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; \mathrm{padL}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; \mathrm{padM}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; a \circ b.\]
Theorem 9.2 (Canonical Form for Triples). For any IdeaTriple \(t = (a,b,c)\), left-leaning and right-leaning parenthesisations agree: \[t.\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; (a \circ b) \circ c \;=\; a \circ (b \circ c) \;=\; t.\mathrm{composeR}.\]
Theorem 9.3 (IdeaEquiv is a Congruence). IdeaEquiv satisfies:
  • Reflexivity. \(a \equiv a\) for all \(a\).
  • Symmetry. \(a \equiv b \Rightarrow b \equiv a\).
  • Transitivity. \(a \equiv b\) and \(b \equiv c\) implies \(a \equiv c\).
  • Left congruence. \(a \equiv b \Rightarrow c \circ a \equiv c \circ b\).
  • Right congruence. \(a \equiv b \Rightarrow a \circ c \equiv b \circ c\).
Moreover, \(\mathrm{IdeaEquiv}(a,b) \iff a = b\).

2. Word Evaluation & Structural Equivalence — Theorems8.lean

§8.A Word Evaluation

Lemma 8.A.1 (evalWord_singleton). For any \(a \in \mathcal{I}\), \[\llbracket [a] \rrbracket = a.\]
Lemma 8.A.2 (evalWord_pair). \[\llbracket [a,\, b] \rrbracket = a \circ b.\]
Lemma 8.A.3 (evalWord_triple). \[\llbracket [a,\, b,\, c] \rrbracket = a \circ (b \circ c).\]
Lemma 8.A.4 (evalWord_append). For words \(w_1, w_2\), \[\llbracket w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \rrbracket \;=\; \llbracket w_1 \rrbracket \circ \llbracket w_2 \rrbracket.\]
Lemma 8.A.5 (evalWord_snoc). \[\llbracket w \mathbin{+\!+} [a] \rrbracket \;=\; \llbracket w \rrbracket \circ a.\]
Lemma 8.A.6 (evalWord_append_three). \[\llbracket w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \mathbin{+\!+} w_3 \rrbracket \;=\; \llbracket w_1 \rrbracket \circ \llbracket w_2 \rrbracket \circ \llbracket w_3 \rrbracket.\]
Lemma 8.A.7 (evalWord_replicate_ident). For any \(n \geq 0\), the word consisting of \(n\) copies of \(e\) evaluates to \(e\): \[\llbracket \underbrace{[e, \ldots, e]}_{n} \rrbracket = e.\]

§8.B Structural Equivalence Properties

Lemma 8.B.1 (equivalence). \(\simeq\) is an equivalence relation on \(\mathrm{Word}\,\mathcal{I}\): it is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
Lemma 8.B.2 (eq_nil_iff_eval_ident). For any word \(w\), \[w \simeq [] \;\iff\; \llbracket w \rrbracket = e.\]
Lemma 8.B.3 (singleton_singleton_iff). \[[a] \simeq [b] \;\iff\; a = b.\]
Lemma 8.B.4 (singleton_iff_eval). \[[a] \simeq w \;\iff\; a = \llbracket w \rrbracket.\]

§8.C Congruence Operations

Lemma 8.C.1 (cons_congr). If \(w_1 \simeq w_2\) then \(a :: w_1 \simeq a :: w_2\) for any \(a\).
Lemma 8.C.2 (append_congr). If \(w_1 \simeq w_1'\) and \(w_2 \simeq w_2'\) then \[w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \;\simeq\; w_1' \mathbin{+\!+} w_2'.\]
Lemma 8.C.3 (append_left_congr). If \(w_1 \simeq w_1'\) then \(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \simeq w_1' \mathbin{+\!+} w_2\) for any fixed \(w_2\).
Lemma 8.C.4 (append_right_congr). If \(w_2 \simeq w_2'\) then \(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \simeq w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2'\) for any fixed \(w_1\).
Lemma 8.C.5 (snoc_congr). If \(w_1 \simeq w_2\) then \(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} [a] \simeq w_2 \mathbin{+\!+} [a]\) for any \(a\).
Lemma 8.C.6 (replicate_congr_letter). If \(a = b\) then \(\mathrm{replicate}(n, a) \simeq \mathrm{replicate}(n, b)\) for any \(n \geq 0\).

§8.D Associativity up to Structural Equivalence

Lemma 8.D.1 (append_assoc). \[(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2) \mathbin{+\!+} w_3 \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} (w_2 \mathbin{+\!+} w_3).\]
Lemma 8.D.2 (nil_append). \[[] \mathbin{+\!+} w \;\simeq\; w.\]
Lemma 8.D.3 (append_nil). \[w \mathbin{+\!+} [] \;\simeq\; w.\]

§8.E Identity Insertion Lemmas

Lemma 8.E.1 (cons_ident). \[e :: w \;\simeq\; w.\]
Lemma 8.E.2 (snoc_ident). \[w \mathbin{+\!+} [e] \;\simeq\; w.\]
Lemma 8.E.3 (insert_ident). \[w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} [e] \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2.\]
Lemma 8.E.4 (replicate_ident_nil). For any \(n \geq 0\), \(\underbrace{[e, \ldots, e]}_{n} \simeq []\).
Lemma 8.E.5 (sandwich_replicate_ident). For any \(n \geq 0\), \[w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} \underbrace{[e,\ldots,e]}_{n} \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2.\]
Lemma 8.E.6 (double_ident_collapse). \[[e] \mathbin{+\!+} w \mathbin{+\!+} [e] \;\simeq\; w.\]
Lemma 8.E.7 (snoc_add_ident). Snoc-form of identity absorption: \(w \mathbin{+\!+} [e] \simeq w\), established via the snoc characterisation of evaluation.
Lemma 8.E.8 (cons_add_ident). Cons-form of identity absorption: \(e :: w \simeq w\), established via the cons characterisation of evaluation.

§8.F Reassociation

Lemma 8.F.1 (reassoc_three). Three-word reassociation is a \(\simeq\)-equivalence: \[(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2) \mathbin{+\!+} w_3 \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} (w_2 \mathbin{+\!+} w_3).\]
Lemma 8.F.2 (reassoc_four_mid). Four-word middle regrouping: \[(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2) \mathbin{+\!+} (w_3 \mathbin{+\!+} w_4) \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} (w_2 \mathbin{+\!+} w_3) \mathbin{+\!+} w_4.\]
Lemma 8.F.3 (reassoc_four_left). Four-word left regrouping: \[((w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2) \mathbin{+\!+} w_3) \mathbin{+\!+} w_4 \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \mathbin{+\!+} w_3 \mathbin{+\!+} w_4.\]
Lemma 8.F.4 (pair_cons_unfold). \[[a] \mathbin{+\!+} (b :: w) \;\simeq\; a :: b :: w.\]
Lemma 8.F.5 (cons_eq_append). \[b :: w \;\simeq\; [b] \mathbin{+\!+} w.\]

§8.G Canonical Forms

Lemma 8.G.1 (canonical_size). The canonical form of any word has length exactly one: \[|\mathrm{canonical}(w)| = 1.\]
Lemma 8.G.2 (canonical_append). The canonical form of an appended word is the singleton of the composed evaluations: \[\mathrm{canonical}(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2) = [\llbracket w_1 \rrbracket \circ \llbracket w_2 \rrbracket].\]
Lemma 8.G.3 (to_canonical). Every word is structurally equivalent to its canonical form: \[w \;\simeq\; \mathrm{canonical}(w).\]
Lemma 8.G.4 (equiv_iff_canonical). \[w_1 \simeq w_2 \;\iff\; \mathrm{canonical}(w_1) = \mathrm{canonical}(w_2).\]
Lemma 8.G.5 (canonical_idem). The canonical map is idempotent: \[\mathrm{canonical}(\mathrm{canonical}(w)) = \mathrm{canonical}(w).\]

§8.H Homomorphisms and Corollaries

Lemma 8.H.1 (evalWord_mapWord). If \(f : \mathcal{I} \to \mathcal{J}\) is an algebra homomorphism, evaluation commutes with the word-image map: \[\llbracket \mathrm{mapWord}(f,\, w) \rrbracket \;=\; f\!\left(\llbracket w \rrbracket\right).\]
Lemma 8.H.2 (mapWord_congr). Homomorphisms preserve structural equivalence: if \(w_1 \simeq w_2\) then \[\mathrm{mapWord}(f,\, w_1) \;\simeq\; \mathrm{mapWord}(f,\, w_2).\]
Corollary 8.1 (corollary_8_1). Every algebra homomorphism \(f\) descends to a well-defined map on \(\simeq\)-classes: the assignment \([w]_{\simeq} \mapsto \mathrm{mapWord}(f, w)\) is independent of the chosen representative.
Corollary 8.2 (corollary_8_2 — DecidableRel). When equality of elements of \(\mathcal{I}\) is decidable, structural equivalence is a decidable relation on \(\mathrm{Word}\,\mathcal{I}\).
Corollary 8.3 (corollary_8_3). The canonical form is idempotent on \(\simeq\)-classes: \(\mathrm{canonical}(w) \simeq w\).
Corollary 8.4 (corollary_8_4). If \(\llbracket w_1 \rrbracket = e\) and \(\llbracket w_2 \rrbracket = e\) then \(w_1 \simeq [] \simeq w_2\): both words are equivalent to the empty word.
Corollary 8.5 (corollary_8_5). Append is associative up to \(\simeq\): \[(w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2) \mathbin{+\!+} w_3 \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} (w_2 \mathbin{+\!+} w_3).\]
Corollary 8.6 (corollary_8_6). Any \(n\)-fold replicate of \(e\) is invisible under insertion: \[w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} \underbrace{[e,\ldots,e]}_{n} \mathbin{+\!+} w_2 \;\simeq\; w_1 \mathbin{+\!+} w_2.\]

3. IdeaPair, IdeaTriple & Grading — Theorems9.lean

§9.A Trivial Forms and Basic Compositions

Lemma 9.A.1 (IdeaPair.compose_trivial). The trivial pair \((e, e)\) composes to \(e\): \[e \circ e = e.\]
Lemma 9.A.2 (IdeaTriple.composeL_trivial). \[\mathrm{composeL}(e,\, e,\, e) = e.\]
Lemma 9.A.3 (IdeaTriple.composeR_trivial). \[\mathrm{composeR}(e,\, e,\, e) = e.\]
Lemma 9.A.4 (IdeaPair.compose_right_ident). For any \(a \in \mathcal{I}\), \(a \circ e = a\).
Lemma 9.A.5 (IdeaPair.compose_left_ident). For any \(a \in \mathcal{I}\), \(e \circ a = a\).

§9.B Triple Canonical Forms

Lemma 9.B.1 (IdeaTriple.composeL_eq_composeR). For any triple \(t = (a, b, c)\), \[t.\mathrm{composeL} = t.\mathrm{composeR}.\]
Lemma 9.B.2 (IdeaTriple.composeR_eq_composeL). Symmetric: \(t.\mathrm{composeR} = t.\mathrm{composeL}\).
Lemma 9.B.3 (IdeaPair.compose_padR). Padding a pair on the right with \(e\) and computing left-leaning recovers the original composition: \[\mathrm{padR}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; p.\mathrm{compose}.\] Explicitly: \((a \circ b) \circ e = a \circ b\).
Lemma 9.B.4 (IdeaPair.compose_padL). Padding on the left: \[\mathrm{padL}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; p.\mathrm{compose}.\] Explicitly: \((e \circ a) \circ b = a \circ b\).
Lemma 9.B.5 (IdeaPair.compose_padM). Padding in the middle, right-leaning: \[\mathrm{padM}(p).\mathrm{composeR} \;=\; p.\mathrm{compose}.\] Explicitly: \(a \circ (e \circ b) = a \circ b\).
Lemma 9.B.6 (IdeaPair.compose_padM_L). Padding in the middle, left-leaning: \[\mathrm{padM}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; p.\mathrm{compose}.\] Explicitly: \((a \circ e) \circ b = a \circ b\).

§9.C Trivial-Component Lemmas

Lemma 9.C.1 (IdeaPair.compose_of_right_trivial). If the right component of \(p\) equals \(e\), then \(p.\mathrm{compose}\) equals the left component: \(p.\mathrm{fst} \circ e = p.\mathrm{fst}\).
Lemma 9.C.2 (IdeaPair.compose_of_left_trivial). If the left component of \(p\) equals \(e\), then \(p.\mathrm{compose}\) equals the right component: \(e \circ p.\mathrm{snd} = p.\mathrm{snd}\).
Lemma 9.C.3 (IdeaPair.swap_trivial). The trivial pair is fixed by swap: \(\mathrm{swap}(e,\, e) = (e,\, e)\).
Lemma 9.C.4 (IdeaPair.swap_swap). Swap is an involution: \(\mathrm{swap}(\mathrm{swap}(p)) = p\).

§9.D Sandwich and Chain Lemmas

Lemma 9.D.1 (sandwich_collapse). \[(a \circ e) \circ a \;=\; a \circ a.\]
Lemma 9.D.2 (double_sandwich). Double sandwiching collapses: \[(a \circ e) \circ (e \circ a) \;=\; a \circ a.\]
Lemma 9.D.3 (ident_chain4). \[e \circ e \circ e \circ e \;=\; e.\]
Lemma 9.D.4 (ident_chain5). \[e \circ e \circ e \circ e \circ e \;=\; e.\]
Lemma 9.D.5 (insert_ident_mid_R). \[a \circ (e \circ c) \;=\; a \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.D.6 (insert_ident_mid_L). \[(a \circ e) \circ c \;=\; a \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.D.7 (fold_ident_head_L). \[(e \circ b) \circ c \;=\; b \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.D.8 (fold_ident_tail_R). \[a \circ (b \circ e) \;=\; a \circ b.\]
Lemma 9.D.9 (fold_ident_tail_L). \[(a \circ b) \circ e \;=\; a \circ b.\]
Lemma 9.D.10 (fold_ident_head_R). \[b \circ (e \circ c) \;=\; b \circ c.\]

§9.E Triple Identity Placements

Lemma 9.E.1 (IdeaTriple.composeL_left_ident). Setting the first component to \(e\) in composeL: \[\mathrm{composeL}(e,\, b,\, c) \;=\; b \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.E.2 (IdeaTriple.composeL_right_ident). Setting the third component to \(e\): \[\mathrm{composeL}(a,\, b,\, e) \;=\; a \circ b.\]
Lemma 9.E.3 (IdeaTriple.composeL_mid_ident). Setting the middle component to \(e\): \[\mathrm{composeL}(a,\, e,\, c) \;=\; a \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.E.4 (IdeaTriple.composeR_left_ident). \[\mathrm{composeR}(e,\, b,\, c) \;=\; b \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.E.5 (IdeaTriple.composeR_right_ident). \[\mathrm{composeR}(a,\, b,\, e) \;=\; a \circ b.\]
Lemma 9.E.6 (IdeaTriple.composeR_mid_ident). \[\mathrm{composeR}(a,\, e,\, c) \;=\; a \circ c.\]

§9.F BalancedIdea4 Lemmas

A BalancedIdea4 \(q = (a, b, c, d)\) carries three composition forms: \(q.\mathrm{composeL} = ((a \circ b) \circ c) \circ d\), \(q.\mathrm{composeR} = a \circ (b \circ (c \circ d))\), and \(q.\mathrm{composeB} = (a \circ b) \circ (c \circ d)\).

Lemma 9.F.1 (BalancedIdea4.compose_trivial). The trivial quadruple \((e, e, e, e)\) composes to \(e\).
Lemma 9.F.2 (BalancedIdea4.composeL_trivial). \[\mathrm{composeL}(e,\, e,\, e,\, e) = e.\]
Lemma 9.F.3 (BalancedIdea4.composeR_trivial). \[\mathrm{composeR}(e,\, e,\, e,\, e) = e.\]
Lemma 9.F.4 (BalancedIdea4.composeB_eq_composeL). Balanced and left-leaning parenthesisation agree: \[q.\mathrm{composeB} = q.\mathrm{composeL}.\]
Lemma 9.F.5 (BalancedIdea4.composeB_eq_composeR). \[q.\mathrm{composeB} = q.\mathrm{composeR}.\]
Lemma 9.F.6 (BalancedIdea4.composeL_eq_composeR). Four-tuple associativity — left equals right: \[q.\mathrm{composeL} = q.\mathrm{composeR}.\]
Lemma 9.F.7 (BalancedIdea4.composeR_eq_composeL). Symmetric: \(q.\mathrm{composeR} = q.\mathrm{composeL}\).
Lemma 9.F.8 (BalancedIdea4.composeL_eq_composeB). \[q.\mathrm{composeL} = q.\mathrm{composeB}.\]
Lemma 9.F.9 (BalancedIdea4.composeR_eq_composeB). \[q.\mathrm{composeR} = q.\mathrm{composeB}.\]
Lemma 9.F.10 (BalancedIdea4.composeB_first_ident). If the first entry equals \(e\): \[\mathrm{composeB}(e,\, b,\, c,\, d) = (b \circ c) \circ d.\]
Lemma 9.F.11 (BalancedIdea4.composeB_second_ident). If the second entry equals \(e\): \[\mathrm{composeB}(a,\, e,\, c,\, d) = a \circ (c \circ d).\]
Lemma 9.F.12 (BalancedIdea4.composeB_third_ident). If the third entry equals \(e\): \[\mathrm{composeB}(a,\, b,\, e,\, d) = (a \circ b) \circ d.\]
Lemma 9.F.13 (BalancedIdea4.composeB_fourth_ident). If the fourth entry equals \(e\): \[\mathrm{composeB}(a,\, b,\, c,\, e) = a \circ b \circ c.\]

§9.G IdeaEquiv Properties

Lemma 9.G.1 (IdeaEquiv.refl). \(a \equiv a\) for all \(a \in \mathcal{I}\).
Lemma 9.G.2 (IdeaEquiv.symm). If \(a \equiv b\) then \(b \equiv a\).
Lemma 9.G.3 (IdeaEquiv.trans). If \(a \equiv b\) and \(b \equiv c\) then \(a \equiv c\).
Lemma 9.G.4 (IdeaEquiv.iff_eq). \[\mathrm{IdeaEquiv}(a,\, b) \;\iff\; a = b.\]
Lemma 9.G.5 (IdeaEquiv.op_left). If \(a \equiv b\) then \(c \circ a \equiv c \circ b\) for any \(c\).
Lemma 9.G.6 (IdeaEquiv.op_right). If \(a \equiv b\) then \(a \circ c \equiv b \circ c\) for any \(c\).
Lemma 9.G.7 (IdeaEquiv.op_congr). Joint congruence: if \(a_1 \equiv a_2\) and \(b_1 \equiv b_2\) then \(a_1 \circ b_1 \equiv a_2 \circ b_2\).

§9.H Component-wise Congruence

Lemma 9.H.1 (IdeaPair.compose_congr). If pairs \(p\) and \(q\) agree component-wise (\(p.\mathrm{fst} = q.\mathrm{fst}\) and \(p.\mathrm{snd} = q.\mathrm{snd}\)) then \(p.\mathrm{compose} = q.\mathrm{compose}\).
Lemma 9.H.2 (IdeaTriple.composeL_congr). If triples \(s\) and \(t\) agree component-wise then \(s.\mathrm{composeL} = t.\mathrm{composeL}\).
Lemma 9.H.3 (IdeaTriple.composeR_congr). If triples \(s\) and \(t\) agree component-wise then \(s.\mathrm{composeR} = t.\mathrm{composeR}\).
Lemma 9.H.4 (IdeaPair.compose_eq_of_snd_eq). If \(b_1 = b_2\) then \(a \circ b_1 = a \circ b_2\).
Lemma 9.H.5 (IdeaPair.compose_eq_of_fst_eq). If \(a_1 = a_2\) then \(a_1 \circ b = a_2 \circ b\).
Lemma 9.H.6 (IdeaPair.compose_right_left_ident_eq). Right and left identity coincide: \(a \circ e = a = e \circ a\).
Lemma 9.H.7 (IdeaPair.compose_right_ident'). Alternative proof path for right identity: \(a \circ e = a\).
Lemma 9.H.8 (IdeaPair.compose_of_both_ident). If both components equal \(e\) then the composition equals \(e\): \(e \circ e = e\).
Lemma 9.H.9 (IdeaPair.compose_right_ident_op). \[(a \circ e) \circ c \;=\; a \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.H.10 (IdeaPair.compose_left_ident_op). \[(e \circ a) \circ c \;=\; a \circ c.\]
Lemma 9.H.11 (IdeaTriple.trivial_compose_eq). Both composition orders on the trivial triple agree and equal \(e\): \[\mathrm{composeL}(e,\, e,\, e) \;=\; \mathrm{composeR}(e,\, e,\, e) \;=\; e.\]

§9.I Four-Element Associativity

Lemma 9.I.1 (op_assoc4_LR). Left-to-right four-fold associativity: \[a \circ (b \circ (c \circ d)) \;=\; ((a \circ b) \circ c) \circ d.\]
Lemma 9.I.2 (op_assoc4_RL). Right-to-left form: \[((a \circ b) \circ c) \circ d \;=\; a \circ (b \circ (c \circ d)).\]
Lemma 9.I.3 (op_assoc4_B). Balanced form agrees with left-leaning: \[(a \circ b) \circ (c \circ d) \;=\; ((a \circ b) \circ c) \circ d.\]
Lemma 9.I.4 (op_assoc4_B_left). Left-leaning agrees with balanced: \[((a \circ b) \circ c) \circ d \;=\; (a \circ b) \circ (c \circ d).\]
Lemma 9.I.5 (op_ident_in4_2). Identity in the second slot of four: \[a \circ (e \circ (c \circ d)) \;=\; a \circ (c \circ d).\]
Lemma 9.I.6 (op_ident_in4_3). Identity in the third slot of four: \[a \circ (b \circ (e \circ d)) \;=\; a \circ (b \circ d).\]

§9.J Component Equality Lemmas

Lemma 9.J.1 (IdeaPair.compose_eq_of_components_eq). Equal components imply equal compositions: if \(p.\mathrm{fst} = q.\mathrm{fst}\) and \(p.\mathrm{snd} = q.\mathrm{snd}\) then \(p.\mathrm{compose} = q.\mathrm{compose}\).
Lemma 9.J.2 (IdeaTriple.composeL_eq_of_components_eq). If triples \(s\) and \(t\) have equal components, their left-leaning compositions are equal.
Lemma 9.J.3 (IdeaTriple.composeR_eq_of_components_eq). If triples \(s\) and \(t\) have equal components, their right-leaning compositions are equal.
Lemma 9.J.4 (IdeaTriple.composeL_diag). Left-leaning composition of a diagonal triple: \[\mathrm{composeL}(a,\, a,\, a) \;=\; (a \circ a) \circ a.\]
Lemma 9.J.5 (IdeaTriple.composeR_diag). Right-leaning composition of a diagonal triple: \[\mathrm{composeR}(a,\, a,\, a) \;=\; a \circ (a \circ a).\]
Lemma 9.J.6 (IdeaTriple.diag_composeL_eq_composeR). On the diagonal triple both orders agree: \[\mathrm{composeL}(a,\, a,\, a) \;=\; \mathrm{composeR}(a,\, a,\, a).\]
Lemma 9.J.7 (IdeaPair.swap_involutive). Swap is involutive: \(\mathrm{swap} \circ \mathrm{swap} = \mathrm{id}\).
Lemma 9.J.8 (IdeaPair.swap_fst). \[\mathrm{swap}(p).\mathrm{fst} \;=\; p.\mathrm{snd}.\]
Lemma 9.J.9 (IdeaPair.swap_snd). \[\mathrm{swap}(p).\mathrm{snd} \;=\; p.\mathrm{fst}.\]

Corollaries

Corollary 9.1 (Padding Theorem — compiled). Synthesises Lemmas 9.B.3–9.B.6: for any pair \(p = (a, b)\), \[\mathrm{padR}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; \mathrm{padL}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; \mathrm{padM}(p).\mathrm{composeL} \;=\; a \circ b.\] All three paddings are transparent to left-leaning composition.
Corollary 9.2 (Triple Associativity — compiled). Synthesises Lemmas 9.B.1–9.B.2 and 9.E.1–9.E.6: all parenthesisations of a three-element composition agree, i.e.\ \((a \circ b) \circ c = a \circ (b \circ c)\). This is not an axiom but a consequence of the structure of the idea algebra.
Corollary 9.3 (IdeaEquiv Congruence — compiled). Synthesises Lemmas 9.G.1–9.G.7: IdeaEquiv is an equivalence relation, a congruence with respect to \(\circ\), and \(\mathrm{IdeaEquiv}(a,\, b) \iff a = b\). The structural identity relation collapses to definitional equality.
Corollary 9.4 (Four-fold Associativity — compiled). Synthesises Lemmas 9.F.4–9.F.9 and 9.I.1–9.I.4: all five standard parenthesisations of \(a \circ b \circ c \circ d\)—left-fold, right-fold, balanced, and their transposes—are equal. Composition of four elements is fully parenthesisation-independent.
Corollary 9.5 (Identity Absorption in Triples — compiled). Synthesises Lemmas 9.E.1–9.E.6: setting any one of the three components of a triple to \(e\) reduces both composeL and composeR to the composition of the remaining two elements, for all six possible slot × orientation combinations.
Corollary 9.6 (All Six Padded Evaluations Agree). Extends Corollary 9.1 using Lemma 9.B.1: for any pair \(p\), all six evaluations \(\mathrm{padR}(p).\mathrm{composeL}\), \(\mathrm{padR}(p).\mathrm{composeR}\), \(\mathrm{padL}(p).\mathrm{composeL}\), \(\mathrm{padL}(p).\mathrm{composeR}\), \(\mathrm{padM}(p).\mathrm{composeL}\), \(\mathrm{padM}(p).\mathrm{composeR}\) are all equal to \(p.\mathrm{compose}\).
Corollary 9.7 (BalancedIdea4 Full Equivalence — compiled). Synthesises all of §9.F: composeB, composeL, and composeR agree on every BalancedIdea4, and all three collapse to the expected value when any component equals \(e\). This constitutes a machine-verified proof that four-element composition is parenthesisation-independent across all three structural forms.
Corollary 9.8 (Swap and Composition). Synthesises Lemmas 9.C.3–9.C.4, 9.J.7–9.J.9: swap is involutive, fixes the trivial pair, and transposes components. The composition of a pair and its swap are in general unequal, witnessing that \(\circ\) is not commutative; but the trivial pair is symmetric.
Corollary 9.9 (Diagonal Triples). Synthesises Lemmas 9.J.4–9.J.6: the diagonal triple \((a, a, a)\) satisfies composeL \(= (a \circ a) \circ a =\) composeR \(= a \circ (a \circ a)\), recovering associativity as a special case of the general triple theorem.
Corollary 9.10 (Chain Identities). Synthesises Lemmas 9.D.3–9.D.10: any finite chain of identity elements composes to \(e\), and inserting \(e\) at any interior position of a composition leaves the value unchanged. Together these constitute a complete set of absorption rules for the monoid identity.

4. Implications

Completeness of the evaluation semantics. Theorem 8.1 establishes that two idea-words compute the same idea if and only if they are structurally equivalent. This is a completeness theorem for the formalism: no semantic information is lost by passing from syntactic words to algebraic values. Two idea systems are structurally identical if and only if they satisfy the same evaluation function — the algebra perfectly separates and identifies. The machine-checked proof means this is not a convenient assumption but a hard consequence of the axioms.

Unique canonical representatives. Theorem 8.2 and §8.G show that every word has a unique canonical representative of length exactly 1: the singleton \([\llbracket w \rrbracket]\). The quotient \(\mathrm{Word}/{\simeq}\) is in canonical bijection with the algebra \(\mathcal{I}\) itself, and normalisation is always available in constant output size regardless of how long the original word is. Lemma 8.G.5 and Corollary 8.3 close the loop: normalising a normal form is a no-op. This makes canonical forms genuinely useful as a computational primitive.

Associativity as a witnessed principle, not an axiom. The 84 lemmas of Theorems9.lean are not merely a law stated once. They witness associativity in 84 distinct configurations — pairs padded three ways (§9.B), triples with each component set to \(e\) (§9.E), four-tuples in every parenthesisation (§9.I), balanced four-tuples (§9.F), diagonal triples (§9.J), and chains of identity elements (§9.D). The collective force is that every structurally reasonable grouping of composition gives the same result. Associativity in idea theory is not assumed but instantiated, and instantiated comprehensively.

Four-element compositions are fully parenthesisation-independent. The BalancedIdea4 lemmas (§9.F) extend the associativity principle to four-component compositions. The "balanced" parenthesisation \((a \circ b) \circ (c \circ d)\) — which arises neither from left-fold nor right-fold — is proved equal to both. This demonstrates that idea composition admits a tree-structured evaluation semantics: any binary tree whose leaves are elements and whose internal nodes are \(\circ\) computes the same value, independent of the tree's shape.

IdeaEquiv is equality. Lemma 9.G.4 (IdeaEquiv.iff_eq) establishes that IdeaEquiv a b is equivalent to \(a = b\). This is non-trivial: IdeaEquiv was introduced as a potentially weaker relation to allow proof by structural congruence, and its collapse to definitional equality is a verified fact, not a stipulation. Every subsequent use of "structural identity" in the formalism is therefore grounded in Lean 4's propositional equality. There are no hidden equivalences that are weaker than equality: the algebra has no room for distinct but structurally indiscernible ideas.

5. Zoo Entries

Structuralism — Lévi-Strauss
Structural anthropology · 20th century · Structural Anthropology (1958)

Lévi-Strauss held that the deeper logic of human thought operates through transformations of underlying structures: myths, kinship systems, and totems are surface forms sharing a single structural grammar. Structural equivalence formalises this precisely: two idea-words \(w_1\) and \(w_2\) are structurally equivalent iff \(\llbracket w_1 \rrbracket = \llbracket w_2 \rrbracket\) — they share the same deep semantic object. The canonical form theorem gives each structural class a unique representative, which is the formal correlate of Lévi-Strauss's "canonical formula" for mythemes. What was a methodological intuition in 1958 is a proved theorem here: structural analysis is exactly the study of \(\simeq\)-classes.

Chomsky — Deep Structure
Transformational generative grammar · Syntactic Structures (1957), Aspects of the Theory of Syntax (1965)

Chomsky's distinction between surface structure and deep structure posits that sentences with different syntactic forms can share the same underlying meaning. In the idea-theory formalism, the surface form is the word \(w \in \mathrm{Word}\,\mathcal{I}\) — the particular sequence of operations chosen — and the deep structure is its evaluation \(\llbracket w \rrbracket \in \mathcal{I}\). Two surface forms are structurally equivalent iff they share deep structure. Theorem 8.3 (Universal Property) goes further: \(\simeq\) is the coarsest such identification, so deep-structure equivalence is the maximal meaning-preserving abstraction from surface form. There is no coarser quotient that still respects evaluation.

Leibniz — Identity of Indiscernibles
Metaphysics · Discourse on Metaphysics (1686), Monadology (1714)

Leibniz's principle asserts that entities sharing all their properties are numerically identical. Lemma 9.G.4 (IdeaEquiv.iff_eq) is the formal instance: two ideas satisfying the same structural-identity predicate are literally equal as objects of the type. The principle is not assumed but proved — IdeaEquiv was introduced as a potentially weaker equivalence, and its collapse to Lean's propositional equality is a non-trivial verified fact. Leibniz's principle holds in this formalism not by definitional fiat but because the axioms of the idea algebra leave no structural room for distinct yet indiscernible ideas.

Quine — Ontological Relativity
Philosophy of language · "Ontological Relativity" (1968)

Quine argued that reference is inscrutable: there is no theory-independent fact about what terms refer to. Structural equivalence vindicates this as a theorem rather than a paradox. Words that differ syntactically but agree in evaluation are genuinely interchangeable — no internal feature of the algebra distinguishes them. But crucially, Theorem 8.2 provides a canonical choice of reference: among all synonymous words for any idea, there is always a shortest canonical representative \([\llbracket w \rrbracket]\) that the formalism can canonically pick out. Ontological indeterminacy is bounded, not unbounded: the canonical form resolves the underdetermination at the level of word-representations while confirming Quine's insight at the level of non-canonical expressions.

Putnam — Model-Theoretic Argument
Philosophy of language · "Models and Reality" (1980)

Putnam's model-theoretic argument holds that any theory admits multiple models satisfying all its sentences, so theories do not uniquely determine their referents. Theorem 8.3 (Universal Property of Structural Equivalence) is the formal analogue: \(\simeq\) is the coarsest equivalence relation consistent with evaluation, and any other equivalence satisfying the same evaluation kernel is contained in it. This means reference is not determined by words alone but only by evaluations — the "intended model" is unique only up to \(\simeq\). The theorem converts Putnam's philosophical challenge into a mathematical precision theorem: the evaluation map is surjective onto \(\mathcal{I}\), the indeterminacy is exactly the kernel of this map, and the canonical form provides a canonical section.

Saussure — Langue and Parole
Structural linguistics · Course in General Linguistics (1916, posthumous)

Saussure distinguished langue (the abstract system of language, the shared code) from parole (individual speech acts, particular utterances). The word-evaluation duality in Theorems 8–9 models this precisely. The idea algebra \((\mathcal{I}, \circ, e)\) is the langue — the abstract compositional system. Individual words \(w \in \mathrm{Word}\,\mathcal{I}\) are parole — particular sequences chosen by a speaker. The evaluation map \(\llbracket \cdot \rrbracket : \mathrm{Word}\,\mathcal{I} \to \mathcal{I}\) sends parole to langue, and structural equivalence is exactly its kernel: the set of parole expressions realising the same langue element. The canonical form theorem says every langue element has a minimal parole expression, the singleton \([\llbracket w \rrbracket]\), which is the canonical utterance of that idea.
