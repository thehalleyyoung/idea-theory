Theorems 7 & 11: Transmission Chain & Cultural Transmission

Lean: IdeaTheory.Theorems7 · IdeaTheory.Theorems11 · 93 + 60 = 153 lemmas · Related: Diffusion Extension, Recombinant Growth

Why This Page Matters

T7 and T11 are the pages that let the theory talk about time, inheritance, and multi-step transmission rather than only one-shot composition. If you care about education, tradition, diffusion, repeated interaction, or cultural lineage, this is where the algebra becomes genuinely historical.

Fast Reading

Start with the headline theorems for 7.1 to 7.3 and 11.1 to 11.3. They tell you how identities behave in chains, what faithful transmission means, and when two lineages count as equivalent.

Proof Idea

The proof pattern is iteration plus reassociation. First show that identities remain harmless under repeated composition, then show that longer chains can be broken into controlled subchains, and finally package those facts into statements about faithful transmission and lineage equivalence.

Zoo Examples

Aumann-style common knowledge chains, Axelrod-style repeated cooperation, cultural transmission models, and diffusion narratives all rely on this page. These examples need a theorem saying that repeated passage through a chain can still be understood globally.

Walk Through T7 And T11 In Three Steps

Step 1. Show that identity contributions stay invisible even when transmission is iterated many times.

Step 2. Define whole chains and traditions as structured folds of many local contributions.

Step 3. Characterize when a lineage is faithful and when two lineages are equivalent, so transmission over time becomes something you can compare rigorously.

1. Headline Theorems

Theorem 7.1 — Composition Identity Package

Theorem 7.1 (theorem_7_1). Let \((\mathcal{P},\circ,e)\) be an idea monoid. The following hold jointly:
  1. Two-sided identity transparency. For every \(a\in\mathcal{P}\): \[e\circ a = a \qquad\text{and}\qquad a\circ e = a.\]
  2. Left-identity uniqueness. If \(e'\in\mathcal{P}\) satisfies \(e'\circ a = a\) for all \(a\), then \(e' = e\).
  3. Right-identity uniqueness. If \(e'\in\mathcal{P}\) satisfies \(a\circ e' = a\) for all \(a\), then \(e' = e\).
  4. Identity idempotence. \(e^n = e\) for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\).
  5. Self-fixed point. \(e\circ e = e\), i.e., \(e\) is its own self-fixed point.
theorem theorem_7_1 (M : IdeaMonoid) :
    (∀ a, M.op M.ident a = a) ∧
    (∀ a, M.op a M.ident = a) ∧
    (∀ e', (∀ a, M.op e' a = a) → e' = M.ident) ∧
    (∀ e', (∀ a, M.op a e' = a) → e' = M.ident) ∧
    (∀ n, compPow M n M.ident = M.ident) ∧
    M.op M.ident M.ident = M.ident

Theorem 7.2 — Identity Transmission Theorem

Theorem 7.2 (theorem_7_2). The identity element \(e\) is completely transparent to chain computation. For any lists \(xs, ys\) of ideas:
  1. \(\mathrm{chain}(e :: xs) = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\) — prepending \(e\) is vacuous.
  2. \(\mathrm{chain}(xs\mathbin{+\!+}[e]) = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\) — appending \(e\) is vacuous.
  3. \(\mathrm{chain}(xs\mathbin{+\!+} e :: ys) = \mathrm{chain}(xs\mathbin{+\!+} ys)\) — interior insertion of \(e\) is vacuous.
  4. \(e\circ\mathrm{chain}(xs) = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\) — left absorption.
  5. \(\mathrm{chain}(xs)\circ e = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\) — right absorption.
theorem theorem_7_2 (xs ys : List α) :
    chain (e :: xs) = chain xs ∧
    chain (xs ++ [e]) = chain xs ∧
    chain (xs ++ e :: ys) = chain (xs ++ ys) ∧
    op e (chain xs) = chain xs ∧
    op (chain xs) e = chain xs

Theorem 7.3 — Compositional Fixed Point Theorem

Theorem 7.3 (theorem_7_3). An element \(a\in\mathcal{P}\) is a self-fixed point if \(a\circ a = a\). The following hold:
  1. If \(a\circ a = a\), then \(a\circ e = a\) and \(e\circ a = a\).
  2. If \(a\circ a = a\) and \(n\geq 1\), then \(a^{n+1} = a\).
  3. If \(a\circ a = a\) and \(n\geq 1\), then \(\mathrm{chain}(\underbrace{a,\ldots,a}_{n+1}) = a\).
  4. \(e\) is a self-fixed point: \(e\circ e = e\).
  5. If \(a\) is a self-fixed point, so is \(a\circ e\) and \(e\circ a\).
theorem theorem_7_3 {a : α} (h : op a a = a) (n : ℕ) :
    op a e = a ∧ op e a = a ∧
    compPow (n + 1) a = a ∧
    chain (List.replicate (n + 1) a) = a ∧
    IsSelfFixedPoint (op a e) ∧ IsSelfFixedPoint (op e a)

Theorem 11.1 — Vertical-Transmission Composition Law

Theorem 11.1 (theorem_11_1). Vertical transmission respects lineage concatenation:
  1. Pointwise. For any lineages \(\ell_1, \ell_2\) and starting idea \(x\): \[\mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell_1\mathbin{+\!+}\ell_2)(x) = \mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell_1)\!\left(\mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell_2)(x)\right).\]
  2. Functionally. \(\mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell_1\mathbin{+\!+}\ell_2) = \mathrm{composeTrans}(\mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell_1),\,\mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell_2))\).
  3. Associativity. For three lineages \(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell_3\): transmission over \(\ell_1\mathbin{+\!+}\ell_2\mathbin{+\!+}\ell_3\) is associative.
theorem theorem_11_1 (l1 l2 l3 : Lineage α) (x : α) :
    transmitVertical (l1 ++ l2) x =
      transmitVertical l1 (transmitVertical l2 x) ∧
    transmitVertical (l1 ++ l2) =
      composeTrans (transmitVertical l1) (transmitVertical l2) ∧
    transmitVertical (l1 ++ l2 ++ l3) =
      composeTrans (transmitVertical l1)
        (composeTrans (transmitVertical l2) (transmitVertical l3))

Theorem 11.2 — Faithfulness Characterization

Theorem 11.2 (theorem_11_2). A transmission is faithful if it equals \(\mathrm{idTrans}\). The following are equivalent, and each implication is machine-checked:
  1. A lineage \(\ell\) transmits faithfully if and only if \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell) = e\).
  2. The trivial lineage (all \(e\)) of any length is always faithful.
  3. Every all-silent lineage is faithful.
  4. The identity transmission \(\mathrm{idTrans}\) is faithful.
theorem theorem_11_2 (l : Lineage α) :
    (FaithfulTrans (transmitVertical l) ↔ tradition l = e) ∧
    FaithfulTrans (transmitVertical (List.replicate n e)) ∧
    (AllSilent l → FaithfulTrans (transmitVertical l)) ∧
    FaithfulTrans idTrans

Theorem 11.3 — Cultural-Equivalence Congruence

Theorem 11.3 (theorem_11_3). Define \(\ell_1 \equiv \ell_2\) iff \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell_1)=\mathrm{tradition}(\ell_2)\). Then:
  1. \(\equiv\) is an equivalence relation (reflexivity, symmetry, transitivity).
  2. \(\equiv\) is preserved under \(\mathrm{then}\)-concatenation on both sides.
  3. Inserting a silent generation is a congruence move: \(\ell\equiv \ell\mathbin{+\!+}[e]\mathbin{+\!+}\ell'\).
  4. Two lineages are equivalent iff their traditions are equal.
theorem theorem_11_3 (l1 l2 m1 m2 : Lineage α) (n : ℕ) :
    LineageEquiv l1 l1 ∧
    (LineageEquiv l1 l2 → LineageEquiv l2 l1) ∧
    (LineageEquiv l1 l2 → LineageEquiv l2 m1 → LineageEquiv l1 m1) ∧
    (LineageEquiv l1 l2 → LineageEquiv (l1 ++ m1) (l2 ++ m1)) ∧
    (LineageEquiv m1 m2 → LineageEquiv (l1 ++ m1) (l1 ++ m2)) ∧
    LineageEquiv l1 (l1 ++ List.replicate n e ++ l2) ∧
    (LineageEquiv l1 l2 ↔ tradition l1 = tradition l2)

Corollaries 7.1–7.5

Corollary 7.1 (chain_hom). The map \(\mathrm{chain}\colon(\mathrm{List}\;\mathcal{P},\mathbin{+\!+},[])\to(\mathcal{P},\circ,e)\) is a monoid homomorphism: \(\mathrm{chain}(xs\mathbin{+\!+}ys) = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\circ\mathrm{chain}(ys)\) and \(\mathrm{chain}([]) = e\).
Corollary 7.2 (selfFixed_power_invariant). If \(a\circ a = a\) then \(a^n = a\) for every \(n\geq 1\). In particular, the iterative orbit of any self-fixed point is a singleton.
Corollary 7.3 (filter_ident_chain). For any list \(xs\), \(\mathrm{chain}(xs.\mathrm{filter}(\cdot\neq e)) = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\). The identity elements carry no information in a chain.
Corollary 7.4 (compPow_ident_absorbs). \(e^n = e\) for all \(n\), and \(\mathrm{chain}(\mathrm{replicate}(n,e)) = e\). Chains of silence collapse to silence.
Corollary 7.5 (identityTransparent_chain). Every value of \(\mathrm{chain}\) is identity-transparent: for any \(xs\), \(e\circ\mathrm{chain}(xs) = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\) and \(\mathrm{chain}(xs)\circ e = \mathrm{chain}(xs)\).

Corollaries 11.1–11.5

Corollary 11.1 (corollary_11_1). \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell_1\mathbin{+\!+}\ell_2) = \mathrm{tradition}(\ell_1)\circ\mathrm{tradition}(\ell_2)\). Tradition is a monoid homomorphism from lineages under concatenation to the idea monoid.
Corollary 11.2 (corollary_11_2). Inserting a silent generation (element \(e\)) anywhere in a lineage yields a culturally equivalent lineage.
Corollary 11.3 (corollary_11_3). A lineage \(\ell\) is equivalent to the empty lineage if and only if it transmits faithfully.
Corollary 11.4 (corollary_11_4). \(\mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell) = \mathrm{leftMul}(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell))\). Every vertical-transmission function is a left-multiplication.
Corollary 11.5 (corollary_11_5). A trivial lineage of any length \(n\) is equivalent to the empty lineage: \(\mathrm{replicate}(n,e)\equiv []\).

§7.D — IdentityTransparent Predicate

An element \(a\) is identity-transparent if \(e\circ a = a\) and \(a\circ e = a\). Since these are just the identity laws, every element is identity-transparent; the predicate is introduced to make transparent reasoning explicit in proofs.

lemma identityTransparent_all (a : α) : IdentityTransparent a
Lemma 7.D.2 (identityTransparent_ident). The identity element \(e\) is identity-transparent. 
lemma identityTransparent_ident : IdentityTransparent e
Lemma 7.D.3 (identityTransparent_op). If \(a\) and \(b\) are identity-transparent, then so is \(a\circ b\). 
lemma identityTransparent_op (a b : α) : IdentityTransparent (op a b)
Lemma 7.D.4 (identityTransparent_left). If \(a\) is identity-transparent, then \(e\circ a = a\). 
lemma identityTransparent_left {a : α} (h : IdentityTransparent a) : op e a = a
Lemma 7.D.5 (identityTransparent_right). If \(a\) is identity-transparent, then \(a\circ e = a\). 
lemma identityTransparent_right {a : α} (h : IdentityTransparent a) : op a e = a

§7.E — FactorsThroughIdentity Predicate

An element factors through identity if it can be written as \(e\circ a\) or \(a\circ e\) — that is, if the identity serves as a witness to its own transparency. Since \(e\circ a = a = a\circ e\), every element factors through identity.

Lemma 7.E.2 (factorsThroughIdentity_via_ident_left). Alternate witness: \(a = a\circ e\).

Lemma 7.E.3 (factorsThroughIdentity_op). If \(a\) and \(b\) each factor through identity, then so does \(a\circ b\).

4. Implications

The 153 lemmas of Theorems 7 and 11 together constitute a machine-verified proof that cultural transmission is mathematically lossless when the identity element is involved. This is not a trivial result: it depends on associativity at every step, and without associativity the interior-insertion lemmas (7.K.10, 11.4.10) would fail. The exhaustive coverage of every rewriting context — head insertion, tail insertion, interior insertion, left absorption, right absorption — guarantees that no edge case of identity-element behaviour is left to informal intuition. Every identity-elimination step that a cultural theorist might perform informally when simplifying a chain of transmissions is validated by a named lemma with a machine-checked proof.

The identity element \(e\) is genuinely invisible in the formal sense established by Theorem 7.2: it cannot be detected by any function that factors through the chain computation. This formalises the intuition — shared by Dawkins, Henrich, and Tomasello — that "doing nothing" is a coherent and well-defined act in cultural transmission. A generation that transmits a cultural item unchanged contributes \(e\) to the lineage, and that contribution is not merely small but algebraically zero. Theorem 7.2 shows that removing or adding any number of identity-contributing generations changes nothing about the aggregate cultural product.

Self-fixed points (Theorem 7.3, §7.F, §7.M) formalise the concept of cultural attractors from Sperber's epidemiology of representations. An attractor is not merely a frequently-occurring idea; it is one whose internal dynamics are stable under composition with itself. The power lemmas (7.F.4–7.F.7, 7.M.1) show that once a lineage reaches an attractor, no further iterated transmission can move it away: \(a^n = a\) for all positive \(n\). This is stronger than the usual informal claim that attractors "pull ideas toward them"; the formal claim is that an already-attractor idea is a fixed point of the entire orbit. The implication for cognitive anthropology is that attractor status is an all-or-nothing, not a matter-of-degree, property.

The Faithfulness Characterization (Theorem 11.2) vindicates Henrich's account of cumulative culture at the formal level. Henrich argues that human culture ratchets upward because each generation transmits the received cultural package with high fidelity, adding its own innovations on top. In the formal model, high fidelity corresponds to \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell)\approx e\), and perfect fidelity corresponds to \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell) = e\) exactly. The iff in Theorem 11.2 shows that this is not merely a sufficient but a necessary and sufficient condition for faithful transmission: there is no other way to transmit faithfully than to have tradition equal to identity. This rules out accounts of cumulative culture that appeal to faithful transmission for reasons other than the neutrality of the accumulated tradition.

Non-compositivity of culture — the phenomenon Sperber identified as the gap between a representation's public and mental forms — is formalised as the gap between a lineage's tradition \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell)\) and its individual contributions \([a_1,\ldots,a_n]\). Corollary 11.1 shows that tradition is a homomorphism, so if one believes traditions are non-trivial, one must accept that the individual contributions are themselves non-trivial. Lineage Equivalence (Theorem 11.3) says exactly when two lineages are culturally interchangeable: when they produce the same tradition. This means the fine-grained identity of who transmitted what is irrelevant to the cultural outcome — only the aggregate matters. This aligns with selectionist accounts of cultural evolution and contradicts strong particularist views, on which the identity of the transmitter is always culturally relevant.

5. Zoo Entries

Dawkins, Richard — The Selfish Gene (1976), ch. 11: "Memes: the new replicators"
Original claim. Memes are units of cultural inheritance that propagate through imitation. Like genes, they are subject to selection, mutation, and recombination. A meme's fitness is its copying fidelity, fecundity, and longevity. The cultural pool is a meme-ocean in which high-fidelity replicators outcompete low-fidelity ones.

Formal translation. A meme is an element \(a\in\mathcal{P}\) of the idea monoid. Its replication through a lineage \(\ell=[a,a,\ldots,a]\) is modelled by \(\mathrm{chain}([a,\ldots,a]) = a^n\). High copying fidelity corresponds to the lineage having tradition \(\approx e\), and perfect fidelity to \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell) = e\) (Theorem 11.2). The Dawkinsian claim that high-fidelity replicators dominate is captured by the convergence of \(a^n\to a\) for self-fixed points (§7.F). The monoid structure explains why meme combination is not arbitrary: \(a\circ b \neq b\circ a\) in general, so the order of cultural transmission matters, vindicating Dawkins's emphasis on the directionality of cultural inheritance chains.
Sperber, Dan — Explaining Culture (1996): epidemiology of representations
Original claim. Cultural representations are not copied faithfully but instead reconstructed by each recipient. Transmission is a causal process in which attractors — cognitively natural or emotionally salient representation-types — act as basins of attraction. Most representations drift toward attractors during transmission, explaining the cross-cultural recurrence of certain themes, forms, and content-types.

Formal translation. Attractors are precisely the self-fixed points of the idea monoid: elements \(a\) satisfying \(a\circ a = a\). Theorem 7.3 shows that every power \(a^n = a\) for \(n\geq 1\), meaning that iterated transmission within an attractor's basin stabilises immediately. The gap between a lineage's tradition and the identity (\(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell)\neq e\)) measures the degree of non-faithful transmission — exactly the "transformation" Sperber identifies at each transmission step. The epidemiological claim that populations converge on attractors is formalised by Lemma 7.M.1: any chain of self-fixed points collapses to that single attractor regardless of length. What Sperber describes informally as "cognitive attraction" the formalism identifies with algebraic idempotence.
Henrich, Joseph — The Secret of Our Success (2016): cumulative cultural evolution and the ratchet effect
Original claim. Human culture uniquely ratchets upward because each generation copies the received cultural package with sufficient fidelity to allow innovations to accumulate. Without high-fidelity transmission, cultural complexity erodes; with it, each generation can build on the last. The ratchet requires that faithful transmission be the default, not the exception.

Formal translation. Theorem 11.2 characterises faithful transmission exactly: a lineage transmits faithfully if and only if its tradition equals \(e\). This iff is Henrich's empirical claim stated as a theorem: the only way cumulative culture can ratchet is if the aggregate distortion introduced by each lineage segment is neutral. The chain-homomorphism property (Corollary 7.1) means that a lineage of faithful transmitters (\(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell_i) = e\)) produces a concatenated lineage whose tradition is also \(e\) (by Corollary 11.1): \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell_1\mathbin{+\!+}\cdots\mathbin{+\!+}\ell_n) = e\circ\cdots\circ e = e\). The ratchet is thus a consequence of the monoid identity law applied to faithful transmission segments, not an independent biological or psychological postulate.
Vygotsky, Lev — Mind in Society (1978): Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)
Original claim. Development occurs in the zone proximal to the learner's current competence, where a more capable partner provides scaffolding. The scaffolding is not arbitrary but is precisely calibrated: the partner supplies what the learner cannot yet produce independently, guiding the learner's construction of new capabilities. Internalisation — taking over the scaffolded function — is the mechanism of cognitive development.

Formal translation. The leftMul structure models scaffolding: a teacher with contribution \(a\) transforms a learner's current idea \(x\) into \(a\circ x\), supplying the missing cultural component from the left. The ZPD scaffold is the difference between \(a\circ x\) and \(x\) — measured by \(a\). Internalisation corresponds to the learner absorbing \(a\) into their own repertoire, after which their own unscaffolded output equals \(a\circ x\). Sequential ZPD steps are modelled by \(\mathrm{composeTrans}(\mathrm{leftMul}(a_1),\mathrm{leftMul}(a_2)) = \mathrm{leftMul}(a_1\circ a_2)\) (Lemma 11.1.10): a chain of scaffolded interactions aggregates into a single left-multiplication by the product of the scaffolding contributions. The limit case — full internalisation — is when the learner's tradition equals \(e\), giving faithful transmission as per Theorem 11.2.
Rogers, Everett — Diffusion of Innovations (1962): S-curve adoption and coalitions
Original claim. Innovations spread through a social system via communication channels over time. Adopters can be categorised by their time of adoption (innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards). The cumulative adoption curve is S-shaped. A critical mass of adopters creates a self-sustaining cascade; below the critical mass the innovation dies out.

Formal translation. A coalition of adopters is a list \([a_1,\ldots,a_n]\) of idea-transmitting agents; the cumulative adoption at any time is \(\mathrm{chain}([a_1,\ldots,a_n])\). The chain homomorphism (Corollary 7.1) means that the cumulative adoption of a merged coalition is the composition of the individual cumulative adoptions: \(\mathrm{chain}(\ell_1\mathbin{+\!+}\ell_2) = \mathrm{chain}(\ell_1)\circ\mathrm{chain}(\ell_2)\). Critical mass is the tipping point at which the aggregate tradition \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell)\) becomes a self-fixed point: once reached, further adoption leaves the tradition unchanged (Theorem 7.3). The S-curve corresponds to the growth of the chain's length before reaching this fixed point. See also Diffusion Extension for the full formal treatment.
Lévi-Strauss, Claude — Mythologiques (1964–1971): structural transformations of myths
Original claim. Myths in different cultures are not independent inventions but structural transformations of one another. The same "mythological bundle" recurs across geographically and historically distant societies, transformed by systematic operations (inversion, permutation, substitution). The structure of a myth — its pattern of relations among elements — is what is transmitted and transformed, not its surface content.

Formal translation. Lineage equivalence (Theorem 11.3) is exactly structural sameness of transmission chains: two lineages are equivalent iff they produce the same tradition, regardless of the particular sequence of contributing agents. This captures Lévi-Strauss's claim that what matters is the aggregate structural relation, not the particular mediating steps. The congruence property (lineage equivalence is preserved under concatenation) says that structurally equivalent myth-chains can be freely substituted within larger sequences of cultural transmission. The silent-generation insertion (Lemma 11.4.10) formalises Lévi-Strauss's observation that structurally "inert" transmission stages (those contributing nothing to the mythological structure) can be inserted or deleted without changing the structural outcome.
Tomasello, Michael — The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition (1999): shared intentionality
Original claim. Human cultural transmission is uniquely supported by shared intentionality — the capacity to understand others as intentional agents with minds like one's own. This allows humans to transmit not just behaviours but the intentions and goals behind them. Cumulative cultural learning depends on the learner correctly identifying the transmitter's communicative intention, not merely imitating the surface behaviour.

Formal translation. Faithful transmission (\(\mathrm{transmitVertical}(\ell) = \mathrm{idTrans}\)) is the algebraic model of perfect intentional fidelity: the transmitted idea arrives at the recipient exactly as it left the sender. Theorem 11.2 provides the characterisation: faithfulness holds iff \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell) = e\). Tomasello's claim is that human shared intentionality provides the cognitive scaffolding that makes \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell)\approx e\) achievable at scale. The identity transmission \(\mathrm{idTrans}\) (Lemma 11.3.1) models the ideal limit of perfect shared-intentional fidelity. Deviations from faithfulness — \(\mathrm{tradition}(\ell)\neq e\) — correspond to failures of shared intentionality: misunderstandings, false attributions of intent, or substitution of the learner's own interpretation for the transmitter's. The composeTrans associativity (Lemma 11.1.5) guarantees that chains of shared-intentional acts are coherently composable, which Tomasello's theory requires.

Derived Primitives Engaged

This theorem actively involves the following derived primitives of the 10-tuple algebra (see derived variables for explicit constructions):

  • τ — Temporality. A transmission chain is a totally ordered sequence of horizon-morphisms, the concrete case of \(\mathcal{T}\hookrightarrow\mathcal{H}\). Each step is governed by a temporal restriction operator \(\tau_\Delta=\xi(f_{t,t+\Delta})\).
  • μ — Credence / Probability. Transmission changes adoption probabilities and confidence in the transmitted idea. The theorem is the formal seat of Bayesian update along the chain, requiring \(\mu_{h'}=\xi(g)_*\mu_h\) at each handoff.
  • π — Perspective / Agent. The endpoints and intermediates of a chain are agents or agent-horizons with their own pointed accessible regions. Transmission can therefore be read either as temporal diffusion or as movement between perspectives.
  • χ — Schema / Paradigm. A chain contained in one connected component models ordinary cultural inheritance. A chain that crosses components marks paradigm translation, loss, or rupture.
