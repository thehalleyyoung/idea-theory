All Theorems

Idea Theory is backed by ~980 machine-verified theorems and lemmas spanning 12 Theorem files and 5 extension modules. Every statement on these pages has been formally verified in Lean 4 with no sorry or admit. The source code is at the Lean library.

How To Read A Theorem Page

Each theorem page has two jobs at once. It has to tell a mathematically trained reader what the exact structural statement is, and it has to tell a general reader why anyone should care. To support both audiences, the theorem index now does the translation work first. Read the theorem label as a compact reference, then read the plain-English description as the real content. When a zoo page cites T4, for example, it means more than "see theorem 4": it means "the amount of novelty created by composing ideas behaves coherently across longer chains, so emergence is constrained rather than mysterious."

The pages are organized from large claim to supporting infrastructure. "Headline theorem" means the main result someone outside the codebase is likely to cite. "Lemmas" are the pieces the proof actually depends on. "Lean declaration" is the machine-checked name. If you are not trying to inspect the proof object itself, you can usually read the page in this order: plain-English summary, the formal statement, the worked examples, then the zoo links showing where the theorem matters.

Plain-English Map of T1-T12

Label What it says in ordinary language Why a non-math reader should care
T1 Ideas can be composed in sequence without ambiguity; there is a genuine "do nothing" idea; repeatable and reversible patterns can be defined precisely. Whenever a page says a theory has stable building blocks, silent steps, reversible reframings, or compositional chains, it is usually leaning on T1.
T2 There is a principled way to measure how well two ideas fit, reinforce, oppose, or illuminate one another, and that measurement extends consistently to larger constructions. Prototype effects, salience, payoff, similarity, explanatory force, and semantic fit all rely on T2-style structure somewhere in the zoo.
T3 When two ideas meet, part of each is preserved, part is cancelled or displaced, and part can rise into something genuinely new. This is the theorem behind dialectic, conceptual blending, scientific revolution, creative destruction, and any example that talks about preservation plus loss plus novelty.
T4 Novelty is not arbitrary. The "extra" created when ideas combine fits a coherence law across longer chains of composition. If a page claims emergence can be measured, bounded, accumulated, telescoped, or compared across examples, T4 is the engine.
T5 Resonance can be asymmetric: the path from idea \(a\) to idea \(b\) can matter differently from the reverse path, and that asymmetry has durable structure. Framing effects, interpretive asymmetry, tension, conflict, path dependence, and one-way salience often amount to T5 phenomena.
T6 Reframing an idea by a context or higher-order operator can transform its appearance while preserving its underlying algebraic role. Context effects, habitus, perspective shifts, belief reports, strategic framing, and situated meaning all cite T6 because they are formalized as controlled reframings.
T7 Long chains of transmission can be analyzed step by step without losing the total picture; silent steps can be inserted or removed without changing the result. Whenever the docs talk about diffusion, education, repeated interaction, narrative inheritance, or cumulative emergence over time, T7 is doing the bookkeeping.
T8 Two syntactically different expressions can still count as the same idea if they evaluate to the same structure, and there is a canonical way to represent that sameness. Synonymy, deep structure, model-theoretic underdetermination, translation, and structuralism all need T8.
T9 Ideas can be assigned a level, degree, or complexity that behaves consistently under composition. Any page about hierarchy, salience, abstraction level, difficulty, ontological rank, or growth across levels relies on T9-style grading.
T10 Groups of agents or sub-ideas can aggregate into larger social structures while preserving detectable cohesion conditions. Coalitions, committees, commons governance, and social alignment examples frequently appeal to T10.
T11 Cultural transmission can itself be treated as a compositional algebra with inheritance, identity cases, and faithful propagation behavior. This matters when the docs move from one-off compositions to historically extended transmission systems.
T12 Novelty can be distinguished from mere recombination: some outputs are genuinely outside a lineage or closure set in a mathematically testable sense. This is the page to consult when a zoo entry claims that something is not merely a rearrangement of earlier material but a real structural innovation.

Part II: All Lemmas by File

What Counts As "Support" For A Theorem?

The theorem pages are long because the public claim and the machine proof sit at different scales. A theorem like T8 may be expressible in one sentence, but it requires dozens of smaller lemmas about words, appending, reassociation, canonical forms, and congruence before Lean can certify it. That is not padding. It is the formal analogue of showing every intermediate step instead of saying "it is obvious."

When a theorem page lists 50 or 100 supporting lemmas, the practical reading advice is: look first at the section names. They tell you what kinds of micro-facts had to be settled to make the headline statement trustworthy. The zoo pages typically cite the headline theorem, but the theorem page shows the scaffolding that makes the citation defensible.

Part IV: How to Read the Theorems

Each theorem page contains every lemma from its source file presented in a <div class="thm"> box with a rendered LaTeX statement. Results are grouped by theme — definitions first, then structural lemmas, then the headline theorem — so you can follow the logical thread from axioms to conclusion without consulting the raw source.

Lean code blocks on each page show the actual verified proof sketch: the theorem or lemma statement as it appears in the .lean file, together with its proof term or tactic block. Because every statement is closed (no sorry), the code blocks are a faithful record of what Lean accepted.

Zoo entries connect formal results to the intellectual history of ideas. Where a theorem has a classical counterpart — e.g.\ the Aufhebung decomposition echoing Hegel, or the emergence 2-cocycle echoing group extension theory — the corresponding Zoo entry provides the conceptual bridge and points to the relevant literature.
