Literature ↔ Formal Bridge

This page is the master index connecting the long-standing informal literature on Idea Theory — the loose constellation of writings on how ideas combine, resonate, sublate, propagate, and give rise to emergent meaning — with the machine-verified theorems formalized in this project's six-volume program. The formal core consists of nine basic-mathematics theorems (composition, pairing, Aufhebung decomposition, cocycle, curvature, conjugation, chains, equivalence, grading) proved in Lean 4 under the IdeaTheory namespace, and five subsequent volumes interpreting that algebraic apparatus across the social sciences, humanities, philosophy of mind and cognitive science, the philosophical problem of emergence, and further advanced applications. Our aim here is neither to summarize the literature nor to advertise the proofs, but to make the correspondence between them auditable: to record, claim by claim, which informal proposition is now a Lean lemma, which definition has been sharpened, where named authors disagree, and what the formal system adjudicates. The page provides a curated map of intellectual schools, a long cross-walk table from informal claims to Lean identifiers, per-concept bridge sub-sections, a grouped bibliography drawn from references.bib, an explanation of the maintenance machinery, and a list of open problems inherited from prior writers. It is intended to be read alongside the concept pages and the Lean source.

Why Bridge?

The phrase “idea theory” has been used, with varying degrees of rigor, by intellectual historians (Lovejoy 1936), philosophers of mind (Fodor 1975; Dennett 1991), cognitive scientists working on conceptual combination (Fauconnier & Turner 2002), sociologists of knowledge (Mannheim 1936; Bourdieu 1984), memeticists (Dawkins 1976; Sperber 1996), and complexity theorists writing on emergence (Anderson 1972; Bedau 1997). Their claims overlap in suggestive ways — ideas compose, ideas resonate, some combinations sublate their parts, novel composites carry emergent content not present in either constituent — but the vocabulary across these traditions is incommensurate, and the proposed laws are stated in prose that resists falsification. A reader cannot easily tell whether Lovejoy's “unit-ideas” are the same objects as Sperber's “cultural representations,” nor whether Hegel's Aufhebung is a special case of Fauconnier and Turner's blending or an entirely different operation.

Bridging matters for three reasons. First, testability: an informal claim such as “the meaning of a composite idea exceeds the sum of its parts” becomes empirically and mathematically tractable only once one has a definite operation of composition, a definite pairing measuring “meaning,” and a definite excess term — exactly what the emergence 2-cocycle ε(a,b) supplies. Second, definition-sharpening: several historically contested terms — resonance, sublation, structural equivalence, conjugation of perspectives — have multiple incompatible glosses in the literature, and forcing each into a Lean signature exposes which gloss the writer was tacitly assuming. Third, adjudication: where the literature openly disagrees — for instance whether resonance is symmetric (Sperber) or asymmetric (Bourdieu's field-theoretic reading) — the formal system makes the disagreement crisp: Theorem 5 (meaning curvature) shows that asymmetric resonance is generic and that the symmetric case is a measure-zero specialization.

A further reason is cumulativity. Informal idea-theoretic literature has historically been re-founded by every generation; one of the costs is that genuine results — Lovejoy's transmission-chain analyses, Mannheim's structural-equivalence intuitions, the cocycle structure implicit in Fauconnier & Turner's “emergent structure” — are repeatedly re-discovered rather than built upon. By pinning each claim to a Lean identifier, we hope to make the formal corpus a substrate that subsequent informal work can cite without re-proving. The five applied volumes (II–VI) are written in that spirit: each of their interpretive theorems is an instance of one of the nine basic-mathematics theorems in Volume I, so an empirical worker in, say, the sociology of taste need only verify the instantiation, not the underlying algebra.

Finally, the bridge is a discipline against inflation. Idea-theoretic prose has a recurring failure mode in which a metaphor (resonance, depth, sublation) is asked to carry too much weight. Forcing each metaphor through a Lean type either earns it the right to its content or exposes it as decorative. Several terms from earlier drafts of our own program were eliminated by exactly this filter; what survives is documented below.

Map of the Literature

We organize the prior literature on idea-theoretic topics into five named schools. The grouping is ours; individual authors often participate in more than one. Each school is described in terms of (i) its core informal claim, (ii) its representative authors and works, and (iii) the corresponding Volume(s) of this project that interpret it formally.

1. The History-of-Ideas School (Lovejoy and successors)

Lovejoy's The Great Chain of Being (1936) introduced the program of isolating “unit-ideas” that propagate, recombine, and decay across intellectual history. Skinner (1969) and Pocock (1975) refined the program by insisting that unit-ideas be indexed by linguistic context, producing what is now called the Cambridge School of intellectual history. The shared informal claim is that ideas form a discrete combinatorial substrate whose dynamics can be tracked over time. Our Volume III (Humanities) interprets transmission chains (Theorem 7) as the formalization of Lovejoy's chains, and structural equivalence (Theorem 8) as the formalization of Skinner's “same-idea-in-different-vocabulary” criterion. See transmission-chain and structural-equivalence.

2. The Cognitive-Combinatorial School (Fauconnier, Turner, Fodor, Murphy)

This school treats ideas as mental representations that combine according to quasi-algebraic rules. Fodor's Language of Thought (1975) gives the strong compositional reading; Fauconnier & Turner's The Way We Think (2002) and Murphy's Big Book of Concepts (2002) give a weaker reading on which composites carry emergent structure not derivable from constituents. The disagreement between strong and weak compositionality is adjudicated formally in our Volume IV (Cognitive Science): the emergence 2-cocycle (Theorem 4) is exactly the obstruction to strong compositionality, and its non-triviality is provable rather than postulated.

3. The Sociology-of-Knowledge School (Mannheim, Bourdieu, Sperber, Latour)

From Mannheim's Ideology and Utopia (1936) through Bourdieu's Distinction (1984) and Sperber's Explaining Culture (1996) to Latour's actor-network writings, this school treats ideas as objects whose “weight” depends on the social field in which they circulate. The recurrent informal claim is that two agents holding nominally identical ideas may bear asymmetric resonance: agent A may be moved by B's articulation more than vice versa. Our Volume II (Social Sciences) interprets the resonance pairing (Theorem 2) and meaning curvature (Theorem 5) as the formalization of these field-asymmetries. See resonance-pairing.

4. The Dialectical / Aufhebung School (Hegel, Adorno, Žižek, Brandom)

The dialectical tradition holds that some pairs of ideas, when combined, neither merely sum nor merely conflict, but undergo Aufhebung: a decomposition into a preserved part, a negated part, and a lifted residue. The informal literature is famously unrigorous about the operation. Brandom's A Spirit of Trust (2019) is the most recent attempt to give it explicit form. Our Theorem 3 (Aufhebung decomposition) supplies the operation as a unique direct-sum splitting in the graded monoid; Volume III (Humanities) interprets the result and Volume V (Emergence) explains why the residue term is precisely what generates emergent novelty.

5. The Emergence / Complexity School (Anderson, Bedau, Kim, O'Connor)

Anderson's “More is Different” (1972), Bedau's analysis of weak emergence (1997), Kim's supervenience arguments (1999), and O'Connor & Wong's surveys (2015) constitute a literature whose core informal claim is that some composite phenomena bear properties not reducible to their parts, yet are not magical either. The recurrent technical worry is the absence of a formal object that is both well-defined and non-trivially non-additive. Our Volume V (Emergence) argues that the 2-cocycle ε of Theorem 4, together with the curvature form of Theorem 5, is exactly such an object: bilinear in its inputs, well-typed, and provably non-zero on a generic class of pairs. This is, to our knowledge, the first place in the literature where weak emergence has a Lean-checked definition.

Per-Concept Bridges

Composition

The composition operation · on ideas, formalized as a graded monoid in Theorem 1, descends from Fodor's compositional Language of Thought (1975) and from the older history-of-ideas tradition (Lovejoy 1936) which already presupposed that “unit-ideas” could be combined. The headline theorems are: (i) associativity, formalizing the informal claim that the order of grouping does not affect content; (ii) the identity law, formalizing the trivial-idea baseline used implicitly by Boghossian (2003); and (iii) the grading-respect sub-lemma, which connects to Theorem 9 and pins down Kim's (1999) supervenience intuition. Where Fodor expected strong compositionality, the conjunction of Theorems 1 and 4 shows that compositionality holds up to a 2-cocycle — neither Fodor's victory nor Murphy's (2002) defeat, but a quantified middle.

Resonance Pairing

The bilinear pairing ⟨·,·⟩ formalized in Theorem 2 is the mathematical successor to Mannheim's (1936) qualitative “weight” of an idea, Bourdieu's (1984) field-relative reception, and Sperber's (1996) relevance. The headline theorems are: (i) bilinearity, which earns the literature's metaphorical “additivity of resonance across audiences” the right to its name; (ii) non-degeneracy on the generic locus, which pins down when an idea has any uptake at all; and (iii) the asymmetry lemma, which connects to Theorem 5 and adjudicates the Sperber / Bourdieu disagreement in Bourdieu's favor on a measure-theoretic basis.

Aufhebung Decomposition

Theorem 3 supplies a unique direct-sum splitting a·b = π(a,b) ⊕ ν(a,b) ⊕ λ(a,b) of any composite into a preserved, negated, and lifted part. This is the formal heir to Hegel's (1812) Aufhebung, refined through Adorno (1966) and Brandom (2019). Headline theorems: (i) existence and uniqueness of the splitting under non-degeneracy of the pairing; (ii) compatibility with conjugation (Theorem 6), formalizing the intuition that “the same dialectic looks the same from any standpoint related by an internal symmetry”; and (iii) the residue non-vanishing lemma, which is the precise statement that lifting is not mere addition.

Emergence Cocycle

Theorem 4 establishes that the deviation ε(a,b) = a·b − (a + b) (in the appropriate ambient module) is a 2-cocycle: it satisfies the cocycle identity ε(a,b·c) − ε(a·b,c) + ε(b,c) − ε(a,b) = 0. This is the formal heir to Anderson's (1972) “more is different,” Bedau's (1997) weak emergence, Fauconnier & Turner's (2002) emergent structure, and O'Connor & Wong's (2015) taxonomy. Headline theorems: (i) the cocycle identity itself; (ii) non-vanishing on a generic class, which adjudicates the Fodor/Murphy debate quantitatively; and (iii) cohomological triviality on the abelianized sub-monoid, which explains why “already-classical” idea systems show no emergence.

Meaning Curvature

Theorem 5 introduces the curvature form κ(a,b) = ⟨a,b⟩ − ⟨b,a⟩ and proves it is generically non-zero, formalizing Bourdieu's (1984) field-asymmetry, the asymmetric reception analyses of Latour (2005), and the implicit asymmetry in Lakoff & Johnson's (1980) source-target metaphor mapping. Headline theorems: (i) antisymmetry; (ii) relation to the cocycle, namely dκ = ε − ε^{op}; and (iii) the flatness criterion, which characterizes exactly the Sperber-style symmetric case as a codimension-one specialization.

Conjugation Action

Theorem 6 establishes that a group G of internal symmetries acts on ideas by conjugation g·a·g⁻¹, preserving composition and pairing. This is the formal counterpart of Pocock's (1975) “languages of political thought,” Latour's (2005) actant translation, and Lakoff & Johnson's (1980) conceptual metaphor. Headline theorems: (i) the action axioms; (ii) pairing equivariance, formalizing “translation preserves resonance”; and (iii) commutation with Aufhebung, formalizing “the dialectic is intrinsic, not perspective-dependent.”

Transmission Chains

Theorem 7 formalizes a transmission chain as a sequence a₀ → a₁ → … → aₙ with bounded resonance loss at each step. This is the formal heir to Lovejoy's (1936) chains, Sperber's (1996) epidemiology of representations, and Dawkins's (1976) memetics. Headline theorems: (i) chain composition is associative; (ii) bounded total drift in terms of step-wise curvature; and (iii) convergence on equivalence classes, linking to Theorem 8.

Structural Equivalence

Theorem 8 defines an equivalence relation a ≃ b iff there exists a conjugation g with g·a·g⁻¹ = b preserving all pairings. This is the formal counterpart of Skinner's (1969) paraphrase criterion, Dennett's (1991) multiple drafts, and Goodman's (1978) ways of worldmaking. Headline theorems: (i) ≃ is an equivalence relation; (ii) quotient is again a graded monoid; and (iii) non-trivial classes exist, refuting any global reduction.

Graded Idea Algebra

Theorem 9 establishes that ideas form a graded algebra I = ⊕_{n≥0} I_n with composition respecting the grading additively. This is the formal heir to Kim's (1999) supervenience, Dawkins's (1976) gene-meme parallel, and the implicit hierarchy in Anderson (1972). Headline theorems: (i) grading additivity; (ii) compatibility with all preceding structure (pairing, Aufhebung, cocycle, conjugation); and (iii) the finite-generation criterion, used in Volume VI for advanced applications.

How the Bridge Is Maintained

The bridge is not a static document; it is kept in sync by a small set of machinery embedded in the project's repository. First, every Lean theorem under IdeaTheory/ carries a docstring citing the informal antecedent in Author Year form, with the citation keyed to references.bib. A continuous-integration check parses those docstrings and fails the build if any cited key is missing from the bibliography or if any bibliography entry is unused. Second, the project maintains a translation table at docs/_data/bridge.csv whose rows are exactly the cross-walk rows above; the HTML on this page is generated from that CSV, and each LaTeX chapter's “From Informal Claims to Formal Statements” table is generated from the same source, guaranteeing consistency across HTML, LaTeX, and Lean.

Third, every concept page under docs/concepts/ includes an In the Literature section whose anchor links resolve to rows of this page; a link-checker run on each pull request verifies that no concept page references a row that no longer exists. Fourth, a designated literature reviewer rotates per volume; when a volume's interpretive chapter is revised, the reviewer is responsible for confirming that any newly cited author appears in references.bib and any retired claim is either re-attached to a new theorem or moved to the Open Problems section below. Together these four mechanisms — docstrings, the CSV translation table, the link-checker, and the rotating reviewer — keep the bridge auditable as the formal corpus and the surrounding literature both evolve.

Open Problems Inherited From the Literature

The following problems are stated informally in the prior literature, are not currently theorems in this project, and we believe are tractable within the present framework.

  1. Curvature and ideology (Mannheim 1936; Bourdieu 1984): identify a class of social fields in which the curvature form κ is uniformly bounded, and show that bounded curvature predicts ideological stability.
  2. Cocycle classification (Fauconnier & Turner 2002): classify the cohomology classes of ε on the free graded idea monoid on two generators; conjecturally a single non-trivial class exists.
  3. Aufhebung uniqueness under degeneracy (Adorno 1966; Brandom 2019): characterize the failure locus of Theorem 3's uniqueness clause and connect it to Adorno's negative dialectics.
  4. Transmission half-life (Sperber 1996; Dawkins 1976): give a closed-form expression for the expected length of a transmission chain before structural equivalence is lost, in terms of step-wise curvature.
  5. Strong vs. weak emergence (Bedau 1997; Kim 1999): show that the cocycle ε distinguishes strong from weak emergence iff its cohomology class is non-torsion.
  6. Conjugation orbits and intellectual history (Skinner 1969; Pocock 1975): determine whether the conjugation action of Theorem 6 has finitely many orbits on a finitely generated idea algebra of a given period.
  7. Metaphor as conjugation (Lakoff & Johnson 1980): identify the subgroup of conjugations realized by attested conceptual metaphors and show it is proper.
  8. Grading and supervenience (Kim 1999): prove or refute that grading-respecting maps are exactly the supervenience-preserving maps.
  9. Worldmaking incommensurability (Goodman 1978): construct two structurally inequivalent ideas with identical resonance against every test idea, or prove no such pair exists.
  10. Memetic fitness landscapes (Dawkins 1976): formalize a fitness landscape as a linear functional on the graded idea algebra and connect its critical points to historically attested intellectual movements.
