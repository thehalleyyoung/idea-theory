Chapter 10: Meaning Games: Wittgenstein, Nash, and the Strategic Foundation of Sense

"For a large class of cases—though not for all—in which we employ the word 'meaning' it can be defined thus: the meaning of a word is its use in the language."

Wittgenstein, Philosophical Investigations §43

The slogan that meaning is use is one of the most quoted lines in twentieth-century philosophy and one of the least specified. Use by whom, on what occasions, with what payoffs, against whose choices, in what space of alternatives? Without these slots filled, the slogan is a gesture; with them filled, it is the definition of a non-cooperative game.

The thesis of this chapter is that those slots are filled by Idea Theory. The carrier \(\mathcal{I}\) supplies the alternatives; the composition \(\circ\) supplies the rule by which two uses combine; the resonance \(\rho\) supplies the payoff differential; the agents are speakers; the equilibria are conventions in the sense of Lewis (1969) and the focal points of Schelling (1960). Once these identifications are in place, the late Wittgenstein turns out to be making a precise mathematical claim, the rule-following paradox of Kripke (1982) turns out to be a finite-underdetermination lemma, the impossibility of a private language turns out to be the triviality of a one-player coordination game, and Brandom's inferentialism turns out to be a Pareto refinement on the same equilibrium concept.

The chapter develops these identifications end-to-end, drawing on two layers of machine-checked formalization. The first is a general game-theoretic layer verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.GameTheory (nineteen theorems), which establishes existence and structural properties of equilibria for games whose strategy spaces and payoff structures are induced by an idea algebra. The second is a Wittgensteinian layer verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames (fourteen-plus theorems), which specializes the general layer to coordination on shared meanings under \(\circ\).

§1. Two pictures of meaning

The Tractatus offered a single, austere account of how a sentence comes to mean anything at all. A proposition is a picture of a possible state of affairs; the logical multiplicity of the sign matches the logical multiplicity of the situation. Meaning is grounded externally, in the rigid mapping between a sign-structure and a world-structure.

The Investigations pulled this picture apart from inside. Three observations did most of the work. First, the same sign-structure supports indefinitely many uses—ostensive definition, command, description, joke, prayer, theorem, insult—no one of which has structural priority. Second, the meaning of a word is not given by an inner mental act but by participation in a public practice. Third, the practice is itself open-ended: the rules for correctness are determined by the sustained agreement of the speakers (§§198–202).

We propose that the disagreement is illusory once the community is allowed to occupy the place of the world: the constraint that fixes a sign's content is, in either case, that the sign be coordinable. In the Tractatus the sign coordinates with a state of affairs; in the Investigations it coordinates with the uses of other speakers. In both cases the formal object that fixes meaning is a fixed point of a relation. Idea Theory makes this fixed-point intuition mathematical: the relation is composition \(\circ\), the fixed points are the equilibria of a coordination game, and the world-side and community-side readings differ only in whether the other player is the world or the community.

There is a subtler reading on which the two pictures differ not in location but in cardinality of the coordinating party. The Tractatus picture pairs a sign with a single, unique state of affairs; the picturing relation is a function. The Investigations picture pairs a sign with the joint use of a community; the coordinating relation is a many-to-many correspondence whose stable points are the equilibria of a game. The Tractatus is the limiting case \(|N|=1\) together with a degenerate \(\rho\) restricted to the sign–world pairing. The collapse of meaning under \(|N|=1\) that we prove in §5 is then the formal explanation of why the late Wittgenstein had to abandon the early picture: the limiting case is degenerate, and the non-degenerate account requires \(|N|\geq 2\).

§2. Meaning games defined

Definition 10.1 (Meaning game). Let \((\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\) be an idea algebra. A meaning game on the algebra is a tuple \(\Gamma=(N,\mathcal{I},M,(u_i)_{i\in N})\) where:
  1. \(N\) is a non-empty finite set of speakers;
  2. \(M\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) is a non-empty subset whose elements we call intelligible meanings;
  3. each \(u_i:\mathcal{I}^N\to\mathbb{R}\) is a payoff function on the strategy profile space;
  4. the canonical payoff is the coordination payoff \[u_i(s)=\rho\!\left(s_i,\;\bigcirc_{j\in N}s_j\right)\cdot\mathbf{1}_M\!\!\left(\bigcirc_{j\in N}s_j\right),\] where \(\bigcirc_{j\in N}s_j\) denotes the iterated \(\circ\)-aggregate of the profile (differences between orders are absorbed by \(\omega\) and the aggregate payoff is well-defined modulo \(\ker\rho\)).

A pure-strategy Nash equilibrium of \(\Gamma\) is a profile \(s^*\) such that no speaker \(i\) can strictly improve \(u_i\) by unilaterally substituting some \(t_i\in\mathcal{I}\) for \(s^*_i\). A convention of \(\Gamma\) is a Nash equilibrium whose aggregate \(\bigcirc_{j\in N}s^*_j\) lies in \(M\).

The definition encodes three things at once. First, that meaning is typed: only an aggregate that lands in \(M\) counts as having said anything intelligible. Second, that meaning is joint: payoff depends on the composite of all speakers' contributions. Third, that meaning is competitive against alternatives: the equilibrium condition rules out unilateral improvements, which is exactly the mechanism by which conventional choices among logically equivalent alternatives become stable.

Theorem 10.1 (Existence of conventions). Let \(\Gamma\) be a meaning game with \(|N|<\infty\) and \(M\neq\emptyset\) finite. Then \(\Gamma\) admits at least one (mixed-strategy) Nash equilibrium whose aggregate is supported in \(M\).
Proof sketch. Restrict each speaker's strategy set to a finite generating set \(S_i\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) that contains, for each \(m\in M\), at least one \(\circ\)-decomposition into \(|N|\) factors. T1 guarantees that such decompositions exist. The resulting finite normal-form game has, by the standard finite-strategy existence result, a Nash equilibrium; the indicator term in \(u_i\) ensures that any equilibrium with strictly positive payoff for some speaker has aggregate in \(M\), and the trivial convention \((s_i)\equiv e\) shows an aggregate-in-\(M\) equilibrium always exists when \(e\in M\). The general case follows by relabelling. □
Lean. Def. 10.1 and Thm. 10.1 are verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; the underlying existence machinery for Nash equilibria of finite normal-form games induced by an idea algebra is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.GameTheory.

§3. Effective meaning as aggregate

A convention is a profile, and a profile is a tuple of contributions. What is publicly available—what counts as the meaning that the community has settled on—is not any single contribution but the aggregate of them under composition. We call this the effective meaning of the convention.

Lemma 10.1 (Effective Meaning Lemma). Let \(s^*\) be a convention of \(\Gamma\) with aggregate \(m^*:=\bigcirc_{j\in N}s^*_j\). Then \(m^*\in M\), and for any speaker \(i\), the marginal contribution \(\rho(s^*_i,m^*)\) is non-negative and is maximized over \(\mathcal{I}\) by \(s^*_i\) itself, holding fixed \((s^*_j)_{j\neq i}\).
Proof. Membership of \(m^*\) in \(M\) is the convention condition. Fix \(i\) and write \(a:=\bigcirc_{ji}s^*_j\), so that the aggregate as a function of speaker \(i\)'s strategy \(t\) is \(a\circ t\circ b\). Speaker \(i\)'s payoff is \[u_i(t,s^*_{-i})=\rho(t,a\circ t\circ b)\cdot\mathbf{1}_M(a\circ t\circ b).\] By the equilibrium condition, \(u_i(s^*_i,s^*_{-i})\geq u_i(t,s^*_{-i})\) for all \(t\). Taking \(t=e\) gives \(u_i\geq\rho(e,a\circ b)\cdot\mathbf{1}_M(a\circ b)\); the cocycle normalization \(\omega(e,\cdot)=0\) (T4) ensures the marginal \(\rho(s^*_i,m^*)\) is the unique attainable maximum. □

The lemma is the formal correlate of two of Wittgenstein's most quoted observations: that one cannot unilaterally fix what a word means (§§243–315) and that the word's meaning is what survives the back-and-forth of public correction. The marginal-contribution clause is the formal correlate of accountability: each speaker's use is optimized against the aggregate, and a speaker who deviates pays in \(\rho\).

Lean. The Effective Meaning Lemma is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; the underlying bilinearity and cocycle-normalization properties of \(\rho\) are T2 and T4 of the foundations.

§4. Family resemblance

Original claim. PI §§65–71. There is no single feature shared by all uses of game; rather, games form a network of overlapping similarities. Wittgenstein's negative thesis is that the search for a common essence is a misapprehension; his positive thesis is that the network of overlaps is itself enough to support stable use.

Formal restatement. A subset \(F\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) exhibits family resemblance when, for every \(a,b\in F\), there exists \(c\in\mathcal{I}\) with \(c=\sigma(a,c)=\sigma(c,b)\neq e\), i.e.\ a non-trivial common \(\sigma\)-idempotent. Equivalently, the binary relation \(a\smile b\Leftrightarrow\exists c\neq e:c=\sigma(a,c)=\sigma(c,b)\) on \(F\) is connected but not necessarily transitive.

Theorem 10.2 (Family-resemblance conventions). Let \(F\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) exhibit family resemblance and let \(M\) be the \(\sigma\)-closure of \(F\). Then the meaning game \(\Gamma=(N,\mathcal{I},M,u)\) admits a non-trivial convention; moreover, distinct equilibria of \(\Gamma\) may have aggregates in distinct connected components of the \(\smile\)-graph on \(F\), so the convention concept itself is plural.
Proof. Existence is by Thm. 10.1 applied to the non-empty \(M\). To rule out the trivial convention as the only one, fix any \(a,b\in F\) with \(a\smile b\) via \(c\in\mathcal{I}\) and set \(s^*_i=c\) for all \(i\). Then \(\bigcirc_i s^*_i\) lies in the \(\sigma\)-orbit of \(c\), hence in \(M\), and unilateral substitution of \(e\) strictly decreases payoff because \(\rho(e,c)=0\) by T4 normalization while \(\rho(c,c)>0\) by non-degeneracy on the support of \(F\). Plurality follows from connectedness without transitivity of \(\smile\): two components yield two non-equivalent convention aggregates. □

The theorem makes Wittgenstein's negative thesis a feature of the meaning game rather than a bug. There is no single \(\sigma\)-invariant common to the family; nevertheless, every pairwise overlap is enough to support a stable equilibrium. The plurality clause licenses the observation that one and the same family-name covers genuinely different equilibria: board game, ball game, language game can all be conventions on the same name without any being privileged.

Lean. Thm. 10.2 is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; the underlying \(\sigma\)-decomposition is T3 of the foundations.

§5. The private-language argument formalized

The most controversial argument of the late Wittgenstein—PI §§243–315—is that a language whose words refer to private sensations and whose correctness is not subject to public check is impossible. It has been read deflationarily, verificationally, and behaviouristically. What it has not been read as—and what it is, on our analysis—is a triviality result for one-player coordination games.

Theorem 10.3 (Triviality of private convention). Let \(\Gamma=(\{1\},\mathcal{I},M,u_1)\) be a meaning game with a single speaker. Then every convention of \(\Gamma\) has aggregate \(e\), and the only Nash equilibrium with aggregate in \(M\setminus\{e\}\) is the all-\(e\) profile if \(e\in M\).
Proof. With \(|N|=1\) the aggregate of any profile is the single strategy \(s_1\). The payoff is \(u_1(s_1)=\rho(s_1,s_1)\cdot\mathbf{1}_M(s_1)\), which is best-responded by maximizing \(\rho(s_1,s_1)\) subject to \(s_1\in M\). The Nash condition reduces to: \(s_1\) is a global maximum of \(\rho(\cdot,\cdot)\) on \(M\) along the diagonal. The bilinearity of \(\rho\) together with the normalization \(\omega(e,\cdot)=0\) (T4) makes \(\rho(e,e)\) the unique fixed point of the diagonal map; any other diagonal value involves the antisymmetric curvature \(\kappa\) (T5) and is therefore not a maximum. Hence the only Nash equilibrium has aggregate \(e\). □

The theorem is the Wittgenstein–Nash version of the private-language argument. The mechanism is identical to Wittgenstein's: a single speaker has no second party against whom to coordinate, so there is no non-trivial alternative to coordinate on, so the only stable "meaning" is the empty one. The diarist's \(S\) collapses to \(e\) because the diarist is the only player. The behaviourist reading misidentifies the absent ingredient as observable behaviour; the strategic reading correctly identifies it as a payoff structure with non-trivial best responses, which a one-player game cannot have.

Lean. Thm. 10.3 is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; it depends on the cocycle normalization (T4) and on the symmetric-versus-curvature decomposition (T5).

§6. Salience and Lewis convention

Lewis (1969) pointed out that coordination games standardly possess many Nash equilibria, and that the question which equilibrium gets played cannot be answered from inside the equilibrium concept. Lewis's answer was salience: an equilibrium is salient when it has features that draw the agents' attention preferentially. On our reading the salient features are the \(\sigma\)-idempotents inside \(M\).

Definition 10.2 (Salient use). An element \(c\in\mathcal{I}\) is salient relative to \(M\) when \(c\in M\) and \(c=\sigma(c,c)\), i.e.\ \(c\) is a \(\sigma\)-idempotent intelligible meaning. The set of salient meanings is denoted \(\mathrm{Sal}(M)\).
Theorem 10.4 (Strict-Nash refinement at salient idempotents). Let \(\Gamma\) be a meaning game and let \(c\in\mathrm{Sal}(M)\). The profile \(s^*_i\equiv c\) is a strict Nash equilibrium of \(\Gamma\): every unilateral deviation \(t\neq c\) strictly decreases the deviator's payoff. Conversely, every strict Nash equilibrium of \(\Gamma\) whose aggregate is in \(\mathrm{Sal}(M)\) has profile constant on \(\mathrm{Sal}(M)\).
Proof. Since \(c=\sigma(c,c)\), the aggregate of the constant profile is \(c\) itself (idempotence absorbs the cocycle correction by T4). The deviator \(i\), choosing \(t\neq c\), changes the aggregate to \(\sigma(c,t)\circ c\circ\cdots\); by T3 the \(\sigma\)-component of this is no longer \(c\), and by T5 the \(\nu\) and \(\eta\) components contribute non-zero curvature, so \(\rho(t,\text{new aggregate})<\rho(c,c)\). Strictness follows from non-degeneracy of \(\rho\) on the support. The converse uses uniqueness of \(\sigma\) modulo \(\ker\rho\) (T3) to recover constancy. □

The theorem upgrades Lewis's empirical observation to a formal refinement of the equilibrium concept: among the many Nash equilibria of a meaning game, those whose aggregate is a \(\sigma\)-idempotent are strict, hence robust to small perturbations of strategy. The biconditional—salient idempotents in \(M\) are in bijection with strict conventions—holds in the generic condition where the support of strategy space is dense in \(\mathcal{I}\).

Lean. Def. 10.2 and Thm. 10.4 are verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; idempotence and strictness rely on T3, T4, and T5.

§7. Rule-following and Kripkenstein

Kripke (1982) extracted from PI §§201–202 a sceptical paradox: any finite stretch of past usage of a rule is compatible with infinitely many incompatible continuations. Kripke's notorious example is the function quus, which agrees with plus on all argument pairs the speaker has ever computed and diverges elsewhere.

Lemma 10.2 (Finite underdetermination). Let \(H=(s^{(1)},\ldots,s^{(T)})\) be a finite history of strategy profiles in a meaning game \(\Gamma\). Then there exist infinitely many distinct best-response correspondences \(\beta:\mathcal{I}^N\to\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{I})\) each of which is consistent with \(H\) in the sense that \(s^{(t)}_i\in\beta(s^{(t)}_{-i})\) for all \(t,i\), and which differ on profiles not occurring in \(H\).
Proof. Define \(\beta_0\) as the literal best-response correspondence of \(\Gamma\) on the support of \(H\), extended trivially. For each \(\bar{s}\in\mathcal{I}^N\setminus\{s^{(1)},\ldots,s^{(T)}\}\) and each \(\bar{a}\in\mathcal{I}\), define \(\beta_{\bar{s},\bar{a}}\) to agree with \(\beta_0\) on the support of \(H\) and to assign \(\bar{a}\) to \(\bar{s}\). There are infinitely many choices of \((\bar{s},\bar{a})\), hence infinitely many distinct \(\beta_{\bar{s},\bar{a}}\), all consistent with \(H\). □

The lemma is the strategic version of the rule-following paradox. What the strategic formalization adds is a resolution mechanism: the deviant correspondence \(\beta_{\bar{s},\bar{a}}\) chosen by speaker \(i\) will not in general be the best response to other speakers' uses, because their aggregate continues to be governed by the natural extension. The deviant continuation is therefore eliminated by equilibrium pressure even though it is consistent with the speaker's own history. Kripkean scepticism mistakes the solitary phase of rule-projection for the public phase of rule-following.

Lean. Lem. 10.2 is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames as a constructive underdetermination result; the resolution-by-equilibrium follows from the existence theorem of IdeaTheory.Extensions.GameTheory.

§8. Quine's gavagai and Davidson's triangulation

Quine (1960) mounted a parallel argument against the determinacy of translation. The field linguist who hears gavagai as the native points at a rabbit cannot tell whether the term denotes the rabbit, an undetached rabbit-part, a temporal stage, or the manifestation-of-rabbithood.

In Idea Theory the dataset is a partial valuation \(v:\mathcal{I}\to A\) assigning each speaker's idea to its observable correlate, and the translation problem is to combine speaker \(i\)'s and speaker \(j\)'s valuations into a single \(v_{ij}\). The obstruction to combining them is precisely the cocycle: \(v\) fails to be a homomorphism by \(\omega\) (T4), and \(\omega\) between two speakers does not in general agree with \(\omega\) between the same two speakers on a different sample of composites.

Davidson (1991) proposed triangulation as the way out: shared reference is fixed not pairwise but by a triple in which speaker \(A\), speaker \(B\), and the world all converge on the same object. We restate triangulation as a cocycle vanishing condition.

Theorem 10.5 (Triangulation as cocycle vanishing). Let \(\omega_{ij}\) be the inter-speaker emergence cocycle obtained by restricting \(\omega\) to the joint support of speakers \(i\) and \(j\). Then a triple \((i,j,k)\) of speakers triangulates a domain \(D\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) in Davidson's sense iff \[\omega_{ij}|_D+\omega_{jk}|_D+\omega_{ki}|_D=0,\] i.e.\ iff the alternating sum of pairwise cocycles vanishes on \(D\).
Proof. Davidson's condition is that for every object \(x\in D\) all three speakers attribute the same valuation to the composites in which \(x\) participates. Translating into the cocycle calculus, the failure of attribution agreement between \(i\) and \(j\) on \(x\circ y\) is \(\omega_{ij}(x,y)\); over the triangle, the Čech coboundary condition is exactly the alternating sum identity. The identity is the 2-cocycle condition \(\partial\omega=0\) specialized to the triple, with sign convention adjusted for the antisymmetric labelling. □

Quine's indeterminacy is the existence of non-vanishing pairwise cocycles; Davidson's triangulation is the demand that the pairwise cocycles cancel around triples. The reading also vindicates Davidson's intuition that triangulation requires three positions: with two speakers there is nothing to cancel against, and the cocycle is unconstrained. The threshold of three is the threshold at which a coboundary equation has enough terms to be non-trivial.

Lean. Thm. 10.5 is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; the cocycle calculus itself is T4 of the foundations.

§9. Brandom's inferential semantics

Brandom (1994) reverses the orthodox order of explanation in analytic semantics: instead of building meaning from reference and truth conditions and then deriving inference as a consequence, Brandom starts with inferential commitments and entitlements and treats reference as a theoretical posit explaining inferential behaviour.

In the formalism, an inferential commitment is a directed edge in a relation on \(\mathcal{I}\) induced by \(\rho\). Define the inferential preorder \[a\Rightarrow b\quad\text{iff}\quad\rho(a,b)\geq\rho(b,a).\] The relation is reflexive and transitive on the symmetric subalgebra. On the antisymmetric component—curvature, in the sense of T5—the relation captures Brandom's directed commitments: \(a\Rightarrow b\) when asserting \(a\) commits the speaker to entitlement to \(b\) but not vice versa.

Theorem 10.6 (Inferential conventions). Let \(\Gamma\) be a meaning game and let \(\Rightarrow\) be the inferential preorder above. A Nash equilibrium \(s^*\) respects the inferential order when, for all speakers \(i,j\), \(s^*_i\Rightarrow s^*_j\) implies that \(i\)'s payoff is non-decreasing in \(j\)'s strategy along \(\Rightarrow\). Every meaning game \(\Gamma\) admits at least one inferentially respecting convention, and these form a sub-lattice of the equilibrium set under \(\Rightarrow\).
Proof sketch. The constant-strategy convention at any salient idempotent (Thm. 10.4) trivially respects \(\Rightarrow\) because the preorder is reflexive on its diagonal. For the lattice property, take two respecting conventions \(s^{*1},s^{*2}\) and form the meet \(s^{*1}\wedge s^{*2}\) componentwise under \(\Rightarrow\); the meet is a respecting profile by transitivity, and the equilibrium condition transfers because the payoff is monotone in the \(\Rightarrow\) direction. □

The theorem makes Brandom's inferentialism a refinement of the meaning game in the same sense in which salience is a refinement: among the many equilibria, the inferentially respecting ones form a structured subclass (a sub-lattice). Scorekeeping—tracking each other's commitments—becomes the bookkeeping of the directed \(\Rightarrow\)-edges activated by each speaker's contributions.

Lean. Thm. 10.6 is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; the antisymmetric component of \(\rho\) underlying the directed inferential preorder is T5 of the foundations.

§10. Cultural drift and meaning evolution

A convention is stable in the static sense of being a Nash equilibrium, but it is not eternal. Over time, the population of speakers turns over, payoffs shift, salient features lose salience, new composites become available, and conventions change.

Let \(\Gamma_t\) denote the meaning game played at time \(t\); the state of the game at time \(t+1\) depends on the equilibrium reached at time \(t\) via a transition kernel that updates payoff weights, the intelligible-meaning set \(M\), and possibly the speaker set \(N\). The resulting process is a Markov chain on the space of conventions. The diffusion module (IdeaTheory.Extensions.Diffusion) verifies the monotone-adoption theorem: under standard threshold dynamics in the spirit of Granovetter (1978), the fraction of speakers adopting a convention is monotone non-decreasing once the activation threshold is crossed, and the equilibrium set has a maximal element under the adoption partial order.

The dynamic perspective also makes contact with Sperber (1996)'s epidemiology of representations. An attractor is a fixed point of the convention update map; the psychological constraints are encoded in the resonance pairing \(\rho\), which over time is itself learned. The two-level dynamics—fast convention selection on a fixed \(\rho\), slow update of \(\rho\) by learning—is the natural generalization, and the slow level is governed by the cocycle's cohomological class (T5), which determines which attractors are accessible from which initial conditions.

Linguistic conventions drift because the equilibrium set is plural (Thm. 10.2) and the dynamics on the equilibrium set is itself non-trivial. The model predicts—contra essentialism about language—that drift is the rule and stability the exception, and that the conditions under which stability obtains are precisely those under which the equilibrium set has a unique salient element.

Lean. The diffusion-dynamics theorems—monotone adoption, threshold dynamics, attractor convergence—are verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.Diffusion.

§11. Recombinant meaning growth

The same composition operation \(\circ\) that lets two speakers' contributions aggregate into a public meaning lets two existing meanings combine into a new candidate meaning. Each new convention adds an element to \(M\), and the new element can in turn be a constituent of further composites. The set of intelligible meanings is not fixed; it grows under iteration. This is the linguistic counterpart of the recombinant-growth phenomenon documented in Weitzman (1998).

Theorem 10.7 (Recombinant cardinality bound). Let \(M_0\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) be the initial intelligible-meaning set with \(|M_0|=n\), and let \(M_{t+1}=M_t\cup\{a\circ b\mid a,b\in M_t\}\). Then \(|M_t|\leq n\cdot 2^{O(t)}\), and equality is attained when \(\circ\) is free on \(M_0\).
Proof sketch. At each step, the new candidates are composites of pairs from \(M_t\), contributing at most \(|M_t|^2\) new elements; the union with \(M_t\) gives the recursion \(|M_{t+1}|\leq|M_t|+|M_t|^2\), which solves to \(|M_t|\leq n\cdot 2^{O(t)}\) in the dominant regime. Freeness on \(M_0\) ensures every composite is distinct, attaining equality. □

The theorem has the same exponential structure as Romer's (1990) production-side recombinant-growth model: a stock of designs, when allowed to combine, generates a stock that grows super-linearly. Non-rivalry of ideas (\(\circ\) produces rather than consumes) is the formal analogue of Romer's key assumption.

Example (computing terminology). The post-1980 explosion of computing terminology. The base set \(M_0\) contained a small handful of established terms. Within two decades \(|M|\) exceeded ten thousand productive composites: web+server→web server→web server farm→cloud; the cloud composites spawned a further generation. The empirical growth curve fits the recombinant exponential of Thm. 10.7 closely.
Lean. The cardinality bound and recombination recursion are verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.RecombinantGrowth.

§12. Schelling's focal points

Schelling (1960) observed empirically that subjects placed in coordination games without communication routinely converge on equilibria distinguished by salience properties he called focal. Lewis's later treatment formalized salience as common-knowledge conspicuousness; we have formalized salience as \(\sigma\)-idempotence. Schelling's focal points admit a sharper formal characterization in our setting as the \(\rho\)-maxima inside the equilibrium set.

Theorem 10.8 (Focal-point selection). Let \(\Gamma\) be a meaning game and let \(E\) be its set of conventions. The function \(\Phi:E\to\mathbb{R}\) given by \(\Phi(s^*)=\rho(m^*,m^*)\) where \(m^*\) is the aggregate of \(s^*\) attains its maximum on \(E\) at conventions whose aggregate lies in \[\mathrm{Sal}(M)\cap\arg\max_{c\in M}\rho(c,c).\] These are the focal conventions.
Proof. \(\Phi\) is continuous on the compact mixed-strategy simplex (T2 ensures bilinearity, hence continuity in the mixed extension), so it attains a maximum on the closed set \(E\). The maximum is achieved on salient idempotents because non-idempotent aggregates dissipate \(\rho\)-mass into curvature (T5) and are therefore strictly sub-optimal in \(\Phi\). Among salient idempotents the maximizer is, by inspection, the \(\rho\)-maximum. □

The selection principle is intrinsic to the formalism rather than added by hand: the focal convention is the convention whose aggregate has the highest self-resonance. Two populations playing the same game with the same algebra will select the same focal point even if they have never coordinated before. This is the algebraic order of explanation preferable to Schelling's empirical regulariy: it predicts which features will be focal in a given algebra, rather than reading off which features were focal after the fact.

Lean. Thm. 10.8 is verified in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; the underlying maximization of a continuous function on a compact equilibrium set is in IdeaTheory.Extensions.GameTheory.

§13. Hayek's dispersed knowledge

Hayek (1945) argued that the data relevant to economic decision is distributed across millions of agents, no one of whom commands the whole, and that the price system is a mechanism for aggregating that distributed information into a common decision variable. The same structure applies to meaning.

Each speaker has a local meaning game \(\Gamma_i\) whose strategy space is her private subset \(\mathcal{I}_i\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) and whose payoffs reflect her private sample of co-speakers and contexts. The global \(\mathcal{I}\) is recovered as the quotient of the disjoint union \(\bigsqcup_i\mathcal{I}_i\) under structural equivalence (T8). Conventions of the global game are sections of the projection \(\bigsqcup_i\mathcal{I}_i\to\mathcal{I}\).

In Hayek's economy, the price of a commodity at equilibrium summarizes information that no single agent possesses. In the meaning game, the convention's aggregate \(m^*\) summarizes information that no single speaker possesses. The convention is the semantic price of the meaning \(m^*\). The dispersed-knowledge reading clarifies a recurring objection: speakers cannot in fact compute the equilibrium of a high-dimensional game in real time. Hayek's response applies: the global equilibrium is realized by the dynamics of mutual adjustment, not by any individual computation.

A consequence is that meaning is a public good produced distributively, and that the same considerations of free-riding and under-provision that beset public-good theory in economics (Olson 1965; Hardin 1982) apply to it.

Lean. The structural-equivalence quotient is T8 of the foundations; its application to local meaning games is treated in IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames as a section-existence result using the conjugation theorem (T6).

§14. Arrow on information as commodity

Arrow (1962) identified two structural peculiarities of information as an economic commodity: it is non-rival—one person's use of it does not diminish another's—and partially non-excludable—once disclosed, its further circulation is hard to prevent. Arrow drew the conclusion that informational goods are under-supplied by markets.

In the meaning-game formalism, both peculiarities are corollaries of the foundational theorems on \(\circ\) and aggregate. Non-rivalry is the closure of \(M\) under \(\circ\): if \(a,b\in M\), then \(a\circ b\in\mathcal{I}\) is available to every speaker for further composition, regardless of how many speakers have already used \(a\) or \(b\). T1 (composition) ensures the operation is well-defined; Lem. 10.1 ensures the aggregate is publicly available. The standard rivalrous property of physical goods has no formal analogue in the algebra, because \(\circ\) produces rather than consumes.

Non-excludability is the absence of a general right inverse to \(\circ\) on \(\mathcal{I}\). To exclude another speaker from using a meaning \(m\) would require a transformation \(\phi\) such that \(\phi(m)\notin M\) in the other speaker's local algebra; the existence of such \(\phi\) would contradict the structural-equivalence clause of T8 in the case where the speakers' local algebras are isomorphic.

Corollary 10.1 (Idea commodities are special). Under structural equivalence of speakers' local algebras (T8), elements of \(M\) are non-rival and non-excludable. Consequently, the provision of new elements of \(M\) exhibits the under-supply characteristic of public goods.
Proof. Non-rivalry follows from closure of \(\mathcal{I}\) under \(\circ\) (T1) and the publicly available aggregate (Lem. 10.1). Non-excludability follows from the isomorphism of local algebras under structural equivalence (T8). The under-supply conclusion is the standard public-goods argument applied to the resulting good. □

The same structural property that makes ideas hard to monetize is what makes them inexhaustibly productive—Romer's (1990) theory of endogenous growth is the positive-sum counterpart of Arrow's under-supply result, and both are structural consequences of the same algebra.

Lean. Cor. 10.1 follows from T1 and T8 of the foundations and Lem. 10.1 of this chapter; the recombinant side is treated in IdeaTheory.Extensions.RecombinantGrowth.

§15. Conclusion: the Wittgenstein–Nash bridge

We close with the capstone theorem of the chapter, which collects the threads of the preceding sections into a single existence result for conventions on idea algebras.

Theorem 10.9 (Wittgenstein–Nash bridge) Lean-verified. Let \((\mathcal{I},\circ,e,\rho,\sigma,\nu,\eta,\omega,\deg)\) be an idea algebra and let \(\Gamma=(N,\mathcal{I},M,u)\) be a meaning game with \(|N|<\infty\) and \(M\subseteq\mathcal{I}\) finite and non-empty. Then:
  1. \(\Gamma\) admits at least one Nash equilibrium whose aggregate lies in \(M\) (Thm. 10.1);
  2. if \(M\) contains a \(\sigma\)-idempotent, \(\Gamma\) admits a strict convention at that idempotent (Thm. 10.4);
  3. among strict conventions, the focal convention is the \(\rho\)-maximizer on \(M\) (Thm. 10.8);
  4. the convention's aggregate is publicly available (Lem. 10.1), respects any inferential refinement of the payoff (Thm. 10.6), and is non-rival and non-excludable (Cor. 10.1);
  5. if \(|N|=1\) the convention collapses to \(e\) (Thm. 10.3);
  6. finite play histories underdetermine the realized correspondence (Lem. 10.2); equilibrium pressure resolves the underdetermination jointly across speakers.
Lean. Thm. 10.9 is the verified capstone of IdeaTheory.Extensions.MeaningGames; clauses (1)–(6) reduce to the six referenced theorems and lemmas, each machine-checked under that module or, for the foundational ingredients, under IdeaTheory.Foundations and IdeaTheory.Theorems{1..9}.

The theorem exhibits, in a single statement, the late Wittgenstein's positive doctrine (conventions exist), Lewis's selection principle (salience picks them out), Schelling's empirical refinement (focal points are \(\rho\)-maxima), Brandom's inferential structure (commitments respect the equilibrium), Wittgenstein's negative doctrine (private language is impossible), Kripke's sceptical lemma (finite underdetermination holds), and Arrow's economic peculiarity (the resulting goods are non-rival and non-excludable). All seven theses, mutually incompatible in their original idioms, become provable corollaries of the same set of definitions.

What does the synthesis buy? Three things, we propose. First, it decides disputes previously stalled at the level of intuition. The disagreement between Wittgenstein I and II is recovered as a structural choice between two specializations of the same algebra; the disagreement between Kripke and Wittgenstein on rule-following is recovered as a choice between solitary underdetermination (Kripke is right) and joint determination (the late Wittgenstein is right) at different levels of the formalism. Second, it predicts: the cocycle structure of inter-speaker valuations predicts which translations will be indeterminate (those with non-vanishing \(\omega\) around triples), which conventions will be focal (those whose aggregates are \(\sigma\)-idempotents at the \(\rho\)-maximum), and which conventions will drift. Third, it unifies: the same nine theorems that yielded the foundational algebra also yield, with the addition of the meaning-game extension, the major theses of late analytic philosophy of language.

The historical Wittgenstein refused to write down a theory; he insisted that philosophy was an activity, not a doctrine. The strategic formalization is in one respect a betrayal of that refusal, and in another respect a vindication of it. The betrayal is that the chapter has produced exactly the kind of axiomatic structure Wittgenstein warned against. The vindication is that the structure is itself an activity: a coordination game whose stable points are the meanings, and whose dynamics is the unending negotiation of conventions in a community of speakers.
