Chapter 1: The Idea Monoid

In which the bare combinatorial skeleton of Idea Theory is laid down. We isolate three axioms — associativity and a two-sided unit — which we defend on independent philosophical grounds; we show that they suffice to derive a full apparatus of powers, chains, sub-ideas, idempotents, cancellation, and invertibility; and we prove the three headline theorems and four corollaries that organize the rest of the volume. Every result of this chapter is machine-verified in the Lean 4 module IdeaTheory.Theorems1. The chapter closes by showing that the bare monoid already suffices to host four classical positions on the nature of ideas: those of Locke, Hume, Frege, and the later Wittgenstein.

1. The axioms of an idea algebra

The whole edifice of Idea Theory rests on three axioms. We isolate them here, before introducing any further analytic structure, in order to make clear which results require nothing more than the bare monoid skeleton.

Definition 1.1 (Idea algebra; bare layer). A bare idea algebra is a triple \(\IdMon\) in which \(\mathcal{I}\) is a set (the set of ideas), \(\circ : \mathcal{I} \times \mathcal{I} \to \mathcal{I}\) is a binary operation (composition), and \(e \in \mathcal{I}\) is a distinguished element (the void idea, or unit), subject to the following three axioms.
  • (A1) Associativity. \((a \circ b) \circ c = a \circ (b \circ c)\) for all \(a,b,c \in \mathcal{I}\).
  • (A2) Left unit. \(e \circ a = a\) for all \(a \in \mathcal{I}\).
  • (A3) Right unit. \(a \circ e = a\) for all \(a \in \mathcal{I}\).

The mathematical content of Definition 1.1 is exactly that of a monoid in the sense of MacLane[5] or Bourbaki[6]; what is new is the interpretation we attach to it.

Why associativity (A1). Associativity is the algebraic shadow of bracket-independence of content. When two ideas \(a\) and \(b\) combine, the result \(a \circ b\) is itself an idea; if a third idea \(c\) is then brought into contact, we obtain \((a \circ b) \circ c\). Yet the same three ideas could equally have been combined in the order \(a\), then \(b \circ c\), producing \(a \circ (b \circ c)\). Axiom (A1) declares that these two ideas are identical, not merely equivalent. The empirical pull behind (A1) is strong: Hume's principle of association[2] is most naturally read as a non-bracketed enumeration; Frege's compositionality[3] requires that sense depend only on parts and their arrangement; Hegel's repeated Aufhebungen and Sperber's chains of cultural attractors all presuppose that bracketing is invisible at the level of content.

We do not assume commutativity. "Newtonian mechanics composed with quantum mechanics" is a different idea from "quantum mechanics composed with Newtonian mechanics."

Why a two-sided unit (A2–A3). The void idea \(e\) models the empty thought — the contentless idea that, when prepended or appended to any other, leaves it unchanged. Every model we shall consider possesses a natural "do nothing" element: in Locke's combinatorial picture[1] it is the trivial complex idea built from no simple ideas at all; in modern semantic-vector models it is the zero context vector. The unit is harmless: if a candidate carrier \(\mathcal{I}'\) has no unit we may freely adjoin one.

Remark. We refer to the structure \(\IdMon\) as an idea algebra rather than an idea monoid for two reasons. First, later chapters extend it with additional algebraic data; the resulting object is no longer a monoid in any standard sense. Second, the philosophical literature uses the word "algebra" loosely, and our use is intended to evoke the entire stack of structure. In this chapter "algebra" should be read as "algebra-with-only-the-bare-monoid-layer."
Notation. Throughout this chapter the symbols \(a, b, c, x, y, z, p, q\) denote arbitrary elements of \(\mathcal{I}\); \(m, n, k\) denote natural numbers; \(e\) is always the unit of \(\IdMon\). We write \(a \circ b \circ c\) for \((a \circ b) \circ c\) (which equals \(a \circ (b \circ c)\) by (A1)), and we write \(a^n\) for the \(n\)-fold composition \(\mathsf{npow}(n,a)\) defined in §3.

2. Uniqueness of the unit and basic congruence lemmas

The first non-trivial fact about \(\IdMon\) is that the void idea is unique as a two-sided unit.

Lemma 1.1 (Uniqueness of two-sided units). Let \(e' \in \mathcal{I}\) satisfy \(e' \circ a = a\) and \(a \circ e' = a\) for every \(a \in \mathcal{I}\). Then \(e' = e\).

By the left-unit hypothesis at \(a = e\) we have \(e' \circ e = e\). By (A3) applied to \(e'\) we have \(e' \circ e = e'\). Equating the two right-hand sides yields \(e' = e\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.ident_unique_two_sided.)

Lemma 1.2 (Left units are units). If \(e' \in \mathcal{I}\) satisfies \(e' \circ a = a\) for every \(a\), then \(e' = e\). Symmetrically for right units.

Take \(a = e\) in the hypothesis to get \(e' \circ e = e\). By (A3), \(e' \circ e = e'\), so \(e' = e\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.left_ident_eq_ident and right_ident_eq_ident.)

Lemma 1.3 (Left congruence). If \(b = c\), then \(a \circ b = a \circ c\) for every \(a\).
Lemma 1.4 (Right congruence). If \(b = c\), then \(b \circ a = c \circ a\) for every \(a\).
Lemma 1.5 (Bilinear congruence). If \(a = c\) and \(b = d\), then \(a \circ b = c \circ d\).

All three lemmas are immediate from the substitution principle. (Verified as IdeaTheory.op_left_congr, op_right_congr, op_congr.)

Lemma 1.6 (Stability of \(e\) under self-composition). \(e \circ e = e\).

Take \(a = e\) in (A2). (Verified as IdeaTheory.op_ident_ident.)

Lemma 1.6 foreshadows the more general fact, proved in §5, that \(e\) is the canonical example of an idempotent idea.

3. Powers of an idea

The first construction made possible by the bare monoid axioms is iteration. This is the formal correlate of repeated metaphor (Hume on the strengthening of association by repetition), liturgical recitation, repeated theorem application, or repeated activation of a neural ensemble.

Definition 1.2 (Iterated composition). The \(n\)-th power of an idea \(a \in \mathcal{I}\), written \(a^n\) or \(\mathsf{npow}(n,a)\), is defined by recursion on \(n\): \[a^0 := e, \qquad a^{n+1} := a \circ a^n.\]
Lemma 1.7 (Elementary identities for \(\mathsf{npow}\)). For every \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) and \(n \in \mathbb{N}\): \[\begin{align} a^0 &= e, \\ a^{n+1} &= a \circ a^n, \\ a^1 &= a, \\ e^n &= e. \end{align}\]

The first two are immediate from Definition 1.2. For \(a^1\): \(a^1 = a \circ a^0 = a \circ e = a\) by (A3). For \(e^n\): induct on \(n\); the base case \(n=0\) is the first identity; for the inductive step, \(e^{n+1} = e \circ e^n = e\) by (A2) and the inductive hypothesis. (Verified as IdeaTheory.npow_zero, npow_succ, npow_one, npow_ident.)

Lemma 1.8 (Right-unwinding of powers). \(a^{n+1} = a^n \circ a\) for every \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) and \(n \in \mathbb{N}\).

Induction on \(n\). For \(n=0\): \(a^1 = a \circ e = a = e \circ a = a^0 \circ a\). For the inductive step, \(a^{k+2} = a \circ a^{k+1} = a \circ (a^k \circ a) = (a \circ a^k) \circ a = a^{k+1} \circ a\), where the second equality uses the inductive hypothesis and the third uses (A1). (Verified as IdeaTheory.npow_succ'.)

Lemma 1.9 (Self-commutativity of powers). For every \(a\) and \(k\), \(a \circ a^k = a^k \circ a\).

Both expressions equal \(a^{k+1}\): the left-hand side by definition, the right-hand side by Lemma 1.8. (Verified as IdeaTheory.npow_comm_self.)

Lemma 1.10 (Additivity in the exponent). For every \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) and every \(m, n \in \mathbb{N}\): \[a^{m+n} = a^m \circ a^n.\]

Induct on \(m\). For \(m=0\) both sides equal \(a^n\). Assuming the claim at \(m=k\): \[a^{(k+1)+n} = a^{(k+n)+1} = a \circ a^{k+n} = a \circ (a^k \circ a^n) = (a \circ a^k) \circ a^n = a^{k+1} \circ a^n.\] (Verified as IdeaTheory.npow_add.)

Lemma 1.11 (Multiplicativity in the exponent). For every \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) and every \(m, n \in \mathbb{N}\): \[a^{mn} = (a^n)^m.\]

Induct on \(m\). For \(m=0\): \((a^n)^0 = e = a^0\). Assuming at \(m=k\): \((k+1)n = n + kn\), so \(a^{(k+1)n} = a^{n+kn} = a^n \circ a^{kn} = a^n \circ (a^n)^k = (a^n)^{k+1}\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.npow_mul.)

Together, Lemmas 1.10 and 1.11 say that for each fixed idea \(a\), the function \(n \mapsto a^n\) is a monoid homomorphism from \((\mathbb{N}, +, 0)\) into \(\IdMon\). These facts are packaged as Theorem 1.3 below.

4. Chains

Iteration treats the same idea applied \(n\) times. More generally we wish to compose a finite sequence of (possibly distinct) ideas. The resulting construction — the chain of a list of ideas — is the bridge between the algebraic apparatus and the empirical phenomena of discourse, narrative, and cultural transmission.

Definition 1.3 (Chain composite). For a finite list \(L = [a_1, a_2, \dots, a_k]\) of ideas, the chain composite \(\mathsf{Ch}(L)\) is defined recursively by \[\mathsf{Ch}([]) := e, \qquad \mathsf{Ch}(a :: L) := a \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L).\] The length of \(L\), written \(|L|\), is its number of entries. The void chain of length \(n\) is the list \(e^{::n}\) consisting of \(n\) copies of \(e\).
Lemma 1.12 (Singleton and constant chains). For every \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) and \(n \in \mathbb{N}\): \[\mathsf{Ch}([a]) = a, \qquad \mathsf{Ch}(\underbrace{[a,\ldots,a]}_{n}) = a^n, \qquad \mathsf{Ch}(\underbrace{[e,\ldots,e]}_{n}) = e.\]

For the singleton: \(\mathsf{Ch}([a]) = a \circ \mathsf{Ch}([]) = a \circ e = a\) by (A3). The constant chain is proved by induction on \(n\), and the void case is a special case with \(a = e\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.chain_singleton, chain_replicate, chain_replicate_ident.)

Lemma 1.13 (Concatenation of chains). For any two finite lists \(L_1, L_2\) of ideas: \[\mathsf{Ch}(L_1 {+\!+} L_2) = \mathsf{Ch}(L_1) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2).\]

By induction on \(L_1\). If \(L_1 = []\), both sides equal \(\mathsf{Ch}(L_2)\) (using (A2) and \(\mathsf{Ch}([])=e\)). For the inductive step with \(L_1 = a :: L\): \[\mathsf{Ch}((a :: L) {+\!+} L_2) = a \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L {+\!+} L_2) = a \circ (\mathsf{Ch}(L) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2)) = (a \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L)) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2) = \mathsf{Ch}(a::L) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2),\] using the inductive hypothesis and (A1). (Verified as IdeaTheory.chain_append.)

Lemma 1.14 (Splitting at an arbitrary index). For every list \(L\) and every \(n \in \mathbb{N}\): \[\mathsf{Ch}(L) = \mathsf{Ch}(\mathrm{take}_n\, L) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(\mathrm{drop}_n\, L).\]

We have \(L = (\mathrm{take}_n\, L) {+\!+} (\mathrm{drop}_n\, L)\). Apply Lemma 1.13. (Verified as IdeaTheory.chain_split.)

Lemma 1.15 (Identity-padding). Let \(L\) be any list and let \(L'\) be obtained from \(L\) by inserting one copy of \(e\) at any position. Then \(\mathsf{Ch}(L') = \mathsf{Ch}(L)\).

Write \(L = L_1 {+\!+} L_2\) where the insertion happens at the boundary. By Lemma 1.13: \[\mathsf{Ch}(L') = \mathsf{Ch}(L_1) \circ \mathsf{Ch}([e]) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2) = \mathsf{Ch}(L_1) \circ e \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2) = \mathsf{Ch}(L_1) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2) = \mathsf{Ch}(L).\] (Verified as IdeaTheory.chain_concat_ident.)

Remark (Chains as discourses). The chain construction is the formal correlate of any phenomenon in which ideas are arranged in a temporal or syntactic sequence. Hume's "trains of thought"[2] are chains; so are the cultural attractor sequences of Sperber, the inferential chains of Brandom, the syntactic compositions of Frege, and the narrative episodes of Ricoeur. The bracket-independence guaranteed by Lemma 1.14 is what entitles all of these authors to treat their primary unit of analysis as a sequence rather than a tree.

5. Sub-ideals and idempotents

Definition 1.4 (Left and right sub-ideals). We say that \(a\) is a left sub-ideal of \(b\), written \(a \le_L b\), if there exists \(c \in \mathcal{I}\) with \(b = a \circ c\). Symmetrically, \(a\) is a right sub-ideal of \(b\), written \(a \le_R b\), if there exists \(c\) with \(b = c \circ a\).
Lemma 1.16 (Order properties of \(\le_L\) and \(\le_R\)). The relations \(\le_L\) and \(\le_R\) are reflexive and transitive. The void idea \(e\) is below every idea: \(e \le_L a\) and \(e \le_R a\) for all \(a\). In particular, \(a \le_L a \circ b\) and \(b \le_R a \circ b\) for all \(a, b\).

Reflexivity: \(a = a \circ e\) by (A3), so \(a \le_L a\) with witness \(c = e\). Transitivity: if \(b = a \circ x\) and \(c = b \circ y\), then \(c = (a \circ x) \circ y = a \circ (x \circ y)\) by (A1), so \(a \le_L c\) with witness \(x \circ y\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.subideal_refl, subideal_trans, subideal_ident.)

Definition 1.5 (Idempotent and commuting ideas). An idea \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) is idempotent if \(a \circ a = a\). Two ideas \(a\) and \(b\) commute if \(a \circ b = b \circ a\); we write \(a \mathrel{\#} b\) for this relation. The centralizer of \(a\) is the set \(Z(a) := \{ b \in \mathcal{I} \mid a \mathrel{\#} b \}\).
Lemma 1.17 (Basic facts about idempotents). (1) \(e\) is idempotent. (2) If \(a\) is idempotent and \(n \ge 1\), then \(a^n = a\) (and so \(a^n\) is itself idempotent).

(1) Lemma 1.6. (2) Induct on \(n\). Base \(n=1\): \(a^1 = a\). Inductive step: \(a^{k+1} = a \circ a^k = a \circ a = a\) by the idempotency of \(a\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.ident_idempotent, npow_idempotent.)

Lemma 1.18 (Basic facts about commutation). The relation \(\mathrel{\#}\) is reflexive and symmetric; the void idea commutes with everything; in particular \(e \in Z(a)\) and \(a \in Z(a)\) for every \(a\).

Reflexivity is immediate. Symmetry: if \(a \circ b = b \circ a\) then \(b \circ a = a \circ b\). \(e\) commutes with \(a\): \(e \circ a = a = a \circ e\) by (A2)–(A3). (Verified as IdeaTheory.commute_refl, commute_symm, commute_ident_left.)

The relation \(\mathrel{\#}\) is not in general transitive: \(a\) and \(b\) may both commute with \(c\) without commuting with each other. This failure reappears in Chapter 5 as the curvature of the resonance pairing.

6. Cancellation and invertibility

Definition 1.6 (Cancellative ideas). An idea \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) is
  • left-cancellative if \(a \circ b = a \circ c\) implies \(b = c\);
  • right-cancellative if \(b \circ a = c \circ a\) implies \(b = c\);
  • cancellative if both.
Lemma 1.19 (\(e\) is cancellative). \(e\) is both left- and right-cancellative.

If \(e \circ b = e \circ c\), then by (A2) \(b = c\). Symmetric on the right. (Verified as IdeaTheory.ident_cancellative.)

Lemma 1.20 (Composition of cancellative ideas). If \(a\) and \(b\) are left-cancellative, so is \(a \circ b\). Symmetrically for right cancellation.

Suppose \((a \circ b) \circ x = (a \circ b) \circ y\). By (A1): \(a \circ (b \circ x) = a \circ (b \circ y)\). Left cancellation by \(a\) gives \(b \circ x = b \circ y\), then by \(b\) gives \(x = y\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.left_cancel_op.)

Definition 1.7 (Invertible ideas). An idea \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) is invertible if there exists \(b \in \mathcal{I}\) with \(a \circ b = e\) and \(b \circ a = e\). In that case \(b\) is called an inverse of \(a\).
Lemma 1.21 (\(e\) is invertible). \(e\) is invertible, with inverse \(e\).

\(e \circ e = e\) by Lemma 1.6. (Verified as IdeaTheory.ident_invertible.)

Lemma 1.22 (Inverses are unique). If \(b_1\) and \(b_2\) are both inverses of \(a\), then \(b_1 = b_2\).

\[b_1 = b_1 \circ e = b_1 \circ (a \circ b_2) = (b_1 \circ a) \circ b_2 = e \circ b_2 = b_2.\] (Verified as IdeaTheory.invertible_inv_unique.)

Lemma 1.23 (Closure of invertibles under composition). If \(a\) and \(b\) are invertible, with inverses \(a'\) and \(b'\), then \(a \circ b\) is invertible with inverse \(b' \circ a'\).

Check: \((a \circ b) \circ (b' \circ a') = a \circ ((b \circ b') \circ a') = a \circ (e \circ a') = a \circ a' = e\), and the left-inverse is symmetric. (Verified as IdeaTheory.invertible_op.)

Definition 1.8 (The group of units). The group of units of an idea algebra \(\IdMon\) is the subset \(\mathcal{I}^\times\) of invertible ideas, equipped with the restriction of \(\circ\), the unit \(e\), and the inversion map \(a \mapsto a^{-1}\) (well defined by Lemma 1.22).

The group \(\mathcal{I}^\times\) measures the reversibility budget of the algebra. In a free monoid (Locke's atomistic picture) we shall see that \(\mathcal{I}^\times = \{e\}\): nothing is reversible. In a group every idea is invertible.

7. Theorems 1.1–1.3 and Corollaries 1.1–1.4

We now consolidate the structural payload of the chapter into three theorems and four corollaries. All seven results are formally verified in the Lean module IdeaTheory.Theorems1.

Theorem 1.1 (Bracketing-independence of chain composition). For every finite list \(L\) of ideas and every \(n \in \mathbb{N}\): \[\mathsf{Ch}(L) = \mathsf{Ch}(\mathrm{take}_n\, L) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(\mathrm{drop}_n\, L).\]

Write \(L = L_1 {+\!+} L_2\) with \(L_1 = \mathrm{take}_n\, L\) and \(L_2 = \mathrm{drop}_n\, L\). Apply Lemma 1.13. (Verified as IdeaTheory.theorem_1_1.)

Theorem 1.1 is the algebraic guarantee that the composite content of a discourse, narrative, or transmission chain depends only on the linear order of its parts and not on any prior decision about how to bracket them. This is the formal version of the informal principle, common to Hume and Sperber, that "the order in which we group the units does not affect the result."

Theorem 1.2 (Uniqueness of the void; closure of the units; uniqueness of inverses). Let \(\IdMon\) be a bare idea algebra. Then:
  1. If \(e' \in \mathcal{I}\) is a two-sided unit, then \(e' = e\).
  2. If \(a, b \in \mathcal{I}\) are invertible, then so is \(a \circ b\).
  3. If \(b_1, b_2\) are inverses of the same idea \(a\), then \(b_1 = b_2\).

(1) Lemma 1.1. (2) Lemma 1.23. (3) Lemma 1.22. (Verified as IdeaTheory.theorem_1_2.)

Theorem 1.3 (Iteration is a monoid homomorphism). For every fixed \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) the function \(n \mapsto a^n\) from \(\mathbb{N}\) to \(\mathcal{I}\) satisfies \[a^0 = e, \qquad a^{m+n} = a^m \circ a^n, \qquad a^{m+n+k} = (a^m \circ a^n) \circ a^k,\] for all \(m, n, k \in \mathbb{N}\). Equivalently, \(n \mapsto a^n\) is a monoid homomorphism \((\mathbb{N}, +, 0) \to \IdMon\).

The first identity is Definition 1.2. The second is Lemma 1.10. For the third, apply Lemma 1.10 twice: \(a^{m+n+k} = a^{(m+n)} \circ a^k = (a^m \circ a^n) \circ a^k\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.theorem_1_3.)

Corollary 1.1 (Reorganization of cultural transmission chains). For any finite lists \(L_1, L_2, L_3\) of ideas: \[\mathsf{Ch}(L_1 {+\!+} L_2 {+\!+} L_3) = \mathsf{Ch}(L_1 {+\!+} (L_2 {+\!+} L_3)).\]

List concatenation is associative on the nose. Apply \(\mathsf{Ch}(\cdot)\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.corollary_1_1.) This corollary will be invoked in Volume II to prove that diffusion of an idea through a population is independent of the choice of intermediate broadcast points.

Corollary 1.2 (Insertion of empty episodes). For any finite lists \(L_1, L_2\) and any \(n \in \mathbb{N}\): \[\mathsf{Ch}(L_1 {+\!+} e^{::n} {+\!+} L_2) = \mathsf{Ch}(L_1 {+\!+} L_2).\]

By Lemma 1.13 applied twice and Lemma 1.12: \(\mathsf{Ch}(L_1 {+\!+} e^{::n} {+\!+} L_2) = (\mathsf{Ch}(L_1) \circ e) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2) = \mathsf{Ch}(L_1) \circ \mathsf{Ch}(L_2)\). (Verified as IdeaTheory.corollary_1_2.) Two narratives differing only by the insertion of empty interludes have the same total content.

Corollary 1.3 (Closure of reversible operations). The set \(\mathcal{I}^\times\) of invertible ideas is closed under composition. In particular, if \(a\) and \(b\) are invertible, so is \(a \circ b\).

Lemma 1.23. (Verified as IdeaTheory.corollary_1_3.) The composition of two undoable operations is itself undoable.

Corollary 1.4 (Idempotents are exhausted by themselves). If \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) is idempotent, then \(a^n = a\) for every \(n \ge 1\).

Lemma 1.17(2). (Verified as IdeaTheory.corollary_1_4.) An absorbed cultural item does not grow under repetition.

8. Three concrete examples

Before turning to the historical zoo, we exhibit three concrete idea algebras to ground the formalism.

Example 1.1 (The natural numbers under addition). Take \(\mathcal{I} = \mathbb{N}\), \(\circ = +\), and \(e = 0\). Axioms (A1)–(A3) are the standard properties of addition. In this model a chain \([n_1, \dots, n_k]\) has composite \(n_1 + \cdots + n_k\), and the \(n\)-th power of \(a\) is \(na\). Idempotents are exhausted by \(0\); invertible elements are again exhausted by \(0\). This model demonstrates that the formalism does not by itself force any non-trivial group of units.
Example 1.2 (Free idea algebra on a generating set \(S\)). Let \(S\) be any non-empty set and let \(\mathcal{I} = S^*\) denote the set of finite words on \(S\). Define \(\circ\) as concatenation and \(e\) as the empty word. This is the free monoid on \(S\), and it is the fundamental object underlying the Lockean reading below. The only idempotent is \(e\), and the only invertible element is again \(e\): in the free idea algebra nothing absorbs and nothing is reversible. These properties are the algebraic shadow of Locke's "inability to invent a new simple idea."
Example 1.3 (A two-atom finite world). Let \(\mathcal{I} = \{ e, a, b, c \}\) with \(a \circ a = a\), \(b \circ b = b\), \(a \circ b = b \circ a = c\), \(c \circ x = c\) for \(x \in \{a, b, c\}\), and \(e\) as two-sided unit. Both \(a\) and \(b\) are idempotent; \(c\) is the "saturated" idea that absorbs everything. This toy model illustrates how the bare monoid can encode the informal idea of "saturation by absorption": once \(c\) is reached, no further composition moves the system.

9. The zoo: four classical positions accommodated by the bare monoid

We now show that the bare monoid structure already suffices to host four canonical positions in the philosophy of ideas. Each entry follows the template: Original Claim / Formal Restatement / Status / Commentary.

Locke (1690): simple and complex ideas as monoid generators

Original Claim

Locke[1] divides ideas into the simple and the complex (Bk. II, Ch. II–XII). Simple ideas are received passively from sensation or reflection; the mind cannot manufacture them. Complex ideas are produced by acts of combination, comparison, and abstraction performed on simple ideas. The mind's productive power is exhausted by these operations: "it being not in the power of the most exalted wit ... to invent or frame one new simple idea." Hume[2] inherits and sharpens this combinatorial picture via the "copy principle."

Formal Restatement

Let \(S \subseteq \mathcal{I}\) be the set of simple ideas. Locke's claim is that \(\mathcal{I}\) is the smallest sub-monoid of itself containing \(S\) and closed under \(\circ\). Equivalently, the inclusion \(S \hookrightarrow \mathcal{I}\) extends to a unique surjective monoid homomorphism from the free monoid \(S^*\) onto \(\mathcal{I}\). Concretely, every \(a \in \mathcal{I}\) is of the form \(\mathsf{Ch}([s_1, \dots, s_k])\) for some \(s_i \in S \cup \{e\}\).

Status

True for any choice of generators \(S\). By Lemma 1.13, the set of chain composites of finite sequences from \(S \cup \{e\}\) is closed under \(\circ\) and contains \(e\), hence is a sub-monoid. The non-trivial content — that \(\mathcal{I}\) equals the smallest such sub-monoid for some philosophically meaningful \(S\) — is a question about the existence of a generating set.

Commentary

The bare monoid is faithful to Locke at the structural level but silent on which ideas count as simple and on whether the generation map \(S^* \to \mathcal{I}\) is injective. In a model where it is injective, Lockean atomism is exactly correct. In a model where it is not, two distinct combinations of simples can produce the same complex idea — the algebraic shadow of Aufhebung, taken up systematically in Chapter 3. Locke's notion of abstraction is recovered as the passage from \(\mathcal{I}\) to a quotient sub-monoid.

Hume (1739): chains as principles of association

Original Claim

Hume[2] (Bk. I, Pt. I, §IV) holds that ideas combine in the mind by three universal principles of association: resemblance, contiguity, and cause and effect. These principles operate on trains of ideas — sequences in which each new term is recruited by association with its predecessor. Locke's[1] own discussion of the "association of ideas" is Hume's immediate source.

Formal Restatement

Identify a Humean train with a finite list \(L = [a_1, \dots, a_k]\). The content of the train is its chain composite \(\mathsf{Ch}(L)\). Hume's first claim is that every complex idea is of this form. Hume's second claim — that the sequence is generated by a fixed associative process — is the demand that there be a function \(\alpha : \mathcal{I} \to \mathcal{I}\) with \(a_{i+1} = \alpha(a_i)\).

Status

Hume's first claim is true on the bare monoid. By Lemma 1.12 every idea \(a\) is itself a chain, and by Lemma 1.13 every composite of a finite collection is a chain. Hume's second claim is contingent. It can be made to hold or fail by appropriate choice of \(\alpha\) on the underlying carrier. The bare monoid is silent on the dynamics; later volumes equip \(\mathcal{I}\) with the resonance pairing \(\rho\) that makes "association by resemblance" formally tractable.

Commentary

Hume's chain principle is one of the most under-defended assumptions in the early-modern theory of mind, and the formalization makes clear why: on the bare monoid it is mathematically empty. A concrete prediction of the formal version is Lemma 1.15: empty episodes inserted into a Humean train do not change its content. Any psychological theory in which inserting a contentless interval changes the train's outcome is incompatible with axioms (A1)–(A3).

Frege (1892): composition of senses

Original Claim

Frege[3] introduces the distinction between Sinn (sense) and Bedeutung (reference). The sense of a complex expression is determined by the senses of its parts and their mode of combination — the principle of compositionality. The principle is reaffirmed in Wittgenstein[4] (Tractatus §3.318), which treats the proposition as a function of its constituent expressions.

Formal Restatement

Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be the set of senses, and let \(\circ\) be the operation "the sense of \(a\)-applied-to-\(b\)" (the saturation of a function-sense by an argument-sense). Compositionality is the demand that \(\circ\) be a function of its inputs. Together with the natural unit (the sense of the empty context), this yields exactly the bare idea algebra \(\IdMon\).

Status

True, but the substantive content is associativity. Frege's compositionality principle is, on its face, only the assertion that \(\circ\) is well defined as a function. Axiom (A1) is what makes the principle useful: it guarantees that the sense of a complex expression does not depend on the order in which its sub-expressions are saturated.

Commentary

The bare monoid is faithful to Frege provided one reads Frege's "mode of combination" as encoded inside \(\circ\). A genuine prediction of the formalization that goes beyond Frege is Theorem 1.1: the sense of a complex expression is invariant under any contiguous re-grouping of its constituents, not merely under binary re-association. The emergence cocycle \(\omega\) (Chapter 5) measures exactly the non-Fregean residue of composition.

Wittgenstein (1953): family resemblance as a quotient monoid

Original Claim

The later Wittgenstein[4] (§65–67) rejects the Lockean–Fregean picture of concepts as having essential content. Instead, the extension of a concept — his example is "game" — is held together by a network of overlapping similarities, which Wittgenstein labels family resemblance (Familienähnlichkeit). This is anticipated by Hume's[2] discussion of resemblance as one of the three principles of association.

Formal Restatement

Fix an equivalence relation \(\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\) on \(\mathcal{I}\) generated by a symmetric "resemblance" relation. The family-resemblance class of \(a\) is \([a] := \{ b : a \sim_{\mathcal{I}} b \}\). Wittgenstein's claim is that the natural object of conceptual analysis is not \(\mathcal{I}\) but the quotient \(\mathcal{I}/{\sim_{\mathcal{I}}}\). The bare monoid hosts this construction provided \(\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\) is a congruence for \(\circ\): that is, \(a_1 \sim_{\mathcal{I}} a_2\) and \(b_1 \sim_{\mathcal{I}} b_2\) imply \(a_1 \circ b_1 \sim_{\mathcal{I}} a_2 \circ b_2\).

Status

Contingent on \(\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\) being a congruence. When this holds, the quotient \(\mathcal{I}/{\sim_{\mathcal{I}}}\) inherits a well-defined operation and is itself a bare idea algebra; the quotient map is a surjective homomorphism. When \(\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\) is not a congruence — the typical case for genuine family resemblance, since composing two "games" need not yield something resembling a "game" — the bare monoid structure does not descend to the quotient.

Commentary

The formalization sharply discriminates two readings. On the algebraic reading, family resemblance is a congruence and Wittgenstein's classes are quotient ideas. On the relational reading, family resemblance is a mere relation that need not respect composition. The choice between the two readings is forced once a candidate \(\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\) is specified, by the testable question of whether it is a congruence. The bare monoid is what makes the question testable.

10. Synopsis and what comes next

Accomplishments. We have isolated three axioms (A1)–(A3) and shown that they suffice to derive: the uniqueness of the void idea (Lemma 1.1); the basic arithmetic of iteration \(a^n\) (Lemmas 1.7–1.11); the homomorphism property of chain composition (Lemmas 1.13–1.15); the order properties of the sub-ideal relations (Lemma 1.16); the closure of idempotents and commuting pairs (Lemmas 1.17–1.18); the uniqueness of inverses and closure of \(\mathcal{I}^\times\) (Lemmas 1.22–1.23); and the three headline theorems (1.1–1.3) with their four corollaries (1.1–1.4). Every result is verified in the Lean module IdeaTheory.Theorems1.

Limitations of the bare monoid. The structure \(\IdMon\) is silent on three questions of immediate philosophical interest. First, it cannot distinguish two ideas that behave identically under composition. Second, it provides no measure of distance or similarity between ideas. Third, it has no notion of weight or magnitude.

Roadmap. The next chapter introduces coalitions and the aggregate operator. Chapter 3 develops the Aufhebung decomposition \(\sigma(a) \circ \nu(a) = a\) and proves its uniqueness modulo \(\ker\rho\). Chapter 5 introduces the emergence cocycle \(\omega\) that measures the failure of any content-valuation \(v : \mathcal{I} \to A\) to be a monoid homomorphism. Chapters 6–9 add conjugation, transmission chains, structural equivalence, and grading respectively.

A methodological closing remark. The conviction underlying the entire monograph is that the abundance of informal claims about "what ideas are" and "how they combine" has created, over centuries, a substantial body of tacit algebra. Untangling this tacit algebra and pinning down its minimal axiomatic core is the task of Idea Theory. The discipline imposed by working in a fully verified setting — the Lean module IdeaTheory contains no sorry and no admit — guarantees that the formal content of every claim is exactly what the prose says it is.
