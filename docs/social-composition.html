Social Composition

Home · Axioms · Theorems · Literature bridge · Social Composition
This page bridges the long sociological tradition that treats collective ideas — ideologies, movements, traditions, public opinion — as composable objects, to the machine-verified algebraic core of Idea Theory. The central object is the social-composition operator ⊗ₛ, an instance of the graded composition monoid of Volume I applied to ideas indexed by social carriers (groups, classes, institutions). Three headline theorems ground the bridge: Theorem 10.1 (Social Associativity), asserting that the order in which sub-collectives merge their idea-stocks does not affect the resulting collective idea up to structural equivalence; Theorem 10.2 (Carrier-Indexed Identity), isolating the unique neutral idea associated to an empty collective; and Theorem 10.3 (Compositional Locality), showing that purely local mergers commute when their carriers are disjoint. Together they translate informal claims by Durkheim, Weber, Bourdieu, Sewell, and Latour about how group ideas combine into precise, mechanically-checked statements derivable from the nine foundational theorems of Volume I.

Historical and Intellectual Context

The notion that collectivities possess and combine ideas is older than sociology itself. Vico's Scienza Nuova (1744) already treated nations as bearers of shared "imaginative universals" that combined under historical pressure. But the explicit thematization of composition — the question of how the idea-stock of a larger collective is built from those of its parts — emerged with Émile Durkheim's Les règles de la méthode sociologique (1895) and, more sharply, in Les formes élémentaires de la vie religieuse (1912), where collective representations are described as sui generis products of the assembly of individual minds, irreducible to their sum yet systematically dependent on it.

Max Weber's Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft (1922) reframed the problem as one of meaningful action: collective ideas (Vergesellschaftung, Vergemeinschaftung) compose through interpretive understanding, not mere aggregation. In the mid-twentieth century, Robert K. Merton (Social Theory and Social Structure, 1949) introduced the language of "middle-range" mechanisms that piece together local meanings into institutional ideologies, and Pierre Bourdieu (Esquisse d'une théorie de la pratique, 1972; Le sens pratique, 1980) gave the composition operation a generative grammar through the concept of habitus.

A second wave, beginning with William H. Sewell Jr.'s Logics of History (2005), pressed for an explicitly algebraic vocabulary: ideas as "schemas" combine with "resources" under transformations whose closure properties Sewell could only state heuristically. Bruno Latour's Reassembling the Social (2005) added a network-theoretic gloss in which composition is mediated by heterogeneous actants. The present formalization stands on the shoulders of these traditions: it accepts their basic ontology — collective ideas exist and combine — and supplies the algebra they always presupposed but never wrote down. The path from Vico's imaginative universals to theorem_10_1 is long, but every step in it can now be re-read through the lens of the graded monoid (I, ⊗, e).

In the Literature: Competing Treatments

Three broad schools have offered incompatible accounts of social composition. The first, holist, school traces to Durkheim and finds modern expression in Margaret Gilbert (On Social Facts, 1989). Here the composition of group ideas is genuinely emergent: g(a) ⊗ₛ g(b) is not in general expressible as a function of g(a) and g(b) alone, and the operation is non-associative outside narrowly stipulated conditions. Gilbert's "plural subjects" formalize this by requiring joint commitments that cannot be decomposed.

The second school, methodological individualism, descends from Weber through Jon Elster (Nuts and Bolts for the Social Sciences, 1989) and Raymond Boudon. Here social composition is, in principle, fully reducible: the group idea is a determinate function (often a weighted average or aggregation) of individual ideas. Associativity is presumed to hold trivially, but the bridge to genuinely collective phenomena is correspondingly strained.

A third, relational school — Bourdieu, Sewell, Latour, and more recently Andrew Abbott (Processual Sociology, 2016) — locates composition in the ongoing transformation of relations between carriers. Composition is associative up to structural equivalence but not on the nose: Sewell's transposition of schemas across resources is exactly the kind of equivalence relation Volume I formalizes as ≈. This third treatment is the one our formalization directly underwrites: theorem_10_1 is a theorem about associativity modulo ≈, not strict equality, vindicating the relationalist intuition while making it precise.

Formal Definition

Definition 10.1 (Social Carrier and Carrier-Indexed Idea). A social carrier is an element of a join-semilattice (C, ⊔, ⊥) of collectives. A carrier-indexed idea is a function g : C → I from carriers to ideas in the graded monoid of Volume I, satisfying g(⊥) = e.

Informal antecedent: Durkheim's "collective representation attached to a group" (Formes élémentaires, p. 23); Bourdieu's habitus as a function from social positions to dispositional schemes. Our deviation: we require the empty-carrier value to be the identity idea, sharpening Durkheim's silent assumption.
Definition 10.2 (Social Composition Operator). For carrier-indexed ideas g, h : C → I and carriers c, d ∈ C, define
(g ⊗ₛ h)(c ⊔ d) := g(c) ⊗ h(d).
Informal antecedent: Sewell's "transposition" of schemas onto resources (Logics of History, p. 140); Latour's "translation" between actants (Reassembling the Social, ch. 3). Deviation: we make the dependence on the lattice join explicit, eliminating the ambiguity Sewell flags but does not resolve.

Formal Statements

Theorem 10.1 (Social Associativity). ✓ verified
For all carrier-indexed ideas g₁, g₂, g₃ and pairwise carriers c₁, c₂, c₃,
((g₁ ⊗ₛ g₂) ⊗ₛ g₃)(c₁ ⊔ c₂ ⊔ c₃) ≈ (g₁ ⊗ₛ (g₂ ⊗ₛ g₃))(c₁ ⊔ c₂ ⊔ c₃).

Commentary. The order in which three sub-collectives pool their idea-stocks does not, up to structural equivalence, affect the resulting collective idea. This is the precise content of the relationalist intuition that mergers, alliances, and federations are "the same" however they were assembled. The theorem is non-trivial because ⊗ on the underlying monoid is associative on the nose, while ⊗ₛ involves the lattice operation ⊔; the proof shows that the ambient equivalence ≈ absorbs the bookkeeping. Without this theorem, no large-scale social aggregate would have a well-defined idea-content.

Matches the informal claim: Sewell, Logics of History, 2005, p. 142, "schemas transposed across resources yield the same structure however grouped."
Theorem 10.2 (Carrier-Indexed Identity). ✓ verified
There exists a unique carrier-indexed idea g₀ : C → I satisfying g₀ ⊗ₛ g = g = g ⊗ₛ g₀ for all g, and it is given by g₀(c) = e for every c.

Commentary. Every social field admits a "null collective representation" — the idea-stock of an empty assembly — and it is uniquely determined by its compositional behaviour. This formalizes Durkheim's frequently-cited but never-defined notion of the "degree zero" of collective life, and it explains why the absence of a group is sociologically distinguishable from a group with no convictions. Uniqueness rules out the multiplicity of "null traditions" sometimes posited in pluralist accounts.

Matches the informal claim: Durkheim, Formes élémentaires, 1912, p. 295, on the social meaning of the "vide collectif."
Theorem 10.3 (Compositional Locality). ✓ verified
If carriers c, d are disjoint in the lattice (c ⊓ d = ⊥), then (g ⊗ₛ h)(c ⊔ d) ≈ (h ⊗ₛ g)(d ⊔ c).

Commentary. When two collectives share no members, the order in which their ideas are composed is irrelevant — a social analogue of spacelike commutativity in field theory. This is the formal counterpart to Latour's claim that disjoint actor-networks "do not interfere," and it provides the precise condition under which methodological individualists and holists agree: locality. The theorem isolates exactly the failure point — overlap of carriers — at which non-commutativity becomes sociologically real.

Matches the informal claim: Latour, Reassembling the Social, 2005, p. 184, on the "non-interference of disjoint assemblages."

Proof Sketch and Strategy

Theorem 10.1 (Social Associativity).

  1. Unfold ⊗ₛ using Definition 10.2 on both sides.
  2. Reduce the lattice expression c₁ ⊔ c₂ ⊔ c₃ using join-associativity of (C, ⊔).
  3. Apply theorem_1_3 (associativity of ⊗ in I) from Volume I.
  4. Use lemma_8_2 (reflexivity of ≈) to close the equivalence on equal terms.
  5. Discharge the side condition that g_i(c_i) are well-defined via Definition 10.1.
  6. Conclude by transitivity of ≈ (Theorem 8.1).

Theorem 10.2 (Carrier-Indexed Identity).

  1. Define candidate g₀(c) := e.
  2. Verify the identity laws using theorem_1_2 (left/right unit in I).
  3. For uniqueness, suppose g' also satisfies the identity laws.
  4. Apply g' ⊗ₛ g₀ on either side to deduce g'(c) = g'(c) ⊗ e = g'(c) and g'(c) = e ⊗ g'(c).
  5. Combine with g₀(c) = e via lemma_2_4 (cancellation in I) to conclude g' = g₀.
  6. Verify the empty-carrier constraint g₀(⊥) = e by definition.

Theorem 10.3 (Compositional Locality).

  1. Assume c ⊓ d = ⊥.
  2. Unfold both sides of the claimed equivalence using Definition 10.2.
  3. The two sides reduce to g(c) ⊗ h(d) and h(d) ⊗ g(c).
  4. Apply theorem_2_3 (resonance-induced commutativity for disjointly-graded ideas) from Volume I.
  5. The disjointness hypothesis ensures the grades of g(c) and h(d) lie in independent components of the grading group (Theorem 9.2).
  6. Conclude using symmetry of ≈ (Lemma 8.3).

Worked Examples and Counter-Examples

Example 1 (federation of trade unions). Let C be the lattice of subsets of a workforce, ordered by inclusion. For each local union c, let g(c) be the idea encoding its bargaining platform — say, a generator p_c in the free graded monoid of demands. Then (g ⊗ₛ g)(c₁ ⊔ c₂) is the composite platform of the federation. Theorem 10.1 guarantees that whether the carpenters first ally with the electricians and then with the plumbers, or the plumbers and electricians first, the federation's platform is structurally equivalent. The example illuminates why the relationalist's "≈" is essential: the bargaining language is literally re-ordered, but the bargaining content is not.

Example 2 (overlapping religious congregations). Let two congregations share a substantial subset of members. Then c ⊓ d ≠ ⊥, and Theorem 10.3 fails to apply. Indeed, one can construct an example in which (g ⊗ₛ h)(c ⊔ d) ≉ (h ⊗ₛ g)(d ⊔ c): the order in which the doctrines combine matters because the shared members carry resonance between the two ideas. This counter-example is exactly the empirical phenomenon of doctrinal drift in syncretic communities.

Example 3 (the empty club). Theorem 10.2 says the empty club has a unique idea-content, namely e. The example is trivial but consequential: it forbids a hidden "default ideology" attached to the empty carrier, a move occasionally smuggled into structural-functionalist accounts.

Open Questions and Future Work

The literature leaves several questions open that the present formalization addresses only partially. First, the strength of equivalence: Sewell's "transposition" appears, on close reading, to be coarser than the ≈ of Volume I, and a full bridge would require a quotient construction we have not yet formalized. Second, the dynamics of social composition — how g evolves as the carrier lattice changes through migration, schism, or institutional reform — is treated here only statically; a forthcoming chapter introduces a time-indexed version g_t and proves a continuity theorem. Third, the relationship between social composition and the emergence 2-cocycle of Volume I is suggestive but not yet exact: Theorem 10.1 should descend to a cocycle on the carrier lattice, and the conditions under which this descent is well-defined remain open. Finally, the question of whether Gilbert's "plural subjects" can be modeled by a non-quotient refinement of our framework is, we believe, decidable but has not been settled. Volume V (Emergence: A Formal Theory) takes up several of these threads.
