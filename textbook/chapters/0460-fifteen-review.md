# Chapter 29 — A unified review of the fifteen theorems

This review is a spoken map of the fifteen theorems. Each theorem names one constraint on how ideas compose, resonate, travel, differ, and survive across horizons. The point is not to memorize labels. The point is to hear the system as one grammar.

## The fifteen in order

Theorem one, Composition, says that ideas can be composed without ambiguity, that silence is unique, and that reversible ideas form a disciplined subsystem. Its main primitives are temporality, schema, and rigid reference.

Theorem two, Resonance, says that fit and opposition extend uniquely from basic ideas to larger constructions when the extension rule is fixed. Its main primitives are intentionality, credence, and signed valence.

Theorem three, Aufhebung, says that every composition splits into preservation, suppression, and emergence. Its main primitives are differentiation, signed valence, and reflexion.

Theorem four, Emergence Cocycle, says that novelty is coherent across regrouped chains and has an invariant remainder. Its main primitives are potentiality, reflexion, and temporality.

Theorem five, Meaning Curvature, says that directional resonance can be closed and topologically durable. Its main primitives are differentiation, intentionality, and signed valence.

Theorem six, Conjugation, says that a frame can preserve internal structure while reweighting external resonance. Its main primitives are perspective, schema, and signed valence.

Theorem seven, Transmission Chains, says that emergence accumulates along chains according to topology and not mere length. Its main primitives are temporality, potentiality, and credence.

Theorem eight, Structural Equivalence, says that two algebras are equivalent exactly when a translation preserves their structure. Its main primitives are rigid reference, schema, and perspective.

Theorem nine, Graded Idea Algebras, says that degrees are unique up to the ordered scale chosen and that cumulative growth can be measured. Its main primitives are potentiality, schema, and temporality.

Theorem ten, Coalitions, says that common ideas in a group form a shared subalgebra and recover common knowledge through agreement across perspectives. Its main primitives are perspective, credence, and schema.

Theorem eleven, Transmission and Diffusion, says that diffusion converges when the network gives enough recurrent faithful transmission. Its main primitives are temporality, credence, and potentiality.

Theorem twelve, Novelty, says that recombinant pools can generate genuinely new ideas at a super linear rate when emergence closure remains open. Its main primitives are potentiality, differentiation, and temporality.

Theorem thirteen, Hermeneutic Sheaf, says that local meanings glue uniquely into a global meaning when they agree on overlaps. Its main primitives are perspective, schema, and credence.

Theorem fourteen, Indexical Pullback, says that preservation and emergence can fail to travel backward across horizons, and that the failure has a first cohomology obstruction. Its main primitives are perspective, differentiation, and rigid reference.

Theorem fifteen, Profundity, says that profound ideas are those that remain stable across many horizons while continuing to generate closure. Its main primitives are rigid reference, potentiality, and reflexion.

## The single pattern

The first nine theorems build the internal algebra. They say how ideas compose, resonate, decompose, generate emergence, carry direction, reframe, travel in chains, translate, and receive degree. The next three theorems move into social and historical dynamics. Coalitions, diffusion, and novelty show how shared knowledge, transmission, and innovation can be studied without abandoning structure. The final three theorems add horizons. Understanding becomes gluing. Indexicality becomes obstruction. Profundity becomes trans horizonal stability.

Together, the fifteen theorems replace three temptations. The first temptation is atomism, the idea that meanings are isolated items. Composition and resonance rule that out. The second temptation is mystery, the idea that emergence and profundity are too deep to constrain. The cocycle, novelty, and profundity theorems rule that out. The third temptation is flattening, the idea that all contexts can be translated without loss. The sheaf and indexical theorems rule that out.

A final way to hear the review is by asking three questions of any later example. First, what composes with what. Second, what resonates, bends, or fails to translate. Third, across which horizons does the result remain stable. These questions locate the example inside the theorem map. A thinker may emphasize one primitive, such as perspective or potentiality, but the primitive is always anchored in the algebra. That is the discipline of Idea Theory: every vivid philosophical claim is invited to show its structural place.


The map also gives a method for disagreement. If two readers disagree, they can ask whether the dispute is about composition, resonance, decomposition, transmission, equivalence, grading, coalition structure, diffusion, novelty, gluing, obstruction, or profundity. Naming the theorem does not settle the dispute, but it prevents different disputes from being blurred together. It makes philosophical conversation more exact while leaving room for history, example, and judgment.

## Review summary

The fifteen theorems form one ladder. At the bottom are composition and resonance. In the middle are decomposition, curvature, transmission, equivalence, grading, coalitions, diffusion, and novelty. At the top are horizons, obstruction, and profundity. The listener should now have a mental map: every later philosophical position can be located by asking which theorem it relies on, which primitive it highlights, and which structural constraint it must obey.
