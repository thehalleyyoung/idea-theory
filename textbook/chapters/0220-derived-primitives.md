# Chapter 12 — The Eleven Derived Variables Walked Through

Idea Theory begins with a few operating names. Composition is the act of putting ideas together. The resonance pairing measures how one idea calls, resists, or illuminates another. Each composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. The emergence cocycle records how novelty behaves across regrouping. Degree or grading measures articulation. Meaning curvature names the durable asymmetry in resonance. Context enters through the horizon category, and through the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — which assigns a full idea algebra to each context.

## Why derivation matters

Many theories treat time, perspective, intention, belief, affect, and reference as primitive furniture. Idea Theory asks whether some of that furniture can be built from the structure already in the room. A derived primitive is not fake or unimportant. It is important precisely because it can be recovered from more basic roles. If temporality, intentionality, perspective, affect, differentiation, rigid reference, potentiality, schema or paradigm, credence, signed valence, and reflexion can be derived, then disagreements about them become more precise. We can ask which part of the horizon category, resonance pairing, grading, or emergence structure is doing the work.

## Time, aboutness, and point of view

Temporality is derived by choosing a totally ordered region of the horizon category. The horizons in that region are time-slices, and the translations between them carry ideas from later or richer temporal settings back to earlier or narrower ones. A process is stable when its idea remains coherent along these temporal translations. Becoming is the failure of such stability. No extra time substance is needed. Time is a special organization of horizons.

Intentionality is aboutness. It is derived from the resonance pairing associated with an idea across horizons. To say that an idea is about an object is to say that it carries a family of directed resonances: here it calls this, there it calls that, and across compatible horizons those calls hang together. Franz Brentano, pronounced approximately bren-TAH-no, and Husserl made intentionality central to phenomenology. Idea Theory hears their insight as a trans-horizonal resonance profile.

Perspective is derived from a connected region of the horizon category together with a home horizon. An agent is not an isolated point but an accessible field of contexts centered somewhere. What the agent can imagine, infer, remember, or share depends on that region. Intersubjectivity appears when two regions overlap or connect through translations. No separate perspective substance is added. A perspective is a shaped access pattern in the horizon category.

## Striving, difference, and reference

Affect, or conatus, is derived from directional differences of grading along horizon translations. If an idea gains degree when the agent moves into a refinement, it has positive charge in that direction. If it loses degree, it is deflated. Spinoza, pronounced approximately spi-NOH-zuh, used conatus for the striving by which a thing persists in its being. Heidegger, pronounced approximately HIGH-deg-er, spoke of care as the directedness of existence. Idea Theory derives this directed charge from grading across horizons.

Differentiation is derived from the suppression part together with resonance. Ferdinand de Saussure, pronounced approximately soh-SOOR, said that language has no positive terms, only differences. In this vocabulary, an idea is marked by the ideas with which it resonates while failing to combine. Its difference-cell is the field of meaningful noncomposition. The idea is not a glowing atom. It is shaped by what it is not, especially by what it cannot preserve with nearby alternatives.

Rigid reference is derived from global stability. A rigid designator is a name whose extension remains a singleton across the relevant horizons. Saul Kripke argued that Aristotle, pronounced approximately AIR-iss-tot-ul, refers to the same individual across possible worlds, even though descriptions of Aristotle can vary. Idea Theory says rigid reference is a global section of the horizon functor with one stable target in every horizon. Names that fail to extend globally are local rather than rigid.

## Possibility, paradigms, and belief

Potentiality is derived by looking toward refinements of a horizon and gathering the resonances an idea could acquire there. Aristotle's potentiality is the gap between what an idea currently resonates with and what it could resonate with under further development. A seed is not yet a tree, but its future refinements are not arbitrary. Likewise, an unfinished theory has possible meanings that are not yet actual meanings.

Schema or paradigm is derived from the connected component of the horizon category containing an agent's home. Kuhn's paradigm, Foucault's episteme, Wittgenstein's form of life, and Quine's web of belief can all be heard this way. Inside the component, translations are possible by some chain of steps. Outside it, no continuous path of interpretation connects the horizons. A paradigm shift is not merely changing opinions. It is moving to another component or rebuilding the connections.

Credence is derived by placing compatible probability assignments over horizons. A Bayesian agent does not need a separate primitive belief box. The agent has measures of confidence that push forward correctly along horizon translations. When evidence refines the horizon, credence updates. When a claim is moved to a broader horizon, credence must translate coherently or reveal a mismatch. This makes Bayesian epistemology a disciplined decoration on the horizon-indexed algebra rather than a rival foundation.

## Valence and self-application

Signed valence is derived from the relation between an agent's self-representation and an idea. If the idea composes with the self in a consonant way, the valence is attractive. If asymmetry and disruption dominate, the valence is aversive. Approach and avoidance, liking and disliking, hope and dread are not added as primitive colors. They are read from resonance, composition, and the structure of self-against-idea.

Reflexion is derived from self-application. An algebra can contain ideas about ideas, and eventually ideas about the algebra itself. Reflexion appears when this self-application stabilizes into a fixed point: the structure can speak about itself without immediately collapsing. Russell's paradox shows a failure of naive self-application. Gödel sentences show stable statements that refer to their own provability. Nagarjuna, pronounced approximately nah-GAR-joo-nah, speaks of the emptiness of emptiness. Husserl's transcendental ego turns reflection back upon the conditions of experience. In each case, reflexion is not a new primitive but the controlled return of the structure upon itself.

## The economy of the theory

The methodological payoff is large. If these eleven notions can be derived, then a philosopher who treats one as primitive may be pointing to a hidden assumption about horizons, resonance, grading, or emergence. A debate about intentionality may become a debate about resonance profiles. A debate about time may become a debate about ordered subcategories of horizons. A debate about probability may become a debate about compatible credence sections. The theory does not make the debates vanish. It makes their location clearer.

## Chapter summary

The eleven derived primitives are temporality, intentionality, perspective, affect or conatus, differentiation, rigid reference, potentiality, schema or paradigm, credence, signed valence, and reflexion. Each is built from the existing horizon, resonance, grading, composition, and emergence structure. Derivation gives the theory economy without denying the richness of the phenomena.
