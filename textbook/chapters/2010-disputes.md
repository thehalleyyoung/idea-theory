# Chapter 150 — Disputes the Framework Dissolves and Disputes It Sharpens

The operating definitions are now familiar. Ideas compose. They resonate directionally. Their compositions have preservation, suppression, and emergence parts. Their novelty is constrained by an emergence cocycle. Their complexity is tracked by grading. Their asymmetry leaves meaning curvature. Their intelligibility varies across horizons through the horizon functor, the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi; from here we say only horizon functor. With that machinery in place, some famous disagreements become less dramatic, while others become more exacting.

## Disputes that dissolve

The first dispute the framework dissolves is the quarrel between inner and outer meaning. One camp says that meaning is in the mind. Another says that meaning is in public language or social practice. Idea Theory replies that a perspective is a connected region of horizons, while a public practice is another region, often larger and more stable. An idea may be local to a mind, local to a community, or global across both. The real question is not where meaning lives, but which horizon supports it and which translations preserve it.

A second dissolved dispute concerns definition and prototype. Classical theories of concepts ask for necessary and sufficient conditions. Prototype theory emphasizes typicality, graded membership, and context. In the framework, definitions are rigid local structures. Prototypes are resonance profiles whose strength varies across horizons. Both are real. They answer different structural questions. A legal concept may need definition at the courtroom horizon, while ordinary categorization may rely on prototype resonance at the kitchen horizon.

A third dissolved dispute concerns realism and constructivism. Realists want stable objects independent of interpretation. Constructivists want to show how categories are made within practices. Idea Theory can say that some ideas are rigid global sections and others are local constructions. Water, in a chemical horizon, behaves more rigidly than witchcraft in an early modern legal horizon. But the theory does not need to declare all meanings equally constructed or all meanings equally real. It asks how broad the support is and how faithfully the idea propagates.

A fourth dispute concerns individual genius against collective transmission. The framework does not deny invention. It also does not imagine invention outside chains. Novelty appears as emergence within composition and transmission. A poet, scientist, or entrepreneur may generate a genuinely new section, but the novelty becomes historically real only as it is taken up, stabilized, misread, repaired, and transmitted across horizons.

A fifth dispute concerns whether context destroys rigor. Many philosophers feared that once meaning becomes context-relative, anything goes. The horizon functor says the opposite. Context-dependence is not the absence of structure; it is structure indexed by horizons. A translation may preserve composition and identity while failing to preserve inference. That failure is not a shrug. It is the exact phenomenon to be studied.

## Disputes that sharpen

The first sharpened dispute is about consciousness. The framework can derive intentionality, perspective, affect, and reflexion. It can describe a self applying its own algebra to itself. It can model the difference between third-person and first-person horizons. But whether this captures phenomenal feel is not settled. The theory makes the question sharper: is consciousness an invariant of reflexive horizon structure, or does it require a primitive not present in the algebra?

The second sharpened dispute is about moral content. Signed valence tells us how an idea attracts or repels an agent within a horizon. Credence tells us how belief can be made compatible with horizon shifts. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem tells us when moral interpretations glue across perspectives. But none of that proves that a moral claim is true. It tells us what stability, conflict, and repair look like. The hard question remains whether normativity is exhausted by stable trans-horizonal valence and inference.

The third sharpened dispute is about mathematics. Mathematical ideas often look maximally profound because they travel across many horizons and propagate faithfully. A proof in one language can often be translated into another. Yet the metaphysical status of mathematical objects is not settled by that fact. The framework can describe why mathematical content behaves as if it were global. It does not by itself decide whether numbers, sets, or categories exist independently of all horizons.

The fourth sharpened dispute concerns translation. Before the framework, disagreements about translation often blurred together vocabulary, inference, reference, and practice. The indexical pullback theorem separates them. A translation may carry words, preserve some resonance, break an inference, and shift signed valence. The question becomes precise: which structural elements survive the horizon shift, and which do not?

The fifth sharpened dispute concerns emergence. Many people use emergence to mean surprise. Others use it to mean irreducibility. Others use it to mean ignorance. Idea Theory demands a stronger test. If a result is merely recombination inside an already closed lineage, it is not deep emergence. If it produces content outside that closure and the emergence remains coherent across chains, then the claim has teeth.

## Why dissolving is not dismissing

To dissolve a dispute is not to say that the disputants were confused. Often it means they were each protecting a genuine feature. Locke, Hume, Kant, Hegel, Saussure, Wittgenstein, Kuhn, and Rosch were not arguing about nothing. They were emphasizing different projections of the same structure. A dispute dissolves when the framework can locate both positions as special cases without flattening their differences.

To sharpen a dispute is not to solve it by decree. It means that evasion becomes harder. If someone says a concept is universal, ask for its horizon support. If someone says a translation is faithful, ask whether inference and valence are preserved. If someone says consciousness is just computation, ask which derived primitives have been constructed and whether phenomenal character remains. If someone says a moral truth is objective, ask what kind of trans-horizonal stability is being claimed.

## The test for a genuine dispute

A genuine dispute remains after both sides can state the same horizon, the same composition, and the same standard of preservation. If the disagreement disappears once those are named, it was mostly a dispute about vocabulary or scale. If the disagreement remains, it has become more interesting. For example, two people may agree that moral language is horizon-relative and still disagree about whether any moral section deserves authority across horizons. That is a real dispute.

## Chapter summary

The framework dissolves disputes that confuse different horizons or different structural projections. It sharpens disputes about consciousness, moral truth, mathematics, translation, and emergence by forcing each claim to name the exact structure it requires.
