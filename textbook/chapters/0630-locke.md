# Chapter 43 — Locke

John Locke lived from 1632 to 1704 in England and the Dutch Republic, within early modern empiricism and liberal political thought. In his own translated words: “No man’s knowledge here can go beyond his experience.”

## The position in its own voice

Hear the doctrine in its own intellectual voice. Locke's Essay Concerning Human Understanding opens with a polemical denial of innate ideas. The mind, at birth, is a blank slate ( tabula rasa ), and all the materials of knowledge come from experience. Experience itself has two springs: sensation, by which external objects supply ideas of color, sound, shape, motion; and reflection, by which the mind observes its own operations of perceiving, doubting, willing, believing. Ideas come in two grades. Simple ideas are atoms received passively from sensation or reflection - the mind cannot manufacture them, and any deficit in the supply (e.g., congenital blindness) leaves a corresponding gap in possible thought. Complex ideas are built by the mind out of simples, by combining, comparing, or abstracting; here the mind is active. Among complex ideas, Locke distinguishes ideas of substances, modes, and relations, and develops a famous account of "real" versus "nominal" essence. The real essence of gold is its underlying microstructure, knowable in principle but not by us; the nominal essence is the cluster of observable properties - yellow, malleable, heavy, soluble in aqua regia - by which we sort things into the kind "gold." Sortal terms refer through nominal essences. Personal identity, on Locke's account, is not the persistence of substance but of consciousness. The voice trains attention by asking what is stable, what must be discarded, and what kind of insight appears only after that discipline has been accepted.

## The idea algebra sub-instance

Before translating, recall the operating definitions. Ideas compose with other ideas. Composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. Ideas resonate with one another, and their non-reciprocal pull is meaning curvature. Ideas also live over horizons: contexts, stages, perspectives, times, practices, or worlds. Locke treats simple ideas from sensation and reflection as generators. Composition builds complex ideas by combining, comparing, and abstracting. The preservation part is experiential content, the suppression part removes particular circumstances for general use, and the emergence part is the complex idea or nominal essence. Resonance is co-occurrence in experience and public naming. Degree measures epistemic strength from bare idea to knowledge and probability. In plain English, the sub-instance asks what counts as an idea, how ideas combine, what survives, what is cancelled, and what new content appears.

## Central claim as theorem

*If all materials of thought come from experience, then complex knowledge is constrained by the simple ideas sensation and reflection supply.*

The theorem rules out the easy opposite of the view. It predicts that across the proper horizons some content will travel, some will fail, and some will arise only through that failure. The theorem identifies the middle structure: invariant, obstruction, or gluing.

Carefully restated, choose the relevant horizon category and let the horizon functor - the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi - assign a complete idea algebra to each horizon. Name composition, identity, resonance, the preservation part, the suppression part, the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, and meaning curvature. Then the theorem says that Locke's central claim is exactly the stable support, failed pullback, or glued agreement described in the preceding paragraph, stated without symbols.

## What the formalism adds

First, the tabula rasa becomes the algebraic claim that the initial state of the idea carrier has no pre-installed generators. All elements of the idea carrier at any time are reachable from the identity role by composition with empirically supplied simples. The empiricism is then a thesis about the bootstrapping of the algebra, not about any particular content. Second, the Aufhebung theorem supplies a unique decomposition theorem that vindicates Lockean analysis. When Locke proposes to analyze any complex idea down to its simples, the formalism guarantees the analysis terminates and is essentially unique. More generally, the formalism adds comparison. It tells the listener whether preservation, suppression, or emergence is carrying the doctrine.

## Where the formalism diverges

First, the nominal/real essence distinction does not map cleanly to a single degree. Real essence is not a higher-degree complex; it is a kind of element the algebra cannot contain at all on Locke's epistemology. The formalism handles this only by treating real essence as a placeholder beyond the algebra, which is consistent but extra-structural. The algebra is therefore an aid to hearing, not a substitute for reading.

## Horizon category and horizon-functor theorem

Locke's the horizon category consists of experiential horizons built from sensation, reflection, simple ideas, complex ideas, and public language. Morphisms compose or abstract from received simple data, but they may lose the real essences that underwrite substances outside the mind. intentionality marks ideas as signs of qualities, rigid reference is fragile because names bind to nominal essences, and credence fits Locke's graded probability where demonstrative knowledge runs out. In this chapter the most central horizon-functor theorem is the Profundity theorem. It names gluing, obstruction, or depth as the central horizon behavior.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

intentionality. Ideas of sensation and reflection are intentional contents supplied by experience to the understanding. differentiation. Nominal essences classify by observed differences among qualities rather than by known real essences. credence. Assent comes in degrees, with probable judgment governing where demonstrative knowledge is unavailable. rigid reference. Substance is a problematic rigid support, the supposed “something I know not what” beneath qualities. These primitives are consequences of the way composition, resonance, degree, and horizon translation behave here.

One more listening cue helps. Ask, at every turn, whether the thinker is protecting a stable reference, exposing a failed translation, or gathering scattered meanings into a whole. That question keeps the formal reconstruction close to the philosophical voice rather than letting it become a merely external classification.

## Chapter summary

Locke gives one answer to what an idea is, how it combines, and how it remains or fails to remain meaningful across horizons. The idea algebra restates that answer by naming preserved content, suppressed illusion, emergent insight, horizon behavior, and diagnostic derived primitives. The gain is clarity; the cost is abstraction. A good listener keeps both in view.
