# Chapter 117 — Charles Jones on Semi-Endogenous Growth

If ideas are so powerful, why did growth rates not explode when research employment rose dramatically? Charles Jones asked this uncomfortable question of optimistic endogenous growth theory. His answer is sobering: ideas remain productive, but the easy ones are found first. The frontier moves away from us as we advance toward it.

## Biographical preamble

Charles Jones, born in the United States in 1967, is an American macroeconomist known for work on growth, productivity, research, and the economics of ideas. His semi-endogenous growth models corrected earlier claims about permanent growth effects from research intensity. In his own words, ideas are getting harder to find. That sentence turns progress into a temporal horizon problem.

## Position

Jones observed that modern economies employ vastly more researchers than before, yet long-run growth rates have not risen proportionally. If research effort alone permanently raised growth, the data should look different. The semi-endogenous answer is that research productivity declines as the accessible frontier is exhausted. More effort is needed to maintain growth because later discoveries are harder, more specialized, and more dependent on prior accumulation. Population growth and the size of the research workforce matter, but they do not remove the fishing-out effect.

## Algebraic re-statement

In Idea Theory, the emergence cocycle measures the novelty produced when ideas compose. Romer emphasizes that non-rival ideas remain available. Jones adds a bound and a temporal frontier. Early horizons contain many high-yield compositions. Later horizons contain more already-tried pairings, narrower problems, and heavier prerequisites. The preservation part carries accumulated knowledge; the suppression part removes already exhausted options; the emergence part remains real but harder to obtain. Degree or grading still rises, but not at an automatically accelerating rate.

## Central claim as theorem

*When emergence is bounded by the moving research horizon, research intensity alone cannot permanently raise growth.*

The theorem rules out the simple extrapolation from more researchers to ever faster growth. It predicts that economies may need increasing research effort merely to hold a steady growth path. It also predicts that frontier position matters: the same number of researchers can have different effects in early and late horizons. Growth remains idea-driven, but idea discovery is historically situated.

Carefully stated, let composition combine existing ideas at a research frontier, let identity preserve the accumulated stock, let the resonance pairing measure tractable fit between problems and tools, and let the emergence cocycle record new productive capacity. If the horizon functor assigns later frontiers lower expected emergence for comparable effort, and if the emergence part is bounded within each finite research window, then total profundity cannot rise permanently from effort alone. This is T15 in limiting form, because support persists but emergence closure is constrained.

## What formalism adds

The formalism makes fishing out precise without making it mysterious. It is not that ideas stop mattering. It is that the horizon category changes. A composition that would have been easy in an early field may already be absorbed into the identity of the discipline, while the remaining combinations require rarer schemas and more distant transfers. Meaning curvature appears when a research tool has high force in one frontier and low force in another. The horizon functor turns historical position into structure.

## Where it diverges

Jones works with macroeconomic models, data, and production functions. Idea Theory offers a qualitative algebraic restatement, not a calibrated growth equation. It does not estimate research productivity or decide the exact rate at which ideas get harder to find. It also risks making the frontier sound too orderly. Real science includes surprise, institutional bottlenecks, funding shifts, and accidental discoveries that can reopen apparently exhausted spaces.

## Horizon structure

The horizon category is ordered by research time: early accessible problems, mature fields, hard frontiers, educational systems, laboratories, and policy environments. Morphisms are training, frontier advance, funding reallocation, instrumentation, and paradigm shift. The horizon functor assigns effort different expected resonance at different stages. T15 is the relevant theorem label, but with emphasis on bounded emergence rather than unlimited expansion.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

The diagnostic primitives are temporality, potentiality, credence, and schema. Temporality is central because productivity depends on where the field stands in its sequence. Potentiality remains, but it is less accessible. Credence guides research investment under uncertain payoff. Schema names the paradigms that define tractable problems. Differentiation also matters, because new fields can raise productivity by creating fresh, non-redundant pairings.

A concrete example is pharmaceutical research. Early discoveries may identify powerful classes of drugs. Later work often requires larger trials, finer molecular targeting, complex regulation, and deeper background knowledge. More researchers may enter the field, but each successful new treatment can demand more coordination than the last. The idea frontier has not disappeared. It has become more remote, more expensive, and more dependent on specialized horizons. Jones turns this pattern into a correction to simple growth optimism.

The important point for the listener is that bounded emergence is not pessimism. It is discipline. A mature field can still produce transformative ideas, especially when new instruments or distant schemas reopen the frontier. But the formalism asks us to distinguish the existence of potentiality from its accessibility. A horizon may contain possible discoveries that are real in principle and unreachable in practice until training, tools, or institutions change.

This gives policy a different tone. Subsidizing research can matter greatly, but it is not a magic lever that abolishes horizon difficulty. Education, instrumentation, data infrastructure, immigration, and scientific openness may change the horizon itself, making previously inaccessible compositions tractable. Jones's correction therefore does not say stop investing in ideas. It says understand whether investment is adding effort at the same frontier or transforming the frontier's conditions of emergence.

The same lens also helps compare fields. Some sciences slow because their questions are exhausted; others slow because their tools lag behind their questions. A new instrument can change the horizon category and make old potentiality newly reachable.

## Chapter summary

Jones corrects the exuberant story of ideas with a frontier constraint. Idea Theory restates semi-endogenous growth as bounded emergence under a moving horizon functor. Research preserves accumulated identity and still creates novelty, but T15 is limited by temporality: later horizons make each new emergence harder to obtain.
