# Chapter 138 — David Lewis on Convention

A convention is not merely a habit. It is a stable way of coordinating because each participant expects the others to do their part, knows that they expect the same, and has reason to continue. David Lewis made this familiar social fact exact, and Idea Theory reads it as a stable section of a meaning horizon.

## Biographical preamble

David Lewis lived from 1941 to 2001, worked mainly at Princeton, and became one of the most influential analytic philosophers of modality, language, and convention. In his own terms, a convention is a regularity sustained because everyone conforms, everyone expects conformity, and alternatives could have served. His book Convention gave language a game theoretic backbone.

## Position

Lewis begins from coordination problems. If drivers must choose a side of the road, either left or right can work, provided most people choose the same side. The rule is not naturally forced. It is stable because each person's best response depends on what others do. Language has the same structure. A sound, gesture, or mark can mean this rather than that because a community has settled into a mutually expected regularity.

The content of a convention is contingent. Another community could use another word, another signal, another ritual. Yet once established, the convention binds expectation. If I say bank in a familiar context, I rely on a shared background that lets you hear financial institution or river edge without inventing the language anew.

## Algebraic re-statement

In Idea Theory, a convention is an element in an idea algebra that has become stable under repeated composition. Composition is the recurrence of coordinated use. Identity is the neutral background before a particular regularity is selected. The resonance pairing measures how well the regularity satisfies the expectations and purposes of the group.

The preservation part carries the signal from one occasion to the next. The suppression part removes rival signals that would disrupt coordination. The emergence part is the social fact that the signal now counts as the convention. Degree or grading helps track the cost of learning, remembering, and applying the convention. A simple convention with high resonance and low degree tends to travel well.

## Central claim as theorem

*A convention is a stable local meaning that becomes global when expectations glue across a community horizon.*

This is the hermeneutic sheaf theorem read through Lewis. The theorem rules out the idea that convention is either private choice or brute natural necessity. It predicts that stable meaning appears where local expectations overlap and reinforce each other. If two villages use the same signal differently, there may be two local sections. If trade, schooling, and repeated correction align them, those sections can glue into one larger convention.

Carefully restated, the horizon category contains agents, signals, occasions, payoff expectations, and background alternatives. The horizon functor assigns to each horizon the regularities available for coordination. Composition repeats successful use. Identity is the absence of a selected convention. The resonance pairing measures mutual fit between signal, response, and expectation. The preservation part carries the pattern, the suppression part sidelines competing regularities, and the emergence part is the shared social standing of the convention. The diagnostic derived primitive is schema, because a convention supplies a repeatable pattern of action.

## What formalism adds

The formalism clarifies why Lewis needs both stability and contingency. Stability is not the denial of alternatives. It is the fact that, after one alternative has become resonant across the community, deviation becomes costly or unintelligible. The same algebra can contain many possible regularities, but only some become self sustaining under the horizon functor.

It also connects convention to meaning without reducing meaning to a dictionary. A word means by being nested in a practice of expectation, correction, and uptake. The formalism can describe a local slang term, a legal formula, a handshake, and a traffic rule in one vocabulary. Each is a regularity whose preservation part is repeated, whose suppression part excludes rivals, and whose emergence part creates reliable social anticipation.

Think of a password shared by a small group. At first it is just a chosen word. After repeated successful use, it becomes a convention within that group. If an outsider says the word without the right occasion, the resonance pairing is weak, because the horizon of membership and expectation is missing. If the group changes the password, the old signal is suppressed and the new one emerges as the live convention. Nothing about the sound alone decides the matter. Convention is the whole coordinated pattern: selection, expectation, recognition, and continuing use. Lewis gives us the social mechanics by which arbitrary signs become binding without ceasing to be arbitrary.

## Where it diverges

Lewis gave common knowledge a central role. Idea Theory can represent common knowledge as horizon gluing, but the algebraic language may understate the psychology of belief about belief. People often follow conventions through habit, trust, imitation, or institutional pressure without explicitly representing the whole expectation structure. The formalism diverges if it makes convention too intellectual. Lewis helps prevent that by reminding us that coordination is practical before it is theoretical.

## Horizon structure

Lewis's horizon is strategic and communal. It includes agents, their expectations, the possible signals, and the alternative equilibrating patterns that might have been selected. It most centrally instantiates the hermeneutic sheaf theorem, because convention exists when local regularities agree on overlaps and become a global pattern for a population. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem also explains why fragmented conventions can coexist without contradiction when their horizons do not overlap.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

The diagnostic derived primitives are schema, credence, and signed valence. Schema names the repeatable pattern. Credence names the expectation that others will conform. Signed valence names the reward of coordination and the irritation, penalty, or confusion attached to deviation.

## Chapter summary

Lewis shows that language is a coordination achievement. Idea Theory restates convention as a stable regularity whose resonance pairing is reinforced by mutual expectation. Composition repeats use, suppression excludes rivals, and emergence gives the convention social standing. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem is central because conventions become real when local expectations glue into a shared horizon. The diagnostic lesson is that convention turns arbitrary selection into reliable public meaning through repeated expectation.
