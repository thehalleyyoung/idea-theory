# Chapter 113 — Kenneth Arrow on Information as a Commodity

Information is strange because learning it changes the learner. A sack of wheat can be inspected, bought, resold, and returned in roughly the same world. An invention, a proof, or a market forecast cannot. Once you hear it, your horizon has already shifted. Kenneth Arrow turns this simple fact into a deep problem for markets, and Idea Theory reads it as a problem about composition without reversal.

## Biographical preamble

Kenneth Arrow, born in New York in 1921 and died in 2017, worked in welfare economics, social choice theory, risk, and the economics of information. He belonged to the mathematical economics tradition, but his best insights often began with plain institutional puzzles. In his own words, information is a commodity with peculiar attributes. That mild phrase hides a radical claim: knowledge cannot be priced like an ordinary object because disclosure partly destroys the very transaction it enables.

## Position

Arrow asked how a buyer can decide whether to pay for information. If the seller fully reveals the information, the buyer no longer needs to buy it. If the seller withholds it, the buyer cannot know its value. This is not a temporary inconvenience caused by bad contracts. It is a structural defect in the commodity form. The price system works best when goods can be compared before exchange and possession can be transferred without changing the buyer's capacity to judge. Information breaks both conditions. It has social value beyond private reward, so competitive markets tend to underproduce invention and discovery.

## Algebraic re-statement

In Idea Theory, the buyer, the seller, and the idea occupy different horizons. The buyer has a pre-disclosure horizon in which the resonance pairing with the idea is only a credence. The seller has a possession horizon in which the same idea has determinate identity. Disclosure is a composition of buyer and idea. That composition has no clean inverse, because after learning, the buyer cannot return to ignorance. The preservation part carries what the buyer already understood; the suppression part cancels prior uncertainty; the emergence part is the new evaluative capacity created by learning. The identity of the idea remains citeable, but the identity of the buyer's horizon changes.

## Central claim as theorem

*Information cannot receive an efficient ordinary price when valuation requires irreversible disclosure.*

The theorem rules out the hope that better bargaining alone can make information behave like a standard commodity. It predicts systematic underinvestment in invention, secrecy, patents, licensing, and other institutions that try to create partial excludability around something whose use naturally spreads. The central obstruction is not greed or ignorance. It is that the act needed to measure value is itself a transformative act.

Carefully stated, let composition join a buyer horizon with an information idea, let identity mark the repeatable content of that idea, let the resonance pairing measure the buyer's capacity to value it, and let the preservation part, suppression part, and emergence part describe what disclosure keeps, cancels, and creates. If the pre-disclosure resonance pairing cannot determine the post-disclosure resonance pairing, and if the composition cannot be undone by any identity-preserving return to ignorance, then no price rule preserves valuation across the transaction. This is a T14 obstruction, because inference fails to pull back from the informed horizon to the uninformed horizon.

## What formalism adds

The formalism makes Arrow's paradox less like a market anecdote and more like a theorem of learning. Information is not merely hard to inspect. It changes the structure that inspects it. The horizon functor assigns one valuation algebra before disclosure and another afterward. Meaning curvature appears because the path from ignorance to knowledge is not symmetric with any imagined path back. Patents, trade secrecy, and reputation are attempts to stabilize identity across horizons while limiting full composition.

## Where it diverges

Arrow often treats the problem in sharp before and after terms. Real information comes in samples, abstracts, demonstrations, partial disclosures, and trusted signals. Idea Theory can represent these as graded horizons, but the simple Arrow paradox becomes more delicate there. A buyer may learn enough to raise credence without receiving the whole idea. The formalism also does not decide which institution is best. It explains why every institution is compensating for a structural asymmetry.

## Horizon structure

The horizon category contains seller informed, buyer uninformed, buyer after disclosure, patent market, public domain, and imitation horizon. Morphisms are disclosure, licensing, publication, patenting, and copying. The horizon functor assigns each horizon a different valuation algebra because the resonance pairing changes when the buyer learns. T14 is the relevant theorem label: the same idea does not support the same inference in pre-disclosure and post-disclosure horizons.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

The diagnostic primitives are credence, perspective, rigid reference, and temporality. Credence names the buyer's estimate before revelation. Perspective names the asymmetry between seller and buyer. Rigid reference appears when a patent, formula, or title lets the idea be named across markets. Temporality matters because learning has a before and an after. Potentiality also matters, since undisclosed information carries possible value without yet being evaluable.

A concrete example helps. Suppose a firm sells a manufacturing trick that lowers energy use. The buyer wants proof, but full proof teaches the trick. A vague hint raises credence but may not justify the price. A patent gives rigid reference, yet it also publishes enough identity for others to imitate around its boundary. Trade secrecy protects the seller, yet it narrows the buyer's horizon and weakens valuation. Every institutional solution moves the obstruction rather than removing it. This is why Arrow's chapter belongs to the logic of horizons: information has to be partly known to be priced, yet knowing is already a composition.

The audiobook moral is simple. When you hear the word information, do not picture a sealed box. Picture a door that changes the person who opens it. The seller can guard the door, label it, insure it, or license passage through it, but once the buyer passes through, the old buyer horizon is gone. That is the irreversible act around which the economics of invention is organized.

## Chapter summary

Arrow teaches that information is not a normal commodity because its valuation depends on irreversible learning. Idea Theory restates this as horizon-changing composition: disclosure preserves some identity, suppresses ignorance, creates new evaluative capacity, and blocks the pullback needed for ordinary pricing. The result is T14 in economic form.
