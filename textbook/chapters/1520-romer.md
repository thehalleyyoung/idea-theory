# Chapter 115 — Paul Romer on Endogenous Growth

Some goods are used up when used. Ideas are not. If you use a recipe, a theorem, or a design, I can use it too. Paul Romer made this simple fact the center of growth theory. Long-run growth is not only more labor and more machines. It is the expanding power of non-rival ideas to be copied, taught, protected, and recombined.

## Biographical preamble

Paul Romer, born in Denver in 1955, is an American economist associated with endogenous growth theory and the economics of technological change. He brought ideas, research, and imperfect competition into the core of growth modeling. In his own words, economic growth occurs whenever people take resources and rearrange them in ways that make them more valuable. The rearrangement is the point: knowledge changes what the same world can do.

## Position

Romer argued that technology should not be treated as an unexplained force falling from outside the economy. People invest in research because ideas can generate returns. Yet ideas are special. They are non-rival, so one person's use does not prevent another's use. They are often partly excludable, so patents, secrecy, and firms can capture some reward. This mixture lets markets finance discovery while letting society benefit from diffusion. Growth becomes endogenous because the creation of ideas is itself an economic activity.

## Algebraic re-statement

In Idea Theory, a technological idea is an element that can enter many compositions without being depleted. Its identity persists across firms, machines, manuals, and classrooms. The resonance pairing measures productive fit between idea and use context. Non-rivalry means that composition with one user does not consume the idea's capacity to resonate with another. The preservation part carries the repeatable design. The suppression part removes older constraints. The emergence part is the new productive possibility opened when an idea meets a process, material, or market. Degree or grading tracks how much productive reach the idea gains across horizons.

## Central claim as theorem

*Non-rival ideas can sustain growth because use preserves identity while enlarging horizon support.*

The theorem rules out the picture of growth as only accumulation of rival inputs. It predicts that teaching, copying, licensing, and recombination can raise total productivity without requiring the original idea to be consumed. It also predicts institutional tension: if ideas diffuse too freely, private incentives weaken; if they are locked too tightly, social growth is slowed. Non-rivalry is powerful precisely because it separates use from depletion.

Carefully stated, let composition join an idea with many production horizons, let identity mark the same design across uses, let the resonance pairing measure productive fit, and let degree or grading record the idea's breadth of support. If repeated composition preserves the idea's identity and does not reduce its resonance pairing with further horizons, then the preservation part remains available while the emergence part can generate new applications. The resulting idea is profound when its support and productive closure expand across horizons. This is T15, because profundity depends on stable trans-horizon support and growing emergence.

## What formalism adds

The formalism distinguishes non-rivalry from mere publicity. An idea may be non-rival even if legally restricted, and it may be public without being easy to use. The horizon functor shows why: the same identity can travel, but resonance depends on skills, complementary tools, institutions, and schemas. Meaning curvature appears when an idea valuable in one production horizon has little force in another. Romer's insight becomes a claim about preserved identity plus expanding potentiality.

## Where it diverges

Romer's economic models depend on incentives, research labor, monopoly rents, and the cost of discovery. Idea Theory captures non-rival use more directly than costly invention. It can say why an idea remains available after use, but it does not by itself price the laboratory, the failed experiments, or the firm strategy that made discovery possible. The formalism also treats degree in broad structural terms, while empirical growth models require measured productivity.

## Horizon structure

The horizon category contains research labs, firms, patent offices, production lines, classrooms, software repositories, and consumer markets. Morphisms are copying, licensing, teaching, adapting, applying, and recombining. The horizon functor assigns the same idea different local resonance pairings while preserving its identity across many uses. T15 is the relevant theorem label: an idea becomes economically profound when its non-rival identity has wide support and its emergence part remains fertile.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

The diagnostic primitives are potentiality, temporality, rigid reference, and schema. Potentiality is central because each idea contains unrealized applications. Temporality appears as accumulation through research and diffusion. Rigid reference is supplied by patents, formulas, names, and standards that keep identity stable. Schema names the research and production paradigms that make an idea usable. Credence also matters, since investors fund research under uncertainty.

A concrete example is software. One team writes an algorithm, another adapts it for logistics, a school teaches it to students, and a hospital uses a version for scheduling. The original idea is not consumed by any of these uses. Its identity is preserved through names, documentation, tests, and examples, while its resonance changes with each horizon. The same is true of a chemical formula, a crop rotation method, or a manufacturing design. The social value of the idea grows because use uncovers further uses.

This is why Romer changes the listener's picture of wealth. A machine can be scarce in the ordinary way, but the design of a better machine can spread while remaining itself. Legal excludability then becomes a horizon-management problem. Too little protection may suppress discovery incentives; too much protection may suppress diffusion and recombination. The formalism keeps both sides visible by separating identity, access, resonance, and emergence.

The same point applies to education. Teaching an idea does not divide it among students like slices of bread. It multiplies the horizons in which the idea can act. Each learner may carry the identity of the idea into a different context, where a new resonance becomes possible. Human capital is therefore not only stored skill; it is a distribution system for non-rival identity. Romer's growth mechanism depends on this spreading capacity.

## Chapter summary

Romer places ideas at the heart of growth because they can be used without being used up. Idea Theory restates this as preserved identity under repeated composition, with expanding resonance across production horizons. The result is T15 in growth form: non-rival ideas become profound when their support and emergence remain open over time.
