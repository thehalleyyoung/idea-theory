# Appendix E — Suggested Listening Routes

The book can be heard straight through, but it was also built for routes. Part Six, the Lean formalization, is intentionally omitted from this audiobook. For those interested in the formal Lean verification, see the project website. The audiobook gives the concepts, the theorem stories, the zoos, and the closing synthesis, but it does not narrate the machine-checked proofs.

## The philosophy major's path

Start with the welcome and the one-sitting overview. Then listen to the formal core through the horizon functor and derived primitives. You do not need every theorem proof on first pass, but you should hear the theorem review. Then move to the philosophy zoo: antiquity, medieval thought, early modern philosophy, German idealism, phenomenology, structuralism, pragmatism, analytic philosophy, and non-Western traditions. End with Part Seven and Appendices B, C, and D.

This route asks: which old disputes were about different horizons, and which remain genuinely unresolved?

## The cognitive scientist's path

Begin with the overview, then hear the chapters on composition, resonance, decomposition, emergence, grading, meaning curvature, horizons, and derived primitives. After that, go to the cognitive science and artificial intelligence zoo. Pay special attention to prototype theory, situated conceptualization, blending, conceptual metaphor, frame semantics, distributional semantics, transformers, cultural evolution, emergence, and the philosophy of mind chapters.

This route asks: what empirical signatures should appear if concepts are horizon-relative resonance structures?

## The economist or game theorist's path

Begin with the overview and the formal core in summary. Make sure to hear transmission chains, structural equivalence, grading, the hermeneutic sheaf theorem, the indexical pullback theorem, and the profundity theorem. Then move to the social-science and game-theory zoo: Arrow, Hayek, Romer, Weitzman, Schumpeter, Kahneman and Tversky, Ostrom, von Neumann and Morgenstern, Nash, Schelling, Aumann, Selten, Bourdieu, Latour, and the Wittgenstein-Nash bridge.

This route asks: when do local signals, preferences, and conventions glue into a stable social order?

## The Lean-curious path

Because Part Six is intentionally not included in this audiobook, the Lean-curious listener should use the website as the technical companion. Hear Parts Zero, One, and Two in the audiobook, then visit the project website for the formal Lean verification, theorem files, and proof pages. Return to Appendix C afterward, because the plain-language theorem glossary will make more sense once you have seen how the proofs are organized.

This route asks: how can informal philosophical claims be made auditable without losing their human meaning?

## The complete bath

The complete bath is every chapter in order: orientation, formal core, theorem stories, philosophy zoo, cognitive science and artificial intelligence zoo, social-science and game-theory zoo, Part Seven, and all appendices. It is meant as roughly forty hours of listening, depending on narration speed. Do it while walking, commuting, cooking, or recovering from other forms of noise.

This route asks the largest question: can one compact formal grammar help you hear the history of ideas, the science of concepts, and the social life of knowledge as one continuous conversation?

## A first-week plan

If you want a practical seven-day route, hear the welcome and overview on day one. Hear composition, resonance, and the threefold decomposition on day two. Hear emergence, grading, curvature, and horizons on day three. Hear the derived primitives on day four. Hear the theorem review on day five. Sample one philosophy chapter, one cognitive science chapter, and one social-science chapter on day six. Hear Part Seven on day seven. After that, choose your specialized route.

## A re-listening route

On a second pass, do not try to hear everything. Choose one motif: resonance, horizon, emergence, or translation. Follow only that motif through the chapters. This turns the book from an encyclopedia into a set of variations. The same chapter will sound different when the guiding question changes. That is not a problem. It is the method of horizons applied to listening itself.

## A route for teachers and reading groups

For a seminar, assign the overview, one formal-core chapter, one theorem chapter, and two zoo chapters each week. Ask participants to bring one example from outside the book and translate it into the vocabulary: composition, resonance, horizon, preservation, suppression, emergence, and curvature. The discussion will quickly reveal which parts of the framework have become usable and which remain only memorized.

For a reading group, rotate roles. One listener tracks examples. One tracks definitions. One tracks objections. One tracks empirical predictions. This makes the group itself a small horizon category, with each participant supplying a local reading that may or may not glue.

## A route for skeptics

If you are skeptical of grand theories, do not begin with the whole system. Begin with the limits chapter, then hear the disputes chapter, then hear the empirical predictions chapter. After that, choose one theorem and one zoo chapter where you already know the material. Ask whether the framework clarifies anything you did not already have words for. If it does not, keep the objection. If it does, move outward slowly.

The skeptical route is important because the book should not win by atmosphere. It should win, where it wins, by making distinctions easier to use.

## Appendix summary

Choose the route that matches your purpose. The whole book is available, but the best first route is the one that gives you enough structure to start using the vocabulary immediately.
