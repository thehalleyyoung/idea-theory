# Chapter 119 — Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky on Framing

The same outcome can feel different when described as a gain or a loss. That fact sounds small until it breaks the classical image of rational choice. Daniel Kahneman, pronounced *KAH-nuh-man*, and Amos Tversky, pronounced *TVER-skee*, showed that decision-making is shaped by frames, reference points, and asymmetric feeling. Idea Theory reads this as horizon-dependent resonance.

## Biographical preamble

Daniel Kahneman, born in Tel Aviv in 1934 and died in 2024, and Amos Tversky, born in Haifa in 1937 and died in 1996, were Israeli psychologists whose collaboration founded much of behavioral economics. Their work joined experimental psychology with decision theory. In their own words, losses loom larger than gains. That sentence condenses prospect theory's challenge to expected utility.

## Position

Classical expected utility theory expects preferences to remain stable under equivalent descriptions. Kahneman and Tversky showed that people do not choose that way. They evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point. They dislike losses more intensely than they like equal gains. They overweight small probabilities and respond differently when the same choice is framed as saving lives or losing lives. The frame is not decoration. It helps constitute the decision.

## Algebraic re-statement

In Idea Theory, a frame is a horizon-setting idea that changes the resonance pairing between a prospect and a preference structure. The underlying lottery may keep identity, but its decision force changes when composed with a gain frame or a loss frame. The preservation part carries the extensional outcome. The suppression part hides alternative descriptions. The emergence part is the felt choice situation created by the frame. Meaning curvature names the asymmetry in how gains and losses resonate around a reference point. Signed valence is the derived primitive that gives the frame affective direction.

## Central claim as theorem

*Choice is not invariant across frames when reference-dependent resonance changes the decision horizon.*

The theorem rules out the expectation that equivalent outcome descriptions must yield equivalent preferences. It predicts framing effects whenever the same prospect is composed with different schemas that alter credence, attention, or signed valence. It also predicts that so-called irrationality may be structurally normal: a non-neutral horizon can change what counts as the salient comparison.

Carefully stated, let identity preserve the extensional prospect across descriptions, let composition join that prospect with a gain frame or loss frame, let the resonance pairing measure preference force, and let meaning curvature register asymmetric evaluation around the reference point. If the preservation part keeps the described outcome while the suppression part and emergence part differ across frames, then the same prospect supports different choices in different horizons. This is T14, because the choice inference fails to pull back unchanged across framing morphisms.

## What formalism adds

The formalism makes framing neither a trick nor a mere bias label. A frame is a horizon operator. It changes which features are preserved, which alternatives are suppressed, and which valuation emerges. The horizon functor assigns different local algebras to gain, loss, certainty, risk, and reference shift. Meaning curvature explains why reversing the description is not neutral. The listener should hear this as a structural claim: people do not evaluate naked outcomes; they evaluate outcomes under horizons.

## Where it diverges

Kahneman and Tversky's achievement was empirical and quantitative. They measured patterns, elicited choices, and estimated the shape of the value function. Idea Theory does not supply those numbers. It explains why frame-dependence is expected, but it does not tell us the exact strength of loss aversion or probability distortion. The formalism can also sound too tidy for actual psychology, where memory, attention, emotion, culture, and experiment design interact messily.

## Horizon structure

The horizon category contains gain frames, loss frames, reference points, probability presentations, experimental prompts, and decision contexts. Morphisms are reframings that preserve the extensional prospect while changing salience and permitted inference. The horizon functor assigns different resonance pairings and signed valence profiles to the same underlying prospect. T14 is the theorem label: prospect theory is the canonical case where inference does not survive a horizon shift.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

The diagnostic primitives are credence, signed valence, perspective, and schema. Credence appears in subjective probability weighting. Signed valence gives gains and losses their unequal force. Perspective marks the decision-maker's situated reference point. Schema names the experimental or everyday frame that codes an outcome as gain, loss, risk, or certainty. Affect also matters because valuation is felt, not merely ranked.

A concrete example is a medical choice described in two ways. One description says a treatment saves many patients. Another says a different number will die. The outcomes may match, but listeners often choose differently. The gain frame preserves survival; the loss frame foregrounds death. Each suppresses a different comparison and generates a different felt decision. Prospect theory begins from these stable reversals and shows that they are not random mistakes. They are patterned horizon effects.

For Idea Theory, this means rationality cannot be tested only by stripping language down to outcomes. The description is part of the cognitive situation. A frame selects identity, orders attention, assigns signed valence, and changes credence. The same person may be locally coherent within each frame while failing invariance across frames. Kahneman and Tversky therefore reveal a deep feature of meaning: choice depends on how a prospect is made present.

This also changes how we hear experiments. A laboratory prompt is not a transparent window onto preference. It is a small manufactured horizon. The wording, order, baseline, and response options help determine which identity the participant perceives. That does not make the result artificial in a dismissive sense. Everyday decisions are framed too. The experiment reveals a general fact by controlling the frame rather than pretending frames can be removed.

The practical lesson is humility. When advising, voting, investing, or choosing medical care, we should ask not only what outcome is offered, but what frame is composing our attention before choice begins.

## Chapter summary

Kahneman and Tversky show that framing changes choice. Idea Theory restates this as T14: identity of outcome can be preserved while resonance, suppression, emergence, and meaning curvature shift across horizons. The same prospect is not the same decision when the frame changes.
