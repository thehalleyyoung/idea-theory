# Chapter 76 — Donald Davidson

This chapter treats Donald Davidson as a test case for idea theory. We keep the operating
definitions audible: composition puts ideas together, the identity idea leaves a composition
unchanged, the resonance pairing measures directed evocation, the preservation part,
suppression part, and emergence part describe what is kept, cancelled, and newly produced,
and the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — lets the
whole algebra vary by context.

## Biographical preamble

Donald Davidson, pronounced approximately *DAY-vid-son* lived from 1917 to 2003, worked in
the United States, and belongs here to analytic philosophy of language and action. In one
compressed sentence, he made belief, meaning, and action intelligible only together. That
sentence gives the chapter its entry point: meaning is not a bare object sitting before a
spectator, but a structured achievement within a field of acts, signs, practices, or
horizons.

## Position

The position can be put simply. Davidson proposes that a theory of meaning for a natural
language can take the form of a Tarski-style truth theory, with the meaning of a sentence
given by its truth conditions. Radical interpretation — assigning meanings to a stranger's
utterances from scratch — proceeds by the principle of charity, maximising the truth and
rationality of the speaker. Anomalous monism holds that mental events are identical to
physical events under some description, but mental descriptions resist strict psychophysical
laws. Belief, desire, and meaning are ascribed together in a holistic interpretation; there
is no first philosophy of any one of them. The point is not merely historical. It tells us
what kind of item is being counted as an idea: not a loose mental image, but a content whose
sense depends on the way it is given, used, interpreted, or carried forward.

## Algebraic re-statement

In the algebraic re-statement, the basic domain is the field of contents named by the zoo
entry. For each sentence s in language L, the truth-theoretic meaning is a T-sentence "s is
true if and only if p" where p is a metalanguage statement. The algebra hosts L-sentences as
ideas speaker ideas and the truth relation as a special the preservation part. Radical
interpretation is a procedure that, given observed assents and dissents in contexts the
content under discussion, produces a triple (truth theory, belief assignment, meaning
assignment) that maximises agreement: the relevant relation subject to systematicity
constraints on T. The principle of charity is a constraint on the emergence ascription:
the interpreter must assign the emergence relations among the speaker's Is such that
they form a largely rational and largely true network. Anomalous monism: each mental event m
coincides extensionally with a physical event p (the resonance pairing) but the mental
description of m and the physical description of p belong to different sub-algebras with no
bridging the preservation laws. Triangulation: meaning emerges only in the three-way
relation between speaker, interpreter, and shared world; the algebra requires a triadic
triangulation operator to fix content. Read aloud, this says that the
thinker supplies a local model of the ten-tuple: a domain of contents, a composition rule,
an identity idea, directed resonance, the threefold division into preservation, suppression,
and emergence, an emergence cocycle, a degree of articulation, and a context-sensitive
horizon structure.

## Theorem re-statement

*The theorem-style claim is this: The meaning of any L-sentence is determined by its T-sentence under a finitely axiomatisable truth theory for L.*

In paragraph form, the claim rules out the fantasy that meaning is already complete before
context enters. The meaning of any L-sentence is determined by its T-sentence under a
finitely axiomatisable truth theory for L. No interpretation that ascribes massive false
belief to the speaker is admissible; charity is constitutive of interpretation, not merely
heuristic. Belief, desire, and meaning are interdefinable; one cannot fix any one without
simultaneously fixing the others. Mental token-identical with physical, mental type-
irreducible to physical, no strict psychophysical laws. The prediction is that failures of
understanding will not be accidental noise. They will cluster where a local content cannot
be carried unchanged across a change of horizon, or where an apparent identity hides
suppressed difference.

Carefully restated, the relevant idea algebra has a domain of contents, an identity idea,
associative composition, the resonance pairing, the preservation part, the suppression part,
the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, the horizon category, and the horizon
functor. The central claim says that the philosopher's privileged object is stable only when
these roles cooperate. Where local data can be glued, the chapter instantiates the hermeneutic sheaf theorem; where
translation fails, the obstruction is also audible as a horizon-functor effect.

## What the formalism adds

What the formalism adds is clarification without replacement. The T-sentence machinery
becomes a specific kind of a preservation relation, integrable with the rest of the
algebra. Charity is exhibited as a maximisation constraint, exposing what is being maximised
and at what cost. Anomalous monism is rendered as a coincidence of the resonance pairing
without coincidence of the preservation laws, sharpening a thesis often felt as
paradoxical. Triangulation receives an explicit triadic operator, distinguishing Davidsonian
externalism from Cartesian solipsism in a structural way. Holism is captured as joint
determination by a system of constraints, vindicating Davidson's claim that no piecemeal
account suffices. In ordinary language, the formalism lets us ask which operation does the
philosophical work. Is the key relation resonance, because one content fulfills or evokes
another? Is it suppression, because meaning appears through contrast? Is it the horizon
functor, because every local act changes when the context changes? The answer differs from
thinker to thinker, but the vocabulary stays stable.

## Where it diverges

The divergence is equally important. The choice of T-sentences as meaning-givers is
contested (Foster's problem, Soames' critique); the algebra inherits the dispute by adopting
the schema. Charity has been criticised as imposing an interpreter's standards on the
speaker; the maximisation framing makes this worry sharper, not less. Anomalous monism walks
a fine line between physicalism and dualism; its rendering as resonance 
coincidence without the preservation laws can be read either way and Davidson's
specific resolution is lost. The pragmatic, context-sensitive elements of language use that
Davidson treats with malapropism and "passing theory" arguments fit awkwardly with a
finitely axiomatised T. The unity of Davidson's project — the way semantics, philosophy of
mind, and metaphysics interlock — is more architectural than the disparate operators of the
algebra suggest. This is not a defect in the reading. It marks the price of any formal
reconstruction: the algebra shows the load-bearing structure, while the original philosophy
often also includes tone, method, ethical urgency, religious practice, literary performance,
or first-person discipline.

## Horizon structure and the horizon-functor theorem

The horizon structure names the context in which the local algebra lives. Davidson's the
horizon category is the triangulated diagram of speaker, interpreter, and shared world, with
passing theories as local horizons of use. Morphisms are charitable translations that
jointly adjust belief, meaning, and truth-conditions while preserving enough rationality for
interpretation to begin. perspective is intersubjective triangulation, credence is
charitable truth-maximisation, and schema or paradigm is the holistic web rather than a
separable language scheme. This is the hermeneutic sheaf theorem: radical interpretation glues speaker-local assent,
interpreter theory, and worldly causes into one coherent truth-theoretic assignment. For
this reason the chapter should be heard through the hermeneutic sheaf theorem. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem is the hermeneutic sheaf theorem,
where local meanings glue into a global one when they agree on overlaps. The indexical pullback theorem is the
indexical pullback theorem, where preservation and emergence fail to translate cleanly. The profundity theorem
is the profundity theorem, where depth comes from breadth across horizons and closure under
emergence.

## Derived primitives

The derived primitives make the diagnosis compact. intentionality: Radical interpretation
assigns aboutness through truth-conditions inferred from speakers' behavior. credence: The
principle of charity distributes credence so speakers mostly come out rational and true.
rigid reference: Tarski-style truth theories stabilize reference without appealing to
private meanings. perspective: Triangulation makes objectivity arise among speaker,
interpreter, and shared worldly cause. None of these is introduced as an extra metaphysical
ingredient. Each is derived from the ten-tuple: intentionality from resonance across
horizons, temporality from ordered horizon-translation, perspective from a home region in
the horizon category, differentiation from suppression, and reflexion from self-application
of the horizon functor.

## Chapter summary

Donald Davidson therefore enters idea theory as a disciplined local algebra of meaning. The
chapter has followed the promised path: biography, position, algebraic re-statement, theorem
re-statement, addition, divergence, horizon structure, and derived primitives. The result is
not a replacement for the thinker, but a listening guide to the formal pressure already
present in the work.
