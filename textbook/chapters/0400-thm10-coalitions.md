# Chapter 23 — Coalitions

This chapter treats Theorem ten, Coalitions as an audiobook theorem. The operating definitions are simple. Ideas compose. There is an identity idea that acts as silence. Resonance measures fit, support, opposition, and salience. The preservation part names what remains, the suppression part names what is cancelled, and the emergence part names what becomes newly available. With those roles in place, the theorem tells us how groups can share ideas without reducing the group to one mind.

## Three voices

*Common ideas in a coalition form a stable shared algebra.*

In paragraph form, the theorem rules out pure individualism, which has no common structure, and crude collectivism, which erases the agents. It predicts that ideas common to a coalition are closed under the relevant operations and satisfy a gluing condition over perspectives. The practical point is that Idea Theory is not just a vocabulary for describing intellectual life after the fact. It gives constraints. If a proposed interpretation violates the constraint, then the interpretation is not merely bold. It is structurally confused. If it satisfies the constraint, then the theorem tells us what kind of stability, novelty, or translation we should expect.

Careful semi formal restatement: consider a social idea algebra with agents, each agent having a perspective, local idea algebras, composition, identity, resonance pairing, decomposition parts, aggregation maps, coalition membership, and a horizon category of perspectives. The ideas common to a coalition are those that restrict coherently to each member and agree on overlaps. They form a subalgebra because composing shared ideas remains shared, the identity idea is shared, and the relevant decomposition remains detectable across the coalition. Aumann common knowledge appears when everyone knows, everyone knows that everyone knows, and this iterative condition stabilizes as a sheaf like agreement over the coalition cover. This restatement names the structural elements without using equations. It also states the burden of the theorem. The result is not about a decorative analogy between mathematics and culture. It is about which operations may be performed while preserving the intended role of identity, composition, resonance, decomposition, emergence, grading, and horizon sensitivity.

## Narrative proof sketch

A committee can share a procedure even when members remember different examples. The shared object is not every private association. It is the structure that survives across the members perspectives and can be used jointly.

The proof treats a coalition as a cover of perspectives. First, local agreement is checked on overlaps. Second, compatible local meanings glue into one coalition meaning. Third, closure under composition shows that shared ideas can be used together without leaving the common stock. The Aumann condition is the fixed point of this mutual availability.

A useful way to hear the proof is as a cleaning operation. Start with a messy claim about ideas. Remove anything that depends only on wording. Remove anything that disappears when identity steps are inserted. Remove anything that changes only because a local description was chosen. What remains is the structure the theorem protects. In a formal proof, that cleaning is performed by definitions and lemmas. In the audiobook, the same movement can be heard as a sequence of disciplined comparisons.

The theorem matters because social understanding is neither telepathy nor polling. A coalition may act because a shared algebra has formed. Its members need not have identical minds. They need enough overlapping structure for instructions, commitments, and expectations to compose. This is the formal basis for institutions, committees, research communities, and coordinated games.

## What it decides

This theorem is decisive for Aumann common knowledge, social choice theory, and commons governance. Each of these positions asks whether some pattern is genuinely stable or merely convenient. The theorem gives a test. It asks whether the pattern survives the operations that the theory itself recognizes. A philosophical position may keep its favorite examples, but it must now say where composition, identity, resonance, decomposition, emergence, grading, or horizon translation enters the example.

The theorem also separates strong claims from inflated claims. A strong claim accepts the theorem's constraint and then shows that a real case satisfies it. An inflated claim borrows the language of structure without doing the work. For the listener, this is a gift. You do not have to choose between vague literary sensitivity and brittle formalism. The theorem tells you how to keep the sensitivity while asking for disciplined evidence.

## Derived primitives

The derived primitives most engaged here are perspective, credence, schema. They are called derived because they are not extra knobs added to the machine. They arise from the existing algebra. Temporality comes from ordered movement through horizons and chains. Intentionality comes from directed resonance. Perspective comes from localized horizons. Affect and signed valence come from resonance and decomposition as they meet a self. Differentiation comes from structured contrast. Rigid reference comes from stability across horizons. Potentiality comes from unrealized but reachable resonance. Schema comes from repeatable organization. Credence comes from stabilized support. Reflexion comes from an idea becoming able to take itself as content.

In this theorem, those primitives do not float separately. They cluster around the theorem's main constraint. That clustering is why the theorem matters beyond its technical statement. It tells us which human phenomena are likely to vary together and which distinctions must be kept apart.

A final listening test is this: ask what the group can do together that no member has to rederive from the beginning. A choir knows a song when the parts coordinate. A court knows a precedent when procedures and citations line up. A research team knows a method when members can rely on one another across partial views. Coalition structure is the difference between many private beliefs and one shared capacity.

## Chapter summary

Coalitions says that common ideas form stable shared algebraic structure. The chapter shows that an idea algebra earns its explanatory power by obeying a specific structural constraint. The theorem rules out loose metaphor, predicts a stable pattern, and shows which derived primitives are most active. Heard in this way, the theorem becomes a guide for interpretation, science, and cultural analysis.
