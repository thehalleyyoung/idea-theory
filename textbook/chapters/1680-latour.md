# Chapter 131 — Bruno Latour on Actor-Network Theory

Bruno Latour, *lah-toor*, invites the listener into a world where facts are not lonely sentences and machines are not passive tools. They are network achievements. Scientists, instruments, inscriptions, microbes, funders, journals, and devices all participate in the making of durable claims. Idea Theory hears Latour as a theory of composition, black boxing, and horizon gluing.

## Biographical preamble

Bruno Latour lived from 1947 to 2022. He was a French philosopher, anthropologist, and sociologist of science associated with actor-network theory and science and technology studies. He studied laboratories, law, religion, ecology, and modernity. In his own translated sense, a fact becomes strong not by leaving society behind, but by enrolling enough allies that disagreement becomes costly and difficult.

## Position

Latour rejects the picture in which science first discovers facts and then society merely accepts them. He follows facts while they are being made. A claim begins fragile. It becomes stronger when instruments inscribe traces, colleagues cite it, laboratories reproduce it, funders support it, journals circulate it, and devices embody it. Human and nonhuman actors are treated symmetrically in the analysis because both can make a difference to what the network can do.

Black boxing is central. When a complex network stabilizes, later users treat it as a single object. A computer, a laboratory test, a published graph, or a scientific fact can become a black box. You do not reopen all the controversies every time you use it. Its internal composition is hidden so that it can travel.

## Algebraic re-statement

In Idea Theory, actors are elements that compose through translation and enrollment. Composition is not peaceful addition. It is the work of making one actor pass through another actor's program. Resonance pairing measures receptivity and alignment. The preservation part is what remains stable when actors are enrolled. The suppression part is what is silenced, ignored, or made irrelevant. The emergence part is the new network capacity produced by the alliance.

A black box is a stabilized composite that behaves as a single generator in later compositions. Its internal network remains real, but later horizons treat it through identity. Meaning curvature appears when an actor recognizes another as obligatory while the reverse recognition is weaker. Reflexion appears because science studies studies the networks that produce authoritative knowledge, including its own.

## Central claim as theorem

*A fact becomes durable when heterogeneous actors glue into a stable network that later horizons can treat as a single generator.*

The theorem rules out the idea that facts become strong merely by correspondence in isolation. It also rules out the idea that facts are only social opinions. It predicts that durability depends on successful composition across humans, instruments, texts, institutions, and material resistances. Once stabilized, the composite becomes available for further composition without requiring every user to reconstruct the whole network.

Carefully stated: let the horizon category consist of laboratories, instruments, inscriptions, journals, funding bodies, machines, publics, and material settings. The horizon functor assigns local idea algebras in which claims, devices, and actors compose. Translation morphisms carry inscriptions and obligations across horizons. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem applies when local inscriptions and alliances are compatible enough to glue into a global section. Black boxing is the stabilization of that section as a portable identity for later use.

## What formalism adds

The formalism distinguishes three Latourian operations. Translation is composition. Enrollment is resonance pairing strong enough to support a composition. Black boxing is the emergence of a stable composite that can function as a generator. These distinctions help explain why facts have careers. A claim must first gather allies, then survive trials, then become portable.

Idea Theory also makes visible the preservation and suppression inside black boxing. A black box preserves reliable use, but suppresses the history of negotiation, repair, and controversy that made reliability possible. When controversy reopens, the black box loses smooth identity and its internal composition becomes audible again.

## Where it diverges

Latour's symmetry principle is methodological. It asks analysts not to decide in advance what counts as nature, society, subject, or object. The algebra can treat all elements uniformly, which fits the principle, but it may still sound too tidy. Real actor-networks involve stubborn materials, institutional power, accidents, bodies, money, and time. Not every resistance is just low resonance.

There is also a divergence around truth. Latour wants to avoid both naive realism and simple social construction. The formalism can model durability and translation, but it does not by itself settle metaphysical questions about truth. It tells us how a claim becomes network-stable. It does not say that stability alone exhausts reality.

## Horizon structure

Latour belongs with the hermeneutic sheaf theorem. The horizon category is heterogeneous: laboratories, machines, inscriptions, microbes, field sites, bureaucracies, journals, and publics. Morphisms are translations, citations, calibrations, replications, and enrollments. The horizon functor tracks how a claim changes as it moves, and how much must be preserved for it to remain usable.

The hermeneutic sheaf theorem names the gluing of local sections. A measurement in one laboratory, a graph in a paper, a grant review, a machine setting, and a replication elsewhere must become mutually compatible. When they do, the network produces a fact that travels.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

Schema appears as the actor-network itself, the provisional arrangement that makes heterogeneous actants mutually translatable. Rigid reference appears in black boxes, which let complex networks travel as stable devices or facts. Potentiality appears when translation enrolls new allies and extends what an actor can do. Reflexion is central because Latour's own analysis follows the production of analysis. Credence matters in citation, trust, replication, and institutional uptake.

A useful diagnostic is to ask when a listener no longer needs the story of assembly. If the device works, the graph persuades, and the citation travels, the network has acquired identity for later users. If failure occurs, the box opens: screws, calibrations, funding decisions, samples, and inscriptions return as active elements. Latour's lesson is that stability is always achieved, never simply given.

## Chapter summary

Latour gives Idea Theory a dynamic account of fact formation. Composition is translation, resonance pairing is enrollment, and black boxing turns a stabilized composite into a usable generator. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem captures the gluing that makes heterogeneous local work travel as durable knowledge.
