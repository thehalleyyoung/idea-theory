# Chapter 45 — Berkeley

George Berkeley, pronounced approximately *BARK-lee*, lived from 1685 to 1753 in Ireland, England, Rhode Island, and Cloyne, within early modern empiricism and Anglican philosophy. In his own translated words: “To be is to be perceived.”

## The position in its own voice

Hear the doctrine in its own intellectual voice. Berkeley's central thesis, esse est percipi (to be is to be perceived), abolishes material substance. The reason is direct: the only things we ever encounter are ideas - colors, shapes, sounds, tactile sensations - and ideas exist only in minds. To posit a mind-independent material substance behind ideas is to posit something whose existence we could never have any evidence of, since all our evidence is itself ideas. The young Berkeley arrives at this conclusion by an Ockham-like cut. Locke had insisted that primary qualities (extension, motion, shape) inhered in mind-independent matter, while secondary qualities (color, taste) existed only in the mind. Berkeley argues that the same considerations that show secondary qualities to be mind-dependent show primary qualities to be mind-dependent: extension is no less perceived than color. What about the persistence of objects when no finite mind perceives them? Berkeley's answer is theological: God is the permanent perceiver. Objects continue to exist because God always perceives them. The order and regularity of nature is the order of ideas in the mind of God, communicated to finite minds in a regular language we have learned to interpret. Crucially, Berkeley denies abstract general ideas. Locke had supposed that the mind, by abstraction, could form an idea of triangle that is neither right nor obtuse nor scalene nor isosceles. The voice trains attention by asking what is stable, what must be discarded, and what kind of insight appears only after that discipline has been accepted.

## The idea algebra sub-instance

Before translating, recall the operating definitions. Ideas compose with other ideas. Composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. Ideas resonate with one another, and their non-reciprocal pull is meaning curvature. Ideas also live over horizons: contexts, stages, perspectives, times, practices, or worlds. Berkeley treats ideas as dependent on perceiving spirits. Composition is perceptual bundling under divine order. The preservation part is regularity in perception, the suppression part removes material substratum and abstract general ideas, and the emergence part is ordinary objecthood without matter. Resonance is perceivability and sign-use among determinate ideas. Degree marks vividness, order, and dependence on finite or divine perception. In plain English, the sub-instance asks what counts as an idea, how ideas combine, what survives, what is cancelled, and what new content appears.

## Central claim as theorem

*If all encountered objects are ideas and ideas exist only in minds, then persistence requires a perceiving horizon rather than material substance.*

The theorem rules out the easy opposite of the view. It predicts that across the proper horizons some content will travel, some will fail, and some will arise only through that failure. The theorem identifies the middle structure: invariant, obstruction, or gluing.

Carefully restated, choose the relevant horizon category and let the horizon functor - the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi - assign a complete idea algebra to each horizon. Name composition, identity, resonance, the preservation part, the suppression part, the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, and meaning curvature. Then the theorem says that Berkeley's central claim is exactly the stable support, failed pullback, or glued agreement described in the preceding paragraph, stated without symbols.

## What the formalism adds

First, the rejection of abstract ideas becomes the precise statement that the emergence part has no image in the idea carrier beyond what is actually perceived. Where Locke needs the emergence part to manufacture an indeterminate triangle, Berkeley insists the operation is undefined; the formalism makes the disagreement turn on whether the emergence part is total or partial, and so localizes a centuries-old debate. Second, treating God as the emergence cocycle makes the persistence of objects a consequence of a universal transmitter rather than a separate metaphysical postulate. More generally, the formalism adds comparison. It tells the listener whether preservation, suppression, or emergence is carrying the doctrine.

## Where the formalism diverges

First, the individuation of minds is external to the algebra. The formalism takes the partition as given, but Berkeley owes us an account of what makes a mind a mind, distinct from other minds, and capable of containing ideas. The algebra alone cannot produce minds; it can only host them. Second, the relation between finite minds and God is asymmetric in ways the algebra elides. The algebra is therefore an aid to hearing, not a substitute for reading.

## Horizon category and horizon-functor theorem

Berkeley's the horizon category is made of finite perceptual spirit-horizons coordinated by the infinite divine perceiver. Morphisms carry ideas from one finite perspective to another only through God's stable ordering; there is no material substratum horizon underneath perception. perspective is central for finite minds, intentionality ties ideas to perceiving spirits, and rigid reference for ordinary objects is secured as a divine global section rather than a material bearer. In this chapter the most central horizon-functor theorem is the Profundity theorem. It names gluing, obstruction, or depth as the central horizon behavior.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

intentionality. Esse est percipi makes objects intentional correlates of perception rather than mind-independent substrates. perspective. Finite spirits are perspectival centers whose ideas vary, while God's perspective stabilizes the world. rigid reference. God provides rigid continuity for sensible things when no finite perceiver currently attends to them. differentiation. The rejection of abstract ideas makes meaning depend on differences among particular perceived ideas. These primitives are consequences of the way composition, resonance, degree, and horizon translation behave here.

One more listening cue helps. Ask, at every turn, whether the thinker is protecting a stable reference, exposing a failed translation, or gathering scattered meanings into a whole. That question keeps the formal reconstruction close to the philosophical voice rather than letting it become a merely external classification.

## Chapter summary

Berkeley gives one answer to what an idea is, how it combines, and how it remains or fails to remain meaningful across horizons. The idea algebra restates that answer by naming preserved content, suppressed illusion, emergent insight, horizon behavior, and diagnostic derived primitives. The gain is clarity; the cost is abstraction. A good listener keeps both in view.
