# Chapter 124 — John Maynard Smith on Evolutionarily Stable Strategies

Maynard Smith moved game theory from calculating minds into populations of living bodies. Strategies need not be chosen by reflective agents. They can be inherited, reproduced, invaded, and selected. Idea Theory hears an evolutionarily stable strategy as an idempotent strategic idea whose resonance holds under mutant pressure across evolutionary horizons.

## Biographical preamble

John Maynard Smith lived from nineteen twenty to two thousand four, a British evolutionary biologist shaped by genetics, mathematical biology, and the modern synthesis. He brought game theory into evolutionary explanation. His core sentence can be rendered simply: a strategy is evolutionarily stable if, once common in a population, no rare mutant strategy can successfully invade it.

## Position

An evolutionarily stable strategy, often abbreviated in ordinary speech as ESS, is stricter than a Nash equilibrium. A strategy may be a best response to itself and still be vulnerable if a mutant does equally well against the resident and better against itself or others. Maynard Smith's idea explains stable animal behavior without assuming foresight. Hawks, doves, displays, contests, and cooperation can be modeled as strategies whose success depends on what other strategies are common.

## Algebraic re-statement

Let strategies be ideas in a population algebra. Composition represents encounter, reproduction, and the way a resident pattern reproduces itself when paired with itself. Identity is the unchanged resident type. Resonance pairing measures fitness payoff when one strategy meets another. An evolutionarily stable strategy is an idempotent resident idea with strong resonance against mutant alternatives. The preservation part is inheritance of the resident form. The suppression part is elimination of less fit variants. The emergence part is the population level pattern that appears from many encounters.

## Central claim as theorem

*A resident strategy is evolutionarily stable when its self reproducing composition remains resonance dominant under nearby invasion horizons.*

The theorem rules out treating equilibrium as mere momentary rest. A biological strategy must survive perturbation by rare alternatives. It predicts that stable behavior is not simply what an individual would choose in isolation, but what a population can reproduce against the field of possible mutants. Stability is therefore ecological, temporal, and comparative.

Carefully restated, consider a horizon category whose objects are populations, ecological niches, resident states, mutant invasion contexts, and payoff regimes. The horizon functor assigns to each horizon a fitness resonance algebra. Composition models strategic encounter and reproductive continuation. Identity is the resident type maintained without change. Resonance pairing evaluates fitness of one strategy in the environment shaped by another. Preservation part carries heritable structure through generations. Suppression part removes variants that fail under selection. Emergence part is the population pattern generated by repeated encounters. Emergence cocycle records the surplus stability or vulnerability created by the strategy meeting itself and others. Degree or grading may rank complexity or cost. Meaning curvature appears when fitness effects are asymmetric between resident and mutant perspectives. T15 applies because evolutionary stability requires support across a family of nearby horizons, not only one local game.

## What formalism adds

The formalism shows why Maynard Smith's concept is related to Nash but not identical with it. Nash stability asks whether a unilateral deviation improves payoff in a fixed strategic environment. Evolutionary stability asks whether a mutant can grow when rare in a population environment. In Idea Theory, both use resonance pairing, but the population horizon adds temporality, preservation, suppression, and invasion testing. That makes the difference audible instead of merely technical.

## Where it diverges

Maynard Smith's biological account depends on actual population dynamics. Different replicator processes, mutation rates, spatial structures, and ecological constraints can change whether a formally stable strategy is reached. The algebraic statement captures the stability condition, but it does not by itself supply the path of evolution. To recover detailed biological predictions, one must add a dynamic horizon that specifies reproduction, encounter rates, and inheritance.

## Horizon structure and theorem

The horizon category contains resident populations, mutant neighborhoods, ecological contexts, and temporal sequences of generations. The horizon functor assigns a fitness algebra to each. T15 governs the chapter because the strategy must remain meaningful and stable across an expanded support: not only the present resident horizon, but also the possible horizons opened by mutation and invasion.


Think of animal contests over territory. A pure hawk pattern fights hard. A pure dove pattern displays and retreats. A mixed or conditional pattern may do better depending on the population around it. The question is not what one animal privately intends. The question is what happens when a type meets copies of itself and rare alternatives. If a resident type reproduces its own conditions and defeats invasion, it has strategic identity at the population level.

This is why the emergence cocycle matters. A behavior can be harmless in isolation and decisive in population composition. Display, retaliation, restraint, and escalation acquire meaning through encounter. The population is not a background container; it is part of the algebra that determines resonance. Maynard Smith's achievement was to let selection replace deliberation without losing strategic form.


The formal ear should also notice that stability is comparative. A resident strategy is not simply strong in the abstract. It is strong against the alternatives that can appear in its ecological neighborhood. Change the neighborhood, and the meaning of stability can change.

The listener should hear selection as a patient editor of strategic forms, not as foresight.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

Temporality is central because stability is tested across generations. Credence appears as population share and encounter probability. Signed valence appears as fitness, marking reproductive gain and loss. Potentiality appears in mutant alternatives not yet common but capable of becoming relevant. Differentiation matters because resident and mutant strategies must be distinguished even when their behavior is similar. Perspective is generalized from individual agent to type, lineage, or niche.

## Chapter summary

Maynard Smith gives Idea Theory a population version of strategic stability. A strategy is not profound because one chooser likes it, but because it reproduces itself and resists invasion across evolutionary horizons. This is T15 in biological form: stable meaning has support beyond its first local setting. This matters because the population itself becomes the listener: it tests a strategy by repetition, not by private deliberation.
