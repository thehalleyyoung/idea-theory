# Chapter 5 — Grading: Ideas Come in Degrees

Idea Theory begins with a few operating names. Composition is the act of putting ideas together. The resonance pairing measures how one idea calls, resists, or illuminates another. Each composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. The emergence cocycle records how novelty behaves across regrouping. Degree or grading measures articulation. Meaning curvature names the durable asymmetry in resonance. Context enters through the horizon category, and through the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — which assigns a full idea algebra to each context.

## The question in plain speech

Grading says that ideas have levels of articulation. Some are simple, some complex, some shallow, some deep, some local, some far-reaching. A degree is not a grade in the schoolroom sense, and it is not the same as probability. It is a structural measure of how much conceptual articulation an idea carries. The idea dog as a child's pointing word has one degree of development. The idea mammal inside evolutionary biology has another. The idea legal person inside corporate law has another again.

Because this book is written for the ear, you do not need to hold a diagram in your head. Imagine instead a small workshop. Ideas arrive at the bench. Some can be joined without strain. Some scrape against one another. Some cancel pieces of one another. Some make a third thing appear, a thing that neither ingredient carried alone. Around the workshop are rooms, and each room is a horizon: a language game, a historical moment, a scientific paradigm, a social role, a possible world, or a private point of view. Moving rooms changes what can be said, what can be inferred, and what counts as the same idea.

## A scene to carry

Picture the idea of freedom. In one horizon, freedom means not being locked in a room. In another, it means civil liberty. In another, it means moral autonomy. In another, it means release from craving. The word may be familiar in all cases, but the degree differs because the surrounding articulations differ. More developed versions connect to institutions, duties, histories, arguments, practices, and counterexamples. Grading lets us describe that growth without saying that later always means better.

## What the structure keeps honest

The structure guards against confusion between complexity and belief. A person may believe a simple conspiracy with great confidence. Another person may assign low credence to a highly articulated scientific hypothesis. Degree belongs to the idea's internal and contextual development. Credence belongs to an agent's probability-like commitment and is derived later. The distinction matters because social life often confuses intensity of belief with depth of thought. Idea Theory refuses that confusion.

## Older conversations inside the new vocabulary

Philosophers have long ranked forms of understanding. Plato, pronounced approximately PLAY-toe, distinguishes opinion from knowledge and visible images from intelligible forms. Aristotle distinguishes potentiality and actuality. Medieval traditions rank being, proof, and explanation. Cognitive psychology distinguishes novice schemas from expert schemas. Mathematics distinguishes short arguments from deep theories whose consequences travel widely. Grading does not simply copy any one hierarchy. It offers a general role for levels of articulation.



## How to listen for it

When you listen for grading, ask how much structure an idea carries and how composition changes that level. Does a phrase become more articulated when joined to a theory. Does a metaphor gain degree as it enters law, science, or ritual. Does an idea lose degree when reduced to a slogan. Grading is one way the algebra hears growth, simplification, profundity, and distortion.

For the listener, the safest method is to hear the same distinction in several registers. In a sentence, ask what is being joined. In a conversation, ask what one speaker preserves from another and what is quietly suppressed. In a scientific dispute, ask which horizon makes an inference natural and which horizon breaks it. In a social movement, ask how the transmitted slogan changes as it passes from mouth to mouth. None of these questions requires symbolic notation. They require patient attention to roles.

## Chapter summary

The chapter's lesson is simple to say and slow to exhaust: Idea Theory is a small vocabulary for tracking composition, resonance, preservation, suppression, emergence, grading, curvature, and context without pretending that meaning floats free of use. Keep the role names in your ear. They are the handles by which the later examples will be lifted.
