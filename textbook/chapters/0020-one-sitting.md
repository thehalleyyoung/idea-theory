# Chapter 0.2 — What an Idea Algebra Is in One Sitting

Idea Theory begins with a few operating names. Composition is the act of putting ideas together. The resonance pairing measures how one idea calls, resists, or illuminates another. Each composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. The emergence cocycle records how novelty behaves across regrouping. Degree or grading measures articulation. Meaning curvature names the durable asymmetry in resonance. Context enters through the horizon category, and through the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — which assigns a full idea algebra to each context.

## The whole shape at once

An idea algebra is a disciplined way to say what happens when ideas meet. It treats an idea as something that can enter relations, combine with other ideas, travel through contexts, and acquire a level of articulation. The theory does not begin by asking whether ideas are private images, public words, neural patterns, social practices, or abstract objects. It begins one step more generally. Whatever ideas are, if they can be put together, if they can resonate, if they can be transmitted, and if their meaning changes by context, then they can be described by the roles of the algebra.

Start with composition. Composition says that putting ideas together is itself a structured act. It can be as simple as joining red and ball to form red ball, or as difficult as joining liberty and equality inside a constitution. Composition is orderly enough that if you build a long thought from smaller thoughts, regrouping the work need not destroy the final construction. But composition is not assumed to be reversible, and it is not assumed to ignore order. The slogan science for policy is not the same as policy for science.

## The three flavors of a joining

Whenever two ideas compose, Idea Theory asks three questions. What was preserved. What was suppressed. What emerged. The preservation part is what survives the meeting. The suppression part is what gets canceled, muted, displaced, or made unavailable. The emergence part is what appears only because the meeting occurred. These three roles are especially helpful because ordinary language often hides them. We say compromise, synthesis, reframing, metaphor, innovation, or contradiction. The algebra asks us to hear the internal anatomy of those words.

Take the phrase artificial intelligence. The idea artificial preserves something from craft, fabrication, and human making. The idea intelligence preserves something from reasoning, adaptation, and problem solving. The combination suppresses other possibilities: it does not mean a handmade chair, and it does not mean human wisdom in its full moral register. It also creates new content: machine agency, automated judgment, computational creativity, and social anxiety about replacement. That emergent content is not found in either word alone. It arrives through composition.

## Resonance and degree

Composition is not enough. Ideas also pull on one another. The resonance pairing measures that pull. It is directional, because one idea may evoke another more strongly than the reverse. It is sensitive, because a joke, a memory, or a trauma can make one association flare and another fade. It is structural, because the theory asks resonance to extend consistently from simple ideas to combinations of ideas. A listener need not picture a formula. Just hear the requirement: once you know how the basic pieces resonate, the resonances of larger constructions must not be arbitrary.

Ideas also come in degrees. Degree or grading measures articulation, complexity, depth, or level. A child's first idea of fairness may have a lower degree than a jurisprudential theory of distributive justice. A sketch of evolution may have a lower degree than a mature population-genetic model. This is not the same as belief. You may believe a simple slogan very strongly and doubt a subtle theorem. Credence is derived later. Degree names the structural richness of the idea itself.

## Context as part of the machinery

The horizon category collects contexts. A horizon may be a moment in time, a community of speech, a scientific paradigm, a possible world, an institutional setting, or an agent's perspective. The horizon functor places an idea algebra over each horizon. This is the decisive enlargement. It means that the same apparent sentence may live in different algebras depending on where it is heard. It also means that translations between horizons can preserve some structure while breaking other structure.

For example, a legal phrase may move from everyday speech into a courtroom. Its composition with other phrases becomes stricter. Its resonance changes. Its degree may increase because it now sits inside a technical network. Its emergence may be constrained by precedent. The horizon translation from ordinary talk to legal doctrine does not simply carry meaning like a sealed box. It repositions the idea inside a different algebra.

## Why call it an algebra

The word algebra can sound forbidding, but here it means a small collection of operations and rules. The operations name what can be done with ideas. The rules say what must remain stable if the description is to be coherent. There is an identity idea, the quiet element that composes without changing anything. There are translations between contexts. There are invariants, features that survive relabeling or reframing. There are obstructions, features that mark why some translations fail.

The point is not to reduce philosophy to calculation. The point is to make comparison sharper. If one thinker says meaning is use, another says ideas have eternal form, another says belief is probability, and another says consciousness is intentional, Idea Theory asks where each claim sits. Is it a claim about resonance. About horizon support. About grading. About rigid reference. About emergence. About the failure of inference to survive a horizon shift. The algebra is a map of possible disagreements.

## Chapter summary

In one sitting, an idea algebra says this: ideas compose, resonate, preserve, suppress, emerge, acquire degree, carry curvature, travel through horizons, and vary with context through the horizon functor. It is small enough to remember, but broad enough to organize many philosophical, cognitive, and social-scientific accounts of ideas.
