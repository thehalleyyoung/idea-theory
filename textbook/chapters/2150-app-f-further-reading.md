## How to move between audiobook and website

A good rhythm is to listen first, browse second, and return third. Listen to a chapter without stopping. Then visit the corresponding website page and look at the definitions, theorem links, and zoo connections. Finally, replay the chapter or its summary. The second hearing is usually clearer because the website has supplied visual anchors that the ear did not have.

For readers with formal training, the theorem pages are the bridge. For readers with historical interests, the zoo pages are the bridge. For readers with programming interests, the Lean pages are the bridge. For teachers, the appendices are the bridge, because they gather pronunciations, theorem statements, primitives, and routes in a form easy to assign.

## A caution about further reading

Do not try to read every primary source at once. The better path is triangular. Pick one formal idea, one historical thinker, and one empirical or social-scientific application. For example, pair meaning curvature with Nietzsche and Kahneman. Pair the hermeneutic sheaf theorem with Gadamer and Hayek. Pair rigid reference with Kripke and word embeddings. Pair emergence with Hegel, Broad, and recombinant growth.

This triangular method keeps the theory honest. If the formal idea cannot illuminate both a historical text and a contemporary case, it may be too vague. If it illuminates both while preserving their differences, it is doing real work.

## Suggested pairings

For Plato, pair the Republic with the website material on rigid reference, grading, and profundity. For Aristotle, pair the Metaphysics with potentiality and emergence. For Hegel, pair the Logic or Phenomenology with the threefold decomposition and emergence cocycle. For Saussure, pair the Course in General Linguistics with differentiation. For Wittgenstein, pair the Investigations with horizons and language games.

For empirical work, pair Rosch with resonance, Barsalou with perspective, Fauconnier and Turner with emergence, Kahneman and Tversky with curvature, Henrich with transmission, Hayek with sheaf gluing, Nash with fixed points, and Aumann with common knowledge. Each pairing is a way of testing whether the formal vocabulary illuminates a real literature rather than merely renaming it.
