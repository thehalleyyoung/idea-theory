# Chapter 2 — Resonance: How Ideas Pull on Each Other

Idea Theory begins with a few operating names. Composition is the act of putting ideas together. The resonance pairing measures how one idea calls, resists, or illuminates another. Each composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. The emergence cocycle records how novelty behaves across regrouping. Degree or grading measures articulation. Meaning curvature names the durable asymmetry in resonance. Context enters through the horizon category, and through the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — which assigns a full idea algebra to each context.

## The question in plain speech

Resonance names the directed pull between ideas. It is tempting to say that resonance is similarity, but that is too weak. A funeral bell and grief are not similar in shape, color, or grammar, yet one may call the other with enormous force. A key may call a door more strongly than a door calls that key. Smoke may call fire, but fire may also call warmth, danger, ritual, industry, or home. The resonance pairing measures evocation, opposition, fit, salience, and illumination as a structured relation.

Because this book is written for the ear, you do not need to hold a diagram in your head. Imagine instead a small workshop. Ideas arrive at the bench. Some can be joined without strain. Some scrape against one another. Some cancel pieces of one another. Some make a third thing appear, a thing that neither ingredient carried alone. Around the workshop are rooms, and each room is a horizon: a language game, a historical moment, a scientific paradigm, a social role, a possible world, or a private point of view. Moving rooms changes what can be said, what can be inferred, and what counts as the same idea.

## A scene to carry

Imagine the idea of migration spoken in three rooms. In a family kitchen, it may resonate with sacrifice, remittance, memory, and hope. In an election debate, it may resonate with borders, labor, fear, and identity. In a biology classroom, it may resonate with birds, whales, insects, and seasonal movement. The word is recognizably related across rooms, but the pull is not the same. Even within one room, migration may call opportunity to one listener and loss to another. Resonance lets us say this without reducing meaning to private feeling.

## What the structure keeps honest

The structure keeps two errors away. The first error says that if two ideas are associated, the association must be mutual. It need not be. A childhood song may call a city to mind because you heard it there, while the city does not call that song to mind for anyone else. The second error says that larger ideas can resonate however we please. They cannot. Once basic pulls are fixed, the resonance of combinations must extend consistently. Otherwise the theory would not describe a structure; it would describe a mood board.

## Older conversations inside the new vocabulary

David Hume, pronounced approximately hyoom, gave modern philosophy a powerful associationist picture: ideas follow one another by resemblance, contiguity, and cause. Cognitive science later described spreading activation, where one mental node lights nearby nodes. Modern language models and word embeddings use patterns of co-occurrence to place terms near or far in semantic space. Speech-act theory adds indexical force, the way here, now, I, and you depend on the situation. The resonance pairing is broad enough to hear all four traditions while insisting that direction matters.



## How to listen for it

When you listen for resonance, do not ask only whether two ideas resemble each other. Ask which one calls which, how strongly, in what horizon, and with what emotional or inferential charge. Ask whether the pull supports composition or resists it. Ask whether a political metaphor is doing its work by resonance rather than by argument. Much of public life moves through resonances that are never named.

For the listener, the safest method is to hear the same distinction in several registers. In a sentence, ask what is being joined. In a conversation, ask what one speaker preserves from another and what is quietly suppressed. In a scientific dispute, ask which horizon makes an inference natural and which horizon breaks it. In a social movement, ask how the transmitted slogan changes as it passes from mouth to mouth. None of these questions requires symbolic notation. They require patient attention to roles.

## Chapter summary

The chapter's lesson is simple to say and slow to exhaust: Idea Theory is a small vocabulary for tracking composition, resonance, preservation, suppression, emergence, grading, curvature, and context without pretending that meaning floats free of use. Keep the role names in your ear. They are the handles by which the later examples will be lifted.
