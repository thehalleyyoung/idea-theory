# Chapter 126 — Robert Aumann on Common Knowledge

Aumann showed that disagreement has a precise architecture. Two rational agents may disagree because they have different priors, different information, or no common knowledge of what each believes. But if a common prior and common knowledge of posteriors are in place, disagreement collapses. Idea Theory hears this as a sheaf condition over perspectives: local credences must glue when the overlaps are publicly stable.

## Biographical preamble

Robert Aumann, *OW mahn*, was born in nineteen thirty in Frankfurt and became a central figure in Israeli and American mathematical economics. His tradition joins game theory, Bayesian probability, and epistemic logic. His famous thought can be rendered simply: people with the same prior cannot agree to disagree when their posterior beliefs are common knowledge.

## Position

Common knowledge is stronger than everyone knowing something. It means everyone knows it, everyone knows that everyone knows it, and so on without breaking the chain. Aumann used this structure to prove a striking claim. If Bayesian agents share a prior and their posterior probabilities for an event are common knowledge, then those posterior probabilities must be the same. Rational disagreement can exist, but it must come from a failure in the assumptions.

## Algebraic re-statement

Let each agent's information be a local subalgebra of ideas. Credence assigns probabilities inside that local perspective. A public statement about belief is an attempted overlap between perspectives. Common knowledge is the fixed point of repeated mutual preservation: what remains after each agent's knowledge of the other's knowledge is preserved again and again. The suppression part removes private distinctions not available at the group horizon. The emergence part is group knowledge, which is not reducible to either agent's first order belief.

## Central claim as theorem

*When posterior credences form a common knowledge global section over agent perspectives, rational disagreement cannot persist.*

The theorem rules out a familiar comfort: that two fully rational agents with the same prior may publicly know each other's posteriors and still disagree about the same event. It predicts that persistent public disagreement marks a structural failure somewhere. Perhaps priors differ. Perhaps information partitions differ. Perhaps the belief reports are not common knowledge. Perhaps the agents are not updating by the same rule.

Carefully restated, take a horizon category whose objects are agent perspectives, information partitions, public announcement contexts, posterior belief states, and group knowledge horizons. The horizon functor assigns to each agent perspective a credence bearing idea algebra. Composition combines belief reports and information refinements. Identity leaves a belief state unchanged. Resonance pairing compares how credences meet across agents. Preservation part carries what remains mutually known. Suppression part removes merely private information at the group horizon. Emergence part is common knowledge as a group level idea. Emergence cocycle records the surplus generated by iterated mutual knowing. Degree or grading can measure depth of iteration or complexity of information. Meaning curvature records asymmetry between what one agent can infer about another and what returns. T13 applies because common knowledge is precisely a gluing condition over a cover of perspectives.

## What formalism adds

The formalism makes common knowledge sound less like an infinite chant and more like a global section. Each agent has local belief data. The overlaps say what is mutually known. If those overlaps are compatible and the common prior supplies a shared schema, the local data glue. Disagreement then has no room to hide. This also clarifies why ordinary disputes persist: everyday conversation rarely achieves the full sheaf condition. We do not actually know that the other knows that we know, all the way down.

## Where it diverges

Aumann's theorem is built inside probability spaces and information partitions. Idea Theory abstracts that into horizon functor, resonance, and gluing. The abstraction is illuminating, but it can blur the exact probabilistic assumptions. Without a common prior, a proper update rule, and well formed partitions, the agreement result does not follow. The formalism must therefore be read as a translation of the theorem's architecture, not as a weakening that makes the conclusion automatic.

## Horizon structure and theorem

The horizon category is epistemic. Its objects are individual information states, shared prior contexts, public announcements, posterior reports, and the group horizon of common knowledge. The horizon functor sends each object to its local belief algebra. T13 governs the chapter because the agreement theorem is a gluing theorem: compatible local credences over a common knowledge cover form one global section.


An ordinary example helps. Two doctors begin with the same medical statistics. Each examines a different test result, updates, and reports only a final probability. If it becomes common knowledge that one says high risk and the other says low risk, and if all the Bayesian assumptions truly hold, then the disagreement itself carries information. Each should update on the fact of the other's posterior. When this updating is publicly stable, the two reported probabilities must meet.

The difficulty is that real life rarely supplies the full structure. People doubt each other's priors, competence, honesty, categories, or evidence. They may also use the same words for slightly different events. Idea Theory makes these failures diagnostic rather than embarrassing. A disagreement is not simply irrational noise. It points to a broken overlap in the horizon category, a missing shared schema, or an unstable reference.


The theorem is austere, but its austerity is useful. It tells us exactly which doors must be open for disagreement to remain. Different priors, private evidence, unstable reference, or failed public iteration each becomes a possible diagnosis.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

Credence is central because the theorem concerns posterior probabilities. Perspective matters because agents occupy different information partitions. Reflexion matters because common knowledge is iterated knowing about knowing. Schema appears as the common prior, the shared form that lets local beliefs compare. Rigid reference matters because the event in question must remain the same event across perspectives. Meaning curvature marks the asymmetry that disappears when common knowledge is achieved.

## Chapter summary

Aumann gives Idea Theory its clearest social use of T13. Local beliefs live in different perspectives, but common knowledge can make them glue. When shared prior, posterior reports, and public iteration line up, disagreement is not merely reduced; it is structurally ruled out.
