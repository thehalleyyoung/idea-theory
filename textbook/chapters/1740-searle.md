# Chapter 137 — John Searle on Intentionality and the Chinese Room

A machine can move symbols in the right order and still, Searle insists, fail to understand what those symbols are about. The challenge is not whether behavior can be copied. The challenge is whether copied behavior carries intentionality, the directedness by which thought reaches beyond itself toward objects, meanings, and states of affairs.

## Biographical preamble

John Searle was born in 1932 in Denver, worked mainly at the University of California, Berkeley, and belongs to the analytic tradition in philosophy of language and mind. In a sentence close to his own view, syntax by itself is not sufficient for semantics. That claim powers both his theory of speech acts and his Chinese Room argument.

## Position

Searle asks us to imagine a person in a room who does not understand Chinese. Slips of paper with Chinese marks enter the room. The person follows an English rulebook for manipulating the marks and returns other marks. From outside, the system may look fluent. Inside, there is no understanding of Chinese. The lesson, for Searle, is that formal symbol manipulation alone does not create meaning.

The missing element is intentionality. A belief is about rain. A desire is for water. A promise is directed toward a future action. For Searle, minds have original intentionality, while computers have at best derived intentionality, because their symbols mean what designers and users take them to mean. Computation can simulate understanding, but simulation is not duplication.

## Algebraic re-statement

Idea Theory restates the Chinese Room as a separation between composition and resonance pairing. Composition tracks the lawful ordering of signs. A computer program may preserve this order perfectly. Identity is the neutral state of the system when no further symbol transformation occurs. But resonance pairing measures aboutness, fit, reference, and semantic grip. Two systems can share the same composition pattern while differing in resonance pairing.

The preservation part of the Chinese Room keeps syntax intact. The suppression part marks the absence of intrinsic semantic uptake. The emergence part would be genuine understanding, if the system produced it. Searle's claim is that rule governed syntax alone never guarantees that emergence part.

## Central claim as theorem

*Identical symbol composition does not by itself guarantee intentional resonance across a horizon.*

This is T14 read through Searle. The theorem rules out the move from correct formal transition to genuine meaning without an account of horizon translation. It predicts that a syntactic horizon may preserve all visible behavior while failing to pull back the preservation part and emergence part of understanding. A chatbot, a clerk, and a fluent speaker may produce the same string, but the horizon in which the string is owned, meant, and understood can differ.

Carefully restated, the horizon category contains bodily agents, computational systems, linguistic communities, referents, and interpretive settings. The horizon functor assigns to each horizon a field of possible signs and acts. Composition governs transitions among symbols. Identity marks no change in symbolic state. The resonance pairing links signs to what they are about. The preservation part carries formal pattern, the suppression part blocks semantic ownership, and the emergence part would be intentional understanding. The relevant derived primitive is intentionality, with rigid reference nearby because reference must remain stable across uses.

## What formalism adds

The formalism makes the syntax and semantics gap precise in ordinary language. Composition can be copied without copying the full resonance pairing. This does not prove Searle's biological naturalism, but it gives his argument a clean structural target. The target is any theory that treats correct formal transition as already sufficient for meaning.

It also separates several questions often tangled together. Can a system pass a behavioral test. Can it preserve syntactic composition. Can it participate in a horizon where its signs have resonance with world and use. Can an emergence part of understanding arise from non biological organization. Searle answers the last question sharply, but Idea Theory leaves it open while preserving the distinction that makes the question meaningful.

A simple contrast helps. A thermostat can respond to temperature, and a person can believe the room is cold. Both systems may trigger heat. But the human belief belongs to a horizon of reasons, perceptions, corrections, and reports. The thermostat's state may be useful to us as a sign, yet its aboutness is assigned by designers and users. Idea Theory does not settle the metaphysical question by stipulation. It asks whether the system's resonance pairing is internal to its own horizon of action and correction, or merely borrowed from an outside interpreter. That question preserves the force of Searle's challenge while leaving room for future accounts of embodied artificial agents.

## Where it diverges

Searle argues that biological brains have causal powers that computers, as computers, lack. Idea Theory is more substrate neutral. It does not decide in advance whether silicon, tissue, social practice, or embodied action can support intentionality. It says only that intentionality requires resonance pairing, not mere composition. This divergence matters. The formalism can honor the Chinese Room as a warning against empty syntax while refusing to identify the needed resonance with one material alone.

## Horizon structure

Searle's horizon is embodied, causal, and semantic. It includes the speaker, the rule following system, the language community, and the world toward which mental states are directed. It most centrally instantiates T14, because the crucial obstruction appears when one tries to translate from a syntactic horizon to an intentional horizon. Preservation of input and output does not ensure preservation of aboutness.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

The diagnostic derived primitives are intentionality, rigid reference, and potentiality. Intentionality names aboutness itself. Rigid reference asks whether a sign keeps hold of the same object across horizons. Potentiality names the disputed capacity of a system to generate genuine semantic emergence rather than behavioral imitation.

## Chapter summary

Searle forces us to distinguish moving symbols from meaning them. Idea Theory restates his point by separating composition from resonance pairing. The Chinese Room preserves formal order but may lack the emergence part of intentional understanding. T14 gives the best horizon structure, because the problem is an obstruction in translation from syntax to semantics.
