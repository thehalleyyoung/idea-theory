# Chapter 121 — von Neumann and Morgenstern on Expected Utility

Game theory begins, for our purposes, when choice becomes a structure rather than a mood. An agent has options, outcomes, beliefs about uncertainty, and a way of ranking what may happen. Idea Theory hears this as an algebra of strategic ideas: composition combines plans, identity is the do nothing plan, resonance pairing evaluates how one plan meets another, and credence turns uncertainty into disciplined expectation.

## Biographical preamble

John von Neumann, *von NOY mahn*, lived from nineteen oh three to nineteen fifty seven, moving from Budapest through German mathematics to Princeton. Oskar Morgenstern, *MORE gen shtern*, lived from nineteen oh two to nineteen seventy seven, moving from Vienna economics to American social science. Their joint tradition joined axiomatic mathematics with economic choice. In their own plain claim, rational behavior can be studied by exact analysis when preferences over uncertain prospects obey stable rules.

## Position

Von Neumann and Morgenstern made two linked moves. First, they described rational choice under risk by expected utility: an agent ranks lotteries as if assigning a utility to outcomes and taking an expectation by credence. Second, for strictly opposed games, they showed that mixed strategies give each player a defensible value. The famous minimax idea says that what one player can guarantee against the worst response is the same strategic level the opponent can hold them to. Randomization is not noise here. It is a deliberate strategic idea.

## Algebraic re-statement

Let the strategic options form an idea algebra. Pure strategies are basic elements. Mixed strategies are credence weighted blends of those elements, still read as strategic ideas. Composition joins plans with contingencies, identity is the neutral plan that changes nothing, and resonance pairing measures payoff when one strategic idea encounters another. The preservation part carries the preference ordering that remains stable across equivalent lotteries. The suppression part removes distinctions that make no difference to choice. The emergence part appears when combining uncertainty and utility produces a new evaluative object: expected utility.

## Central claim as theorem

*Expected utility turns rational preference into a resonance functional, and minimax glues opposed local safeguards into one stable strategic value.*

The theorem rules out a picture of rational choice as mere appetite. If preferences over uncertain prospects obey the standard consistency requirements, then an agent behaves as if each outcome has a utility and each uncertainty has a credence. In strictly opposed play, the theorem predicts that strategic safety is not merely subjective caution. There is a shared value of the game, recovered from both perspectives, once mixed strategies are allowed.

Carefully restated, take a horizon category whose objects are strategic situations, payoff descriptions, opponent perspectives, and mixed strategy contexts. The horizon functor assigns to each such horizon a payoff resonance algebra. Within that algebra, composition combines strategic ideas, identity preserves the idle option, resonance pairing evaluates payoff, preservation part retains utility relevant ordering, suppression part removes irrelevant description, emergence part produces expected value from outcome and credence, emergence cocycle records the surplus or loss introduced by strategic combination, degree or grading ranks complexity of strategies, and meaning curvature records asymmetry between opposed perspectives. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem applies when compatible local maximin and minimax constraints glue into a global saddle like section.

## What formalism adds

The formalism separates the two achievements that often get blended in the story. Expected utility is a theorem about preference representation. Minimax is a theorem about opposed resonance under mixed strategy horizons. In ordinary language, both are about rationality. In Idea Theory, one concerns credence transformed into utility evaluation, while the other concerns the resonance pairing between strategic perspectives. This distinction helps explain why the same mathematical mood travels into decision theory, bargaining, military strategy, and economics without meaning exactly the same thing in each place.

## Where it diverges

Von Neumann and Morgenstern require a strong probabilistic interpretation. The agent must be representable as if probabilities and utilities combine in a disciplined way. Idea Theory can model that structure, but it does not force every resonance pairing to be a probability weighted expectation. Some strategic meanings are symbolic, habitual, or institutional. The formalism therefore broadens the setting. That broadening is useful, but it loses the special sharpness of their axioms unless credence and utility are explicitly supplied.

## Horizon structure and theorem

The relevant horizon structure is a two player strategic horizon with payoff representations, mixed strategy spaces, and transformations of utility scale. The horizon functor sends each representation to its local resonance algebra. Player perspectives are different subhorizons over the same game. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem is the guiding theorem because the result is a gluing claim: local security requirements from each perspective meet in a stable global value.


A simple example helps the ear. Suppose a commander can choose bold attack, cautious defense, or delay, while the opponent can choose reinforce, feint, or withdraw. In ordinary speech these are options. In the algebra they are strategic ideas. Mixing them means placing credence over them as part of the plan itself. The payoff is not attached to an isolated act but to the resonance pairing between one plan and the opponent's plan. A cautious plan may resonate well against a feint and poorly against reinforcement. Expected utility collects these local encounters into one evaluative voice.

This also explains why minimax has such philosophical force. The player does not need to know the opponent's actual choice. The player asks what can be preserved under the most hostile admissible response. The opponent asks the matching question from the other side. If the two questions meet, the game has a stable value. The result is not optimism. It is disciplined pessimism made exact by mixed strategy.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

Credence is primary because probabilities over actions make mixed strategy meaningful. Perspective matters because each player reads the same game from an opposed location. Signed valence appears as utility, where outcomes carry positive or negative weight. Reflexion appears because each player chooses while internalizing the anticipated optimization of the other. Schema also matters because the normal form of the game supplies the shared pattern in which strategies and payoffs can be compared.

## Chapter summary

Von Neumann and Morgenstern teach Idea Theory that strategic reason is an algebra of evaluated possibilities. Expected utility makes credence audible as disciplined preference, and minimax makes opposition audible as a stable resonance value. Their chapter is governed by the hermeneutic sheaf theorem: when the local constraints of opposed strategic horizons are compatible, they glue into a single defensible form of play.
