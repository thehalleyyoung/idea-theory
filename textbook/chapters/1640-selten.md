# Chapter 127 — Reinhard Selten on Refinements of Equilibrium

Selten taught game theory to distrust equilibria that survive only because no one looks off the path. A threat may support a Nash equilibrium and still be incredible. Idea Theory hears refinement as horizon testing: an equilibrium idea must restrict coherently to subgames and remain stable when small errors, trembles, and forgotten possibilities are admitted.

## Biographical preamble

Reinhard Selten, *RINE hart ZEL ten*, lived from nineteen thirty to twenty sixteen, a German economist in the traditions of game theory, bounded rationality, and experimental economics. His work refined Nash equilibrium for sequential games. His guiding claim can be rendered simply: an equilibrium is credible only if it remains rational at every point where play might actually arrive.

## Position

Nash equilibrium can allow noncredible threats. In an extensive game, a player may threaten an action that would be irrational if the threatened moment were reached. Selten's subgame perfect equilibrium requires strategies to form a Nash equilibrium in every proper subgame. His trembling hand perfection goes further. It asks whether an equilibrium remains stable when every action has a small chance of being played by mistake. Credibility becomes robustness.

## Algebraic re-statement

Let a game tree generate subalgebras, one for each subgame horizon. A full strategy profile is an idea in the larger algebra. Restricting the profile to a subgame is a horizon morphism. Subgame perfection says the equilibrium idea remains an equilibrium under every such restriction. Trembling hand perfection perturbs the resonance pairing by small probabilities of error. A refined equilibrium is an idempotent strategic idea whose best response status survives these restriction and perturbation tests.

## Central claim as theorem

*A credible equilibrium is a stable strategic idea that remains locally optimal under subgame restriction and small tremble horizons.*

The theorem rules out equilibria held together by empty off path threats. It predicts that sequential rationality requires more than no profitable deviation at the beginning. Each later horizon must also validate the strategy. It also predicts that probability zero events cannot simply be ignored, because small chances of error reveal whether a strategy is truly robust.

Carefully restated, take a horizon category whose objects are game tree nodes, proper subgames, full strategic profiles, perturbed games, and limiting tremble contexts. The horizon functor assigns to each object a local payoff resonance algebra. Composition forms strategies through sequential plans. Identity preserves the current profile. Resonance pairing evaluates payoffs against possible continuations. Preservation part carries strategic commitments through the tree. Suppression part removes noncredible threats when a subgame is tested. Emergence part is the sequential outcome generated by the whole plan. Emergence cocycle records the added stability or fragility created by off path contingencies. Degree or grading may rank refinement strength or strategic complexity. Meaning curvature appears when a threat has value from one horizon but fails from the later horizon where it would be used. T15 applies because credibility requires support across subgames and perturbation horizons.

## What formalism adds

The formalism makes refinement a family of restrictions. Nash equilibrium is not thrown away; it is placed at the first level. Subgame perfection asks the equilibrium section to remain valid under subhorizon maps. Trembling hand perfection asks it to remain valid when the local algebra is perturbed by small credences assigned to mistakes. This makes Selten's hierarchy sound like a sequence of increasingly demanding support conditions over the same strategic object.

## Where it diverges

Selten's reasoning is deeply temporal. Backward induction works because the game tree has an order: later choices are evaluated first, then earlier choices are disciplined by that evaluation. A clean algebraic restriction can hide this temporal force. Idea Theory captures the structural requirement that equilibria survive subhorizons, but it must explicitly retain temporality if it wants to recover the persuasive logic of sequential rationality.

## Horizon structure and theorem

The horizon structure includes the initial game, every proper subgame, perturbed versions with small mistakes, and limiting cases where trembles become negligible. The horizon functor assigns local payoff algebras to each. T15 governs the chapter because a refined equilibrium is credible only when its support extends through these related horizons and remains stable under restriction.


A simple bargaining story shows the need. One player threatens to burn a surplus if the other refuses a demand. At the initial node the threat may shape behavior. But if refusal actually occurs, burning the surplus may hurt the threatener too much to be rational. Nash equilibrium can record the threat as part of a stable plan. Selten asks whether the plan still makes sense in the subgame where the threat must be carried out.

Trembles sharpen the same lesson. If an event has very small probability, it still tests the equilibrium's meaning. A strategy that works only when mistakes are impossible may be a theatrical pose rather than a credible commitment. The formalism treats this as support across perturbed horizons. Credibility is not purity; it is survival under small impurities.


Refinement also changes how we hear threats and promises. A promise is credible only if the promised action remains rational when the promised moment arrives. A threat is credible only under the same test. Sequential structure turns words into horizon dependent commitments.

So refinement is equilibrium tested by the futures that the game itself makes possible.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

Potentiality is central because trembles expose off path possibilities that ordinary equilibrium may conceal. Temporality matters because extensive games unfold through histories. Credence appears as small probabilities assigned to mistakes. Reflexion matters because a proposed equilibrium is tested against its own continuation requirements. Perspective matters because each decision node creates a local view of what is rational. Signed valence appears in the payoff consequences of carrying out or abandoning threats.

## Chapter summary

Selten gives Idea Theory a discipline of credibility. An equilibrium is not enough if it collapses in a subgame or under small errors. Refinement asks whether the strategic idea has transhorizonal support. That is why the chapter belongs under T15: credibility is stability across the horizons where play might go. This matters because credibility is not an ornament added after equilibrium; it is the test that lets equilibrium survive time.
