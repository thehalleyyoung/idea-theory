# Chapter 122 — John Nash on Equilibrium

Nash equilibrium is the still point of mutual adjustment. Each player asks, given what the others are doing, whether a change would help. If no one can improve by changing alone, the profile rests. Idea Theory hears that rest as a fixed pattern in a strategic idea algebra: composition gathers actions into a profile, resonance pairing evaluates unilateral deviation, and identity marks the profile that reproduces itself under best response.

## Biographical preamble

John Forbes Nash junior lived from nineteen twenty eight to twenty fifteen, working in the American mathematical tradition at Princeton and beyond. His early papers transformed economics with astonishing compression. His sentence can be rendered simply: in a finite game, there is an equilibrium point where every player is doing as well as possible against the others' choices.

## Position

Nash showed that strategic rationality does not require a single planner. In a game with several players, each has a set of strategies and payoffs. A Nash equilibrium is a strategy profile in which no player can gain by unilateral deviation while the others remain fixed. The power of the concept is its modesty. It does not say the outcome is fair, efficient, wise, or morally attractive. It says only that the incentives hold the profile in place.

## Algebraic re-statement

Let each player's strategy be an idea in a shared strategic algebra. A full profile is a composition of player indexed ideas. The resonance pairing gives the payoff of one player's idea against the composed background of the others. A unilateral deviation is a local alteration inside one perspective while the rest of the profile is preserved. Identity is the operation that leaves the profile as it is. Equilibrium is the point at which the best response operation returns the same profile rather than generating a new one.

## Central claim as theorem

*A strategic profile is stable when every local best response validates the same global composition.*

The theorem rules out explanations that depend on a commanding center. Stability can arise from mutual constraint alone. It also rules out any profile where at least one player has a profitable unilateral deviation, because that profile contains an internal pressure against itself. The theorem predicts that social patterns may persist even when everyone would prefer some other coordinated world, so long as no one can reach it alone.

Carefully restated, begin with a horizon category whose objects are player perspectives, unilateral deviation contexts, payoff descriptions, and full strategy profiles. The horizon functor assigns to each object a local payoff resonance algebra. Composition forms a joint profile from player strategies. Identity leaves a player's current action unchanged. Resonance pairing evaluates that player's payoff against the others. Preservation part keeps the opponents' strategies fixed during a unilateral test. Suppression part ignores changes outside the deviating player's horizon. Emergence part is the profile level outcome created by the combination of actions. Emergence cocycle records gains or losses produced by interaction rather than by isolated choice. Degree or grading may rank strategy complexity. Meaning curvature appears when one player's improvement worsens another's position. T13 applies because local best response sections glue into one global equilibrium section when they are mutually compatible.

## What formalism adds

The formalism shows that Nash equilibrium is not only a game theoretic object. It is a pattern of local optimality under restricted perturbation. Whenever a system has perspectives, local payoff evaluations, and a rule for composing actions into outcomes, Nash style stability can appear. A traffic convention, a linguistic usage, a market routine, or a bureaucratic habit may be stable in this sense. The algebra makes the common skeleton audible without pretending that all these cases share the same psychology.

## Where it diverges

Nash's existence result for finite games depends on mixed strategies and fixed point reasoning over well behaved spaces. Idea Theory can describe the fixed point pattern, but the bare algebra does not automatically supply convexity, continuity, or a probability simplex. If those are needed, they must be added as horizon structure. The formalism captures what equilibrium means once present; it does not by itself prove that every strategic world contains one.

## Horizon structure and theorem

The horizon category is the product of player horizons, together with maps that restrict attention to a single player's deviation problem. The horizon functor assigns each player a payoff algebra over the shared profile. T13 is the guiding theorem: the equilibrium profile is a global section assembled from local optimality conditions, one for each perspective.


A small social example makes the idea vivid. Imagine several commuters choosing routes into a city. Each route's value depends on how many others choose it. A driver may dislike the present congestion, yet if every alternative route would be worse given current traffic, the pattern is an equilibrium. No central intelligence designed it. No driver loves it. Still, the profile holds because every local deviation has been tested and rejected from the relevant perspective.

This is why Nash equilibrium became so portable. It describes markets, voting blocs, conventions, arms races, and household routines because it asks a spare question: what happens if one participant changes while the rest stay fixed? Idea Theory calls that a horizon restriction. The whole profile is too large to judge at once, so the algebra examines each local deviation. If all the local tests return the original composition, the profile has strategic identity.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

Perspective is central because each player sees the same profile through a distinct payoff horizon. Credence enters when mixed strategies are used to stabilize play. Reflexion is the fixed point character of the concept: each best response confirms the pattern that called it forth. Schema appears in the normal form that makes strategies and deviations legible. Signed valence appears in the payoff ordering that distinguishes improvement from loss.

## Chapter summary

Nash gives Idea Theory its cleanest model of mutual stability. Composition creates a profile, resonance pairing evaluates deviations, and equilibrium is the point where every local test returns the same global arrangement. The chapter belongs under T13 because compatible local best responses glue into a strategic whole.
