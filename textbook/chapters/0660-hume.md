# Chapter 46 — Hume

David Hume lived from 1711 to 1776 in Scotland, France, and England, within Scottish Enlightenment empiricism and naturalism. In his own translated words: “Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions.”

## The position in its own voice

Hear the doctrine in its own intellectual voice. Hume radicalizes the Lockean program. All the contents of the mind are perceptions, divided into impressions (vivid, immediate) and ideas (faint copies of impressions). The mind possesses no idea that is not derived from a prior impression; the famous test is to demand the impression that any putative idea copies, and to dismiss what fails the demand. Perceptions are connected by three principles of association: resemblance, contiguity in space and time, and cause and effect. These principles are not deliverances of reason but features of the imagination's spontaneous activity. They function as a "gentle force" that organizes the stream of perception into something like an experienced world. The most consequential application is to causation. Hume argues that we never observe a necessary connection between cause and effect; we observe constant conjunction, and our minds, by habit, project an expectation of the second on the appearance of the first. The "necessity" of causal connection is a feature of the observer, not of the world. The same skeptical analysis dissolves the self. When Hume looks inward, he finds only a bundle of perceptions, each itself a fleeting impression, with no enduring substantial self underlying them. Personal identity is a fiction the imagination weaves out of resemblance and contiguity among successive perceptions. The voice trains attention by asking what is stable, what must be discarded, and what kind of insight appears only after that discipline has been accepted.

## The idea algebra sub-instance

Before translating, recall the operating definitions. Ideas compose with other ideas. Composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. Ideas resonate with one another, and their non-reciprocal pull is meaning curvature. Ideas also live over horizons: contexts, stages, perspectives, times, practices, or worlds. Hume treats impressions and ideas as fleeting perceptions. Composition is association by resemblance, contiguity, and habit. The preservation part is copied vivacity from impression to idea, the suppression part removes hidden necessity and substantial selfhood, and the emergence part is belief as a lively idea produced by custom. Resonance is associative force, and degree measures vivacity and confidence. In plain English, the sub-instance asks what counts as an idea, how ideas combine, what survives, what is cancelled, and what new content appears.

## Central claim as theorem

*If the mind never perceives necessary connection, then causal belief is an emergent habit rather than insight into hidden power.*

The theorem rules out the easy opposite of the view. It predicts that across the proper horizons some content will travel, some will fail, and some will arise only through that failure. The theorem identifies the middle structure: invariant, obstruction, or gluing.

Carefully restated, choose the relevant horizon category and let the horizon functor - the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi - assign a complete idea algebra to each horizon. Name composition, identity, resonance, the preservation part, the suppression part, the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, and meaning curvature. Then the theorem says that Hume's central claim is exactly the stable support, failed pullback, or glued agreement described in the preceding paragraph, stated without symbols.

## What the formalism adds

First, Hume's three principles of association become a weighted resonance metric whose weights are empirically tunable. Modern cognitive psychology and machine learning have produced algorithms that look strikingly similar to weighted association; the formalism makes Hume their natural ancestor. Second, the "missing shade of blue" becomes a precise gap in the generator set: an element interpolatable by the idea carrier's structure but not directly supplied by experience. This formalizes Hume's reluctant concession against the strict copy principle and shows it is a structural concession, not an ad hoc one. More generally, the formalism adds comparison. It tells the listener whether preservation, suppression, or emergence is carrying the doctrine.

## Where the formalism diverges

First, the formalism assumes a well-defined carrier set the idea carrier. Hume's deepest skepticism - about the persistence of perceptions when unobserved, about the reality of the external world - undermines exactly this assumption. The algebra requires a stable inventory of elements that Hume's analysis intermittently denies. Second, the bundle theory makes the very identification of "the same" perception across moments problematic. The algebra needs to talk about specific elements; The algebra is therefore an aid to hearing, not a substitute for reading.

## Horizon category and horizon-functor theorem

Hume's the horizon category is the stream of impressions, ideas, habits, and social sentiments, ordered by vivacity rather than metaphysical depth. Morphisms copy impressions into ideas and associate ideas by resemblance, contiguity, and cause, but no morphism reaches a necessary connection behind the sequence. temporality governs succession, credence governs belief as enlivened expectation, and signed valence captures the sentimental valence behind morals. In this chapter the most central horizon-functor theorem is the Profundity theorem. It names gluing, obstruction, or depth as the central horizon behavior.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

credence. Probability and custom replace rational necessity, assigning credence through habit after repeated conjunctions. differentiation. The impression/idea distinction and relations of ideas versus matters of fact organize differential content. temporality. Personal identity is a bundle succession, not a stable substance persisting unchanged through time. conatus. Passions supply affective force, with reason serving rather than originating practical motivation. These primitives are consequences of the way composition, resonance, degree, and horizon translation behave here.

One more listening cue helps. Ask, at every turn, whether the thinker is protecting a stable reference, exposing a failed translation, or gathering scattered meanings into a whole. That question keeps the formal reconstruction close to the philosophical voice rather than letting it become a merely external classification.

## Chapter summary

Hume gives one answer to what an idea is, how it combines, and how it remains or fails to remain meaningful across horizons. The idea algebra restates that answer by naming preserved content, suppressed illusion, emergent insight, horizon behavior, and diagnostic derived primitives. The gain is clarity; the cost is abstraction. A good listener keeps both in view.
