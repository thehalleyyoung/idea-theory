# Chapter 78 — Nāgārjuna

This chapter treats Nāgārjuna as a test case for idea theory. We keep the operating
definitions audible: composition puts ideas together, the identity idea leaves a composition
unchanged, the resonance pairing measures directed evocation, the preservation part,
suppression part, and emergence part describe what is kept, cancelled, and newly produced,
and the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — lets the
whole algebra vary by context.

## Biographical preamble

Nāgārjuna, pronounced approximately *nah-GAR-joo-nah* lived from around 150 to 250, worked
in India, and belongs here to Madhyamaka Buddhism. In one compressed sentence, he taught
that whatever is dependently arisen is empty of self-standing essence. That sentence gives
the chapter its entry point: meaning is not a bare object sitting before a spectator, but a
structured achievement within a field of acts, signs, practices, or horizons.

## Position

The position can be put simply. Nāgārjuna's Mūlamadhyamakakārikā argues that all phenomena
are empty (śūnya) of intrinsic nature (svabhāva): nothing exists in and of itself; every
entity is dependently originated in relation to causes, conditions, and conventional
designations. The two truths — conventional (saṃvṛti) and ultimate (paramārtha) — coexist:
conventional discourse is functionally adequate, while ultimate analysis dissolves all
foundationalist metaphysics. The tetralemma, called catuṣkoṭi, tests affirmation, denial, both affirmation and denial, and neither affirmation nor denial, then shows that none has intrinsic finality on metaphysical questions. The point is not merely
historical. It tells us what kind of item is being counted as an idea: not a loose mental
image, but a content whose sense depends on the way it is given, used, interpreted, or
carried forward.

## Algebraic re-statement

In the algebraic re-statement, the basic domain is the field of contents named by the zoo
entry. Emptiness is the universal claim that no idea has an intrinsic nature relation to itself. Each idea is constituted by its preservation network with other ideas, not by a standalone svabhāva. Dependent origination means that an idea identity is exhausted by its place in a relational graph. Remove the relations and the supposed self-standing item vanishes. The two truths become two levels of analysis: conventional analysis works within the algebra and yields useful determinations, while ultimate analysis dissolves the claim that any determination has independent essence. The tetralemma tests affirmation, denial, both, and neither, and shows that their relation to the world is conventional rather than ultimate.
Read aloud, this says that the thinker supplies a local model of the ten-tuple: a domain of
contents, a composition rule, an identity idea, directed resonance, the threefold division
into preservation, suppression, and emergence, an emergence cocycle, a degree of
articulation, and a context-sensitive horizon structure.

## Theorem re-statement

*The theorem-style claim is this: No idea has intrinsic existence; all are relationally constituted.*

In paragraph form, the claim rules out the fantasy that meaning is already complete before
context enters. No idea has intrinsic existence; all are relationally constituted. The identity of an idea is the totality of its preservation relations; no residue remains.
Conventional and ultimate analyses coexist; conventional operates within the algebra,
ultimate dissolves foundationalist claims about it. For any metaphysical claim, none of the standard alternatives is ultimately final. The prediction is that failures of understanding will
not be accidental noise. They will cluster where a local content cannot be carried unchanged
across a change of horizon, or where an apparent identity hides suppressed difference.

Carefully restated, the relevant idea algebra has a domain of contents, an identity idea,
associative composition, the resonance pairing, the preservation part, the suppression part,
the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, the horizon category, and the horizon
functor. The central claim says that the philosopher's privileged object is stable only when
these roles cooperate. Where local data can be glued, the chapter instantiates the indexical pullback theorem; where
translation fails, the obstruction is also audible as a horizon-functor effect.

## What the formalism adds

What the formalism adds is clarification without replacement. It exhibits emptiness as a
precise denial of self-standing predication, distinguishing Madhyamaka from nihilism (which
would deny existence outright). Dependent origination becomes a graph-theoretic identity
condition, making explicit the relational constitution of every entity. The two truths are
rendered as two operators on the same algebra rather than two ontologies, clarifying their
compatibility. The tetralemma is captured as a four-fold exhaustion test, exposing what
Nāgārjuna's analysis demands. The reflexive emptiness of emptiness is a fixed-point property
of ultimate analysis, preserving the doctrine from self-refutation. In ordinary
language, the formalism lets us ask which operation does the philosophical work. Is the key
relation resonance, because one content fulfills or evokes another? Is it suppression,
because meaning appears through contrast? Is it the horizon functor, because every local act
changes when the context changes? The answer differs from thinker to thinker, but the
vocabulary stays stable.

## Where it diverges

The divergence is equally important. Madhyamaka is a soteriological project: emptiness
liberates from suffering by undoing clinging. The algebra captures the metaphysics but not
the practice. The tetralemma is sometimes interpreted non-classically (as rejecting
bivalence) and sometimes classically (as a dialectical exhaustion); the algebra's rendering
is compatible with both but commits to neither. The interlocutors of the
Mūlamadhyamakakārikā are specific Abhidharma schools whose svabhāva-realism gives
Nāgārjuna's polemic its force; abstracted, the polemic loses its target. Buddhist
commitments — karma, rebirth, the path — are background conditions for emptiness-doctrine
that the algebra does not invoke. This is not a defect in the reading. It marks the price of
any formal reconstruction: the algebra shows the load-bearing structure, while the original
philosophy often also includes tone, method, ethical urgency, religious practice, literary
performance, or first-person discipline.

## Horizon structure and the horizon-functor theorem

The horizon structure names the context in which the local algebra lives. Nāgārjuna's the
horizon category has at least the conventional and ultimate horizons, but neither is a final
substance-view. Morphisms of Madhyamaka analysis translate any asserted svabhāva into
dependent origination and then dissolve the analysis itself. R is decisive in emptiness of
emptiness as fixed-point self-application of the horizon functor, differentiation prevents
positive essence, and intentionality is conventional designation without intrinsic target.
This is the indexical pullback theorem plus R: the two truths are horizon-levels of the presheaf, and every attempt to
make emptiness invariant as a doctrine fails reflexively. For this reason the chapter should
be heard through the indexical pullback theorem. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem is the hermeneutic sheaf theorem, where local meanings glue into a
global one when they agree on overlaps. The indexical pullback theorem is the indexical pullback theorem, where
preservation and emergence fail to translate cleanly. The profundity theorem is the profundity theorem, where
depth comes from breadth across horizons and closure under emergence.

## Derived primitives

The derived primitives make the diagnosis compact. R. Śūnyatā śūnyatā is a double-reflexive
fixed point: even emptiness is empty. differentiation: Pratītyasamutpāda is pure
differentiation, where things have identity only through dependent relations. rigid
reference: No rigid designators survive ultimate analysis, because svabhāva is denied for
every putative referent. schema or paradigm: Conventional and ultimate truths are distinct
horizons, neither reducible to a metaphysical substrate. None of these is introduced as an
extra metaphysical ingredient. Each is derived from the ten-tuple: intentionality from
resonance across horizons, temporality from ordered horizon-translation, perspective from a
home region in the horizon category, differentiation from suppression, and reflexion from
self-application of the horizon functor.

Nāgārjuna therefore enters idea theory as a disciplined local algebra of meaning. The
chapter has followed the promised path: biography, position, algebraic re-statement, theorem
re-statement, addition, divergence, horizon structure, and derived primitives. The result is
not a replacement for the thinker, but a listening guide to the formal pressure already
present in the work.
