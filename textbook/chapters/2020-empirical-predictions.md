# Chapter 151 — Empirical Predictions

The core definitions remain our guide. Ideas compose, resonate, preserve, suppress, and generate emergence. Novelty is constrained across chains by the emergence cocycle. Grading tracks depth or complexity. Meaning curvature records directional asymmetry. The horizon functor, the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi, makes the whole system vary by context; after this first mention we call it the horizon functor. A theory this broad earns its keep only if it risks predictions. Here are five families of tests.

## Cultural evolution

The first prediction concerns transmission chains. If the theory is right, high-fidelity cultural items should not simply be the shortest or catchiest items. They should be items whose preservation part remains strong across many horizons and whose emergence part does not explode into incoherence. A proverb, ritual, proof technique, or interface convention survives when local reinterpretations can be glued back into a recognizable global section.

This predicts that cultural variants with moderate local adaptability will outperform variants that are either rigid in every detail or endlessly plastic. Too much rigidity breaks under horizon shift. Too much plasticity loses identity. The sweet spot is trans-horizonal stability with bounded emergence. In cultural evolution experiments, one should therefore see durable traditions that preserve a core while allowing local elaboration.

The second prediction is about weak ties. Network bridges should not merely increase diffusion speed. They should increase novelty when they connect horizons with compatible overlap and increase noise when they connect horizons without gluable overlap. A weak tie between neighboring disciplines may generate productive metaphors; a weak tie between incompatible horizons may produce slogans without inference.

## Cognitive psychology

In categorization, the theory predicts that typicality effects should vary with horizon shifts in a structured way. A robin is a typical bird in one ordinary horizon. A penguin becomes more salient in a polar ecology horizon. A bat may resonate with bird in a child's perceptual horizon and fail in a biological taxonomy horizon. These changes should not be random. They should track which local resonance profiles the task activates.

In framing studies, the theory predicts durable directional asymmetries. If meaning curvature is real, then switching the order or frame of ideas will not merely add bias as noise. It will systematically change which inferences are licensed. Loss frames, gain frames, moral frames, and identity frames should leave measurable residues even when informational content is held constant.

In learning, the framework predicts that instruction works best when it provides horizon translations rather than isolated facts. A learner moves from a home perspective to a refined perspective. Teaching should therefore succeed when examples, analogies, and exercises preserve enough resonance for the learner to cross the translation while introducing enough emergence to expand the schema.

## Distributional semantics

Distributional semantics already measures resonance through patterns of co-occurrence. Idea Theory adds two predictions. First, asymmetric contexts should matter. The phrase that follows a word and the phrase that precedes it may not contribute the same meaning. Models that preserve directional resonance should better capture entailment, metaphor, and framing than models that treat co-occurrence as merely symmetric similarity.

Second, semantic innovation should be detectable as emergence outside a lineage closure. When a word such as cloud, viral, or platform shifts domains, the new usage is not only a nearest-neighbor movement. It is a horizon translation that preserves some resonance, suppresses other resonance, and creates new inferential possibilities. Good diachronic models should distinguish mere drift from structural emergence.

The theory also predicts that hallucination in large language models is often failed sheaf gluing. The model has local fragments that sound coherent in nearby horizons, but the fragments do not agree on overlaps. Better systems should test whether local claims glue into a single stable section before presenting them as knowledge.

## Behavioral economics

Behavioral economics should find that framing effects cluster by meaning curvature. Some frames merely change salience. Others reverse valence. Others break an inference that seemed stable. Prospect theory, endowment effects, and social preference effects can be sorted by which structural element shifts across horizons: credence, signed valence, preservation, or suppression.

In market settings, the framework predicts that prices work best where local knowledge can be glued through shared constraints. Hayek's dispersed knowledge thesis becomes testable as a sheaf condition: local signals must agree enough on overlaps for a global price to coordinate action. Market failure should appear when overlaps are corrupted, hidden, or strategically distorted.

In game theory, focal points should be ideas with high support inside a shared paradigm component. They are not magic saliences. They are common sections that many agents can reach from their home horizons. Experiments should show that focality rises when participants share cultural or perceptual horizons and falls when those horizons fragment.

## Philosophy of mind

The theory predicts that reports of intentionality, agency, and selfhood will track derived structure. A system will be treated as minded when observers can assign it stable intentional correlates, perspective, signed valence, and reflexion. This does not settle consciousness, but it predicts attribution patterns. People will grant mind more readily to systems whose behavior supports coherent cross-horizon interpretation.

For artificial agents, the prediction is sharper. Merely producing correct text will not be enough for robust mind attribution over time. The system must preserve commitments across horizon shifts, repair contradictions on overlaps, show stable valence-like priorities, and distinguish self-application from ordinary output. Failures in these areas will be experienced not just as errors, but as failures of mindedness.

## How the predictions should be tested

A useful test of the framework should compare horizons rather than merely compare isolated stimuli. In cultural evolution, this means running transmission experiments where the same item passes through communities with different background practices and then asking whether preserved cores and local innovations can be separated. In cognitive psychology, it means designing tasks that hold information constant while shifting the active horizon: bodily context, professional role, emotional frame, or linguistic setting. In distributional semantics, it means measuring whether asymmetric context windows and diachronic shifts detect meaning curvature better than symmetric similarity alone.

The theory also predicts failures. Some meanings will not glue. Some translations will preserve vocabulary but break inference. Some apparent innovations will collapse into recombinations once their lineage is reconstructed. A strong empirical program should welcome those failures, because they tell us where the horizon category has been drawn incorrectly or where a claimed primitive is not actually derived.

## What would count as bad news

The framework would be weakened if horizon shifts produced no patterned effects beyond ordinary noise. It would be weakened if asymmetric resonance never improved semantic prediction. It would be weakened if cultural items survived only by simplicity, with no measurable role for bounded adaptability. It would be weakened if focal points appeared independently of shared horizons. Most importantly, it would be weakened if local interpretive agreement failed to predict global understanding better than mere repetition of vocabulary.

## Chapter summary

The framework predicts bounded adaptability in cultural transmission, structured horizon effects in cognition, directional resonance in semantics, sheaf-like coordination in markets, and mind attribution tied to intentionality, perspective, valence, and reflexion. These are empirical risks, not decorative analogies.
