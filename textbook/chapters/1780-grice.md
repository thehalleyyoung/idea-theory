# Chapter 141 — H. P. Grice on Implicature

Conversation works because speakers do not say everything and hearers do not hear only what is said. Grice showed that much meaning lives in the space between literal content and cooperative expectation. Idea Theory treats implicature as resonance recovery across a conversational horizon.

## Biographical preamble

H. P. Grice lived from 1913 to 1988, worked in Oxford and later at Berkeley, and helped shape analytic philosophy of language. His essays on meaning and conversation gave pragmatics its classic form. In his own terms, make your conversational contribution such as is required by the accepted purpose of the exchange.

## Position

Suppose a professor writes a recommendation letter that says only, Her attendance was excellent. The sentence is positive on its surface. Yet the reader may infer that the student was not academically strong. The inference comes from the assumption that a cooperative writer would have mentioned intellectual ability if there were something good to say. The meaning is implicated, not literally stated.

Grice organized such cases under the Cooperative Principle and maxims of quantity, quality, relation, and manner. Say enough, say what you have reason to believe, be relevant, and be clear. These are not rigid laws. They are expectations that can be followed, flouted, suspended, or exploited. Irony, understatement, tact, and hinting all depend on them.

## Algebraic re-statement

In Idea Theory, a conversation is a horizon category of speakers, hearers, purposes, background knowledge, and permissible moves. Composition links one utterance to another. Identity is the state in which no new conversational pressure is added. The resonance pairing measures fit between utterance, purpose, truthfulness, relevance, and style.

The preservation part carries literal content. The suppression part marks what the speaker leaves unsaid, blocks, or cancels. The emergence part is the implicated meaning recovered by the hearer. Degree or grading tracks conversational economy. A high degree utterance may be too elaborate. A low degree utterance may be too thin unless the horizon supplies the missing resonance.

## Central claim as theorem

*Implicature is the emergence of intended meaning from the gap between literal content and cooperative horizon.*

This is T14 read through Grice. The theorem rules out a simple transfer model in which the hearer receives only preserved content. It predicts that meaning can arise precisely where preservation fails to carry everything. The obstruction is productive. A brief remark, a pause, or a selective compliment can generate an emergence part because the hearer translates the utterance through cooperative expectations.

Carefully restated, the horizon category contains speaker, hearer, topic, purpose, background knowledge, and norms of cooperation. The horizon functor assigns to each situation the utterances that would count as informative, truthful, relevant, and orderly. Composition places an utterance inside the exchange. Identity is no change in common ground. The resonance pairing measures fit with the cooperative purpose. The preservation part is literal content, the suppression part is omitted or cancelled content, and the emergence part is the implicature. The diagnostic derived primitive is credence, because the hearer estimates what a cooperative speaker would have meant.

## What formalism adds

The formalism unifies the maxims as constraints on resonance and degree. Quantity and manner concern grading, because the utterance should be neither needlessly large nor unusably small for the task. Quality and relation concern resonance, because the utterance must connect to truth and context. The Cooperative Principle is not an extra decoration. It is the horizon condition that lets hearers recover emergence from limited preservation.

It also explains cancellability. If the speaker says, Her attendance was excellent, but I do not mean to imply anything about her ability, the speaker changes the horizon. The earlier emergence part is suppressed by a later utterance. Conversation is dynamic composition, not a single sentence frozen in isolation.

A dinner invitation shows the structure. You ask whether a guest is coming, and your friend says, Her car is in the repair shop. Literally, that sentence reports a car problem. In the cooperative horizon, it implies that the guest may not come, or may need a ride. The preservation part is the car report. The suppression part is the unsaid direct answer. The emergence part is the practical conclusion you are licensed to draw. If your friend adds, but she lives next door and will walk, the earlier implicature is cancelled. Grice's brilliance is to show how fragile, efficient, and ordinary this process is.

## Where it diverges

Grice's maxims are defeasible and psychologically subtle. Idea Theory can sound too optimized, as if speakers always solve a clean problem. Real conversation includes distraction, politeness, hierarchy, joke, fear, and strategic ambiguity. The formalism diverges if it treats every implicature as a tidy computation. Grice is better heard as giving a norm of interpretation that people use flexibly, not a mechanical law.

## Horizon structure

Grice's horizon is conversational and cooperative. It includes shared purposes, social expectations, background knowledge, and the possibility of cancellation. It most centrally instantiates T14, because implicature depends on a horizon translation in which preservation of literal content is not enough. Meaning appears through the obstruction between what is said and what cooperation makes hearable.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

The diagnostic derived primitives are credence, signed valence, and perspective. Credence measures the hearer's estimate of speaker intention. Signed valence captures trust, suspicion, politeness, and irony. Perspective distinguishes what the speaker says from what the hearer is licensed to infer.

The same structure explains why silence can speak. If a response is expected and withheld, the withholding itself becomes a conversational move. Its resonance depends on the horizon: silence after an accusation, silence after a toast, and silence after a routine question do not mean the same thing.
 This is why listeners can hear tact as meaning, not absence.

## Chapter summary

Grice shows that conversation carries more than literal content. Idea Theory restates implicature as an emergence part created by cooperative resonance across a horizon. The preservation part is what is said, the suppression part is what is left unsaid or cancelled, and the emergence part is what is meant. T14 is central because pragmatic meaning arises through horizon translation.
