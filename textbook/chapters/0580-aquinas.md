# Chapter 38 — Aquinas

Thomas Aquinas, pronounced approximately *uh-KWY-nus*, lived from 1225 to 1274 in Italy and France, within medieval scholastic Aristotelian Christianity. In his own translated words: “Grace does not destroy nature, but perfects it.”

## The position in its own voice

Hear the doctrine in its own intellectual voice. Aquinas's distinctive metaphysical move is the real distinction between essence ( essentia ) and act of being ( esse ). For every creature, what it is and that it is are genuinely distinct: the essence specifies what kind of being a thing would be, but it requires an additional, ontologically distinct act of existence to be at all. Only in God do essence and existence coincide; God's essence simply is to-be ( ipsum esse subsistens ). This distinction supports a metaphysics of participation. Creatures have being; God is being. Creatures participate in esse as receiving from a source; God communicates esse as the source. The chain of causes therefore terminates in a being whose existence is not received, and this is the metaphysical core of the Five Ways: each begins from some feature of finite being and traces it back to a non-derivative ground. Mediating the distance between God and creatures is the analogy of being ( analogia entis ). Univocal predication would collapse God into the creaturely order; equivocal predication would make God-talk meaningless. Analogy is the middle way: the same term applies to God and to creatures with related but proportionally distinct meanings, and so allows substantive theological speech without ontological confusion. The voice trains attention by asking what is stable, what must be discarded, and what kind of insight appears only after that discipline has been accepted.

## The idea algebra sub-instance

Before translating, recall the operating definitions. Ideas compose with other ideas. Composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. Ideas resonate with one another, and their non-reciprocal pull is meaning curvature. Ideas also live over horizons: contexts, stages, perspectives, times, practices, or worlds. Aquinas treats created ideas as essences receiving act of being. Composition is participation: finite beings receive being from the source that is being itself. The preservation part is real likeness by causal dependence. The suppression part prevents creaturely limits from being projected onto God. The emergence part is analogical meaning, neither univocal nor empty. Resonance is grounded participation, and degree marks ascent from natural reason through faith toward beatitude. In plain English, the sub-instance asks what counts as an idea, how ideas combine, what survives, what is cancelled, and what new content appears.

## Central claim as theorem

*If creatures receive being while God is being itself, then meaningful speech about God must be analogical rather than univocal or merely equivocal.*

The theorem rules out the easy opposite of the view. It predicts that across the proper horizons some content will travel, some will fail, and some will arise only through that failure. The theorem identifies the middle structure: invariant, obstruction, or gluing.

Carefully restated, choose the relevant horizon category and let the horizon functor - the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi - assign a complete idea algebra to each horizon. Name composition, identity, resonance, the preservation part, the suppression part, the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, and meaning curvature. Then the theorem says that Aquinas's central claim is exactly the stable support, failed pullback, or glued agreement described in the preceding paragraph, stated without symbols.

## What the formalism adds

First, the formalism makes the analogy of being a precise metric. Aquinas's discussions of analogy of attribution and analogy of proportionality are notoriously slippery; resonance gives a definite numerical reading that lets one compare different analogical predications and ask which preserves more structure. Second, the compatibility of faith and reason becomes the assertion that the emergence part (theological reframing) and composition (philosophical composition) commute. Where they fail to commute, one has either heresy (theology overreaching) or rationalism (philosophy overreaching); the algebraic frame supplies a precise diagnostic for these failures. More generally, the formalism adds comparison. It tells the listener whether preservation, suppression, or emergence is carrying the doctrine.

## Where the formalism diverges

First, the real distinction between esse and essentia is more than a factorization in a fixed algebra; for Aquinas it is a genuine metaphysical composition with its own causal structure. The formalism captures the factorization but not the dynamic by which essence "limits" esse - a process Aquinas treats as a genuine constituent of every finite being. Second, divine simplicity forbids any composition in God whatsoever, including any attempt to identify God with a constructed pair of empty essence and maximal being. The algebra is therefore an aid to hearing, not a substitute for reading.

## Horizon category and horizon-functor theorem

Aquinas' the horizon category is the analogical order of nature, grace, reason, revelation, and beatific vision. Morphisms translate creaturely predicates toward God by analogy rather than univocity, preserving causal dependence while altering semantic mode. potentiality matters for potency and act, intentionality for intellect's orientation to being, and schema for the coordinated but distinct horizons of philosophy and sacred doctrine. In this chapter the most central horizon-functor theorem is the Profundity theorem. It names gluing, obstruction, or depth as the central horizon behavior.

## Diagnostic derived primitives

potentiality. Potency and act structure every creature, while God as actus purus has no unrealized remainder. rigid reference. Ipsum esse subsistens functions as rigid divine reference, grounding analogical predication across created horizons. intentionality. Intentional species explain how intellect is directed toward forms without materially becoming the objects known. credence. Faith and demonstratio assign different credence grades to mysteries and truths accessible to natural reason. These primitives are consequences of the way composition, resonance, degree, and horizon translation behave here.

One more listening cue helps. Ask, at every turn, whether the thinker is protecting a stable reference, exposing a failed translation, or gathering scattered meanings into a whole. That question keeps the formal reconstruction close to the philosophical voice rather than letting it become a merely external classification.

## Chapter summary

Aquinas gives one answer to what an idea is, how it combines, and how it remains or fails to remain meaningful across horizons. The idea algebra restates that answer by naming preserved content, suppressed illusion, emergent insight, horizon behavior, and diagnostic derived primitives. The gain is clarity; the cost is abstraction. A good listener keeps both in view.
