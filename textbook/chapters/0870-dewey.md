# Chapter 67 — John Dewey

This chapter treats John Dewey as a test case for idea theory. We keep the operating
definitions audible: composition puts ideas together, the identity idea leaves a composition
unchanged, the resonance pairing measures directed evocation, the preservation part,
suppression part, and emergence part describe what is kept, cancelled, and newly produced,
and the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — lets the
whole algebra vary by context.

## Biographical preamble

John Dewey, pronounced approximately *DOO-ee* lived from 1859 to 1952, worked in the United
States, and belongs here to pragmatism and democratic philosophy. In one compressed
sentence, he understood inquiry as transforming an indeterminate situation into a
determinate one. That sentence gives the chapter its entry point: meaning is not a bare
object sitting before a spectator, but a structured achievement within a field of acts,
signs, practices, or horizons.

## Position

The position can be put simply. Dewey's instrumentalism treats ideas as tools for solving
problems within experience. Inquiry begins in a problematic situation — a felt indeterminacy
that disrupts ongoing activity — and proceeds through the formulation of hypotheses,
experimental testing, and the achievement of a unified, determinate situation. Truth is what
inquiry yields when it succeeds. The traditional philosophical dualisms — mind versus body,
subject versus object, theory versus practice, reason versus experience — are diagnosed as
obstacles. Each pole is an abstraction from a primary unity; treating them as independent
realities generates pseudo-problems that have plagued philosophy since Descartes. The point
is not merely historical. It tells us what kind of item is being counted as an idea: not a
loose mental image, but a content whose sense depends on the way it is given, used,
interpreted, or carried forward.

## Algebraic re-statement

In the algebraic re-statement, the basic domain is the field of contents named by the zoo
entry. Take the idea domain to be the set of situations and the inquiries that traverse
them: items as integrated patterns of experience, problematic or resolved. Each element is
tagged by its degree of indeterminacy and its position in an inquiry-arc. The identity idea
is the integrated situation prior to disruption — the unproblematic ongoing activity.
Composition is inquiry: the temporal sequence by which a problematic situation is
transformed into a resolved one through hypothesis-formation, testing, and integration.
composition is constrained by the situation's indeterminacy and by the resources of the
inquirer. The resonance pairing is functional similarity: the degree to which two situations
admit similar inquiries and yield similar resolutions. Read aloud, this says that the
thinker supplies a local model of the ten-tuple: a domain of contents, a composition rule,
an identity idea, directed resonance, the threefold division into preservation, suppression,
and emergence, an emergence cocycle, a degree of articulation, and a context-sensitive
horizon structure.

## Theorem re-statement

*The theorem-style claim is this: The first theorem-shaped claim is that inquiry is the basic operation composition on situations.*

In paragraph form, the claim rules out the fantasy that meaning is already complete before
context enters. The first theorem-shaped claim is that inquiry is the basic operation
composition on situations. Knowledge is not a state but the achievement of a transformation
of situations. This is a the composition theorem claim with strong instrumentalist content. The second claim is
that ideas are tools: the resonance pairing tracks instrumental adequacy. The prediction is
that failures of understanding will not be accidental noise. They will cluster where a local
content cannot be carried unchanged across a change of horizon, or where an apparent
identity hides suppressed difference.

Carefully restated, the relevant idea algebra has a domain of contents, an identity idea,
associative composition, the resonance pairing, the preservation part, the suppression part,
the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, the horizon category, and the horizon
functor. The central claim says that the philosopher's privileged object is stable only when
these roles cooperate. Where local data can be glued, the chapter instantiates the hermeneutic sheaf theorem; where
translation fails, the obstruction is also audible as a horizon-functor effect.

## What the formalism adds

What the formalism adds is clarification without replacement. First, inquiry as the central
operation gives Dewey's philosophy an algebraic shape: composition is the inquiry-
transformation, and the pattern of inquiry (problematic situation, hypothesis, test,
resolution) is the structural shape of composition-action. Second, the rejection of dualisms
becomes the structural fact that the algebra is one connected component. Apparent partitions
(mind/body, theory/practice) are not algebraic partitions but historical-philosophical
projections. Third, the instrumental conception of truth becomes the assertion that the
resonance pairing tracks fitness for inquiry-purposes rather than correspondence to a mind-
independent fact. Truth is functional, not pictorial. In ordinary language, the formalism
lets us ask which operation does the philosophical work. Is the key relation resonance,
because one content fulfills or evokes another? Is it suppression, because meaning appears
through contrast? Is it the horizon functor, because every local act changes when the
context changes? The answer differs from thinker to thinker, but the vocabulary stays
stable.

## Where it diverges

The divergence is equally important. First, the practical engagement that animates Dewey's
work — his interventions in school reform, his political activism, his sustained public
writing — is a substantive commitment the algebra cannot generate. Dewey's pragmatism is
meant to do work in the world; the formalism describes structural facts. Second, the
substantive content of "the problematic situation" depends on what the inquirer takes to be
a problem, which depends on her interests, history, and culture. The algebra does not fix
what counts as problematic; that is a contextual question. Third, the religious humanism of
A Common Faith — Dewey's late attempt to articulate a religious quality of experience
without supernaturalism — depends on substantive commitments outside structural relations.
This is not a defect in the reading. It marks the price of any formal reconstruction: the
algebra shows the load-bearing structure, while the original philosophy often also includes
tone, method, ethical urgency, religious practice, literary performance, or first-person
discipline.

## Horizon structure and the horizon-functor theorem

The horizon structure names the context in which the local algebra lives. Dewey's the
horizon category is the ecology of problematic situations, inquiry, habits, publics,
schools, and democratic institutions. Morphisms transform an indeterminate situation into a
resolved one by experimental action, not by spectatorship. temporality is inquiry's temporal
arc, potentiality is potentiality in the situation, credence is warranted assertibility, and
perspective is social intelligence distributed across a community. This is the hermeneutic sheaf theorem: knowledge
glues local operations, consequences, and revisions into a reconstructed situation whose
meaning is publicly testable. For this reason the chapter should be heard through the hermeneutic sheaf theorem. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem
is the hermeneutic sheaf theorem, where local meanings glue into a global one when they
agree on overlaps. The indexical pullback theorem is the indexical pullback theorem, where preservation and emergence
fail to translate cleanly. The profundity theorem is the profundity theorem, where depth comes from breadth
across horizons and closure under emergence.

## Derived primitives

The derived primitives make the diagnosis compact. potentiality: Inquiry begins in a
problematic situation, developing potentials into warranted resolution. temporality:
Experience is transactional and temporal, reorganizing habits through consequences over
time. schema or paradigm: Habits and democratic publics are schemas for coordinating inquiry
and social learning. credence: Warranted assertibility replaces certainty with
experimentally stabilized credence. None of these is introduced as an extra metaphysical
ingredient. Each is derived from the ten-tuple: intentionality from resonance across
horizons, temporality from ordered horizon-translation, perspective from a home region in
the horizon category, differentiation from suppression, and reflexion from self-application
of the horizon functor.

## Chapter summary

John Dewey therefore enters idea theory as a disciplined local algebra of meaning. The
chapter has followed the promised path: biography, position, algebraic re-statement, theorem
re-statement, addition, divergence, horizon structure, and derived primitives. The result is
not a replacement for the thinker, but a listening guide to the formal pressure already
present in the work.
