# Chapter 11 — The Three Horizon-Functor Theorems

Idea Theory begins with a few operating names. Composition is the act of putting ideas together. The resonance pairing measures how one idea calls, resists, or illuminates another. Each composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. The emergence cocycle records how novelty behaves across regrouping. Degree or grading measures articulation. Meaning curvature names the durable asymmetry in resonance. Context enters through the horizon category, and through the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — which assigns a full idea algebra to each context.

## Why these three theorems matter

Once context becomes first-class structure, three large questions appear. First, when do local interpretations fit together into one coherent understanding. Second, when does translation between contexts preserve inference, and when does it break it. Third, what makes an idea profound rather than merely popular, intense, or old. The three horizon-functor theorems answer these questions in order. They are called the Hermeneutic Sheaf theorem, the Indexical Pullback theorem, and the Profundity theorem.

These theorems are not decorative. They are the formal hinge between the older algebra of composition and the richer theory of context. The first theorem says that understanding has the shape of gluing. The second says that indexicality and incommensurability arise when gluing and translation fail in a precise way. The third says that depth is not a mysterious aura. It is trans-horizonal stability joined to closure under emergence.

## The Hermeneutic Sheaf theorem

*Understanding is achieved when local meanings agree where they overlap and can be glued into one coherent global meaning.*

The theorem rules out the idea that understanding is merely a private flash inside one mind. It also rules out the idea that public agreement is just averaging many opinions. A complex horizon, such as a public debate, a legal tradition, or a shared text, can be covered by smaller horizons: conversations, cases, readings, communities, or situations of use. If the meaning-data in those smaller horizons agree on their overlaps, then one global meaning-section exists. If the overlaps clash, understanding fails locally. If the local pieces agree but cannot be glued, the failure is more subtle: the community may be equivocating, speaking in bad faith, or using a word whose local uses cannot be reconciled into one stable whole.

Careful semi-formal restatement: Given a horizon in the horizon category and a covering family of smaller horizons that jointly present it, suppose each smaller horizon carries local idea-data supplied by the horizon functor. If, whenever two smaller horizons overlap, their local data restrict to the same meaning on that overlap, then there is one and only one global meaning-section over the larger horizon whose restrictions recover the local data. The preservation part specifies what must remain common on overlaps. The emergence part licenses the construction of the larger meaning. The resonance pairing tests whether the local readings answer to the same idea rather than merely sharing a label.

A simple example is a classroom discussion of a poem. One student notices grief, another notices rhythm, another notices political anger, and another notices religious imagery. These are local readings. They need not be identical. The question is whether they agree where they overlap. If the political reading depends on denying the grief that the elegiac reading requires, the overlap fails. If the readings can be reconciled, the class achieves a global interpretation richer than any one reading alone. That is a fusion of horizons in Gadamer's sense, but expressed as a structural condition.

The theorem also speaks to markets and common knowledge. Friedrich Hayek, pronounced approximately HIGH-ek, argued that prices coordinate dispersed information without any one person holding the whole. The price system works when local signals can be glued into global coordination. Robert Aumann, pronounced approximately OW-mun, made common knowledge precise by showing how agreement depends on shared information structure. The Hermeneutic Sheaf theorem hears both as versions of the same pattern: local data must agree on overlaps and glue uniquely.

## The Indexical Pullback theorem

*Some inferences break when pulled back into a new horizon, and the obstruction is the formal seat of indexical meaning.*

The theorem rules out the dream of perfect context-free translation. It does not say that translation always fails. It says that the basic machinery of composition, identity, and resonance may translate cleanly along a horizon shift while preservation, emergence, and grading do not always translate cleanly. An inference valid in one horizon can become invalid in another because the background that made it work has changed. The obstruction is not merely psychological confusion. It is a structural failure of invariance across context.

Careful semi-formal restatement: Given a horizon translation from one horizon to another, the horizon functor supplies a translation between the idea algebras living over those horizons. This translation preserves the core compositional structure and the identity idea. It also preserves enough resonance to keep comparison meaningful. But it need not preserve the preservation part, the emergence part, or the degree of an idea. Therefore an inference licensed in the source horizon may fail in the target horizon. The obstruction is the pattern of these failures across the horizon category. If the obstruction can be removed by coherent adjustment, the inference is repairable. If it cannot, the failure is irreducibly indexical.

Consider the sentence I am here now. In one mouth, at one place, at one time, it is true and useful. Move the same words to another speaker and the reference changes. The composition of I, here, and now depends on horizon. That is the easy case. Harder cases occur in science and politics. A term like rational may carry one inferential role in economics, another in ethics, another in ordinary apology, and another in psychiatry. A statement may preserve its grammar while losing the inference that made it convincing.

Quine's translation indeterminacy, Davidson's radical interpretation, Kuhn's incommensurability, Wittgensteinian language-game relativity, and Foucauldian epistemic discontinuity all become versions of this theorem. The point is not that everyone is trapped in private worlds. The point is more exact: translations preserve some structure and fail to preserve other structure. The task of interpretation is to locate the failure rather than deny it.

## The Profundity theorem

*An idea is profound when it survives across many horizons and remains closed under the emergence it generates.*

The theorem rules out several confusions. Profundity is not mere popularity, because a popular slogan may travel widely while losing coherence. It is not mere difficulty, because a difficult technicality may live in one narrow horizon. It is not mere age, because an old prejudice can persist without depth. Profundity combines breadth of support across horizons with faithful propagation under emergence. A profound idea can be translated, developed, and recomposed without becoming unrecognizable or empty.

Careful semi-formal restatement: For an idea carried by the horizon functor, consider the collection of horizons in which that idea admits a coherent restriction. This collection is its horizon support. Also consider the closure of the idea under the emergence cocycle, meaning the family of new contents generated by composing and transmitting the idea in ways that remain faithful to its structure. The degree of profundity factors through exactly these two features: breadth of horizon support and stability under emergence. Maximal profundity belongs to global sections that survive horizon translations and remain stable as their consequences unfold.

Take the idea of equality. It appears in mathematics, law, politics, ethics, religion, and everyday speech. It does not mean exactly the same thing everywhere. Equality of numbers, equality before the law, equality of dignity, and equality of opportunity are different. Yet the idea has broad horizon support because translations among these uses are often possible and fruitful. It also has emergence closure because new applications, such as equal access to digital spaces or equal concern in medical triage, can arise while still answering to the older structure. That is one reason equality feels profound rather than merely common.

Plato's Forms, Spinoza's eternal truths, Frege's thoughts, deep mathematical theorems, great metaphors in art, and universal validity claims in moral discourse all become candidates for profundity in this sense. The theorem also clarifies the opposite. Some ideas resist generalization. They matter because they are local, wounded, embodied, or singular. Theodor Adorno, pronounced approximately ah-DOR-no, called attention to non-identity, the remainder that concepts fail to capture. The Profundity theorem does not erase that remainder. It shows why low horizon support can be philosophically meaningful in a different way.

## Hearing the three together

The three theorems form a sequence. The Hermeneutic Sheaf theorem says when local meanings become one understanding. The Indexical Pullback theorem says why some meanings and inferences do not survive context shift. The Profundity theorem says which ideas remain stable and fertile across many such shifts. Together they turn context from a vague warning into a working structure. They also explain why interpretation, translation, and depth are linked. A deep idea is not merely one that many people repeat. It is one whose local readings can be glued, whose translations retain enough inferential life, and whose emergent developments remain faithful.

For the listener, these theorems are best remembered through three verbs: glue, break, endure. Meanings glue from local readings when overlaps agree. Inferences break when horizon translation changes the preservation, emergence, or grading that supported them. Profound ideas endure because they have broad support and stable emergence. The verbs are simple. The consequences are large.

## A longer example of the three verbs

Imagine a medical guideline moving through three horizons: a research hospital, a rural clinic, and a public news story. In the research hospital, the guideline is surrounded by trials, statistical thresholds, specialist vocabulary, and institutional review. In the rural clinic, it meets shortages, patient histories, transportation limits, and the practical wisdom of nurses who know which recommendations can actually be followed. In the public news story, it becomes a headline, a reassurance, a warning, or a political sign. The same guideline is not simply copied through three empty spaces. It is restricted, reweighted, and recomposed.

The Hermeneutic Sheaf theorem asks whether the local readings can glue. The researcher, clinician, and patient advocate may each hold part of the meaning. If their readings agree where they overlap, a public understanding of the guideline can form. If the researcher says the guideline is conditional and the news story presents it as absolute, the overlap fails. If the clinic's constraints are treated as irrelevant, the global meaning is false to one of its local pieces.

The Indexical Pullback theorem asks which inferences break during movement. In the research horizon, an inference from trial result to recommendation may be licensed by controlled conditions. In the rural clinic, the same inference may fail because storage, follow-up, or coexisting illness changes the preservation part of the idea. In the news horizon, the emergence part may change again, producing fear or hope that was not present in the technical document. The words travel, but not every inference travels with them.

The Profundity theorem asks whether the guideline has depth beyond local usefulness. A shallow rule may work in one hospital and collapse elsewhere. A profound medical principle, such as do not harm, survives many horizons and generates faithful new applications. It can guide surgery, consent, triage, research ethics, and public communication without losing its identity. Its profundity lies in support across horizons and in stable emergence under new cases.

This example shows why the three theorems belong together. Gluing without attention to breakage becomes naive consensus. Breakage without attention to gluing becomes cynicism about understanding. Profundity without both becomes mere admiration. The algebra asks for all three: local agreement, honest tracking of translation failure, and a test of durable depth.


## Chapter summary

The three horizon-functor theorems say that understanding glues from compatible local meanings, context shift can break inference in a measurable way, and profundity consists in broad horizon support plus faithful closure under emergence. These claims anchor the contextual extension of Idea Theory and prepare the later applications to hermeneutics, indexicality, paradigms, and depth.
