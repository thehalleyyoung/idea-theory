# Chapter 73 — Jerry Fodor

This chapter treats Jerry Fodor as a test case for idea theory. We keep the operating
definitions audible: composition puts ideas together, the identity idea leaves a composition
unchanged, the resonance pairing measures directed evocation, the preservation part,
suppression part, and emergence part describe what is kept, cancelled, and newly produced,
and the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — lets the
whole algebra vary by context.

## Biographical preamble

Jerry Fodor, pronounced approximately *FOH-dor* lived from 1935 to 2017, worked in the
United States, and belongs here to philosophy of mind and cognitive science. In one
compressed sentence, he defended the view that thought has a language-like structure. That
sentence gives the chapter its entry point: meaning is not a bare object sitting before a
spectator, but a structured achievement within a field of acts, signs, practices, or
horizons.

## Position

The position can be put simply. Fodor argues for a representational and computational theory
of mind. Cognitive processes are formal operations performed over syntactically structured
mental representations, and these representations form a language of thought (Mentalese)
with the productivity, systematicity, and compositionality that natural languages display.
The central argument from systematicity is that anyone who can think aRb can also think bRa:
thoughts come in systematically related families, and the best explanation of this fact is
that thoughts have constituent structure — they are built up from atomic concepts by
combinatorial operations. Modularity of mind, in The Modularity of Mind (1983), holds that
perception and language processing are accomplished by encapsulated, domain-specific,
mandatory modules. Higher cognition (central systems) is, by contrast, isotropic and Quinean
— and therefore, Fodor pessimistically concludes, less amenable to scientific theorizing.
The point is not merely historical. It tells us what kind of item is being counted as an
idea: not a loose mental image, but a content whose sense depends on the way it is given,
used, interpreted, or carried forward.

## Algebraic re-statement

In the algebraic re-statement, the basic domain is the field of contents named by the zoo
entry. Take the idea domain to be the set of mental representations in Mentalese: items as
syntactically structured tokens in a language of thought. Each element is tagged by its
constituent structure, its semantic content, and its computational role. The identity idea
is the trivial representation — the empty syntactic frame waiting for content. Composition
is syntactic composition: the construction of complex representations from atomic concepts
via phrase-structure-like combinatorial operations. composition preserves structural
relations needed to support productivity and systematicity. The resonance pairing is
inferential role: how much the relevant content licenses the same inferences as the
content under discussion within the cognitive economy. Read aloud, this says that the
thinker supplies a local model of the ten-tuple: a domain of contents, a composition rule,
an identity idea, directed resonance, the threefold division into preservation, suppression,
and emergence, an emergence cocycle, a degree of articulation, and a context-sensitive
horizon structure.

## Theorem re-statement

*The theorem-style claim is this: The first theorem-shaped claim is compositionality: the language of thought preserves compositional structure.*

In paragraph form, the claim rules out the fantasy that meaning is already complete before
context enters. The first theorem-shaped claim is compositionality: the language of thought preserves
compositional structure. The semantic content of a complex representation is determined by the contents
of its constituents and the way they are combined. This is a T6 and T1 claim jointly:
reframing preserves composition-structure. The second claim is that the language of thought
is a free monoid generated by atomic concepts. The prediction is that failures of
understanding will not be accidental noise. They will cluster where a local content cannot
be carried unchanged across a change of horizon, or where an apparent identity hides
suppressed difference.

Carefully restated, the relevant idea algebra has a domain of contents, an identity idea,
associative composition, the resonance pairing, the preservation part, the suppression part,
the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, the horizon category, and the horizon
functor. The central claim says that the philosopher's privileged object is stable only when
these roles cooperate. Where local data can be glued, the chapter instantiates T14, T15;
where translation fails, the obstruction is also audible as a horizon-functor effect.

## What the formalism adds

What the formalism adds is clarification without replacement. First, Fodor's productivity
argument becomes the free-monoid property of composition: finite atoms plus structured
composition yield an infinite set of constructible representations, and the systematicity of
related thoughts is a structural consequence. Second, the formalism makes explicit that
compositionality requires the language of thought to preserve compositional structure strictly — failures of
compositionality are failures of T6. Pragmatic effects, idiomaticity, and context-
sensitivity that violate strict compositionality become structural deviations from the
homomorphic ideal. Third, modularity becomes the structural assertion that certain sub-
algebras of the idea domain admit composition-closure restricted to their own elements:
representations within the module cannot interact with arbitrary other representations. The
Fodorian modularity thesis is given structural form. In ordinary language, the formalism
lets us ask which operation does the philosophical work. Is the key relation resonance,
because one content fulfills or evokes another? Is it suppression, because meaning appears
through contrast? Is it the horizon functor, because every local act changes when the
context changes? The answer differs from thinker to thinker, but the vocabulary stays
stable.

## Where it diverges

The divergence is equally important. First, the symbol grounding problem — how Mentalese
gets its content — requires connecting the idea domain to a causal theory outside the
algebra; the formalism cannot host intentionality without such an extension. The algebra
describes structure; content must be hooked into the world by some additional theory.
Second, the radical innateness of concepts that Fodor late in his career endorsed (most
lexical concepts are innate) is a substantive empirical commitment the algebra does not
require. The formalism is consistent with innateness, learning, or any mixture; Fodor's
specific position is independent. Third, the pessimism about central cognition — the claim
that central inferential processes are too holistic for cognitive science to model — sits in
tension with the algebra's optimism about systematic structure. This is not a defect in the
reading. It marks the price of any formal reconstruction: the algebra shows the load-bearing
structure, while the original philosophy often also includes tone, method, ethical urgency,
religious practice, literary performance, or first-person discipline.

## Horizon structure and the horizon-functor theorem

The horizon structure names the context in which the local algebra lives. Fodor's the
horizon category is the computational architecture of modules, central cognition, mental
representations, and causal-informational links to the world. Morphisms preserve syntactic
structure across computations while attempting to secure semantic content by nomic
relations. intentionality is mental aboutness, rigid reference is the atomistic concept as
stable symbol, schema or paradigm marks modular domains, and T14 pressures arise where
holism threatens compositionality. This is T15 for the language of thought: productivity and
systematicity require representational types stable across indefinitely many computational
horizons. For this reason the chapter should be heard through T14, T15. T13 is the
hermeneutic sheaf theorem, where local meanings glue into a global one when they agree on
overlaps. T14 is the indexical pullback theorem, where preservation and emergence fail to
translate cleanly. T15 is the profundity theorem, where depth comes from breadth across
horizons and closure under emergence.

## Derived primitives

The derived primitives make the diagnosis compact. intentionality: The language of thought
gives mental representations determinate intentional content. rigid reference: Informational
semantics seeks stable reference through lawful mind-world covariation. schema or paradigm:
Modularity partitions cognition into domain-specific input systems with proprietary
processing horizons. differentiation: Compositional syntax differentiates thought-
constituents so complex thoughts inherit systematic structure. None of these is introduced
as an extra metaphysical ingredient. Each is derived from the ten-tuple: intentionality from
resonance across horizons, temporality from ordered horizon-translation, perspective from a
home region in the horizon category, differentiation from suppression, and reflexion from
self-application of the horizon functor.

## Chapter summary

Jerry Fodor therefore enters idea theory as a disciplined local algebra of meaning. The
chapter has followed the promised path: biography, position, algebraic re-statement, theorem
re-statement, addition, divergence, horizon structure, and derived primitives. The result is
not a replacement for the thinker, but a listening guide to the formal pressure already
present in the work.
