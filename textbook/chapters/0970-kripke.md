# Chapter 77 — Saul Kripke

This chapter treats Saul Kripke as a test case for idea theory. We keep the operating
definitions audible: composition puts ideas together, the identity idea leaves a composition
unchanged, the resonance pairing measures directed evocation, the preservation part,
suppression part, and emergence part describe what is kept, cancelled, and newly produced,
and the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi — lets the
whole algebra vary by context.

## Biographical preamble

Saul Kripke, pronounced approximately *KRIP-kee* lived from 1940 to 2022, worked in the
United States, and belongs here to modal logic and analytic philosophy. In one compressed
sentence, he showed how a name can designate rigidly across possible situations. That
sentence gives the chapter its entry point: meaning is not a bare object sitting before a
spectator, but a structured achievement within a field of acts, signs, practices, or
horizons.

## Position

The position can be put simply. Kripke's Naming and Necessity overturns the descriptivist
theory of names: proper names are rigid designators, referring to the same individual in
every possible world in which that individual exists. Reference is fixed by an initial
baptism and propagated through a causal chain of communication, not by a cluster of
associated descriptions. Necessary a posteriori truths (e.g. "water is H 2 O", "Hesperus is
Phosphorus") and contingent a priori truths (e.g. "the standard metre is one metre long")
demonstrate that necessity and apriority come apart. The point is not merely historical. It
tells us what kind of item is being counted as an idea: not a loose mental image, but a
content whose sense depends on the way it is given, used, interpreted, or carried forward.

## Algebraic re-statement

In the algebraic re-statement, the basic domain is the field of contents named by the zoo
entry. Add to the algebra a set W of possible worlds and a designation function d n for each
name n: d n: W O where O is a domain of individuals. Rigidity is the constraint that d n(w)
is d n(w') for every w,w' where d n is defined. Initial baptism is an event b n is (n,o,c 0)
introducing the name n for object o at context c 0; the causal-historical chain is a
sequence of the emergence cocycle-transmissions of n preserving its referent. Necessary a
posteriori: an identity statement n 1 is n 2 where both are rigid is necessary if true at
all (since both designate the same o in every world), but its truth is established
empirically; thus the preservation part( (n 1 is n 2), evidence ) is non-trivial. Contingent
a priori: stipulating "let s be the length of this rod at t 0" makes "rod has length s at t
0" knowable a priori in the actual world while remaining false in worlds where the rod is
shorter. Kripkenstein: the rule-following paradox is a non-existence result for facts that
determine the preservation relation between two ideas for novel cases — no finite set of past uses fixes a
unique extrapolation; meaning, if it exists, is constituted by community assent (the
emergence part community). Read aloud, this says that the thinker supplies a local model of
the ten-tuple: a domain of contents, a composition rule, an identity idea, directed
resonance, the threefold division into preservation, suppression, and emergence, an
emergence cocycle, a degree of articulation, and a context-sensitive horizon structure.

## Theorem re-statement

*The theorem-style claim is this: Proper names are rigid; descriptions are typically not.*

In paragraph form, the claim rules out the fantasy that meaning is already complete before
context enters. Proper names are rigid; descriptions are typically not. Reference of a name
is determined by the causal chain from baptism to use, not by satisfaction of associated
descriptions. T-Kripke-3 (necessary a posteriori). Identity statements between rigid
designators are necessary if true; their truth is often a posteriori. The prediction is that
failures of understanding will not be accidental noise. They will cluster where a local
content cannot be carried unchanged across a change of horizon, or where an apparent
identity hides suppressed difference.

Carefully restated, the relevant idea algebra has a domain of contents, an identity idea,
associative composition, the resonance pairing, the preservation part, the suppression part,
the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, the horizon category, and the horizon
functor. The central claim says that the philosopher's privileged object is stable only when
these roles cooperate. Where local data can be glued, the chapter instantiates the profundity theorem, the indexical pullback theorem;
where translation fails, the obstruction is also audible as a horizon-functor effect.

## What the formalism adds

What the formalism adds is clarification without replacement. Possible-world semantics gives
a precise framework for distinguishing rigid from non-rigid designators, recasting old
debates in modal-logic terms. Causal-historical chains are exhibited as sequences in the
emergence cocycle, connecting reference to a tractable transmission relation. The split
between necessity and apriority becomes a structural fact about which dimensions of the
algebra (modal, epistemic) are involved. The rule-following sceptic is sharpened into a non-
existence claim, revealing what would be needed to refute it. The modal argument against
type-identity in philosophy of mind is reconstructible as: if pain and C-fibre firing are
both rigid and identical, then necessarily so, but there appear to be possible worlds where
they come apart. In ordinary language, the formalism lets us ask which operation does the
philosophical work. Is the key relation resonance, because one content fulfills or evokes
another? Is it suppression, because meaning appears through contrast? Is it the horizon
functor, because every local act changes when the context changes? The answer differs from
thinker to thinker, but the vocabulary stays stable.

## Where it diverges

The divergence is equally important. Kripke insists his lectures are intuitive and informal;
the heavy modal apparatus of possible-world semantics is one model among others, and Kripke
himself is often careful about its commitments. The causal theory of reference faces well-
known problems (Madagascar cases, qua-problem, twin earth) the algebraic chain does not
automatically solve. The treatment of natural-kind terms as rigid is more controversial than
the proper-name case, and the algebra glosses the distinction. Kripkenstein is presented as
an interpretation of Wittgenstein, whose own status as Wittgenstein-exegesis Kripke
explicitly brackets; the algebra makes no such hermeneutic gesture. The intuitive force of
Kripke's modal arguments depends on responsiveness to particular thought-experiments (Nixon,
Goldbach, gold); the algebra renders the conclusions but not the dialectical pull. This is
not a defect in the reading. It marks the price of any formal reconstruction: the algebra
shows the load-bearing structure, while the original philosophy often also includes tone,
method, ethical urgency, religious practice, literary performance, or first-person
discipline.

## Horizon structure and the horizon-functor theorem

The horizon structure names the context in which the local algebra lives. Kripke's the
horizon category is the category of possible worlds with accessibility morphisms, enriched
by baptismal contexts and causal chains of transmission. Rigid designators are exactly
constant global sections rigid reference: the name's referent remains fixed while
descriptions vary by world. credence separates epistemic evidence from modal necessity, and
schema or paradigm appears in rule-following as the community horizon that fixes practice,
if anything does. This is the profundity theorem for names and the indexical pullback theorem for rules: rigidity is trans-horizonal
stability, while finite past use fails to pull back into a unique future the preservation
part. For this reason the chapter should be heard through the profundity theorem, the indexical pullback theorem. The hermeneutic sheaf theorem is the hermeneutic
sheaf theorem, where local meanings glue into a global one when they agree on overlaps. The indexical pullback theorem
is the indexical pullback theorem, where preservation and emergence fail to translate
cleanly. The profundity theorem is the profundity theorem, where depth comes from breadth across horizons and
closure under emergence.

## Derived primitives

The derived primitives make the diagnosis compact. rigid reference: Rigid designators are
rigid reference by definition, naming the same object across possible worlds. credence:
Modal structure separates epistemic credence from metaphysical necessity, as in necessary a
posteriori truths. intentionality: Essentialist intentionality lets names and natural-kind
terms aim at origins and essences. schema or paradigm: Possible worlds provide the modal
schema within which necessity and contingency are evaluated. None of these is introduced as
an extra metaphysical ingredient. Each is derived from the ten-tuple: intentionality from
resonance across horizons, temporality from ordered horizon-translation, perspective from a
home region in the horizon category, differentiation from suppression, and reflexion from
self-application of the horizon functor.
