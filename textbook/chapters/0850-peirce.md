# Chapter 65 — Charles Sanders Peirce

This chapter treats Charles Sanders Peirce as a test case for idea theory. We keep the
operating definitions audible: composition puts ideas together, the identity idea leaves a
composition unchanged, the resonance pairing measures directed evocation, the preservation
part, suppression part, and emergence part describe what is kept, cancelled, and newly
produced, and the horizon functor — the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi —
lets the whole algebra vary by context.

## Biographical preamble

Charles Sanders Peirce, pronounced approximately *purse* lived from 1839 to 1914, worked in
the United States, and belongs here to pragmatism, logic, and semiotics. In one compressed
sentence, he said that meaning is found in conceivable practical effects. That sentence
gives the chapter its entry point: meaning is not a bare object sitting before a spectator,
but a structured achievement within a field of acts, signs, practices, or horizons.

## Position

The position can be put simply. Peirce founds pragmatism (later "pragmaticism," to
distinguish it from James's looser usage) with the maxim: to consider what effects, that
might conceivably have practical bearings, we conceive the object of our conception to have
— then our conception of these effects is the whole of our conception of the object. Meaning
is exhibited in conceivable practical consequences. The categories of Firstness, Secondness,
and Thirdness organize all phenomena. Firstness is qualitative immediacy (a felt quality),
Secondness is brute reaction (the resistance of one thing to another), Thirdness is
mediation (law, habit, sign-relation). Every phenomenon involves all three categories in
different proportions. The point is not merely historical. It tells us what kind of item is
being counted as an idea: not a loose mental image, but a content whose sense depends on the
way it is given, used, interpreted, or carried forward.

## Algebraic re-statement

In the algebraic re-statement, the basic domain is the field of contents named by the zoo
entry. Take the idea domain to be the set of signs in their full triadic structure: items as
standing for objects to interpretants. Each element is tagged by its sign-type (icon, index,
symbol), by its object-relation, and by the chain of interpretants it generates. The
identity idea is the bare sign-position — the empty triadic placeholder awaiting
determination. Composition is sign-combination: the composition of signs into more complex
signs, with the interpretant of one becoming a sign for the next. composition is constrained
by the categorial structure (Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness) and by the conventions and
habits operative in the community. The resonance pairing is interpretive proximity: how
strongly two signs participate in a common chain of interpretants and so converge on common
practical consequences. Read aloud, this says that the thinker supplies a local model of the
ten-tuple: a domain of contents, a composition rule, an identity idea, directed resonance,
the threefold division into preservation, suppression, and emergence, an emergence cocycle,
a degree of articulation, and a context-sensitive horizon structure.

## Theorem re-statement

*The theorem-style claim is this: The first theorem-shaped claim is that signification is irreducibly triadic: composition cannot be factored into dyadic operations.*

In paragraph form, the claim rules out the fantasy that meaning is already complete before
context enters. The first theorem-shaped claim is that signification is irreducibly triadic:
composition cannot be factored into dyadic operations. The Peircean insistence on triadicity
is a structural fact about the algebra. This is a T1 claim with constitutive triadicity. The
second claim is the pragmatic maxim: meaning is exhausted by conceivable practical bearings.
The prediction is that failures of understanding will not be accidental noise. They will
cluster where a local content cannot be carried unchanged across a change of horizon, or
where an apparent identity hides suppressed difference.

Carefully restated, the relevant idea algebra has a domain of contents, an identity idea,
associative composition, the resonance pairing, the preservation part, the suppression part,
the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, the horizon category, and the horizon
functor. The central claim says that the philosopher's privileged object is stable only when
these roles cooperate. Where local data can be glued, the chapter instantiates T15; where
translation fails, the obstruction is also audible as a horizon-functor effect.

## What the formalism adds

What the formalism adds is clarification without replacement. First, the irreducible
triadicity of signs gains structural form: composition is essentially three-place, and any
attempt to reduce it to a sequence of dyadic relations distorts the structure. Second, the
pragmatic maxim becomes the operational definition of the resonance pairing: meaning-
equivalence is convergence of conceivable practical consequences. The maxim is not a
stipulation but a structural rule for identifying elements of the idea domain. Third,
abduction as emergence becomes a precise inferential operator distinct from
deduction (the emergence cocycle-derivation) and induction (statistical the emergence
cocycle-extrapolation). The Peircean recovery of inference to the best explanation is given
algebraic location. In ordinary language, the formalism lets us ask which operation does the
philosophical work. Is the key relation resonance, because one content fulfills or evokes
another? Is it suppression, because meaning appears through contrast? Is it the horizon
functor, because every local act changes when the context changes? The answer differs from
thinker to thinker, but the vocabulary stays stable.

## Where it diverges

The divergence is equally important. First, the architectonic ambition of Peirce's mature
philosophy — the attempt to articulate a complete system encompassing logic, semiotics,
metaphysics, cosmology, and theology — far exceeds what any algebraic instantiation can
capture. Peirce was a system-builder; the algebra is a fragment. Second, Peirce's
evolutionary cosmology — the universe as growing in habits, with chance and law as
historical phases — depends on substantive metaphysical commitments outside the structural
facts. Third, the regulative status of long-run convergence is a hope, not a theorem. The
algebra cannot guarantee that the emergence cocycle converges, and Peirce's insistence on
the regulative ideal is partly a confession of this. This is not a defect in the reading. It
marks the price of any formal reconstruction: the algebra shows the load-bearing structure,
while the original philosophy often also includes tone, method, ethical urgency, religious
practice, literary performance, or first-person discipline.

## Horizon structure and the horizon-functor theorem

The horizon structure names the context in which the local algebra lives. Peirce's the
horizon category is the semeiotic and communal-inquiry space of signs, objects,
interpretants, habits, and future investigation. Morphisms carry a sign to further
interpretants, extending meaning through an indefinitely open chain constrained by reality.
intentionality is triadic aboutness, temporality is inquiry's future orientation, credence
is fallibilist credence, and schema or paradigm is the community of investigators as a
connected horizon. This is T15: truth is the trans-horizonal stability toward which inquiry
would converge under unlimited continuation of interpretant-morphisms. For this reason the
chapter should be heard through T15. T13 is the hermeneutic sheaf theorem, where local
meanings glue into a global one when they agree on overlaps. T14 is the indexical pullback
theorem, where preservation and emergence fail to translate cleanly. T15 is the profundity
theorem, where depth comes from breadth across horizons and closure under emergence.

## Derived primitives

The derived primitives make the diagnosis compact. intentionality: The sign-object-
interpretant triad formalizes intentional aboutness as mediated semiosis. potentiality:
Abduction opens potential explanations, proposing hypotheses before deduction and induction
test them. credence: Fallibilism assigns corrigible credence, oriented toward the long-run
community of inquiry. R. Semiosis is reflexive and self-correcting, since interpretants
themselves become further signs. None of these is introduced as an extra metaphysical
ingredient. Each is derived from the ten-tuple: intentionality from resonance across
horizons, temporality from ordered horizon-translation, perspective from a home region in
the horizon category, differentiation from suppression, and reflexion from self-application
of the horizon functor.

## Chapter summary

Charles Sanders Peirce therefore enters idea theory as a disciplined local algebra of
meaning. The chapter has followed the promised path: biography, position, algebraic re-
statement, theorem re-statement, addition, divergence, horizon structure, and derived
primitives. The result is not a replacement for the thinker, but a listening guide to the
formal pressure already present in the work.
