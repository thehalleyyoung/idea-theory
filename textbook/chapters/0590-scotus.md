# Chapter 39 — Duns Scotus

John Duns Scotus, pronounced approximately *dunz SKOH-tus*, lived around 1266 to 1308 in Scotland, England, France, and Cologne, within Franciscan scholastic philosophy. In his own translated words: “Being is predicated univocally of God and creatures.”

## The position in its own voice

Hear the doctrine in its own intellectual voice. Duns Scotus introduces the univocity of being (the concept of being applies in the same sense to God and creatures, though with infinite intensive difference), the formal distinction (less than a real distinction but more than a merely conceptual one), and haecceitas (the "thisness" that individuates a substance beyond its common nature). He prioritises the will over the intellect and grounds moral law in divine command, while preserving substantial intellectual content in moral reasoning. The voice trains attention by asking what is stable, what must be discarded, and what kind of insight appears only after that discipline has been accepted.

## The idea algebra sub-instance

Before translating, recall the operating definitions. Ideas compose with other ideas. Composition has a preservation part, a suppression part, and an emergence part. Ideas resonate with one another, and their non-reciprocal pull is meaning curvature. Ideas also live over horizons: contexts, stages, perspectives, times, practices, or worlds. Scotus treats being as a thin univocal concept spanning God and creatures while allowing infinite difference of mode. The preservation part is that shared concept. The suppression part blocks both crude sameness and total equivocation. The emergence part is formal distinction, where aspects are intelligible without being separate things. Haecceity supplies rigid thisness for individuals. Resonance tracks common concept, degree tracks intensity, and potentiality tracks real contingency. In plain English, the sub-instance asks what counts as an idea, how ideas combine, what survives, what is cancelled, and what new content appears.

## Central claim as theorem

*If metaphysics can reason about God and creatures in one science, then being must have univocal stability while individuals are fixed by thisness.*

The theorem rules out the easy opposite of the view. It predicts that across the proper horizons some content will travel, some will fail, and some will arise only through that failure. The theorem identifies the middle structure: invariant, obstruction, or gluing.

Carefully restated, choose the relevant horizon category and let the horizon functor - the rule mathematicians label with the Greek letter xi - assign a complete idea algebra to each horizon. Name composition, identity, resonance, the preservation part, the suppression part, the emergence part, the emergence cocycle, degree, and meaning curvature. Then the theorem says that Duns Scotus's central claim is exactly the stable support, failed pullback, or glued agreement described in the preceding paragraph, stated without symbols.

## What the formalism adds

It supplies a precise three-tier distinction system (real, formal, conceptual) through the interplay of , , and resonance-coincidence. Univocity is rendered as sameness of the preservation part-form combined with intensive grading, separating semantic and metaphysical questions usually conflated. Haecceitas is operationalised: the individuator is exhibited as a specific kind of idea with a singleton extension, available for further analysis. The will/intellect order is captured by an operator-priority relation, replacing vague talk of "primacy" with a specific algebraic claim about which operator fixes which class of facts.

A second gain is the separation between common being and individual thisness. The model can say that a shared concept travels across horizons while the individual is not reduced to a bundle of descriptions. A third gain is the account of contingency. Scotus’s modal imagination treats alternatives as genuinely open under divine willing, not merely as signs of our ignorance. The horizon reading makes that openness audible without collapsing it into randomness. More generally, the formalism adds comparison. It tells the listener whether preservation, suppression, or emergence is carrying the doctrine.

This also helps distinguish Scotus from both Aquinas and Ockham. Against pure analogy, the shared concept of being has real logical stability. Against nominalist thinning, the individual has more than a name or a bundle of signs. The algebraic profile therefore hears the Subtle Doctor as subtle: commonness, difference, and singularity are all preserved. It is exactly this balanced pressure that made his scholastic arguments so influential for later metaphysics.

This point matters greatly.

## Where the formalism diverges

The Scotist formal distinction is famously delicate; intensive grading and capture the structure but not the metaphysical motivation about preserving simplicity-with-distinction. Haecceitas is for Scotus a positive entity, not just an indexical ; the algebra's individuator is functionally specified and lacks the positive-yet-non-qualitative character Scotus insists on. Voluntarism risks appearing arbitrary in formalisation; The algebra is therefore an aid to hearing, not a substitute for reading.

Morphisms preserve the thin concept of being across God and creatures while adding modal and haecceitic determinations in local contexts. rigid reference is not merely naming but thisness as a constant individuating section, differentiation differentiates formalities without real separation, and credence enters through the modal space of synchronic alternatives. ## Chapter summary

Duns Scotus gives one answer to what an idea is, how it combines, and how it remains or fails to remain meaningful across horizons. The idea algebra restates that answer by naming preserved content, suppressed illusion, emergent insight, horizon behavior, and diagnostic derived primitives. The gain is clarity; the cost is abstraction. A good listener keeps both in view.
